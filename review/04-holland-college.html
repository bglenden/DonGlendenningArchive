extending-school-far-beyond-the-classroom-walls

Tara García Mathewson's article profiles expanded learning opportunities at Robert W. Traip Academy in Kittery, Maine, where competency-based education enables students to earn credits pursuing individual passions. James Lawrence, 2017 graduate and aspiring welder, completed art credit by carving duck decoys at a local technical center. Traip Academy requires students meet approximately 50 graduation standards, each addressing competency rather than seat time. Other examples include a student earning veterinary credentials through local internship and another gaining retail certification. Susan Johnson, expanded learning opportunities coordinator, notes such programs engage at-risk students who don't see value in traditional academics. Approximately 25% of spring graduates experienced expanded learning opportunities, with goals to expand significantly. Johnson emphasizes competency-based systems must change conversation about learning expectations, moving beyond traditional delivery models. Her advocacy for personalized, passion-based learning earned recognition as Lawrence W. O'Toole Award recipient from Nellie Mae Education Foundation for advancing student-centered learning in New England high schools.

Reason: Article written by Tara Garcia Mathewson (December 7, 2017), a third-party journalist. Don does not appear as author, subject, or cited source in the content preview. This is external reference material saved by Don, not his own writing.
