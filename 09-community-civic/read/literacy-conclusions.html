A comprehensive analysis of PEI's literacy crisis with observations on service fragmentation, insufficient school accountability, stigma around literacy programs, and disconnect between provision and needs. Don recommends separating literacy from school contexts, establishing clear standards, offering flexible scheduling, and creating specialized reading clinics. Includes appendices with demographic data and practical guidance for parents and policymakers.







Observations/conclusions


  • Clients fall into two broad categories – adults and young people still in school; there has been little improvement in levels of literacy in either group in past decade

  • There is little exchange of information among service providers

  • Services to adults, except for programs offered at Holland College, are quite ad hoc and often depend on annual applications for funding

  • There is a stigma attached to the term literacy; literacy may be an acceptable umbrella term but offerings to individuals should more specific - reading, problem solving, etc.

  • One piece of research suggested that what is provided is at odds with what is needed.

  • There is a view that the way schools are organized is at odds with requirements for successful literacy programs (classroom schedules, annual reassignments of teachers, etc)

  • Schools could benefit from some approaches used by private schools

  • No person or position has an overall view of literacy or is in charge of the response

  • Bursaries can play be an important motivator especially for people moving from Level two to level three.

  • It has been suggested that the “best bang for the buck” comes from helping people move beyond level two

Reading: Key to Education


  • Belief: Reading is a foundation skill, the golden key to education



The magnitude of the problem

  • 42 percent of the adult population does not possess level 3 literacy – the level thought to be needed to take full advantage of opportunities present in the global economy.

  • IALSS Study “Literacy rate did not change between 1994 and 2003

  • Globe and Mail Sept 15, 2009:children who aren’t reading well by the end of Grade 3 are at risk of dropping out or failing to graduate

  • Globe and Mail Sept 15, 2009: estimates that by the end of Grade 6, at lest 30 percent of Canadian students can’t read or write well enough to succeed in school

  • New York Times, March 4, 2011: “About three-quarters of the 17,500 freshmen at the community colleges this year have needed remedial instruction in reading, writing or math, and nearly a quarter of the freshmen have required such instruction in all three subjects. In the past five years, a subset of students deemed “triple low remedial” — with the most severe deficits in all three subjects — has doubled, to 1,000.”

  • Many articles in the Guardian

  • Many grandparents have told me that they have paid or are paying for private instruction in reading

  • That should not happen – unless a specialist says that the child cannot learn to read



Places to intervene

  • Identify communication /reading as a separate skill and include it on report cards

  • Set clear standards and adhere to them

  • Develop a rating scale

  • Make time the variable

  • Most students could earn an “A” if given a bit more time

  • Office admin program – 14 to 18 months to reach standard

  • Reading on report cards

  • Offer summer classes, night school, Saturday classes

  • Tutors

  • Establish reading clinic with specialists

  • 1956- visited a school in England where mornings were given to math , English and science and every students fitted into classes appropriate for their level

  • Drop the worm literacy and use either communication or reading

  • Benchmarks

  • Every teacher a reading teacher but we also need specialists

  • One article I read said that teachers only receive 24-36 hours of instruction in reading


What can you do?


  • Review your child’s report card. Do you understand it? Does it make sense? That doesn’t necessarily mean that you agree with it but does it make sense?

  • Talk to your child’s teacher about vocabulary, word recognition,

  • What action does the teacher recommendations?

  • Remember that the Minister sets the standard, defines the curriculum, licenses the teacher, hires teachers, sets the time allocations and other conditions under which your child learns.

  • If your concern is broader than just about your child, talk with other school officials, Department of Education officials or the Minister of Education.

  • Ask your teacher where she trained and how much training she had in the teaching of reading

  • Ask to see your child’s school file

  • Speak to other parents

  • Get together with other parents and talk with your teacher

  • Let people know that you are concerned
