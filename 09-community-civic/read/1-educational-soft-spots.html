A comprehensive essay identifying gaps and shortcomings in Canada's education system, particularly in PEI. Don critiques the rigidity of outcomes-based education (OBE), lack of school autonomy, absence of student advocates, and disconnection from job qualifications. He proposes five key reforms: updating OBE, increasing school-level autonomy, separate status for early childhood development, family education advisors, and establishing a qualification authority.


Educational soft spots

Education

It is widely recognized that education is a key factor in determining one’s quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of our citizens will determine to a significant degree the future of our community and country. Because of this, Canada is among the world leaders in our investment in ensuring that our citizens, especially our youth, have access to a variety of educational activities and experiences. Our education system developed over decades and served us well, however, but it is becoming rigid when the world is demanding flexibility. There is a gap between what Islanders have and what they want and Islanders want to play a meaningful role in bringing about the desired changes.

Education is a lifelong process of engaging in experiences that have a formative effect on the way we feel, think and act; this effect is usually expressed in terms of a person’s attitudes, skills and knowledge. Major contributors to one’s education include one’s self, one’s home and extended family, schools and the community at large. Schools play a unique role in the provision of education in that they are the only provider of educational services allowed to award credentials, the folded paper, so esteemed by our society.

This paper

In recent months, the writer has re-engaged in education by reading widely, conversing often, and writing occasionally; this paper is the result. He found a widespread interest in and understanding of the need for change, agreement as to the nature of the desired system, but little understanding among the public as to how the system works and how to bring about change. Based on these discussions and the writer’s knowledge and experience, the writer is of the view that the changes that will make a difference are unlikely to be put in place without some interventions from outside the system; this paper is presented as one small step in the change process.

As a sector, education has some unique features, monolithic, dogmatic and bureaucratic; promotes from within; almost totally government operated; offers very limited choice to users, tightly controls its members. It is probably the last major holdout to technology and modernization. As a result, the writer is of the view that the changes desired can only be brought about by demand from users, learners, parents, employers and the community at large. For this reason, this paper is written with the user rather than the provider in mind.

Soft spots

Through the filter of his education, experience and biases the writer has identified a number of soft spots, places that, in the view of the writer, could benefit from reinforcement or even reconstruction. None are cosmetic additional attention, even devellpment. Places Places where, if changed, may even be multilpliers of improvement. They include:



  • Lack of autonomy where learners meet the provider

Research shows there is a direct relationship between school performance and school autonomy. In the words of the DECD Report "In countries where schools have greater autonomy over what is taught and how students are assessed, students tend to perform better.





  • A tired OBE model

Curriculum is a key determinant of what happens in school. PEI, and the other Atlantic provinces are committed to outcomes based education. But the model is tired; updating is in order.


  • Absence of a Advocates

Getting a good education is both important and complicated; it is also a community matter. learners and their parents need help in their decision making. The current delivery model is light on advice and counselling


  • Early childhood Development is getting lost

There is increasing evidence as the importance of early childhood education; there is also increasing calls for a focus on development rather than migrating sxhool studies downward.


  • Broken links/Recover the link to Qualifications

Schools have a responsibility to prepare skills needed for the next level of education or to enter employment; the latter link has been lost.


A rebuilding plan


Being of the view that criticism should be accompanied by suggestions for improvement, in the pages that follow are a number of suggestions for ; seldom is there one way and one way only to institute improvement; again through the filter of my education, experience and biases, I have selected five changes; not the only five but ones that I believe will both improve the system now and enable positive changes for the future


The writer suggests the following focus for rebuilding:

  • Update OBE

  • Increase autonomy at school level

  • Separate status for youngsters

  • Family education advisors

  • Qualification authority
