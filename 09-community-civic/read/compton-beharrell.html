Letter from Don Glendenning to Cindy Beharrell (Simon Compton's daughter) following his death, offering CFPEI's services for establishing a memorial fund. Don reviews community foundation benefits and suggests potential fund purposes aligned with Simon's interests (church, Camp Gencheff, Bangor cemetery). Community Foundation & Civic Life

Cindy Beharrell


Dear Cindy:



I write, Cindy, to introduce the Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island and to offer our services should you be thinking of establishing a memorial in your Dad’s memory.  Your Dad was aware of the work of the foundation and made a contribution to one of our early initiatives.


First some comments about community foundations in general and the Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island in particular.


Community foundations, and there are 170 in Canada today, create and administer permanent endowments for charitable purposes.  The goal is to improve the lives of individuals and their communities.  This is accomplished by making grants for the purposes, and only the purposes, designated by individual donors.   Funds from donors are pooled and invested in perpetuity; earnings are used to make grants.  

The Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island serves the entire Province, is governed by a volunteer board widely representative of Island life, maintains an office in Charlottetown, has assets approaching $1.5 million, makes grants annually in excess of $50,000, is being used increasingly by Islanders to host their charitable legacies, is registered with both the Canada Revenue  Agency and the IRS,  is transparent and open while following sound business practices.

Investments are professionally managed, books are audited annually and reports are public.  Although one of the newer community foundations in Canada, we are well experienced in creating and administering  endowments (over 60 at the moment) for the  benefit of Island organizations and individuals. .

Historically, donors made separate donations to each organization they wanted to support; unfortunately, that approach results in the legacy being dispersed; in addition, not all charitable organizations are in a position to handle endowments effectively.  The alternative is to place one’s charitable legacy with a community foundation, thereby keeping the legacy intact and flowing grants to the individual charities of interest on a regular basis and in perpetuity.  It is the latter approach that we recommend and the one increasingly being chosen when establishing legacy funds.  

The establishment of a fund with CFPEI is quite simple; it involves a one page agreement setting out the name and purpose of the fund together with the names of the causes or organizations to which grants are to be made.  In the case of your Dad, interests could include his church, Camp Gencheff, the Bangor cemetery, or any number of other causes.  Should your interest be in a capital project, we could be of assistance also.


I am enclosing information about the Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island; should you have any questions, please do not hesitate to get in touch.  If you think it useful to discuss ideas and options, whether or not they include the Community Foundation, do not hesitate to call.  Whatever your decision, please know that I am willing to help in any way that I can.


I must tell you that my recent visit to Fox Meadow, my first since Simon died, brought back many memories and emotions of our friendship generally, but especially about the years  that we lived in the same Park in Mesa.  While at the Clubhouse, it was nice to have a conversation with Trudy, brief and all as it was.  We looked for Paul but he was not working that day.


My warmest wishes to all the family.


Sincerely


Don Glendenning, 

Director
