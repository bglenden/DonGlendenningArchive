Skill development needs a context


The meaning of skill development is not clear; at least that was the view of participants at a recent workshop on the subject. After some discussion the group settled on “increasing one’s skill to do “something”. That “something” could be citizenship, employment, further study or for all three, but it needs to be clear. Furthermore, the foundation for skills development lies in the home and the K-12 education system and Successful skill development programs require a high level of communication between all parties involved.


As well as learning in school or college, many people have learned new skills on the job. It would help if the new skills were documented. An independent qualification authority was also proposed as a way of improving consistency in grade levels across the Island; it would also be a way of recognizing skills learned on the job, through on line courses or even night classes.


Other ideas included offering short technical courses to help young people get their first job (three months was suggested) and then providing them with a voucher to pay for further training after they get some experience. Other suggestions included vouchers covering costs of courses on an employee’s own time even at night or on weekends. Traditional report cards lack details about skills and levels learned. One remedy is for institutions to follow the lead of PEI Workplace Learning in giving each student a list of competencies on completion of a that they can show to prospective employers.


These and other suggestions will be forwarded by Education 20/20, host of the evet, to booth Provincial and federal authorities.
