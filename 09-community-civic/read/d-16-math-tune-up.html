A 2021 essay by Don Glendenning examining mathematics education and youth preparedness for post-secondary transition. Drawing on the Community Foundation's 2013 youth survey, it discusses educational deficiencies and proposes education-focused solutions, reflecting Don's long-standing commitment to education reform and youth development through the Education 20/20 initiative.

ABOUT MATHEMATICS

June 2021

____________________________________________________________________________


In late 2013, The Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island (CFPEI) undertook a survey of over 200 Island youth to identify issues that directly affected them; the report, Taking the Pulse of Island Youth, available on the Foundation’s website at www.cfpei.ca, provides a snapshot of the findings. Bright spots include a positive sense of community, emerging entrepreneurial spirit, volunteerism and new postsecondary offerings. The main concerns include a lack of jobs, surviving on low-paying jobs, educational deficiencies and drug and alcohol addictions. On the subject of education and learning, the study reports a high rate of completion of high school and postsecondary education, a high level of inclusivity and relatively affordable tuition costs; young people also expressed concerns about their readiness for the next stage of life including:


“preparedness gaps with regard to readiness for

postsecondary study and the workplace” – Vital Signs, CFPEI, 2013


Following the publication of the CFPEI Report, Education 20/20 organized six public discussions for the purpose of informing the community of the report and its findings, learning more about youth issues in our communities, exploring issues of education and learning, and to identify actions to be taken to close gaps. Discussions were lively and wide ranging and carried on well after formal sessions ended. Education 20/20 retained an interest in the subject and was encouraged to resume discussions with providers and users of educational services, especially following the report of the Council of Ministers in July of 2016, which said


“A student’s ability to transition from secondary school to post-secondary education and the labour market is a key measure of the education system’s success for students” –

CMEC, July 2016


Since then, transition through education has been the subject of a number of informal discussions with educators, employers, students and the community at large. It is the writer’s view that, at the minimum, there is an issue of communication among parties involved or interested in the subject, a communication gap resulting from the number of service providers, lack of joint planning and limited ongoing coordination.


Mathematics is the study of the science of number, quantity and space; it is in demand to some degree by all citizens, employers, institutions and the community at large. The Oregon Department of Education contends that math provides “a foundation for the study of science and technology as well as the interpretation of quantitative information in other subjects. Mathematics may be structured in a number of ways but structure does not appear to have a particular influence on the quality of instruction.



NUMERACY

Numeracy is also mathematics, the application of math to solve problems encountered in every day life. Being numerate has been described as “having the confidence and skill to use numbers and mathematical approaches in all aspects of life - at work, in practical everyday activities at home and beyond, as consumers, in managing our finances, as parents helping our children learn, as patients making sense of health information, as citizens understanding the world about s an essential skill needed to some level by all citizens and can be learned to the citizen level by all or practically all Islanders. Furthermore, helping citizens acquire math skills for citizenship is one of the key roles of our public-school system but there appears to be no agreement, however, as to the nature and level of citizen math and how it should be determined.


MATH EDUCATION

A person’s education begins early; the ability to count is often quoted in reference to a child starting school and is often used by parents to support a view that their child is “bright”. At the same time, many parents, unintentionally I am sure, encourage the view that math is difficult, and the literature tells us that a person’s beliefs about mathematics can have a substantial impact on their interest, their enjoyment and their motivation in math classes.


Providers of math instruction: in PEI include:

  1. The public-school system plays a key role in increasing and formalizing each student’s knowledge of math. The school system accepts all children and not only provides but requires math instruction each year through to graduation. Math to be learned is identified for each grade and achievement is reported to parents and the public alike. Performance across course offerings has become a screening mechanism in the transition process.



  1. Postsecondary institutions provide math instruction in support of programs or majors generally; in addition, UPEI offers a math major and most recently created a School of Mathematics and Computational Science.



  1. SkillsPEI is a federally funded agency that purchases training under federal criteria. The writer is unclear as to how instructional content is determined or achievement measured Instruction in math may be provided by provincial schools and institutions, by employers and their organizations or NGO’s on behalf of clients. Content is determined or agreed upon at time of purchase. Except for adult upgrading, there appears to be little coordination of math content across courses. It doesn’t appear that NGO operators can or do tap into available provincial instructional resources prepared elsewhere in the system

  2. Private schools offer instruction with content being aligned with the relevant provincial curriculum.

  3. Self study by individuals to improve their math skills on their own or with the help of family, friends, tutors, on line courses and other personal learning strategies. Independent learners lack a simple mechanism for demonstrating their proficiency levels


Outcomes and competencies themselves are “gradeless” meaning that that any competency can be studied in public school, college and university or independently. What changes is the level achieved on each outcome. As to proficiency levels, there appears to be some agreement that differences in math levels may be attributed to three factors:

• The number of skills that can be demonstrated

• Levels achieved on skills demonstrated, and

• Nature or degree of clustering of skills learned.


Grade levels in the public-school system are established by Department of Education; individual student proficiency levels are awarded by teachers. Levels in postsecondary institutions are set in the institution. This in spite of the fact that awarding levels is a regulatory and not a teaching function. Furthermore, in the public domain regulatory functions are usually the responsibility of a separate body.

FINDINGSS

Our search revealed:

• A 2002 multi-country study by the European Commission found that, if the national average educational attainment level is increased by a single year, aggregate productivity increases by 6.2 per cent right away and another 3.1 percent in the long run.

• The Conference Board of Canada (undated) reports (1) a “C” grade on numeracy skills, (2) that forty five percent of Canadian adults have inadequate numeracy skills – a significant in crease from a decade ago and, (3) no Province earns above a “C” grade for numeracy skills

• A report by the PEI Department of Education on The Early Numeracy Intervention Pro-gram (ENIP) which found that numeracy “gives students the tools and the skills to solve problems logically, and to developmental flexibility, independence of thought, self-efficacy, and the ability to persevere.” Typical numeracy lists are somewhat situation specific – personal, workplace, community and further learning.

• The UK introduced a new measure of numeracy proficiency … for all young people at the age of 14

• University of Canberra (Australia) reports success in “Delivering Numeracy in remote indigenous contexts”.

• Extensive references are found to “Closing the Numeracy Gap in Ontario”

• Math instruction could benefit from increased practice, increased writing and conversation about the subject.

• The PEI government has struck a task force to investigate transition from public school yo further education and to work.

CONCLUSIONS

Based on the literature, the above noted discussions and the writer’s experience, the writer concludes that:

1. Math instruction in PEI is fragmented

2. Cooperation among providers is hampered by the lack of an agreed- upon definitions of a number of key concepts.

3. Broadly speaking, education lacks an overarching statement of philosophy and direction;

4. There is no formal mechanism for coordination among math providers

5. Evaluation criteria for math should be the same for all levels of education.

6. Improving transition requires cooperation of all math instruction providers.

7. There is an interest among math providers in cooperating on a pilot project


RECOMMENDATIONS:

Based on our exploration, Education 2020 recommends:

1. An over-arching statement of beliefs and principles

2. Numeracy be offered as a separate subject on PEI

3. Numeracy curriculum be based on skills used by citizens in their everyday lives

4. Levels of numeracy be established separate from student grade levels

5. Numeracy programs be competency based.

6. The Province strike a numeracy advisory committee that is broadly representative of the community served.

7. PEI adopt a dynamic community-based rating scale for numeracy

8. Student progress be recorded on a competency-by-competency basis.

9. Instruction be based on skills practised in sector or community served

10. Each student be given a master list of skills as a basis for planning and tracking progress.

11. A student learning guide be developed for each competency

12. Have students’ self- rate as part of the learning process

13. Adopt a common platform for curriculum development in mathematics to include field analysis, evaluation criteria and rating scale

14. Prepare a lexicon of relevant education terms – outcomes, competencies, rating scale, etc.

15. Identify key math profiles i.e., citizenship, employment entry and entry to post secondary institutions

16. Develop a learning guide for each competency.

DEFINITIONS:

OBJECTIVE

To initiate changes in the math offerings that maximizes opportunities for Islanders to gain the math skills needed to thrive in in future years.


EDUCATION

Our futures and the future of our community will depend in large measure on the nature and level of education gained by our citizens. Provinces have a responsibility to help their citizens develop attitudes, skills and knowledge at least to the level of good citizenship; It is my view that Provinces also have a responsibility to ensure that their communities have the skilled people needed to care for the community in which they live. A 2003 multi-country study by the European Commission found that, if the national average educational attain-ment level is increased by a single year, aggregate productivity increases by 6.2 per cent right away and by a further 3.1 per cent in the long run.


Education is offered through a public-school system devoted to serving the needs of young people, a relatively autonomous postsecondary sector, the private sector and a conglomeration of services operated or administered through provincial ministries of learning, training and workforce development; much of the latter is paid for by federal dollars. There is a gap, however, between what education offers and what the community expects. The Council of Ministers of Education Canada (CMEC) highlighted this concern in its statement of July 6. 2016 when it said the need to improve transition is shared by many educators, employers, certification agencies and the community at large.


MATHEMATICS EDUCATION

The application of mathematics to every day life is considered essential in every day life and, for the most part, is organized around the conventional structure of mathematics: arithmetic, algebra, geometry, trigonometry, calculus, probability and statistics. The goal for students of PEI Grade 9 mathematics is to “become mathematically literate adults”. It is an achievable goal, at least for most students but, if math test scores and public comment are used as the measure, the goal remains a challenge rather than an accomplishment.

Providers who deliver math services to youth follow a curriculum approved by the Department of Education based on a Curriculum Based Outcomes model. Other providers base math instruction on a variety of sources. On the other side of the coin, “receivers” of students have a variety of math interests and needs not always well- documented. The format and wording of existing math competencies reflect educator-thinking rather than learner needs.


MATH ISSUES

1. Communication, coordination, collaboration and social capital building between and among stakeholders.

2. Extent to which resources organized for learning

3. Need for over-arching philosophy, goals and principles.

4. Standards and assessment

5. Transition

6. Structural impediments

7. Need for a learner centered delivery model

8. Better reporting of learner progress


RECOMMENDATIONS:

Education 20/20 recommends that PEI:

1. Adopt competencies as a basis for program and curriculum development

2. Update curriculum development practices including the adoption of a universal cur-riculum platform suitable for all grade levels

3. Allow students beyond Grade 9 to study at their own pace

4. Develop or adopt a dynamic/holistic rating scale applicable for study beyond Grade nine.

5. Report student achievement in Grades 10, 11 and 12 on the basis of competencies demonstrated and levels achieved

6. Adopt a qualification stance on grading by establishing an independent assessment body to establish levels, undertake assessment and certify levels achieved.

7. Establish a user-based math advisory committee

8. Encourage greater communication and teamwork among math professionals.


BENEFITS

1. Improves support for independent learning

2. Improve communication among stakeholders

3. Increase relevance

4. Increases engagement of learners

5. Improves consistency across the system
