Letter dated August 14, 2007, from Don Glendenning to Barbara McInnis addressing her book on community foundations. Don provides thoughtful observations on asset-building challenges in small communities, out-migration of young entrepreneurs, and competitive fundraising landscape dominated by universities, hospitals, and cultural institutions. He emphasizes community foundations' overlooked leadership role in convening stakeholders to solve problems and advocates for viewing them as essential community infrastructure. References his international development experience and explores how community foundations might serve international development contexts.

Date: August 14, 2007


To: Barbara McInnis


From: Don Glendenning


Subject: Community foundations


Greetings from Prince Edward Island.


I am not sure that I have any observations of value when writing your book but here are a few comments: 


First let me point out some of the unique problems of asset building in a small community and small province.  It is likely that many of this same problems exist in other remote communities in this country and countries as well.


Headquarters for many of potential corporate donors/sponsors are most often located in another Province not just another part of the Province.  The larger the company the more likely that its headquarters is Montreal, Toronto or Vancouver.  It is not easy, and sometimes expensive, to meet the decision-makers.  

We have a large out migration of young  people, therefore, many of our successful young entrepreneurs do not live on PEI.

On the other hand, contacting and convening key decision-makers or stakeholders is, I believe, easier to accomplish.

In the case of PEI, the big four in fund raising are the University, two hospitals and the Confederation Centre.  While we have cordial relations with all, we have not found a way yet to partner in a significant common venture.


With regard to your specific questions:


I’d like to see more emphasis on the leadership role of community foundations.  We underestimate the importance of our non-monetary contributions.  The process of convening stakeholders to solve problems has tremendous potential.  The issue of asset development versus leadership/liaison poses a dilemma.  A community foundation needs an endowment to address local needs (make strategic grants), at the same time, many important contributions can be made by convening stakeholders. 

 

Community foundations should be viewed as part of the infrastructure of a community.  Like a highway, a power line, a bank or a telephone system, a community foundation is a mechanism for the use of persons who have the means to effectchanges in their communities


While the ideal is undesignated funds, in fact, in PEI there are millions of dollars in small funds managed/invested by volunteers out of duty rather than interest.  I believe the current system to be inefficient and see the management of existing funds as a real service to our community.  I know that this is at odds with much of the thinking of many community foundations.


I’ve had some involvement in international education in a number of countries.   The idea of self-help in the charitable sector did not enter any discussions.  Would there be any value in providing development officers/NGOs with an understanding of community foundations as a tool for community development? 


In Eastern Europe, I believe, the concept of charitable giving is not well developed.  Stressing convening rather than asset development may be a more acceptable weay to initiate change.


Women are often a primary force for community development in other cultures.  Whether or not this has any import for what you are writing, I do not know.


Good luck with your book.


Warm personal regards


Don Glendenning
