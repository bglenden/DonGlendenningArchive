Follow-up


Following some items in Issue 31, Jack Wilson tells me that he purchased some items from a sale at the John W. MacNaughton property including a octagonal plant stand inscribed on the; under side “Fraser MacKay, June 22, 1944”.  I think that was the year Fraser graduated.


Plant stands were a standard shop project.  There are probably not many homes in Black River today without one.  If my memory serves me right, they were octagonal, eight and certainly no more than 10 inches across, had four legs and stood about 24 to 26 inches tall.  Most were finished with shellac and varnish; some may have been stained – usually walnut.


My brother Ken reminded me that my little red tractor did not come from Santa after all.  It was a gift from one of the “MacDonald Sisters” probably Frances and may have been a thank you for the milk which we delivered to them while they were in residence in Black River.  They lived in Portland but usually summered in the old home, the Ken MacDonald place, on the spot where Cameron Macdonald now lives.  (Ken’s Hill originated from the fact that Ken MacDonald owned the property and lived nearby.)


I appreciate hearing recently from Robin Farrell, a first Cousin once removed, (figure that out) now living with his family in Rochester, New York.)


The Electric Iron


During the first years of their married life, Dad worked at construction sites, usually dam sites as far as I know.  Some of the sites had electricity, probably to power some of the equipment but also available to the homes where the families lived.  As a result, Mum had an electric iron among things stored in the closet at the head of the stairs (the equivalent of the attic in novels and movies).  Sometimes on a stormy wintry day, I was allowed to root around in the attic.  That’s where the electric iron was kept.


Before the advent of wrinkle free or wrinkle resistant fabric, most clothing required pressing.  (Even men’s dress pants were pressed from time to time.)  Irons 


for pressing usually came in sets of three and were warmed on the stovetop.  A detachable handle was used to change irons when a hot one was needed.   Ironing was a necessary and frequent task when raising a family of nine – and I understand that Walter always wore a white shirt when courting Agnes and he courted most nights of the week.


Anyway, with the arrival of electricity, the old iron was retrieved from the closet, plugged and provided a number of additional years of useful service.


Flinch


Flinch was a very popular game at our home.  Although I played it, I especially remember a crowd of young people, only the boys I think, gathered around our dining room table of an evening.  I remember the Harpers, Adams and our own crew and I think Roy and Win and others.  


Flinch is played with one or more packs of cards; cards are numbered from one to 15.  One has a hand, a flinch pile where you lay down a card at the end of your turn and a stack of cards from which players take new hands.  The objective is to get rid of your flinch pile first.  If you had a card in your flinch pile and did not play it, you were flinched – that is, the player who spotted it gave you a card from his pile.


I know that we played it at home, Venetia played it with her family, Ken and his family played it and probably others.  Flinch was first produced by the Flinch Card Company in Kalamazoo, Mich.  in 1902.  Parker Brothers produced it under license from 1904, and later bought the rights.  It was produced in Canada under license by Copp Clark Co. Ltd.  The Museum of Games at Waterloo University bought a copy of the game in 1979. 


I’ve asked a number of people and Flinch seems to have been unique to our family.  My game was produced by Parker Brothers in Salem, Mass.  Doreen (Glendenning) Nicol suggested that the game came to us from my sister, Grace, who lived at one time in Salem and who probably sent it as a Christmas gift.  Makes sense.


Carol and I spent Christmas in Ottawa; I took my Flinch game along.  Carol, Doreen, Janet and I played Flinch – and enjoyed it.  I wonder if any readers remember the game.


Glendinning


There was a village on Megget Water near Langholm, Scotland named Glendinning; it was a center of antimony mining.  The village has disappeared.  Anyone interested can look it up on the web.


Vehicles


Dad owned a Model T Ford; I do not remember it.  Lillian thought that he bought it from McLennan’s garage in Chatham - in the corner of the park across from the Post Office?  The gun registry?   Once, when bringing Grace home from Success Business College in Moncton, (Gladys MacNaughton also came up with them) he rolled the car at Gilbert’s Corner.  Gilberts Corner was the junction where the road from Moncton met the road north to Chatham.  Grace broke her wrist in the accident and Dad had some cuts and bruises.  Lillian went with Dad.


On the way home also, someone tried to run Dad off the road – Lillian thought they were rum runners.  Dad pulled in to a store and phoned the RCMP in Newcastle.  Someone came, probably Bedford Peters, Dad’s cousin.  There were no sign of the people but Lillian rode back to Black River in the police car.


Later Dad purchased a 1929 Model A, two-door sedan from the Babineau (sisters, I believe) who owned the cinema in Chatham.  I remember it sitting in the wagon shed at the west end of the barn presumably it was in storage because money was scarce.  Walter and Dad cut wood up Bay du Vin River the winter they bought the car.


Later in the 1930’s it was taken out of storage and used around the farm by all the family except me.  Returning from a trip to Bathurst, Walter and Dad brought a half-ton of green cod, towed a trailer with two horses he bought, and had two geese on the trunk carrier on the rear.  I remember stories of Ken falling out on a turn and being held by Venetia until the car stopped, because fuel was gravity feed having to back up the Tabusintac Hill, Norm and Ken walking to the bottom of the Tabusintac Hill for water because Dad did not want to stop at the bottom in case he could get all the way to the top; raising the hood and seeing the red hot manifold when Walter had come home through deep snow, Norman taking out the back seat and bringing home 5 sheep, Elinor and I riding with Dad and his sister Venetia when he decided to show her the Mac Property (he was not a good driver).  


Next we had a chocolate-brown, 1939 Ford half-ton that I think that John bought from Bonnie Flanagan.  Bonnie Flanagan lived just north of Kouchibouguac, Lakeville I think, and traveled around the country buying meat.  He was a very outgoing man.  John always kept the truck in tip-top condition but we seemed to have had many problems with the fuel pump and brakes.  Many a time I remember taking the truck to Carmen (?) Nevin’s Garage (or nevin and Golette?) in Chatham to be there when the garage opened at 8:00 am.


Once while haying in Napan, a property near Edith Bremner?, I was designated to take the truck in town for brake work.  As I came to MacDermiad's, they were unloading hay and the horse was crossing the road on the forker line.  I couldn't stop.  Fortunately, they tripped the load in the barn and the forker line dropped to the road and I rode over it.  If it hadn’t dropped, I am not sure just what would have happened.


Homemade Tractor


Speaking of vehicles, my brother, John and Brydone, his brother-in-law, built a homemade tractor, I think just before Christmas, November maybe, of 1940.  (Brydone was home on leave and they did the actual work in the old shed/barn at Walter’s.  I don’t think they had the benefit of a welder although they may have had some pieces welded in Chatham.


My recollection is that the engine and frame was an Oakland car (Alex MacDonald’s car, maybe) and a Godferson rear end.  (Didn’t A&R Loggie Company use Godferson trucks?)  The tractor used a transmission from each.  So if both were in reverse, the tractor went forward.


There were two weaknesses, as I recall, it was very heavy and got mired easily.  One could not work wet land or land early in the spring.   The other was the torque when in low gear, broke the bolts holding the transmissions together.  I remember the first time John drove it to the Branch (a wet, snowy, mean day) and I recall having a drive on it.
