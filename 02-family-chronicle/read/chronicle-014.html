Home-made Saw


Winter was the time to cut wood, not only for our own use but to sell; we usually sold 24' furnacewood.  The yard above the house would have a huge pile left in long lengths.  In the spring, the wood cutter would be set up to cut the wood into stove (16 in.) or furnace (24 in.) lengths.  Logs too large to be wrestled to the table of the wood-cutter, were cut with a cross-cut saw.


I do not know where all the wood was sold but for about 20 years several cord of wood was sold to Vince Flaherity in Loggieville.  Dad also sold wood to the Branch school.  The story is told that Dad had sold a set number of cords of wood to the Branch school for a number of years and at the end of each school year there was a bit more left over than was the case the previous year.  Then the contract was changed to another supplier for the same number of cords.  Gradually the surplus was used up and the number of cords per year had to be increased.


In early years the wood was hauled by sled or wagon; later a truck was hired to take the wood to Loggieville.  I recall one fall day when the truck was being loaded, the driver saying@you=re being too honest@ with your measure.


Walter and John would go to the woods and cut, split and load a half cord on each of two sleds; the next day, they would deliver the wood to Chatham or Loggieville  - two men, two horses, two days plus the value of the wood for $5.  Walter also told me about he and Dad coming out from the woods in late afternoon with a load of wood when the wagon broke (I cannot recall what part broke).   They unhitched the team and came on home for supper; later that night, they returned to the woods and Walter held the lantern while Dad cut a tree and fixed the wagon.


Anyway, what I really intended to tell was about one winter when the yard seemed fuller than usual of huge maple and beech trees cut on the Aridge@.  Dad, presumably with Walter and John helping, rigged up a powers saw to cut the logs into two-foot furnace wood.  The saw was set up in the back yard.


My recollection is that there was a bed, perhaps 15 feet long, made of 2 x 8 or 10 inch plank 12 to 15 feet long and 2-3 feet wide; in it there were rollers to make it easy to roll the log along.  Toward the left end and cutting across the log was a Across-cut@ saw to which had been attached a handle.  The other end of the saw had a wooden shaft, not unlike that for a mowing machine.  The end of the shaft was attached to a mowing machine wheel with the lugs removed.  When the wheel turned, the saw moved back and forth, again like a mowing machine knife.  There was a second mowing machine wheel, which acted as an idler wheel,  mounted on the same shaft.  A belt was run from the engine to the idler wheel.  The belt was pushed from the idler wheel to the wheel with the saw in order to make the saw move back and forth.  Walter or John guided the end of the saw with the handle.


It was crude but , as I recall, it worked; on the other hand, I cannot recall it being used a second winter



Drownings


I was trying to recall the year that the two men were drowned at Victoria Bridge.  Seems to me they had been cutting or peeling pulp at Hexham or Bayside; I am not sure who they were working for.  Seems to me that one was named Larry Brideau and the other was ? Paul.  After work on Saturday, they went for a swim and both drowned.  I know that a diver was brought in to search for the bodies.


Hexham


Speaking of Hexham, wasn=t there a MacLenaghan girl killed in an accident at the Hexham corner?  Seems to me that a stick came off as a pulp truck turned the Hexham corner and the stick went through a car window killing the girl.  Would that have been a sister of Marjorie (MacLenaghan) Gibson?


That is the corner where Uncle Bob and Aunt Bioney lived but I=ll save stories about them for a later Chronicle.


Cutting Hay at the Brook


Brydone told me that when Dad was sick, and he wasn=t well for a number of years, that he had Walter take him on the wheel barrow down to the brook where the men were scything hay.  While Dad was not well enough to scythe, he would sit in the wheel barrow with his whetstone and sharpen extra blades so that the men had a sharp blade waiting each time they came by.. 


Uncle Jim=s Machine Shed 


While visiting a pioneer village in Russia recently, I saw where they used to store machinery on the second floor of a barn or shed.  You do not see that very often.  In fact, the only one I can remember was at Uncle Jim=s.  The end of the shed away from the road had a ramp and machinery was stored on the second floor.  Were there other machinery storage sheds like that?  Are any still standing?  Strange how one thinks of things.


Extinct Equipment


When I travel, I like to visit museums especially agricultural museums.  In all of my visits, I have never encountered an actual or even a working model of a mud digger.  I guess, because they were large and awkward, and made of wood, they did not survive.  


The other piece of equipment I have only encountered once is a horse-operated hay press - a forerunner of the hay baler.  I have not found one in a Canadian museum although a friend said that he saw one in a museum in Ontario.  I found two, in fact, at a museum in Las Cruces, New Mexico the year we visited Janet.  These were heavy pieces of equipment and would likely go for scrap.

 

The hay press would be set up at a barn and was powered by a horse walking in a circle.  My job was to keep the horse moving and not to let it nibble at the bale as it emerged from the press.  Bales usually weighed from 90 to 110 pounds.


John travelled from place to place in the fall with a team of horses and the hay press.  Later, when Norman purchased the tractor, the were able to travel even further from one end of the Miramichi to the other.
