Second Family Chronicle (November 4, 1997) explores ancestral origins and family connections. Don notes a discrepancy regarding his Great-Grandmother—he believes it was Elspeth Jardine (not Elizabeth). Includes a 1991 Scot's Magazine letter from Denis Glendining about ancestors and historical records. Provides earliest documented family dates and discusses how many generations removed Don is from various ancestral lines (6th generation Glendenning, 5th generation Watling/MacDonald/MacAskill/MacNaughton/Leach/Rae, etc.). Fascinating section on "Wanderers" describing how many Glendennings and Watlings emigrated to Portland, Maine and other US locations in the early 1900s, including Don's parents' journey. Vividly describes Jane Watling (Don's mother) arriving in Portland January 24, 1908—the day Portland City Hall burned.

Jardine Connection


There is a discrepancy between my information and that found in the recent Jardine history.  I believe that my Great Grandmother was Elspeth Jardine who married John MacDonald; the Jardine family history shows Elizabeth Jardine married to John MacDonald.  I would appreciate help in sorting this out.


Scots Magazine


A friend of mine gave me a clipping, actually a letter to the editor, from the Scot=s Magazine from 1991.  It reads as follows:


Villa d=Este 4,

50 Park Drive

Port Elizabeth 6001

RSA


Dear Sir, 

One of my ancestors, Simon Glendoning, fought at Bannockburn, and his sword is known to have been in Parton,  Kirkcudbrightshire, as late as 1809 when my great-grandfather, William Glendonwyn, a sea captain, died.  He was succeeded by his daughter, Xaveria, who died unmarried in 1858.  Her successor was Frederick Scott who married a Miss Cameron.  These two adopted the name Glendonwyn.  


Can anyone help me trace the burial places of the Glendonwyn/Glendoning/Glendining family ?  Also, where is the sword now ?

Denis Glendining

Montague, PEI


Recently, I visited the Montague Museum.   Their collection includes a photo of three children - Harriet, Ruth and Findley Glendenning.  The photo was taken by “Montreal Studio”, and was found in a house in the Montague area.  Any ideas?


Daley


Walter Glendenning was asking about our connection to Hart Daley.  According to Joy (Glendenning) Winton, Hart, James and Wilfred were brothers.  Their father was John Daley who was married to Susan Peters, a sister to my grandmother, Elizabeth (Peters) Glendenning.



Knowles


A sister of Gramma (Peters)Glendenning, Jane, married Henry Knowles.  They had five children: Rufus, Sam, Evelyn, Lillian and Elizabeth.  I still think that there is a connection through the Harleys but have not been able to confirm it.




“Mac” MacDonald


AMac MacDonald, a first cousin of my mother, was killed as a young man while working on the Newcastle (Morrissy) Bridge.  He was a brother, I believe, of Alex, Vada, Frances, Isabel and Elspeth.  Was Mac a nickname?  Was his name really Hugh?


Origin of Names


Until about 1000 AD, most people in Europe had only one name.   Later, to distinguish one person from another, a second name was added.  The second name was usually taken from the person’s occupation, location, father’s name or some other characteristic.


Sandra Devlin, who writes a regular column on genealogy, provides the following: Brewster one who makes beer, Clarke from clerk, Cooper one who made hoops for barrels, Roper was a rope or netmaker, Travers was a toll bridge collector, etc.


The Glendinning is believed to be locational in nature and probably means “one who came from the hidden dale”.  Glen is a common prefix and signifies a narrow valley bounding a river or stream.  While the two most common spellings are Glendenning and Glendinning,  Glendining, Glendowyne, Glendinnin, Glendynwyning, Glendouwyne, Glendoun and Glendoning are all considered part of the same family.  Some of the oder Scottish maps still show a place called “Glendinning” about 25 miles north of Annan.  It was known for its rich antimony mines.


My Great-grandfather, who came to New Brunswick in 1830, was born at Callister Hall about 25-30 miles north of Annan.  Callister Hall still stands and is shown on some Scottish maps.  It is a one story stone cottage on a rise in sheep grazing country.  Local information says it was a stage stop at one time.  I was told also that it had burned but, being stone, the damage was only to the interior and roof.  It is still used as a home today.


Our Great-grandfather sailed from Annan about 25 miles from where he lived.  At one time Annan was a busy seaport but now the mouth of the river is heavily silted. 


Wanderers


One is struck by the fact that the Glendennings and Watlings, especially of my mother’s and father’s generation, were great travellers.  As with so many Maritimers, they found their way to the ‘Boston States”.  That generation seemed to have picked Portland as a place to work.  Among the Glendennings we know to have worked and/or lived there were Elmer, Venetia, Frank, Margaret and Ray.  Among the Watlings were George, Jack, Janet, Jim and Jane.


Also Chipman and Guy Glendenning worked in South America, Leonard Glendenning lived in or near Palerma, Maine.  Ernest Watling worked with Elmer Glendenning in Tennessee; he also worked for a while in Niagara Falls.  It is likely, also, that others of the family worked away from New Brunswick.


On the Glendenning side, Venetia (Glendenning) Doring, who was married and lived in Falmouth Foreside, near Portland, may have provided place for her brothers to stay on arrival in Portland.  We do not really know.


Jane (Watling) Glendenning’s travel dates are better known than most.  I remember her telling me that she arrived in Portland on the day that the Portland City Hall burned.  A phone call to the Portland Library revealed the date to be January 24, 1908 - just a month before her 18th birthday.  She soon became aware of, if not to know, the Glendennings because she also said that she could have gone to my Grandfather=s funeral, February 14, 1908.  She did not go but baby sat someone’s children.


Interesting to speculate about connections between the two families.  I believe that Jane stayed with her brother George when sh first arrived in Portland; George, I believe, worked for a while with Elmer Glendenning.  Then again, Mum’s brother Jim worked up in the woods with one winter with Elmer Glendenning.
