Typical Community Events


School closings – the Little Branch School, like other one-room schools, held both a Christmas and June school closing; the public was invited to both.  The school was swept and tidied up, Christmas or floral designs were stenciled on the blackboards and student names and standing by grade were also listed on the blackboard.  Then there was singing (no piano) recitations and skits organized so that every student had a role.  I imagine that Santa Claus visited although I cannot really recall it.  There was fudge and Christmas candy for all.  We must have exchanged gifts but again, I do not recall and there would be a gift for teacher.


MRHS – By 1940, there was also a Christmas concert and June graduation at the Miramichi Rural High School.  The Christmas concert usually included items from each of the 8 feeder schools plus a pageant or play by high school students.  Unlike the feeder schools, the high school had electric lights (so the function could be held at night) and a real stage with curtains.


For the graduation, the auditorium was decorated with blueflags.  During the morning of the gradation, the school bus would take us to an area where we picked blueflags (below Jim Cameron’s, as I recall).  Seems to me that we also had some cedar boughs.  The graduation program included a valedictorian the reading of a class will and a speaker from the community. The graduation was followed by a dance. (Would any reader have a copy of a class will?)



Harvest Home – On Labour Day, St. Stephens United Church organized a festival and fund-raiser called the Harvest Home; it was first held in 1890.  People came from miles around to enjoy the event and to meet old friends.  There were games for both young and adults and, of course, a delicious dinner, usually salmon and turkey? – and all the food was home-cooked.  One year, at least, the program included a calf judging event.


Church suppers – St. Paul’s, and I am sure St. Stephen’s as well, held church suppers both as a social gathering and a fundraisers. The Presbyterian one was usually held at one of the homes.  Oyster stew, although a lot of work, was popular; I also remember the lemon meringue pie.


Tap night was a bit like Halloween except one did not play tricks.  Usually one went from door to door with friends and the homeowner gave food.  Then the gang would gather at one house and eat it.  Interestingly, I searched the internet and found nothing about Tap night.  It seems to me that it as held on New Year’s Eve.


Halloween – was a young person’s night.  We went to local doors and usually received candy or an apple.  Occasionally we had firecrackers, either the kind you lighted or sometimes ones that detonated when thrown against the hard ground or side of a building.  Pranks were quite mild although occasionally an outhouse would be upset. 


Friday night dances – I went to my first dance in the Black River Hall in 1946, about a week before I graduated from High school. It was probably a Friday night since dances on Saturday night would be expected to end by midnight.  Dances were held in a number of halls and schools in the area and also at Mel Adams’ Hall in Bayside after the war.  I limited myself mostly to Friday night dances, however, my brothers would go more often.


Arbour Day – fell on May 1 and was a day for cleaning both the school building and the school yard.  That day, we took rakes, pails, scrub brushes, and likely BonAmi, to clean the windows.  We usually finished by 1:00 pm and were allowed to go home.


I recall Arbour day as quite special; on that day, Mother let me carry a lunch to school.  Although I do not literally remember, it was likely in a “five pound” Domestic Shortening can.  Lunch also included a bottle of milk (an empty vanilla bottle).  A piece of twine was tied tightly around the neck and the bottle was hung in the brook under the Miller’s Bridge.  Cold milk was quite a contrast with the milk kept cool on the basement floor (we did not have electricity at the time and, therefore, no fridge.


Dominion Day, July 1 – Mother was quite patriotic.  Once a year, either Victoria Day or July 1, one or two small flags were put up at our home.  I remember the one at the corner of the verandah usually held in place by a couple of fence staples


Places


Bayshore – most beach events were held at the Bay Shore where there was public access to the beach. 

Ben’s Canteen – was a popular eating and meeting place in Chatham.  As I recall, people looking for a ride home would wait at Ben’s and drivers about to head home drove past Ben’s to see if anyone needed a ride.


Point aux Carr road – was a shortcut between Black River and Point Aux Carr.  We were more frequent users, probably, because that is where Venetia and Cam lived. 


The Forks – somewhere well above Fowlie’s Mill, the Little Branch River divided; that junction was known as The Forks.   


MacKenzie Road – actually started at the Little Branch School, followed the south side of the Little Branch River and eventually joined the Richibucto Road.  While I have not searched the matter, I understand that the MacKenzie road was the main artery between settlements on the south side of the Miramichi Bay and Richibucto, Rexton and points south.  Furthermore, at one time, there was an Inn (half way house) at the Forks, operated by a Gibson, for travellers at the Forks along the MacKenzie Road


Suspension Bridge – was a post war development providing a walking bridge to people on the both sides of the Eel River.  I imagine it was built initially for the benefit of school children.


Victory Café – on Water Street near the train station in Chatham was a favourite restaurant and meeting place during WW2.
