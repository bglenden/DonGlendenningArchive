The Family Chronicle

No.23 November 24, 2001                                                                                                                        __________

The previous newsletter listed names of relatives who served in a branch of the military.  In this news letter, I decided to jot down some wartime recollections.  I recognize that what I say may not even be accurate but these jottings are my recollections.  These notes are in random order.


I was age 10 when war was declared.  I recall  discussions in the schoolyard about the war and if we might be attacked.


We were encouraged to save old newspapers and tin foil (?).  We bought loose tea in packages with metallic (lead) covering.  We always saved the lead.  I recall this material stacked in the entry way of the Little Branch school.


Ken had gone to Moncton to enlist.  He phoned Mother to say that he would be passing through Newcastle that night on his way to Toronto.  Walter (?) drove Mum to Newcastle to see Ken on the way through.


I was in the Miramichi Rural on June 6, 1944 when a car came to the school to pick up Francis MacNaughton.  His father was killed on D-Day in the Normandy landing.  A hush fell over the school and the school bus later in the afternoon..


Vance MacNaughton said that he was in the Little Branch School on  May 8, 1945 when the school door flew open.  My mother entered unannounced to shout to all that the war was over in Europe.


Some items were rationed including sugar and sugar products, oil, gas, butter, tea (?).  We had our coupon books and a coupon was removed every time we made a purchase.  Because we lived on a  farm and there were a number in the family, rationing was never a problem.  For a while John had a big truck and therefore had extra stamps for gasoline.


Roy Watling stopped at our house one cold winter night on his way home from purchasing gallon of molasses in a glass bottle.  He proudly displayed his purchase which he may have been able to get with less than the normal required stamps.  He went on home.  Later we learned that Aunt Kate had been housecleaning and had changed the location of a chair.  It was now just inside the front door.  When Roy stepped in, the cold glass bottle of molasses struck the chair and shattered.  Need I say more?


Military vehicles were often seen on our roads and would occasionally stop at the house to ask directions.


I was told once that officers were paid $5.00 per day.  That seemed a pretty exorbitant wage.




Canadians were encouraged to purchase war saving bonds and war saving stamps.  The latter, as I recall were 25 cents each.  Mum bought bonds and encouraged us to buy stamps.  She also made a deal whereby I received saving stamps for delivering phone messages to Alex MacDonald who did not have a telephone.  Alex worked on dredges and around wharves.  As I recall, he worked for man with a French name who lived in Buctouche or Richibucto.  He often phoned our place to get a message to Alex.  A few days after I delivered a message, I would receive two war saving stamps in the mail.  We stuck them in a stamp book and turned them in when we had 40 stamps.


The women of the community knit socks and mitts for soldiers - and not just for immediate family members.  Also, and especially at Christmas, boxes would be sent to solders overseas.  Into the boxes would go socks, mitts, fudge, and canned goods.  Fruitcake and cookies were favourite.  People also wrote letters to soldiers even though they did not know them 


Ken brought an airman from south east British Columbia with him at Christmas, 1942.


The Chatham airport was the home to #10 Air Observer School and #31 Elementary Flying Training School.  The first used Avro Ansons and the latter used a bi-wing plane - a Moth maybe.  The Miramichi area was designated a low-flying area and it was not unusual for landing gear to catch on radio aerials at farm homes in the area.  One pilot actually flew under the Newcastle Bridge.

 A plane crashed one snowy winter day about half way in Herb MacDonald=s lane.  The bus from school stopped at the end of the lane on the way home and we walked in to the crash site.  I do not think that anyone was killed


Another crash occurred in mid-summer at Alf MacLean=s shore where one airman was killed - a man by the name of Neilly, I think.  There were two in the plane following the course of the Little Branch River below Fowlie=s Mill when one wing clipped a tree.  I believe Harry MacLean was first at the scene.


A practice bombing range was cleared east of the road between Napan and Loggieville.  The woods was cleared and targets were built.  My recollection is that targets were wooden platforms build 2-3 feet off the ground.


If a plane crashed in the area, people were asked to report any sightings which might be related.  I remember taking turns sitting at the window in the down stairs bedroom looking out the window looking for flares.


I built model airplanes from balsa-wood kits and powered by rubber bands.  I built a Spitfire, a  Norduyn Norseman and one other.  The Spitfire would lift off the floor in the length of the kitchen.
