Selkirk Settlers


My cottage is on a peninsula overlooking Orwell Bay where, 200 years ago, the Selkirk Settlers landed on PEI.  These were not the only Selkirk settlers, but I believe that they were the first.  The notes that follow are based on an article in the Charlottetown Guardian on August 2, 2003.


Thomas Douglas, Earl of Selkirk, brought 800 settlers into this area in 1803.  Most of them came from the Isles of Skye and Rassay off the north west coast of Scotland.  Interestingly, this is the same year that Alexander MacDonald, my Great Grandfather, arrived in this country.


“In the mythology, Highlanders left Scotland because they were cleared off the land by landlords who wanted to raise sheep.  But the reality of the story is more complicated.  At the time, their landlord, Baron MacDonald of Slate was doing three things.  He was auctioning off leases on the Scottish tenants lands.  Rents were going up and he was starting to move tenants to new land.  


As well, MacDonald was trying to raise a regiment of Highland soldiers for the British army and was disappointed that the volunteer rate was low.  And so he was basically threatening the families on his estates that if they did not supply younger sons for his regiment, they would face the consequences.  The backdrop is the collapse of the clans in Scotland.”


I am sure that much of what was faced by the Selkirk settlers applied to our ancestors 



as well, many of who came to Canada about the same time.


The President of the Belfast Historical Society had this to say about the legacy of the Selkirk Settlers.  “They had determination, a willingness to work, strong family ties, wonderful loyalty to country, family and friends, and a heart for hospitality.” I am sure that the same could be said about our Scottish ancestors.


The interpretive centre notes that these settlers had some money, having sold their possessions in Scotland.  Although poor, I believe that, in the Scottish tradition, they also had some schooling.


Prior to this, most of the Scottish settlers went to the USA especially the Carolinas.  It was Douglas’ view, that British North America would be a more appropriate place for them to settle.  The success of the Selkirk Settlers on PEI apparently was a turning point and many Scottish settlers were soon to follow.


Forest Fires


This item is triggered by news of the massive forest fires in British Columbia and Alberta.  


I know that the Miramichi has had its share of forest fires with the Miramichi Fire of 1825 being the big one.  The first one I remember was the Loggieville Fire in the early 1930’s.  It burned on both sides of the road between Napan and Loggieville and took years to reforest.  (I am writing this at my cottage and do not have access to my reference material but think that the fire may have been in 1935.)  There was a Presbyterial Meeting at St. Paul’s church that summer and they prayed for rain – and it rained.  


There was a fire in the woods on the south east side of the Black River near Route 11.  I believe it was a Saturday afternoon.  Norm and I were in town and heard about it.  We came home, did the chores and then went to help fight the fire.  I recall carrying and using a water bag and pump.  It was a small fire on the river bank and was contained that night.  I also remember the lunch of hard tack and bully beef.


We had a couple of small fires at our place.  One came through the woods from At. Finno’s and burned a pile of wood in the backfield behind where Eric Glendenning now lives).  I do not actually remember the fire but remember the burned-over trees.  Another was a fire that got away when Dad was burning brush in the back field (again behind Eric’s place).  I recall the neighbours coming with wagons and barrels and hauling water from the Branch Bridge to douse the flames.  I think that fire was out by nightfall.


I know that I am repeating this story from an earlier Newsletter, but Dad had ordered a permit to burn brush and it had still not arrived.  He decided to burn anyway in the expectation that the permit would be in the mail.  It wasn’t.  The fire got away.

One of our neighbours, John Fowlie, was the fire warden and was taking Dad to task for burning with out a permit.  Along came the RCMP, in the person of Bedford Peters who was stationed in Newcastle.  He was also a cousin of Dad’s.  When he walked over to where Dad and John Fowlie were, he handed Dad a paper and said, “Elmer, here is your permit”.  Apparently he had stopped and picked it up at the office before coming to Black River.

After I left home, I think that it was in the fall of 1947, there was a bad forest fire in the area.  I think that it started in Bay du Vin but came close to the Little Branch area.  Seems to me that I came home one weekend to see great clouds of smoke to the east of us.


Update 


Chronicle No. 40 included a list of teachers at the Little Branch School.  Recently I came across an old school record that shows that Annie Ellis taught at the Little Branch School during the 1924-25 school year.


School Buses


Some readers may recall the first school bus serving the Miramichi Rural High School.  It was owned and operated by Mack and George Godfrey.  Eddie Godfrey thinks that it was a 11/2 ton Dodge chassis.  I recall that it was dark blue in colour.  I seem to recall that the exterior was fabricated in Chatham and the interior by Edgar Fowlie… but I am not sure.


The second Godfrey bus was a large manufactured bus in beautiful blue and grey.  I still recall the first time I got on it.


Later Tom and Cliff Adams operated the bus system but I do not recall any details.


If readers have information or photos?  I’d love to provide more detail including hotos.
