Teachers


During a recent visit to the Miramichi, Venetia, Ken and I were trying to recall our teachers at the Little Branch School. The following is a preliminary list:


Margerite Bell (Mrs. George MacDonald)

Katie (MacNaughton) Neilson

Annie Ellis – 1925?

Jeni Oleskevis

Charles Spencer – 2 years

Donald MacLean – 2 years

Rilma Tracy

Mary (Godfrey) Williston – at least three years

Bertie MacLean – 1938?

Marion Smith

Venetia Russell – 1955-1958

Mrs. Freddie Fowlie


We know there are others. Please help.


Point Aux Carr Trappers


Ken stayed with Venetia and Cam during the winter of 1937-38 and attended the Point Aux Carr school. One weekend, Norman went to visit. He crossed the ice at McKnight’s Point and walked out the Point Aux Carr Road. Ken walked in and met him about half-way.


Ken and Fowlie Gillis had been catching a few rabbits. On Saturday morning, Ken and Fowlie went out early to the snares taking a couple of rabbits caught the day before. Into one of the snares they put two rabbits.


Later in the morning, Norm and Mac Gillis were invited along when they went to “fish” their snares. Imagine the excitement of Norm and Mac when they found two rabbits in one snare.


The Muddy Trail


I was born in Old Black River

Over sixty years ago.

It was a Sunday night and it was dark

My neighbour told me so.

It was the eleventh day of May

In nineteen hundred and eight.

When a neighbour went for a stork.

They say her name was Kate.

He had about four miles to go

Through deep ruts in the mud

And some places on the south side.

The wagon steps they drug.

That night I came into this world.

The stork was waiting there.

She had just arrived five minutes before.

There was no time to spare.

This lady took me in her arms.

She laid me on the “bug’

And when I opened my blue eyes

The first thing I saw was mud.

As time went on and years rolled by.

Eventually I grew.

So I got long and slinky

And now I’m six foot two.

I haven’t got no learning

For I never went to school.

I never learned about Tom’s dog.

Or was taught the golden rule.

I take great delight in wild life.

For I hunted all my life.

But the women wouldn’t look at me.

That’s why I have no wife.

I trapped all kinds of animals

From a weasel to a bear.

I’ve shot all kinds of venison.

From a bull moose down to a hare.

I never wandered far from home.

I’m still here on the “bug”.

For every time I made a move

I was spinning in the mud.

I walk this side road twice a week

To buy something to eat.

But I never feel the worse of it.

For it’s soft and easy on my feet.

Now there isn’t much traffic on this side.

For it is a soopy trail.

For every mile you have to stop

To wash your lights and bail.

You can’t be in a hurry

For a spray goes o’er your hood.

If a walker chances to meet a car.

He’s got to take the woods.

They call it just a dump hole

And I believe it’s true.



For every few yards in the ditch

There’s a garbage bag or two.

It was this side road that started mini-skirts.

They didn’t start before the flood.

For if they wore their skirts down to their knees.

They’d be dropping in the mud.

We would like to have this side road built.

Or the people they’ll soon raz.

For if the mud gets any deeper.

They’ll need mud flaps on their bras.

You go along the side of it

And pick your way across.

But we know someone who will pave our road.

Our old stand by Jack Frost

They promised to fix this road.

For as long as I can mind.

They say we have it on our list.

But it’s on the bottom line.

They brought one load of gravel.

About two years ago;

They didn’t spread it on the road.

For it would make an awful show.

They hauled it up and down the road.

Looked in every little nook.

They seen no place that needed it.

Dumped it o’er the bank of Wells’ Brook.

They came again with mud stone.

And as they worked past the gate.

They spread it just about as thick

As frosting on a cake.

They worked on both ends of it.

With mud-stone mixed with ground.

They had to be very careful

To have enough to go around.

We had all kinds of members.

Enough to form a ring

And when it comes to governments

It didn’t mean a thing.

If Larry Gorman was living now

I’m sure he’d write a song.

He’d take the weight of everything.

And it wouldn’t take him long.

Now some folk when they read this poem.

May think that I’m a goof.


But when they sum the whole thing up.

You’ll find that it’s the truth.


An old tax-payer

Stuck here in the mud



Note: I found this among my papers; the source, writer and date are not known -- Editor
