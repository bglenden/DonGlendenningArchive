WW1


There were over 600,000 Canadians overseas during WW1.  Most served in the Canadian Expeditionary Force; many served in the British army.  The Canadian Archives has developed a database of most but not all of the names.  For anyone wishing to search, the web site is Awww.archives.ca@  A search revealed 10 Glendennings including (Uncle) Guy Wellington Glendenning (Regimental Number 445210) and (Aunt) Margaret Violet Glendenning (No Regimental Number - she was a nurse) There also were 16 Glendinnings.  


There were 21 Watlings including (Uncle) Donald Ernest Watling.  (#70039) The other Watlings which could have been related include Archie (#46798), George Maxwell (#901129), William Jubal (#832788)


Home Children


The Home Children listed on the Internet include 1 Glendenning and 5 Watlings.


Another uncle?


My search of the NB Archives turned up a Walter Glendenning born April 19, 1881 to Walter Glendenning and Elizabeth Peters.  At first I thought that Dad had another brother, however, the month and year, but not the date, matches that of Uncle Leonard.  My conclusion is that either someone copied the names and dates incorrectly, that they had trouble deciding on a name, or that they actually named him Walter and changed the name later. 


Refrigeration


Some time ago I told you my story about our new refrigerator; more recently, Loisanne mentioned getting a new fridge.  


Refrigeration at home, as I remember it, was the basement floor.  Butter and milk were kept in the basement; the butter in a crock and the milk, probably in a shortening bucket with a lid and presumably cream for our strawberries..  Inevitably, or so it seemed to me, I was the one who had to go down to the basement for the milk and butter.


It seems to me that one year , perhaps more, we had ice in what was or became the henhouse.  One would scrape away a bit of the sawdust and put things on the ice.  I remember that George Adams, who ran the store across the river from Norman=s, seemed always to have ice and stored perishables there.  The only things I can ever remember buying at Adams@ store was bologna and AMuskrat@ juice.  The latter was really Monserrat (an Island in the Carribean) lime juice which we mixed with water and a bit of sugar.  We drank gallons of it at haying time.


Later probably John or Walter sunk a barrel in the brook just above the Miller=s Bridge and we kept a five-gallon cream can there.  We placed stones on the cover of the barrel to keep the cream can down.  Each day after milking and separating, we took the fresh cream down and added it to the can.  When someone came to pick up the cream (was it a Dick ?)  The cream can, now nearly full, became very heavy to carry up to the road to load on the wagon. 


Normally, we came home for lunch with the school being so close.  Arbour Day, however, was one day that Mum let me take a lunch.  I do not remember much about the lunch but I do remember that it included a bottle of milk; as I recall it was a large vanilla bottle.  We tied a string around the neck of the bottle and placed it in the brook under the Miller=s Bridge.  It sure tasted good.


By the way, I still wonder about the origin of the name AMiller@s@ Bridge.  Is the brook known as Miller=s Brook?  Just wondering.


Wartime radio messages


AAttention all lighthouse keepers in the East Coast Area, instructions >A=, >A= is for apple, are to be carried out.@  Many a time this message was heard over our radios.  Obviously it was a code; what it meant, I was never sure but I believe that it had something to do with the movement of ships in or out of port; it could also have meant the movement of vessels along the coast.  

I often wondered what action was required as a result of that message.  We know that enemy submarines sank ships in the St Lawrence River and operated generally in the Gulf.  Some may have even entered the Miramichi Bay but I have not read any reports to confirm this.


A spy in Black River?


The Ken MacDonald house stood where Cameron MacDonald now has his home.  It was a large, weather beaten, one and one-half story house; it remained vacant, as I recall, except for each summer when the MacDonald sisters returned from Portland.


Early during WW2, a stranger appeared in the community and took up residence in the Ken MacDonald house.  The name, as I recall, was Tom Coll; he was an Englishman.  I believe that he was married but they kept pretty much to themselves although I do recall him coming to our house.  After the war, he disappeared; I have no idea where he came from or where he went.  He had a pair of wooded skis and travelled across the fields in winter.


We, children, thought this to be myster- ious and wondered if he was a spy.  I=d be interested in knowing the real story.


War Savings Stamps


People were encouraged to purchase War Savings Bonds to help with the war effort.  There were also war savings stamps valued at 25 cents each.  


Because we were one of the few places to have a telephone, we frequently ended up delivering messages to neighbours including Alex MacDonald who, as I recall, operated a dredge for a company from Buctouche. (Was the owner a Michaud?  Was he a Senator? I don=t know.)  Mum negotiated an arrangement with Michaud to send me two stamps each time I delivered a message.  I remember the stamps arriving in the mail and putting them in my savings book.
