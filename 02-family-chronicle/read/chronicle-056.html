Miramichi Visit


I made these notes while on my annual pre-winter visit to the Miramichi; it was a busy four days.  I arrived about noon on Thursday and went back to PEI on Sunday morning.  While there I spoke to the Chatham Rotary Club about the establishment of a comm-unity foundation.  I stayed with Ken and Verne.


I am interested in all the early settlers of Black River even though they may not be ancestors.  I still have many gaps but Ernie MacLean provided me with background on the MacLean families.     


Ken and I made a trip to the Bathurst area to visit relatives including Guy Glendenning who I mentioned in an earlier newsletter.  Also stopped at the New Bandon Cemetery where my grandfather, Walter, and his wife, Elizabeth Peters, are buried.  Also buried in the New Bandon Cemetery are Grand-father’s brother, John and John’s wife Ellen Dickey.  I have not been able to find where my Great Grandfather and Grandmother, John and Margaret Glendinning, are buried.  Also had a brief visit with Marion Glendenning and Carson Daley.  So it was a good day although a couple of others we normally visit were not at home when we stopped by.  Had a visit with Sandra (Taylor) Shea on the way home.


Of course, I had a visit with Norm and Jean and family a couple of times and everywhere I go I try to pursue my interests in genealogy.  Marjorie Young also filled in some gaps.



Neighbours


With Ken and Norm’s help, I am trying to recall the neighbours in Black River when I was young – say the early 1940’s but I will save the results of our discussion for a later Chronicle. 


Slovens


A couple of times in the past month mention was made of a sloven.  George Adams who lived across the river from us owned the only one that I can recall in Black River.  A sloven is a farm wagon with large wheels like any truck wagon but with axles slung low to the ground.  The platform was about 12-18 inches off the ground as I recall.  Slovens were used, I am told, to move large barrels and puncheons and were great for hauling heavy items, such as a thresher, because of the platform being low to the ground.  


I asked my cousin, Guy Glendenning in Canobie and he recalled one in the community when he was young.  I found since that the Monaghan Company manufactured slovens on Prince Edward Island.  In fact an Island friend thought that slovens may have been “invented” in Prince Edward Island.  George Adams who had owned the one in Black River had come to Black River from PEI so maybe his was manufactured there.  I’ll keep you posted if I learn anymore.


Horse Scoop


No, a horse scoop is not a pooper-scooper that people carried around to pick up after  horses.  Horses generally had the freedom to poop whenever and wherever they wanted.

I was reminded of this subject because Ken and I saw a scoop on the side of the lane leading to the New Bandon cemetery.    


A horse scoop was shaped a bit like a large snow shovel or sugar scoop.  It was used when moving dirt around an excavation.  Walter used it, for example, when he excavated the basement for his home on the Dan MacLean property.  The scoop had two handles projecting from the rear so that a person could stand between them and lift the back of the scoop.  It was pulled by a horse and when on the level could move dirt quite easily.  When you wanted to load it, you simply lifted the handles a bit so that the mouth of the scoop would dig into the ground.  When the shovel was full, the horse dragged it to another location and you lifted the handles again.  This time the mouth of the scoop dug in and upset the load leaving an empty scoop ready for another load.


A Near-fire


Ken and I were recalling a near fire at home, I am not sure of the year.  As I recall, Ken, Norm and I were hauling wood one spring day, although there was still lots of snow around.  We were hauling the wood on a large hand-sled, John’s sled we always called it.  Ken smelled smoke and was met by smoke when he opened the cellar door.


At our place, the furnace firebox was set inside a brick box about 5’x5’ x5’.  Another brick wall was built outside the first leaving an air space between the two walls of brick acted as an insulator.  The furnace itself would handle a stick of wood four feet long.  Forming a cap over the furnace was a sheet metal covering with a number of openings to which pipes could be attached to lead the warm air to various rooms above.  On top of the sheet metal was a layer of sand to also act as insulation.  Seems that the cats were playing on the sand on top of the furnace and pushed off one of the covers.  At the same time, the cast iron furnace had cracked allowing flames to enter the chamber.  Something caught fire.  


As I recall, Ken came to the rescue and quickly got pails of water to douse the fire.


It was shortly thereafter, as I recall, they replaced the cast iron furnace.  It was very heavy and they used a rope to hold it back when sliding it down the cellar stairs.  Norman MacKenzie, who was a big, husky man, helped Walter and John ease the furnace down to the basement and replace the old one.


Thanks


Thanks to Judy (Mrs. Chester) Glendenning for corrections to family names and dates appearing in Family Chronicle No. 52.


Arbour Day 


Arbour Day was always special.  It was a day to clean the school and yard and we always got “out” early.  It was also one of the few occasions when I was able to take a lunch to school.  Normally, because we lived so close to the school, I went home for lunch.
