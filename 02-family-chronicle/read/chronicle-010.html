Tenth Family Chronicle (December 1, 1999) uses Crown land grant records to document early settler families. Alexander McDonald Sr. granted Lot #2 (225 acres) June 3, 1812—property now owned by Norman Glendenning. Lists other original grantees (Sinclair, Fowlie families) with acreages and dates. References "Pioneers of the Bay Chaleur" showing John Glendinning purchased Lot #7 in Salmon Beach (granted to Fidele and Joseph Arceneau 1812), sold 1855 to Abram Buttimer, and received Canobie land grant 1868. Documents land grants to Glendenning males: John Glendinning Jr. (Canobie, Lot #31, August 17, 1868), Walter Glendenning (Canobie, Lot #30, February 17, 1873), Atton E. Glendenning (Canobie, Lot #28, July 6, 1901). Notes unusual practice: settlers often occupied land years before receiving title. Mentions FWH's "Boyhood Memories of New Brunswick" (c. 1940) manuscript at Mount Allison University describing Canobie life. Queries about Miller's Bridge origin and other local place names.

Another Cousin


Cameron MacDonald pointed out that the headstone in the Black River Cemetery belonging to William and Margaret Godfrey includes the following AJohn William, infant son of Kenneth and Katherine MacDonald@.  That would result in the Ken MacDonald family being as follows:


$ Alex

$ Frances

$ Vada

$ Isabel

$ Hugh (Mac)

$ Elspeth

John William


FWH


In the Bell Holdings at Mount Allison University, I found Boyhood Memories of new Brunswick, written by FWH about 1940.  It is a typewritten manuscript rather than a book and is about his boyhood memories in Canobie.  Among other things, he mentions:


an Uncle who was a Stevens and another who was a Knowles.

he was born in Clifton, near the stone quarry.

his grandparents lived in Newcastle during the Miramichi Fire (1825)

Tim Savage was a near neighbour. (Lot #27 in Canobie was owned by Tim Savage.)

his mother was a school teacher who received $200 from the government nd $200 from the district.


He also tells a ghost story based at a camp in the woods just south of Bathurst on the ARed Pine Section of the Intercolonial Railroad@.  From what he said, his mother was at that camp.  He ends his manuscript with his departure from Bathurst for Maine.


It is likely that he was a Henry, grandson of Mary Anne Harley and John Henry.  I do not have the names of many of her family but understand that her husband, John, died in 1870 an she moved to Portland shortly thereafter.  My records also show that one of Mary Anne=s daughters married a Knowles and another married a Stevens.


Land Grants


The Department of Natural Resources, Fredericton has maps showing the first owners of land granted by the Crown.  They also have a book listing all the original owners.  Information in the book and maps should be the same, but isn=t always.  The following information was gleaned from these sources.


Alexander McDonald Sr. (not MacDonald) was granted Lot #2, 225 acres, on 1812-06-03. This is the property now owned by Norman Glendenning.


On the same date and further up the Branch


Kenneth Sinclair was granted Lot #3 with 220 acres.

Angus Sinclair was granted Lots 4 & 5 with 410 acres.

Alexander McDonald (the book says Sr. but the map shows Jr.) was granted Lot #6 consisting of 140 acres.

The next land was granted to George Fowlie but numbers and boundaries are not clear to me.


The west side of the Alexander McDonald property forms the rear line of the following properties:

Lot A - Enoch Godfrey

Lots # 1, 2 & 3 - John McLean

Lot #4 - Alexander Gunn

Lot #5 - Robert MacBeath

Lot #6 - John MacLeod


On 1936-09-16, Elmer Glendenning was granted 100 acres, Lot #7s.  This would be John=s woodlot.  The front half of this property was owned by Thomas Patterson.


Jubal Watling, Jr. was granted Lot #30, 100 acres, on the south side of Black River on 1881-04-20.


Next to Jubal, towards the highway, were Gardiner Archibald, Lot #29, granted in 1880-05-10 and Richard Hutchison, Lot #28, granted in 1849-0604.


Next to Jubal, up river, was Donald Cameron on Lot #31 granted in 1854-01-30.


Canobie


I cannot find any original grant to John Glendinning therefore, he must have purchased from someone else. APioneers of the Bay Chaleur@ says he bought part of Lot #7 in Salmon beach; this land was granted originally to Fidele and Joseph Arceneau in 1812-06-01.  The@Pioneer Book@ also says that John Glendinning sold his land in 1855 in Salmon Beach to Abram Buttimer and was granted land in Canobie in 1868



Lot #31, 100 acres, in Canobie was granted to John Glendinning, Jr. in 1868-08-17 so it appears that the property was put in the son=s name.


It was not unusual for early settlers to live on land for a number of years before receiving title;  they may have even been required to clear a number of acres and even build a house.


Lot #30, 100 acres, in Canobie was granted to Water Glendinning in 1873-02-17.


Lot #28, 100 acres, was granted to Atton E. Glendenning on 1901-07-06.  Would this be, Dad, Alton E. Glendenning?
