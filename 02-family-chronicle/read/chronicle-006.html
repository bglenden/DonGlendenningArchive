Issue 6 · Nov 20, 1998
Sixth Family Chronicle (November 20, 1998) is a Christmas issue recounting Don's childhood Christmas memories at Black River. Vivid anecdotes: mother saying "Santa's birdie" was checking if children were being good; magic pantry drawer that produced extra goodies for visiting relatives; stockings with oranges, candy, nuts, pennies; sad memories of Uncle Chip dying suddenly Christmas Eve 1948 (heart attack while shopping), and two fatal car accidents (Earl Taylor 1969, Gerald and Elaine Taylor 1994). Positive gift memories include a wind-up caterpillar tractor and a train set. Notable entries: 1958 family trip to Florida with siblings in a new Buick; Christmas 1958 at home with homegrown meal; 1957 Christmas in Stamford, Connecticut when Don gave Carol (his wife) a diamond engagement ring. Reveals Don's warm family relationships and sentimental nature.

CHRISTMAS ISSUE – 1998


I=ve decided to make this my Christmas letter and, in it, to record, as best I can remember, some Christmas events and activities.  Memory plays tricks and so, the events and the dates my not be exactly as described by me or remembered by someone else.  Notes are written in the order that I recall them, not necessarily in the order in which they occurred.  In doing this, it is my hope that it will spark discussion about your own Christmas memories.  


The Little Birdie


As Christmas drew near, Mother had a favourite way of getting our attention and getting us to settle down.  She would say, AThere=s one of Santa=s birdies.@  Obviously, the birdie was checking to see if we were being good.  The birdie flitted up to or past the window.  By the time I turned to look, it was gone.  I never saw the birdie but Mother frequently did.  As I recall, it was only seen  during the evening or night.  That=s probably when the children got restless.


The Pantry


By the end of Christmas Day, the apples, oranges, nuts and candy would have been finished off.  Usually, on Christmas night some of the family not living at home would drop in.  Sometimes cousins would land along also.  Magically, extra goodies appeared during their visit.  It was only as I got older that I realized that the supply of goodies as kept in a drawer in the pantry.


Stockings


I do not remember much about what was in our stockings except that there was always an orange stuck in the toe.  There were usually some candy and nuts as well and, as I recall, a few shiny pennies or maybe a nickel.  I really cannot remember getting gifts from Santa.  One Christmas, Walter was supposed to have found a turnip in his stocking because he had not been a good boy.


Sadness at Christmas


Not all Christmas recollections are happy ones.  Three sad ones come quickly to mind.


One Christmas Eve, 1948, Mum received a phone call saying that Uncle Chip had died suddenly.  He had gone over town (Bathurst) to do some last minute shopping and took a heart attack.  I believe that he died in the drug store.  Sterl Williston had a new car and Norman arranged for Sterl to take us to the funeral in Bathurst.  We couldn=t have had a vehicle then.


On December 23 1969, Earl Taylor was struck by a car and killed in front of his home.  He was coming home from work and carrying a Christmas turkey.


On December 20 ,1994, Gerald and Elaine Taylor were killed in a car accident near Bathurst.  They were making a pre-Christmas visit to Elaine=s family.



Gifts


One Christmas, I received a Acaterpillar@ tractor as a gift.  It had a key to wind it up and then it would crawl around.  I remember taking it up to my bed and letting it crawl over the bed clothes.  This may well have come from Grace and Roy, but I do not recall.


Another year, the Walkers, where Lillian worked, sent a train set with an oval shaped track.  It was self propelled with a key to wind the spring.


Christmas Eve


I remember going out to the clothes line to help Mum bring in clothes one clear, cold frosty Christmas Eve.  Norman was in town and came home shortly after on the bus.


Running Water


As I recall, we got electric power in the fall of 1948.  I seem to recall that Norman bought a pump and installed cold water at Christmas 1948 and hot water at Christmas, 1949.


Florida


John and Mary bought a new Buick in 1958.  In a visit to Moncton, probably in November, John suggested that we go with them to Florida over Christmas.  I got an extra week off and John, Mary, Margaret, Jeannie, Carol and I drove to Florida.


A Home-grown Meal


Carol and I spent Christmas 1958 at the Branch.  We had bought a huge turkey.  It was so large that Mum stayed up all night to tend the fire so that it would be cooked in time for dinner.  Our Christmas meal was entirely of hone grown food. Mmmm-good.




A Christmas Visitor


When Ken came home for Christmas, I think that it was the first year he was in the service, he brought another airman home with him.  This fellow came from the South-eastern part of British Columbia (Near the Alberta border).  Apparently scalloped oysters were a Christmas tradition at his home so Mum made scalloped oysters for him.


1957


Carol was anxious to see me again so I decided to spend Christmas in Stamford, Conn.  where she was.  Gerald Taylor drove to Boston and stayed at Grace=s. I went to Stamford and between Christmas and New Years, came back to Boston for a couple of days.  That was the Christmas I gave Carol the diamond. 




I wish all readers a very Merry Christmas and a healthy and Happy New 1999.
