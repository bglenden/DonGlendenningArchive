The Family Chronicle

No.13           August 20, 2000

                                                                                                                                  

Re-union


We had a great gathering on July 28 - 30; most readers were there so I will keep this item short.


Friday evening was registration and conver- sazione; Saturday morning saw people gather at the old homestead for breakfast, unveiling of a plaque, burial of a time capsule and planting of an oak tree; afternoon saw Ahighland Games@ and barbeque at Ken and Verne=s cottage; evening entertainment under the big tent; Sunday included a church service with a choir full of family, an open house for extended family and friends, and another barbeque.


I found it especially interesting to hear some of the Ayoung folks@ reminiscing; I would like to have heard them talk when the older folk were not around.


Feed-back was very positive; thanks to all for making it possible and enjoyable.  Remember, Re-union No. 23 will be held on Saturday, July 28 2001.


Spring


I forgot to look to see if the spring still flows on the side of the road down the hill from the concrete post?  Horses were taken there for water, cows were herded there once a day for water, I believe that one calf met its end there, and even passers by used it.  The water ran from the spring down to the brook.


As I recall, it had a box around it about 3 feet square; I do not recall a lid.  The box is gone but does the spring still flow?

Black sheep in the family?


I was looking through a book on AEnglish Convicts in Colonial America@ and found where John Watling was convicted (I do not know the crime) and sent to America aboard the AThames@ in April, 1753.  Obviously, he would not have been one of our ancestors.


Another Branch?


Loisanne had a visit at her booth at the Moncton market from Bill Glendenning who has been a customer of hers for quite a while.  Seems that his father was Ken Glendenning and his Grandfather was Walter Glendenning from  New Mills, N.B.  He also mentioned Glendennings in Galloway, near Rexton.   


Glendenning (Glenn) Steeves


When Uncle Guy Glendenning visited us from Florida he usually went to Dorchester, NB to visit a WW1 buddy.  I believe the name was Steeves.  I believe, furthermore, that the friend had a son named Glendenning after Uncle Guy.  Glenn Steeves, I believe, lived/lives in Moncton.  Does any reader know the story behind the name?


Summer Treat?


Before he joined the army, Brydone drove truck for A&R Loggie Company of Loggieville.   People would phone in their grocery orders and once or twice each week Brydone would deliver groceries down river as far as Escuminac.   There were only two places he could leave groceries without being paid on the spot and one of them was at home.


Some times Brydone would take me, and I am sure others also had a turn, in the truck when he was down river delivering groceries and it included having lunch in Escuminac.  It was quite a treat.  


Once when we (Norman, Roy, George and I and maybe Ken?) were swimming at our shore at the mouth of the cove across from the bridge, we saw Brydone=s truck coming.  We watched as he approached and decided that when the truck emerged from the covered bridge, we would jump out of the water and wave.  We faced the bridge, as the truck came out of the bridge, we jumped and waved  dressed in our birthday suits.  It was only later that we learned that day he had a woman traveling with him that day.


Old Swimming Hole


As I mentioned above, we swam at our shore usually at fairly high tide.  Although we lived by the river we did not have a boat of any kind.  I can remember helping to build a raft and poling it out into the deep water and then jumping off it.  One of the things we would do is try to touch bottom when we jumped in.  There was one place, in the river behind Normans barn, where we could not touch bottom.  I am assuming that was the spot where the mud had been dug the previous winter.


My sore toe


Presumably when Dad put up the concrete post, he widened the lane.  I recall the stub of a fence post sticking up a couple of inches or so in the middle of the lane.  I not only recall seeing it but also recall feeling it when I stubbed my toe.  It hurts yet when I think about it. 




Queen Mother=s Birthday


The celebration of the Queen Mother=s birthday reminds me of the visit by the King and Queen to Newcastle in 1939.  We travelled to Newcastle in Hassie (?) Smith=s truck.  Or was it Harry or BW= truck?  As I recall, it was a sunny day and we were picked up at the Branch school about 5:30 am.  We stood in the back of the truck; I cannot recall who all was there but believe it included Ken, Norm, Roy, George, Harry MacLean, etc.  Probably 20 to 25 all together.


We saw the King and Queen and then came home.  As I recall, Mum packed a lunch but I am not sure.  We arrived home sometime in late evening, singing all the way.


The King and Queen motored to Fredericton from Newcastle.  The story is told about the two sisters in Blackville or Boiestown who waited on the side of the road for the King and Queen to pass.  They has each bought a camera and film to ensure that at least one would get a photo.  Just before the Royal Party came along, the sisters decided that one should be on each side of the road so that, whichever way the Royal couple faced, they would have them in the photo.  The Royal couple drove past smiling and waving.  They had seen their beloved Monarch. 


They could hardly wait to have their photos developed.  Imagine their surprise when they got their photos developed; they missed the  royal couple when they whizzed past but they had a great photo of each other.
