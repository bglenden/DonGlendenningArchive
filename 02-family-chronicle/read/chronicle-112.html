Riddles and flails


I was reading about PEI manufacturers of early agricultural equipment and came across terms not heard very often these days.


Flails were used to knock seeds and grain (oats, wheat, barley) from their stalks or from their pods (peas and beans). A flail, as I recall, was a small pole perhaps 5 feet long with another pole, perhaps 2-3 feet long tied to one end with a rope or piece of wire. The only time I remember seeing one in use was when Uncle Jim Watling used one to “flail” some beans. Dried bean stocks were put into a jute bag and tied then the flail was used to batter it about to knock the beans from their pods.

Winnowing was a way of separating dust and chaff from seeds. A piece of canvas or a sheet was placed on the ground. With a breeze, a bucket of beans was held at high and gently tipped so that the beans dropped to the canvas. Beans being heavy dropped straight down while the chaff and dirt blew beyond the canvas. I remember winnowing peas and beans.


Threshers were machines used to strip grain from the straw.


Fanners were used to clean the dust, dirt and chaff from grain and other seeds.


Riddles were found in threshers and fanners; they were screens placed horizontally in the thresher or fanner; Riddles shook so that smaller seeds dropped through the holes when passing over and larger seeds and chaff kept on going. Riddles could be changed depending on the kind of seeds to be separated.


Babbet was used for bearings. One could not just go out and buy a new set of bearings when needed; instead, one melted babbet, not unlike solder, and poured it around the shaft. I remember once seeing the old stationery engine owned jointly by Dad and Uncle Jim being repaired this way. I do not remember the detail, but remember Walter and Uncle Jim removing the bearing cap, taking out the old bearing, melting the babbet and poured it, presumably into a mould for the shaft.


The Class of ‘34


I am pleased to have a copy of the REGISTER for the Little Branch School , District 71/2, for the school year ending June 1934; the teacher was Donald J. McLean who held a Second Class License, had taught for four years and was paid $380.00 by the District and $120 in the form of an annual government Grant.
