Alabama


Thanks to Robert Stevens, a Harley descendant and cousin, I learned that Uncle Frank Glendenning went to Portland in 1898, and, in the year 1900 he was a teamster and boarding with his second cousin, Henry Stevens.


Robert also provided an undated registration form which shows Uncle Frank to be 41 years old (he was born in 1876) working as a labour foremen at Plant No.1, US Nitrate Company in Sheffield, Alabama. His employer was the J. G White Engineering company, the same company my father worked for in Langley Field, Virginia. Uncle Frank’s permanent address was 543 Forest Ave, Portland Maine


We know that my Father and Mother were in Mussel Shoals, Alabama, (the same area) in 1919 I am wondering now if Dad may have worked on the construction of the Nitrate plant rather than the Wilson Dam.


Telling A Horse’s Age


The following poem, source and date unknown, is believed to have been written by Jimmy Whelan


To tell the age of any horse,

Inspect the lower jaw, of course;

The six front teeth the tale will tell,

And every doubt and fear dispel.


Two middle “nippers” you behold

Before the colt is two weeks old,

Before the eight weeks two more will come;

Eight months the “corners” cut the gum.


The outside grooves will disappear

From the middle two in just one year;

In two years, from the second pair;

In three, the “corners too, are bare.


At two the middle “nippers” drop,

At three the second pair can’t stop;

When four years old the third pair goes;

At five a full new set he shows.


The deep black spots will pass from view,

At six years, from the middle two;

The second pair at seven years,

At eight the spot each “corner’ clears.


From middle “nippers”, upper jaw

At nine the black spots will withdraw;

The second pair at ten are white,

Eleven finds the “corners” light.


As time goes on the horsemen know

The oval teeth three-sided grow,

They no longer get, project before,

Till twenty, when we care no more.






Agricultural Society #103


Mention was made recently about Agricultural Society #103. My father was Secretary Treasurer for nearly 20 years and brother, Walter, for about 15 followed by Harry Watling. In conversation with Barbara Watling, she told me that Harry had turned in the books to the local Agricultural Agent. Vernon Taylor, Agricultural Representative for the Miramichi, in turn said that he had forwarded Society books to the NB Archives in Fredericton. Since then, I have confirmed that NB Archives has Society records that are likely the ones in question.
