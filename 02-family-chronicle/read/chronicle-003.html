Third Family Chronicle (December 1, 1997) clarifies "Mac" MacDonald's full name as Hugh McGraw MacDonald (died 1914, Newcastle Bridge). Reports on a November 15 Watling descendants gathering at Black River with ~20 attendees sharing local history. Provides detailed land history of Alexander MacDonald property, noting his original grant and its subsequent division between children John and Jessie.                                                                                                                               “Mac” MacDonald


Thanks to David Taylor, the mystery of “Mac” MacDonald has now been cleared up.  His full name was Hugh McGraw MacDonald.  He died in 1914 while working on the Newcastle (Morrisey) Bridge.  Thanks, David.


Watling Descendant’s Gathering


On Saturday evening, November 15, about 20 people (I didn’t really count) with Watling interests and/or family connections gathered in the basement of St. Paul’s Church Black River, to talk about family tree matters.


Among things which I remember are the following:


George Loggie said that Alexander MacDonald spent the first winter in Point Aux Carr after coming from Cape Breton

Ernie MacLean said that the original Alexander MacDonald grant is the land now owned by Norman Glendenning and Neil Watling.

The original home was situated above Norman Glendenning=s barn near the old apple orchard.

Teddy Fowlie said, as I recall, that his father, when a boy on the way to school, would stop at a MacDonald’d home in the field above Norman=s to get warm.  This, I believe, was in the field where an old pump was (still is ?).

I believe that the Alexander MacDonald property was divided between his son, John, married to Elspeth Jardine, getting the east half and his daughter, Jessie, getting the West half.  

John’s half went to Jubal and Grace (MacDonald) Watling.  Jubal left his property to James A and Ernest Watling; James bought out Ernest’s share.   This information is based on hearsay not on research.

When  Jessie died in 1912, her property appears to have gone to her nephew, Alexander MacDonald from whom Elmer Glendenning bought it in 1914.


Bertha Glendenning


We seem to have lost all connections with Frank Glendenning daughter Bertha (Glendenning) Crocker’s family.  According to my information, Bertha (1905 - 1974) married Clayton Crocker (Died 1975) They had two children, Joan and Franklin.  .


Joan did not marry but worked for her father and, after he died, in a retail store.  I talked with Joan once when passing through Portland.  By then she was retired and living in the old family cottage (winterized) at Sebago Lake.  I sent her some family tree information which was returned unopened.  Later I tried to phone, but she could not be reached 


Franklin is married and lives in Stone Mountain, Georgia.  As I recall, he has 2 children.  A recent letter to the address given to me by Joan, was returned marked "Insufficient Address”.

Other Glendennings


The following information comes from notes provided by Lawrence W McGill of Seattle Washington.  He is descended from Glendinnings who came from Mousewald near Dumfries, Scotland and settled in New Brunswick.


John Glendinning married Janet Davidson.  They had four children all born in Scotland.  James was born in 1797 and died in Saint John, New Brunswick in 1846.  George was born in 1798 and died in Truemanville, Nova Scotia in 1870.  Sarah was born in 1800.  She married Andrew Haugh and died in Barony, New Brunswick about 1880.  Thomas was born in 1801 and lived in New York.  


It appears that John=s wife Janet Davidson, died and John then married Jean Johnston.  They had a number of children, all born in Scotland.  David was born in 1802 and died in Oxford, Nova Scotia about 1880.  Janet was born in 1812.  John was born in 1814 and died in Nitheville,  Dunedin, New Zealand in 1900.  Anne was born in 1817. 


I reviewed the information which Lawrence McGill provided but found no connection to my Great-grandfather, John Glendinning who came to Canada in 1830.


It is interesting to note, however, that that branch also went through a name change from Glendinning to Glendenning.
