From Donald Edge


There is a family story that Duncan MacNaughton (1787-1824) was going down the Black River on a raft of logs and commented that Alexander Gunn's Point would be a wonderful place for a cemetery. Not long after he was killed by a falling tree while clearing his land on the Little Branch back road.  He was the second person buried in the common burial ground on Gunn's Point that was owned by Enoch Godfrey and that was to become the site of St. Stephen's cemetery.  The first buried there was Robert MacBeath in 1823.

There was indeed a gravel pit immediately west of John W's.  I don't know the ownership--possibly Rankin MacLean--but lots of different folks would get gravel there, and MacNaughton household children would play there on the way to and from school. It was especially popular during pollywog season.  After  John W's death and the closing of the sawmill, much of the land on the north side of the road became a giant gravel pit to feed construction needs for miles around. The area looked like a moonscape; the sight brought tears to the eyes of my Uncle Mac.

I spent a lot of time at Rankin MacLean's --Marvin was my very best friend   I also went next door to see Ramsay--his home was spartan bare and very clean.  Ramsay chewed tobacco and had badly stained teeth, and he was fond of giving ladies young and old a kiss on the lips, something that the ladies quickly learned to ward off.
