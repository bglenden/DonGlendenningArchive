The Family Chronicle

No.45               July 18, 2003

                                                                                                                                 ________________Black River Cemetery


“What a beautiful spot,” the stranger said,

As he paused and reverently bared his head.

The golden rays of the setting sun

Shone on slab and shaft, where many a one

Now free from labour and earthly care

In a narrow bed lies resting there.


The Black River Water waters flowing peacefully by

Reflected the blue of the summer sky;

Through the leafy branches of the trees

Softly rustled a gentle breeze.

Not far away echoed sweet and long

The notes of the robin’s evensong.


Remote from the noise of the busy highway

This “Village of Silence” so peacefully lay;

The little white church stands guard nearby

With its spire pointing up to the sky;

Here when sounds the bell on the Sabbath day,

The people meet, to worship and pray.


The church of their fathers’ stood nearer the shore,

It has gone, like all things that are no more.

‘Twas the labour of those who came first to this place

A vigorous, God-fearing, industrious race,

To build homes and wrest a living from the soil was their goal

Yet they did not neglect the deep need of the soul.


Many that sleep in this place of rest

Are far from their homeland, and those they loved best.

While we see the names of some who lie low

In “Flanders Fields” where red poppies grow,

These brave lads and their comrades suffered hardship and pain

God grant that such sacrifice may have not been in vain.


There comes to us too a memory sweet

Of a pastor, who guided the erring feet

Of his flock. For more than thirty years

He shared all their joys, their sorrows and fears.

When he answered the call and “entered into rest”

He was laid in this spot that we know he loved best.


The robin was silent and all else was still,

The shadows were falling on river and hill;


The deep prayerful silence of that evening calm

Fell o’er the spirit like a blest soothing balm,

As we turned from the spot marked by newly turned sod

Our thoughts were turned upward and nearer to God.


May 21st, 1938



About the poem


My father, Elmer Glendenning, was buried in the Black River Cemetery on May 21, 1938.  The poem reprinted here appeared shortly after.  Although I have a copy of the poem, it does not show the name of the paper or the date of printing but I assume that it appeared in the Chatham Gazette.  I always understood that it was written by Mrs. F. A. (Bessie Watling) Fowlie.  The stranger, referred to in the opening line, may have been Sgt. Bedford Peters, an RCMP officer and cousin of my father who was stationed in Newcastle at the time


Callister Hall


I was pleased to have a phone call recently from Joy Ayer, a first cousin once removed, telling me that she had just returned from a visit to Scotland including a visit to Callister Hall.  My Great Grandfather, John Glendinning was born at Callister Hall on April 2, 1807 and came to Canada in 1830, purchased property along the east coast of the Bay de Chaleur but eventually moved to Canobie.  Callister Hall is a one-storey stone building with stucco in the midst of sheep country.  Joy also visited some cemeteries in the area.


Callister Hall had recently changed hands and she visited on moving day.  The new owners invited her in to look around.  Although I had visited Callister Hall I was not able to go inside.


Joy has another link with that part of Scotland through the Beatties; her father was a Beattie.  My
Grandfather’s Grandfather, James Glendinning, married Isabel Beattie but we’ve not yet connected our families through the Beattie line.


Obituaries


The Chatham Gazette of February 22, 1915 carried the following:


"The funeral of the late Jubal Watling was held on Thursday afternoon from his late residence, service started at 2:00 o'clock and being conducted by Rev. George Grant of Black River.  The choir of St. Stephen's Church, Black River, was present and rendered appropriate hymns.  Interment was in St. Stephens burying ground and the pall bearers were Messr. Alex Watling, William Watling, Donald Watling and Malcolm Watling, brothers of the deceased and John MacRae and Matthew Dickson, brothers-in-law.  The attendance at the service was very Large, those paying last tribute of respect to the deceased coming from miles around and testifying to their feeling of regret at the demise of a fine and widely respected citizen."


The Tri- Weekly (Chatham, NB) included the following item for September 28, 1934.


"Many friends in Little Branch and vicinity heard with regret of the death of Mrs. Jubal Watling, which occurred at her home yesterday afternoon.

The late Mrs. Watling was aged 86 years and before her marriage was Miss Grace MacDonald of Black River.


The funeral will be held on Saturday afternoon Service will commence at 2:30 o'clock and will be conducted by Rev. A. R. Gibson of Chatham.


Surviving are four daughters and four sons, Mrs. J. R MacKenzie, Loggieville; Mrs. J. W. MacNaughton ton, Mrs. J. R. Godfrey, Mrs. Elmer Glendenning, Black River; George and John, New Hampshire, James A. with whom she resided; Ernest Watling, Loggieville."

In my obsession is the following undated clipping:


"MRS. JUBAL WATLING


The funeral of the late Mrs. Jubal Watling took place from her late residence Saturday afternoon and was largely attended.  The service was conducted by Rev. A. R. Gibson of Chatham assisted by Rev. J. I. Morrison, of Black River, and the Rev. J. E. Forbes of Loggieville.  Mrs. F. A. Fowlie rendered a solo ‘Go Bury Thy sorrow.'  The eighth chapter of Romans was read as Mrs. Watling had requested.  ‘The Lord's My Shepherd' and ‘Safe in the Arms of Jesus,' favourite hymns of the deceased were sung.


The floral offerings were many and beautiful.


The pallbearers were Norman MacKenzie, Walter Glendenning, Gordon MacNaughton, Alex Newton (grandson), Jubal Dickson and James Jardine.


Interment was made in St. Stephen's cemetery.


Mrs. Watling was a lifelong Presbyterian, a sincere Christian, woman whose kingdom was her home and her children rise up to call her blessed.  Her gentle kindly ways endeared her to all who knew her and she will be greatly missed in the community."


The GLEANER and Northumberland, Kent, Gloucester and Restigouche Commercial and Agricultural Journal (p30) reports the following death in 1843:


"On the 31st of October, of dropsy, after an illness of eight months, which he here with truly Christian resignation to the divine will, Mr. Alexander MacDonald, of Little Branch, Black River, in the 68th year of his age, thus leaving a disconsolate wife, in the evening of her days, two sons and six daughters, to mourn the loss of an affectionate husband and tender father.  He calmly resigned his spirit into the hands of his Maker, in the firm hope of blessed immortality.  "Blessed are they who die in the Lord.'


Mr. MacDonald was a native of the Parish of Aasaynt, Rosshire, Scotland and emigrated to this country in the year 1803."


The Union Advocate, Newcastle, Miramichi, NB, A Weekly Journal Dedicated to Literature, Education, Politics, Agriculture, General Intelligence and Useful Information, reported the following death on Wednesday, March 4, 1874:


"At Little Branch, Black River, on the 23rd of February, Grace, relict of Alexander Macdonald, aged 93 years.  Deceased was a native of Co. Sutherland, Scotland and came to this country in the year 1803."
