Christmas Present


As I write this I am preparing to travel to Ottawa to spend Christmas with our daughter, Janet.  After traveling the world, Janet settled in Ottawa and works for the Federal Government.  A couple of months ago, she purchased her first home.  We also have a niece, Doreen, in Ottawa as well as some friends so we look forward to our visit.


We spent last Christmas in Socorro, New Mexico with our son, Brian, and his wife, Marie, and daughters Lisa and Laura.  This year, Brian and his family will travel to California where Marie’s father and mother, Mario and Estelle Carota, will be celebrating their 60th wedding anniversary.  

Congratulations.


Both Brian and Marie work at the National Radio astronomy Observatory.  Lisa is in her second year at the University of New Mexico at Albuquerque and Laura is in her last year of high school.  





The year has been a good one.  We spent April in Victoria, British Columbia where we drank in spectacular scenery, entertained two of Carol’s friends from nursing school, visited Islanders now living there and saw again colleagues I worked with over the years.


After a summer at the cottage, we enjoyed a two-week bus tour of Central Europe and then spent two weeks in Ottawa helping Janet move to her new abode.  Following this, I made my usual fall visit to the Miramichi.  And now, we are about to go on the road again.


Carol keeps busy with her painting classes and painting (watercolour) , her bridge and reading.  I keep busy with genealogy, writing and some volunteer work with the Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island.




Christmases Past


Christmas is a time to recall happy occasions (although not all Christmases qualify).  Following are some random recollections and thoughts.


Santa brought a red “caterpillar” style tractor.  I recall taking it up to my room, the one at the front of the house above the parlour, winding it up and watching it crawl over the bedclothes.

Lillian came home for Christmas and the people she worked for sent me a train, a Lionel”, including tracks.  It was not electric; one wound it with a key.

When I was older, I did not think there was a Santa Claus, but I did not want to take any chances.  When I got about half way up the stairs on my way to bed on Christmas Eve, I called my sister, Elinor, over to the side of the stairs and asked her to hang up a stocking for me.

I think that I was about sixteen when I went for the first time by myself to cut a Christmas tree.  I recall crossing the brook and wading through the snow to the back field.  Finally, I found a tree, probably on Uncle Jim’s property, chopped it down and dragged it through the snow to the house.

Mrs. Jim (James R.) Cameron always had a beautifully decorated tree that was loaded with ornaments.  As I recall, she had real candles on the tree and would sometimes light them when she had company.

Mum always made fudge on Christmas Eve and often wrapped some as a gift for Uncle Jim.  I remember taking it across the road on Christmas Eve.

Long after Lillian was married, Mum would make a Christmas fruit cake for her.  She made it in a tin can, about the size of a one-pound coffee can, and would mail it to Lillian in time for Christmas.

One Christmas, 1939 I think, I received seven books as gifts.

We didn’t open our gifts, except the ones from Santa Claus, until after the morning chores were done.  Then we would all sit around while the gifts were passed out.

Our Christmas stockings were clean woolen stockings of the kind we wore every day.  They usually included an orange and/or an apple, a few pennies or a nickel, peanuts, walnuts, maybe pecans, and hazelnuts, and candy.

Family members usually dropped by in the evening for a visit.  I remember going to the pantry to find extra candy for our guests.

Mum also knitted many pairs of socks and mitts as Christmas gifts.


Thanks


Over the year, I heard from a number of friends I hadn’t seen or heard from for many years.  Thanks for your notes and information.



With Best Wishes For 2003
