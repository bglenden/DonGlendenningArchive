New Bandon, NB


The following notes about New Bandon were taken from an undated article by Kevin Mann, Jr.  Although the source is unknown, the font size and style reminds me of the Moncton Times Transcript and is thought to have been written about 1982.

Seventy families came from Bandon, Ireland to New Bandon, NB in 1816

Ships sailing to St. Peters, now Bathurst brought settlers and returned to Ireland with timber, especially white pine.

Voyages took from 4-7 weeks

According to the 1861 census, about 60 percent of the residents were members of the Church of England; others were primarily Presbyterian and Methodists.

In 1861 there were five Jago households in the New Bandon area; Jago comes from McEgo and MacKigo.

The Dempseys consistently distinguished themselves in battle

The Daley family is one of the great names in Irish literature

Ellis is an English Christian name but is spelled Eyls in Ulster.

Good and Hornibrook names existed since the sixteenth century in county Cork

Buttimer is also a County Cork name and comes from Boytmer in England.

There was one Wiseman family, Joseph, together with his wife and 6 children in the New Bandon area in the 1861 census.

Sealy comes from the county Cork and includes the name Shally

Sergeant is originally and English name.

Payne at one time was spelled Pine

The Eddys came in 1826; it was also spelled Eadie and Aidy but has also been use synonymously with Adams.


Jean (Wilson) Smith wrote: 


Many thanks to Jean (Wilson) Smith for her recent note and kind words. For the record, Jean grew up in Black River but has roots in Dumfriesshire, Scotland; we are related through the Jardines.  Jean wrote:


“Another question is the 'lancer'.   We spent many winter noon hours dancing that at MRHS, as well as at school dances,

probably at community dances (which I never attended), and at the "Jamboree" at the Exhibition bldg in Chatham”


The lancer certainly was popular in the Miramichi; I recall going to a dances in Kouchibouguac where they danced the quadrille

 

Lancers are danced by sets of four couples; each dance is made up of five figures with music unique to each figure. The lancer is thought to have developed from a military quadrille.  The main source of settlers to Black River, it seems to me, were either disbanded English or United Empire Loyalist soldiers or Scots fleeing the land clearances.  Given its origin, it could well have been of English origin – but I am not sure.

 

I was talking to brother, Ken, just a few days ago about itinerants in Black River.  One, Abe Napke, sold small items from a box on his wagon.  Then there were people walking from Chatham or Newcastle to their homes Bay du Vin, Hardwicke or points beyond.  Sometimes they stayed overnight on a couch in the dining room; after a hearty breakfast, they would be on their way. I've heard of people sleeping in barns but cannot recall any specific examples.


I am not aware of railroad hoboes in Chatham.  Newcastle is a more likely place since it was a junction and on the main line between Halifax and Montreal.

 

A Jack Knife and an Apple


Yesterday, October 1, I sat on a bridge listening to the ripple of a brook below while enjoying the day and a picnic lunch.  My dessert was an apple.


As I peeled my apple, tossing the bits of peeling into the brook below, I recalled the many times as a youngster I had tried to peel an apple in a single apple peel; not always easy but it could be done.


Of course, that also brought back memories of the jack knife, especially one with a Boy Scout logo.


Is peeling an apple in one piece is on any child’s agenda today?  If so, is doing it in one piece worthy of mention?  Then I wondered if youngsters today even aspire to owning a jack knife. 

 

Telephone Lines


Recently I had a note from David and Ada Dorn who are related to the Cameron and Williston families in the Miramichi.  David mentioned a visit to Minnie (Williston) Fowlie, an Aunt, in 1974.  In giving directions to her house, Minnie said “just follow the telephone lines.”  


The NB Telephone Co. served Glenelg Parish including Black River; homes in Hardwicke Parish were served by “the Government System.”   George Adams, directly across the Little Branch River from my home was served by the Government system. I am not sure just how the Government lines reached Hardwicke parish – perhaps from up Bay du Vin River but, more likely, I think, from Pointe Sapin. In any case, Minnie was correct, if one followed the telephone lines through Black River they would end at the Fowlie home: telephone lines through Black R would not take one to Hardwicke. 


Bottle Brushes


Do names still have bottle brushes? Our house doesn’t.  They’ve been replaced with throw-away containers.  No one cleans bottles anymore.  Obviously milk bottles would have been a major use for bottle brushes.
