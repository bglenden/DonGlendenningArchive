Mrs. Jubal Watling


The Tri- Weekly (Chatham, NB) included the following item for September 28, 1934.


AMany friends in Little Branch and vicinity heard with regret of the death of Mrs. Jubal Watling, which occurred at her home yesterday afternoon.


The late Mrs Watling was aged 86 years and before her marriage was Miss Grace MacDonald of Black River.


The funeral will be held on Saturday afternoon Service will commence at 2:30 o=clock and will be conducted by Rev. A. R. Gibson of Chatham.


Surviving are four daughters and four sons, Mrs. J. R MacKenzie, Loggieville; Mrs. J. W. MacNaughton, Mrs. J. R. Godfrey, Mrs. Elmer Glendenning, Black River; George and John, New Hampshire, James A. with whom she resided; Ernest Watling, Loggieville.@


Jubal Watling


The Chatham Gazette of February 22, 1915 reported the following:


AThe funeral of the late Jubal Watling was held on Thursday afternoon from his late residence, service started at 2:00 o=clock and being conducted by Rev. George Grant of Black River.



The choir of St. Stephen=s Church, Black River, was present and rendered appropriate hymns.  Interment was in St. Stephens burying ground and the pallbearers were Messr. Alex Watling, William Watling, Donald Watling and Malcolm Watling, brothers of the deceased and John MacRae and Matthew Dickson, brothers-in-law.  The attendance at the service was very Large, those paying last tribute of respect to the deceased coming from miles around and testifying to their feeling of regret at the demise of a fine and widely respected citizen.@ 


Woodcutter


As I write this item, we are having beautiful but cool weather and the ground is still covered with snow.  I am reminded of similar mornings, 60 years ago, of being wakened about 6:30 to go across to Harper=s or Adams= to get an extra man or two for the woodcutter.  Perhaps someone was not able to come at the last minute or an extra person was needed.


So I would hie across the crust and in the lane about 6:30 to 7:00 am looking for someone.  Other than the beautiful morning and hiking across the crust, the thing I remember most was my fear of Harper=s dog.  I am sure, in hindsight, that the dog was had no intention of biting me but at the time I couldn=t tell what he was thinking.


Collecting Sap


We always tapped a few maple trees on the ridge when the nights were cool and the days were warm.  The sap was good to drink but had little if any taste.


I recall occasions of going to the Ridge with Ken early in the morning to empty the sap buckets.  Ken hitched the horse and we took milk cans, of the kind used to ship cream to the creamery and I recall once taking the barrel from the churn to bring the sap to the house.


On occasion, and maybe frequently, Mum would boil the sap to make maple syrup.  It takes a barrel, 45 gallons, of sap to make a gallon of syrup and it seemed to take forever to reduce sap to syrup.  It took even more boiling to reduce sap to maple sugar. Maybe it seemed like a long time because  because it was usually my job to keep the woodbox filled.


Glendenning Family Fund


Since last writing, a  Glendenning Family fund has been established.  The fund is set up in honour of Elmer and Jane (Watling) Glendenning by their descendants.  Grants from the Fund will be made for any charitable purpose in the Black River Area where they lived.  


Contributions totalling $2,200 have been received to date from the following donors:

Venetia Russell, Kenneth Glendenning, Norman Glendenning, Don Glendenning, Margaret Gibson, Brian Glendenning, Janet Glendenning, 


Other descendants have been or will be contacted.  If you want further information, please let me know.  Cheques should be made out and sent directly to The Community foundation of Prince Edward Island, 119-121 Queen Street, Charlottetown, PE C1A 4B3.  Please direct it to the Glendenning Family Fund.  Donations are tax deductible.


Frequency of Names


Janet directed me to a Website which provided the following tidbits.  Glendenning is the 37,937th most common surname in the United States;  Watling is the 87,290th.  I knew from looking in phone books that Watlings were scarcer than Glendennings.


Generations


The Spring 2001 issue of Generations, the NB Genealogical Journal, includes two articles about Black River by Ernie MacLean.  The first is about the Harper Family who came to Black River from Newtown in 1928.  The bought the John Mick property across the little Branch River on the West side of the road.  


Mr. Harper was a well-respected for his Avetinerary@ skills.  I can remember him coming to our place when an animal required attention.  Norm and Ken can tell stories about driving Mr. Harper on calls throughout the county. 


The second article is a brief history of St. Stephen=s Church.
