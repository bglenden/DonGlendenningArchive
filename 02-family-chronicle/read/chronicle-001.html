Why A Newsletter


The Chronicle is an occasional Newsletter devoted to genealogy.  More specifically, it deals with the genealogy of my father, Elmer Alton Glendenning, and mother, Jane Elizabeth Jardine Watling.  It=s purpose is to both share and seek information.  It will be published from time to time as the spirit moves me.


In addition to Watling and Glendenning, my ancestors include people named Harley, Peters, Sealey, Beattie, Rae, MacDonald, McAskill, MacNaughton, Leach and Jardine.  And I am sure that there are others I have not discovered yet.


In order to provide a perspective, I may refer to people as my Grandfather, Walter Glendenning or my Great-grandmother, Elspeth Jardine etc. This intended to show both a relationship and to help position people by generation.


I believe my information to be accurate, however, there are likely some errors in what I have.  If you find any, please let me know; I will not be offended.  


I also need help in gathering information; my focus, is on information about earlier generations but I will take whatever you provide.  Reports of weddings, births and funerals often provide information about family connections.  Make a copy and send it along.   


Beginning The Search


I started gathering family tree information, on the Glendenning side, over forty years ago.  My first source was the family Bible of my Great-grandfather, John Glendinning, who emigrated from Scotland in 1830.  The Bible was passed down to my father. I also contracted with the Scottish Ancestry Research Society in Edinburgh, Scotland to do a search.


Later, I distributed a questionnaire to my aunts and uncles on my father=s side of the family.  Then there was a long period when I did nothing.  During the last decade I re-started the process with attention to my mother=s side. 


Much of the more recent information was gathered during conversations with family members.  They, in turn, sorted through memories, clippings and notes.  My sister, Venetia, and cousin, Clifton Watling, were especially helpful in providing information on the Watling side.


As I travel, I frequently skim through phone books looking for the name Glendenning or Watling.  I frequently find Glendenning but seldom find Watling.  I have now concluded that there are many fewer Watlings than Glendennings.


Earliest Glendinnings


My Great-Grandfather, John Glendinninng was born in Callister Hall, Scotland on April 2, 1807.  His father was John Glendinning and his mother was Mary Rae.  I believe, but have not been able to prove yet, that his Grandfather was James Glendinning and his Grandmother was Isabel Beattie.


Callister Hall is, or at least was, shown on maps of Scotland.  It is between Annan and Langholm and not far from Dumfries and Lockerbie.  It is also close to a little hamlet named Glendinning but I think that the latter has disappeared from recent maps.


I wish I could tell you that Callister Hall  is a grand castle surrounded by walls and a moat but it is not.  Callister Hall is a simple one-story stone building out in the hills without another house in view although one can see for miles around.  When I reach the stage of having a scanner, I will include a picture in a future newsletter.


A number of years ago I visited Callister Hall and

spoke with the current occupants; they are not Glendinnings.  Apparently for a number of years Callister Hall was a coach stop.  Furthermore, I was told, that Callister Hall suffered a serious fire.  Being stone, however, the damage would be contained.


The Family Bible includes the following entries:


> John Glendinning (came) to this country in 1830 - (Married in 1833) from Annan, a small seaport In Scott Land.  Was himself a native of the aforesaid country.  Born in Callister Hall, Parish of Middlebie, in 2 of April 1807 "


A In Memory of John Glendinning who died May 15th A.D. 1877 in Canobie, Parish of New Bandon, County of Gloucester.  A native of Scotland, Dumfries.  Born April 2nd A.D. 1807.  Died May 15th 1877.  Aged 70 years. @


Harley Connection


John Glendinning married Margaret Harley on, I believe, August 23, 1833.  She was born on October 23, 1803 and died on October 21, 1895.  It is likely that she was related to George and John Harley, shipbuilders, who worked at Russell=s mill on what is now Beaubear=s Island.  I have not done any investigation yet of this side of the family.  Uncle Guy Glendenning told me once that John Glendinning and Margaret Harley are buried in the Presbyterian Cemetery in New Bandon, Gloucester County, New Brunswick.


Knowles Connection


On a headstone in the Anglican Cemetery in Salmon Beach (?) One finds the following:


William Knowles - 1783 - 1881

Elizabeth Harley - 1796 - 1876


Harley is not a common name and, therefore, it is likely that Margaret and Elizabeth were related - maybe even sisters.  This relationship is further supported by two other beliefs.  My sister Venetia remembers visiting A Aunt Jane Knowles @.  Furthermore, when my sister Lillian first went to work in Boston, she visited a Sam Knowles who, she thought, was related to our father. (Information on this likely connection would be greatly appreciated)



Peters Connection

Grandmother Glendenning was a Peters.  Her father was John Peters and mother was Jane Sealy.  Grandmother had a brother Norman (after whom my brother was named), a brother Robert, and a sister Susan.  (Hopefully a reader will be able to provide more information)



... and the other went West.


It seems that every family tells the story of  two brothers coming to Canada at the same time but they got separated either before or after the crossing.  The other brother always A went west@.  It may be true in our case but I have not found any evidence yet.


I have some family tree information about other Glendennings in the Maritimes but have not been able to establish any connection.  I am aware of Glendennings in St. John, Moncton, Amherst, Spring Hill, Sussex, Harvey Station and other places but have not found a link so far.  It is likely, however, if we could trace back another generation or two in Scotland, that a definite link could be established.


Male Descendants


One is struck, when examining the male side of the 

Glendenning family, how few of the males produced male offspring.  The first generation born in Canada to John and Elizabeth (Harley) Glendinning included two males (John and Walter) and three females (Margaret, Mary Ann and Elizabeth Jain).  


John had one son, John, who according to my record died at age 23.  Walter, my Grandfather, had 12 children, seven being boys.  My generation includes 46 first cousins on the Glendenning side with only 14 being male.  I do not have information about the next generation.


My plea


There is not much more that I can do in gathering family tree information without your help.  It doesn=t have to be fancy; just send the information on a cigarette pack, shingle, cheque, grocery list, toilet paper (unused) or whatever.  I will be glad to get it and will acknowledge it in my next newsletter.  ______________________________________
