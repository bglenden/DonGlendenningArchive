Correction


Walter Glendenning was born Sept 29, 1913 not Sept. 23 as given in the last Family Chronicle.  My records were correct but I copied it incorrectly.  Sorry.


An Added Focus


While I will continue to search for family connections and dates, I plan to devote more attention to family members, family activities and even favourite family stories.  While I hope to be accurate, readers must remember two things.  First, I was the youngest of the family and much of what I write would have come to me second hand; Second, we remember selectively.   One=s memory of yesterday may not always be accurate; it is likely, therefore, some of the dates, names, persons or places will be incorrect.  I ask readers to let me have corrections or at least their versions of the event. 


Jeanette Watling


I was browsing the LDS database on the internet recently and came across the name of Jeanette Nickolson Watling who was born on May 28, 1852 in Napan, NB.  She married in Dover, Mass. But we do not know her husbands name.  Her father was John Watling and mother was Jessie Carruthers.


Adams


I also found information about Uncle Tom Adams.  He was one of 10 children of James Adams and Ellen Farish; all were born in







Margate, PEI.  Sometime after 1880 Tom, his father and mother and his brothers George (who lived across the Little Branch River from Norman Glendenning) and Jonathon moved to Little Branch.  Tom and Aunt Tine were married on August 17, 1895; Tom died on Sept. 29, 1902; they had three children Gertrude, Willard and James. (Aunt Tine married John MacKenzie in August, 1907) I remember someone saying that George Adams travelled one time to PEI by horse and wagon to visit relatives.


Alexander MacDonald Property


The following was taken from the book by A. W. MacDougall.


AAlexander MacDonald came from the Highlands with the 1803 immigration.  He settled on Lot #3 of the Third Division, on the west side of the southwest branch of Black River...


It has been told of the elder Mr MacDonald , that when the Government Surveyors were allotting the land to the prospective settlers, he was told to proceed along the shore till he had secured a sufficient frontage for his lot, the surveying party following him downstream in a canoe.  Land being plentiful, Mr. MacDonald decided to secure as large a grant as possible.  After he had gone a considerable distance, his further progress was barred by a creek, but he plunged in and waded across, only to be brought to a halt on the other side by the government men, who considered that he had already acquired sufficient.@  (Would this be Miller=s Brook?)

I have not searched the titles but believe that the Alexander MacDonald property would be the properties now owned by Norman Glendenning I have not searched the titles but believe that the Alexander Macdonald lproperty would be the properties now owned by Norman Glendenning and Neil Watling.  


I do not know, but it is likely that the Alexander MacDonald home was located above the home now occupied by Norman Glendenning.  There was a depression, like that from an old basement, near the orchard.  Since the Little Branch River provided transportation, it would make sense to be close to the water.  Lillian recalls old lumber piled in the field roughly in that area.  It is possible, however, that the house was actually in the next field above the orchard (Uncle Jim=s field).  At one time there was a pump in this field.


If I=m correct, half the property was given to or purchased by his son John and the other half passed to or was purchased by daughter, Jessie (1823-1912).  Dad bought Jessie=s property in 1912.


The John MacDonald property passed to Jubal and Grace (MacDonald) Watling.  They moved from Upper Black River (above MacBeath=s Bridge) to the Branch to look after John and/or Elspeth (Jardine) MacDonald and were given the property in return. (Believed to be before 19030.  As I further understand it, Jubal left the property to sons James and Ernest; later Ernest sold his share to James (Uncle Jim)


A Close Call


It was Christmas of 1938, the year Dad died; Lillian came home from Boston where she was working.  Walter was driving the Model A taking us to shop in Chatham.  In those days, one always travelled via Loggieville on the way to Chatham.  Walter was driving, Mum was in the front passenger seat, I was in the middle. 


 In the back seat were Ken, Norm and Lillian.  John and Elinor were home; Grace was married and living in Boston, Venetia was married and living in Point Aux Carr.


The front right window in the Model A was broken; it was a clear cold day and the window had been replaced with a piece of cardboard.


Between Loggieville and Chatham, we caught up to a brownish truck with a wooden box belonging to George Adams Sr. ( Or would it have been Jesse or Tommie Adams?  There were some people riding on the back of the truck but I cannot recall who.


Just as we reached the railway crossing, we heard a train whistle; a train was bearing down on us from the right on its way from Chatham to Loggieville.  Applying the brakes would likely mean sliding right on to the tracks in front of the train; Walter pushed the gas pedal to the floor and we just crossed the tracks when the train whistled across the road behind us.  It was a close call.  


Later in Chatham, someone from the back of the Adams= truck said that they were so certain that the train was going to hit us that they put their head down so as not to witness the crash.
