Issue 22 · Nov 11, 2001
Compiled on Remembrance Day, this issue documents family members' military service across WWI, WWII, and the Boer War. Includes Don's uncles, siblings, and first cousins with service details (units, dates, decorations). Notable entries: Don's uncle Guy Wellington Glendenning (55th Battalion, wounded three times, leg amputated), Uncle Donald Ernest Watling (26th Battalion, MM), brother Kenneth in RCAF (1942-1945, reenlisted to 1984). Don notes the North Shore Regiment's D-Day landing at Saint-Aubin-Sur-Mer. Important family contribution record.

No.22 November 11, 2001

                                                                                                                                  __________

I am drafting this newsletter on Remembrance Day to record the names of family members who were in the service; not all served in a theatre of war.  While I have included all I am aware of, it is likely that some have been omitted and the information on some may even be incorrect.  I would welcome additional information about any or all including identification number, unit or regiment, dates of service, etc.  


I=ve limited this list to siblings, uncles and first cousins.  I would be happy to expand to second and third cousins, and there are

many, but do not have the information.


Uncles/Aunts


Glendenning, Guy Wellington enlisted on July 3, 1915 in the 55th Btn of the Canadian Overseas Expeditionary Force, commenced duty in France on April 10, 1916, was wounded three times, had a leg amputated on October 12, 1918, returned to Canada on Feb 2, 1919 and was discharged on June 11, 1919


Glendenning, Margaret Violet served overseas as a Nursing Sister with the British Expeditionary Force during WW1.


Watling, Donald Ernest served overseas in the 26th Btn. (#70039) during WW1, was wounded at Manchy,  and won the MM 


Siblings


Glendenning, Kenneth Elmer served in the RCAF as an airframe technician during WW2 (1942-1945) and reenlisted in the RCAF in 1949 retiring in 1984.


Glendenning, Norman Peters served in the RCEME during WW2 (1944-1945). 


Glendenning, Walter Jubal served in the army during WW2.


Brothers-in-law


Taylor, Earl Alexander joined the Royal Canadian Army Medical Corps in 1942 (#G23970 and was discharged in 1946.


Weeks, Kenneth Brydone joined the North Shore Regiment in 1939 went overseas but returned to be an instructor.  He was discharged in 1945.


First Cousins


Adams, James of Chippewa served overseas in WW1 and served in Canada in WW2.


Chase, Hartley served overseas with North Shore Regiment.


Cormier, James Walter served in Army


Cormier, Sanford George served overseas in North Shore Regiment in WW2


Doring, Walter Glendenning served in the US Army of Navy in WW2


Doring, Verna Elizabeth served with the US military as a nurse in the South Pacific during WW2.


Glendenning, Gordon Franklin served overseas with the North Shore Regiment during WW2


Glendenning, Marson Burton served in Navy during WW2.


Glendenning, Sterne Richard served overseas with the North Shore Regiment during WW2.


Glendenning, Venetia Louise served in the RCAF during WW2


MacNaughton, Luella Gertrude served in US Army during WW2.


MacNaughton, Malcolm James served in RCAF in WW2


Newton, Alexander served overseas (Italy) during WW2.


Watling, Clifton served in the Army.


Watling, Ernest served overseas with the North Shore Regiment and won the MM.


Watling, George Elbridge served overseas with the US Navy during WW2


Watling, Roy Mills served overseas during WW2.


Watling, Winfield Jubal served in the RCAF during WW2.  Postings included Iceland.


Others


Although not as directly related, I=ve decided to include the following:


Everard MacLean served overseas with the North Shore Regiment and was a POW for a number of years.






MacNaughton, Archibald served in both WW1 and WW2, was killed on D-Day and is buried at Beny-Sur-Mer.


Mills, Archie was a brother to Aunt Kate (Mills) Watling and was killed overseas during WW1.


Watling, Jubal served in the Boer War.  After the war, he married and continued to live in South Africa where he died in 1962.


The North Shore Regiment went ashore on D-Day at Saint-Aubin-Sur-Mer in Normandy.  The people of the village erected a memorial to the North Shore at the spot where they came ashore.  There is also an  official monument but I was not able to visit it.  


Across the road from where the North Shore Regiment landed is the large house which frequently appears in video footage of the Canadian landing.  Also in the same area I found the ANew Chatam Restaurant Creperie@ presumably honouring the home town of many of the liberators.
