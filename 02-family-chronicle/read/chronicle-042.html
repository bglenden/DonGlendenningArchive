Traces Watling family from Jubal Watling's marriage to Susan Leach in England (1814) through settlement in Canada. Documents land grants and property transfers at Little Branch (Meadows and Little Branch Road areas). Includes extended interview transcript with Uncle Jim Watling (age 94) recalling 1890s Christmas celebrations, simple gifts, homemade fun, and family values. Also includes poem "Black River" by F. J. MacDonald celebrating the river and ancestral connections. Extensive genealogical narrative establishing connection to land holdings. Published June 7, 2003.

The Family Chronicle

No.42               June 7, 2003

                                                                                                                                 ________________Meadows to Little Branch


Jubal Watling married a Susan Leach in Metfield, Suffolk County, England on November 14, 1814 and moved to Syleham, Suffolk County, England where their son, Jubal, was baptized on August 17, 1823.  They likely stayed in England, however, the following children came to Canada:

Jubal who married Christina MacNaughton

William who married Mary MacNaughton

Mary Ann who married William Maxwell

George who moved to the USA

Emma who married William Eddy


Jubal Watling petitioned for land (F4243) on the south side of Black River on April 9, 1851, presumably this is the Jubal who married Christina MacNaughton.  They also had a son, Jubal. 


Alexander MacDonald was granted Lot #2 on the Little Black River on June 2, 1812.  Half the property passed to his son, John, (where Uncle Jim Watling lived) and the other half passed to his daughter, Jessie.  My father bought the Jessie MacDonald property on July 21, 1914.


Grace, the daughter of John and Elspeth (Jardine) Watling married Jubal Watling on Dec 22, 1874 and, moved to Lot #30 in The Meadows.  (The Meadows has long since been reclaimed by the forests.)  All of their children were born there.


I believe that the elder Jubal died in 1900 and his wife, Christina died in 1901; Grace’s father, John MacDonald also died in 1900.  Graces mother was not well  (She died of “debility” in 1907) and Grace’s brother, James, was living at home but was not able to look after the farm. An agreement was struck late in 1901 for Elspeth MacDonald and her son, James, to turn over the property at Little Branch to Grace and Jubal.  In return, Grace and Jubal, and their sons, agreed to “keep, support, maintain and provide the said James J. MacDonald and his mother, Mrs. John D. MacDonald, each of them and the survivor of them during the respective terms of their natural lives with good and sufficient house, roof, meat, drink, washing, clothing, bedding, fire, light, medicine, medical attendance, personal expenses and all such necessaries as persons of their ages and conditions in life usually require and are accustomed to.”  The 

agreement was also signed by all of the sons.


My Grandfather, Jubal, died on February 16, 1915 and Uncle Jim came home from Portland to take over the farm and look after his mother.


Janice Cowan, presumably of the Miramichi Press, interviewed Uncle Jim and wrote the following:


Recalls Christmas in 1890’s

   The smell of delicious cooking in an Upper Black River Farmhouse kitchen during the 1890’s is what James Watling remembers about his first boyhood Christmases.

   Watling, 94, now a resident of the Miramichi Senior Citizens Home said: We always had plenty to eat.  I can’t remember turkey, but we had chicken and roast pork.”

   On Christmas Eve after the eight Watling children had finished the farm chores they hung up their stockings at the foot of their beds. 

   “We were tickled to death to find an orange, an apple or candy in them in the morning.  Or perhaps a pair of newly knitted socks,” he said.

   There were no luxuries.  Things were homemade, but we made do with very simple things.

   The sheep provided us with wool, which was carded and woven into jackets, pants and sweaters.  The girls could make clothes for their dolls.

   People go overboard at Christmas these days.

   “It seems to be ‘get all that you can’ and ‘give all you can’.  It’s not what Christmas is supposed to be at all.”

What Christmas Means

   “Christmas to a great many people is just the chance for another holiday.  My parents were Christian people.  They tried to bring us up as best they could with the knowledge that Christmas meant the birth of Christ.  We had Bible reading at the table.  All this has disappeared.

   “Too much money is spent these days, especially when we know many people in other parts of the world are starving to death.  This is not the Christian spirit”.

   James Watling’s father, Jubal Watling was a lumberman.  He and his crew were in the woods in wintertime including Christmas.  

   So the chores were done by the children, Christina, Elspeth, George, Janet, Jack, James, Jane and Ernest.


   The Watling family also celebrated New Year’s Day “as most Scotspeople do.

   We seemed to have more snow in those days,” laughs Watling.  “I think that’s because the roads weren’t snowploughed.  They were kept open using the horses.

   Sometimes a storm would confine us to our home.  We were a mile from the more settled part of Black River.  We had two close farming neighbours, Donald Cameron and Gardiner Archibald.

Made own fun

   “At Christmas time we would sometimes meet at each other’s house and play games, or dance or sing.

   “People were very friendly and made their own fun in those days.”

   “Watling cannot remember having a Christmas tree in those early days.”

   “I don’t think that we bothered with them.  There was no such thing as decorations or trimming.”

   “He went to school in Upper Black River.

   “We walked in the horse tracks through the snow in the winter,” Watling recalled.  It was about a mile to school.”

   The school was a one-room school, with one teacher for 40 students.

   “Out of that class came a doctor, lawyer, bookkeeper and three or four teachers,” said Watling. 

Home made outings

On Christmas Day, the children would play out on their homemade toboggans.  They also had snow shoes made out of pieces of straight cedar.

   “We split them down thin and shaved them.  We called them racquets.  They would carry you easily through the snow and into the woods.”

   The youngsters had skates too, and James remembers skating on a wide drain, falling down hitting his head and “seeing stars”.

   “People were happy in those days”, said Watling.  “They lived off the land.  The community was helpful if you were in trouble.”

   Watling said the women in those days, including his own mother Grace (MacDonald) worked hard.

   “The main thing is there was substantial food and we all enjoyed it,” added Watling.

   He took over the Black River Farm from his father and he and his wife Catherine lived there with their four sons and a daughter.

My recent Email included a number of items about Frances Jane MacDougall MacDonald, daughter of Kenneth and Catherine (McGraw) MacDonald.  Here is a copy of the poem she wrote.


BLACK RIVER


Flow gently Black River, flow on to the bay

By the happy old homes, as you pass on your way.

As I stand by your bank in the hush of the dawn,

I think of the people and days that are gone.


From a small, rippling brook, over many a ridge

You come dancing along ‘neath the old covered bridge;

And you blink at MacNaughtons, MacLeans and MacRaes,

As you glisten so brightly in the sun’s sparkling rays.


But softly you glide as the point comes in view,

There our loved ones are sleeping, it seemed that you knew

That once, they too watched you go rippling by

Then a moment you paused there, then passed with a sigh.


But on you must hasten, past MacDougalls, McKnights,

And then from MacDonalds, through dim misty light

Echoes music and laughter like a bird that has flown,

And I stand by the river – and dream there alone.


Flow calmly Black River what stories you know

Of courage and vision. of sorrow and woe

Of the plans for the future of joys yet to come

And the happy reunions as we met at each home.


Flow softly Black River may your spell always last,

And watch o’er our kin as you have in the past;

May they learn to love you and find as you flow,

The secret of courage and faith you bestow.


Flow ever Black River, flow on to the sea

And bear on thy water this message from me

To those who now watch you, there’s faith deep and strong

To be found in thy depths, so meet life with a song.


We’ve left thee Black River, but ever we know

Your influence and vision around us will flow;

And your sweet lullaby will hush us to sleep,

When we rest at “The Point” and your vigil you keep.


F. J. MacDonald, Portland, Maine
