Peter Stewart 1812-1891

Catherine (Stewart) MacNaughton was my Great, Great. Great Grandmother. Her brother, Peter shown above, would be my Great, Great, Great Great Uncle


Thanks to Ellen MacNaughton for the above photo and the following note:


“Attached are two letters I scanned this evening.  The letters are from Peter Stewart.  I also wanted to share with you that after my last email I had a reply from Helen Proudfoot to say that Peter Stewart was her great, great grand uncle (1812 - 1891).  He was completely blind and would have dictated the letters.  I have included an excerpt from Helen's email below:

"One of his eyes was destroyed by some kind of illness or infection and the other by an accident in childhood and there is a story of my great grandmother, still newly married, rushing to get him a lamp when she found him working in the shoemakers' workshop - and then being agonizingly embarrassed when her husband's uncle explained he could "see as well in the dark as the light".

"He was a basket maker and was locally famous for his fishing flies, as well as composing Gaelic hymns."

The post script of one of the letters scanned this evening states that he is not able to write the letters himself.

I then sent an Email to Helen Proudfoot and below is her response:


"Ellen has sent me your email. In it I find you expressing an opinion which I have held about 90% for four or five years now. I think I might be up to 95% as a result of all the detail in these letters, though I still keep in mind that Highlanders are bad for talking about "cousins" when the relationship is more distant or complicated than full cousin. I myself sometimes address emails to Cousin Colin - but he is my fourth cousin. 

 

The Alexander and Peter who wrote these letters were respectively the oldest and "youngest surviving" sons of Allan Stewart who farmed at Stronuich and Invermearan, and who seems from the letters to have been tacksman of Kenknock as well.

 

Allan of Stronuich was, according to my great grandfather, the son of Alexander Stewart who was a tenant in Milton Eonan, so this Alexander would have been Catherine Stewart or McNaughton's father if the writers and recipients of these letters were full cousins. Alexander of Milton was in turn one of the two known sons of Alex Stewart, who founded the line at Woodend, so this first Alex would have been Catherine's grandfather. Unfortunately I have no reliable information about the wives of these Alexanders, but I do have other information about the family if you are interested.

 

My great grandfather Alexander Stewart was the youngest child of Alexander and Jean Stewart at Woodend, who were second cousins, descended from both sons of the first Alex at Woodend. Jean, my great great grandmother, was a sister of the Alexander and Peter who wrote the letters.

 

The two deaths at Woodend described in the two most recent letters were of two of my great grandfather's older sisters, Ann and Catherine. Catherine died of influenza - I have the death certificate. There was an influenza pandemic in 1857, so the description of her death from this illness which Peter says "commences like an ordinary cold" has added horrible fascination just at the moment.


MacDonald Anniversary


According to my records, Alexander MacDonald was granted Lot No. 2, 225 acres, on June 03, 1812 – that is the lot where Norman, Alton and Eric Glendenning as well as Neil Watling now live. The property has been owned by Alexander’s descendants ever since.


Alexander was born in Assynt, Scotland, came to Cape Breton in 1802, spent some time in Point Aux Carr, and eventually settled in Little Branch.


Other grants awarded on the same date include Kenneth Sinclair, Angus Sinclair, MacLean siblings, Farquhar McRaw, Kenneth MacDonald, Duncan MacNaughton, Enoch Godfrey, John MacLean, Alexander Gunn, Robert MacBeath and John MacLeod.


Alexander’s descendants married into the Mackenzie, McKnight, MacKay, Robertson, Ward, Loggie, Allen, Cameron, Jardine, Watling, Fowlie, MacDiarmid, McGraw and Godfrey families. Given the above, many Black River residents can claim him as an ancestor.


Seems to me that 2012 marks an important anniversary, that of settling along the Little Black River. I urge readers to consider celebrating Settlement Day, the 200th anniversary of the first settlers arriving in Little Branch
