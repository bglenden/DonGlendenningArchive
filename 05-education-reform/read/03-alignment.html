Occasional paper exploring "alignment" concept in education systems. Discusses alignment meanings including curriculum-assessment coherence (common definition) and broader alignment between education system design and community needs/goals (Don's focus). Examines alignment areas: policy, strategy, standards, assessment, curriculum, professional development, school-life integration, reporting, and management. Provides extensive examples of alignment applications in school improvement, curriculum design, teacher preparation, and standardized testing. Argues school principals, as designated instructional leaders, should have authority to adjust alignment variables. Offers practical advice for parents whose children struggle: review course outcomes against child's needs, check report card-outcome alignment, request progress criteria, and discuss school support with principal. Alignment


Occasional Paper D-0-A March 28, 2015

Alignment is a key characteristic of skills training, that is the manner in which curriculum and assessment are linked and aligned; and that characteristic seem to pervade training at all levels whether it be for the CEO or the newly hired front line worker. No, the actual training is not the same but the planning process is founded on similar principles. I reached this conclusion many years ago when working with the fledgling Canadian Society for Training and Development, this was confirmed a friend who was, before retirement, a senior bank executive, and recently I witnessed it again during a presentation by the Director and staff of Workplace Learning PEI.

Alignment has a variety of meanings but is used most in education to refer to curriculum development and refers to coherence among outcomes , instruction and assessment. There is a broader view than is of the coherence between the education provided and the needs of both his or her education and the community it serves. It is the latter that is the focus of this paper, defined as the degree or manner which an education system organizes to help its community to achieve desired goals.


Areas of alignment include:

  1. Policy – alignment of policy and practices those of the public sector generally; gender equality, equal pay for work of equal value, access,


  1. Strategy

  • Strategy: School leaders may work to “align” the organization and operation of a district or school, including how students are taught, with a given school-improvement plan, reform strategy, or educational model. In this case, the alignment process might entail a wide variety of reforms—from reallocating budgetary expenditures to restructuring school schedules to redesigning courses and lessons—in ways that are intended to achieve the objectives of the improvement plan, while also ensuring that its parts are working together coherently and effectively. For a related discussion, see action plan

  1. Standards

  • Criteria, benchmark, criteria and assessment practices that are consistent with those foung in the public domain

  1. Assessment

  • Teachers may “align” assessments, standards, lessons, and instruction so that the assessments evaluate the material they are teaching in a unit or course. Test-development companies also “align” standardized tests to a state’s learning standards so that test questions and tasks address the specific concepts and skills described in the standards for a certain subject area and grade level. In individual cases, teachers may align assessments and lessons more or less precisely, but developers of large-scale standardized tests utilize sophisticated psychometric strategies intended to improve the validity and accuracy of the assessment results (although this is a source of ongoing debate). For a related discussion, see measurement error.

  • Curriculum: Educators may “align” curriculum in different ways, but perhaps the most common forms are (1) aligning curriculum—the knowledge, skills, topics, and concepts that are taught to students, and the lessons, units, assignments, readings, and materials used in the teaching process—with specific learning standards, and (2) aligning various curricula within a school, such as the curriculum for a particular course, with other curricula in the school to improve overall coherence and effectiveness. In the second case, for example, educators may align curricula by making sure that courses follow a logical learning sequence, within and across subject areas and grade levels, so that new concepts build on previously taught concepts. For a more detailed discussion, see coherent curriculum.

  • Professional Development: School leaders, educational experts, reform organizations, and government agencies may “align” professional development—such as training sessions, workshops, conferences, and resources—with the objectives of specific policies, improvement plans, or educational models. For example, state education agencies may provide training sessions for superintendents and principals to help them implement new teacher-evaluation requirements, or districts and schools may contract with experts and outside organizations to help their faculties learn new educational approaches or teaching techniques.





School principals have been designated as instructional leaders in their schools and, presumably, have authority to adjust the above variables as needed.



Not all alignment problems arise from school variables under the control of the school but, If your child is having a problem with a subject, services for your child, I suggest the following (1) review the outcomes for the course or grade of concern to see if they match your child’s needs, (2) check the most recent report card to see if the subjects listed match the outcomes planned, (3) ask for a copy of the criteria used to check your child’s progress, and (4) talk with the principal to see if the school can arrange more study time, if needed.
