Our Money’s Worth

Each year we spend about $10,000 for each child in the public school system; that’s over $100,000 by graduation.  For that, schools provide great child care services but a somewhat uncertain level of education.  We can all point to students who used the school system and went on to great accomplishments, but too often I am hear stories of students not served well.  And there is a rising level of frustration in the land about the gap between what we expect of schools and what schools provide.  I see it as an organization problem rather than an education 

But first we have to understand that the school system does not operate on the usual rules.   Normally a person or organization provides a service and users decide whether or not to use it.  In most cases, the user of the server has choices first whether or not to participate at all and, if so, on the choice of provider:; , and the providers income reflects in some way the nature and level of service provided.  Not so in education so there is a reduced need to communicate and few consequences if they don’t.

The matter is further complicated by the fact that government decides the school you go to, the courses available, the teacher you’ll have, the qualifications of teachers, the texts you’ll use, how long you’ll stay, the documents you’ll reveive and the kind of information you receive about your progress.  And government is not inclined to listen to your ideas, ignores your complaints and makes you feel uncomfortable for asking questions, etc., etc.  And it appears that, for whatever reason,  the gap between user and provider has widened and, Over the years, it
