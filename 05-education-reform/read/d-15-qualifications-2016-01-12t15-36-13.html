Achievement levels


As I write, I’ve just finished a conversation, the third in three days, which included the matter of student achievement; to be more specific, part of each conversation included discussion of the confusion arising from the practice of giving two meanings to the word “Grade”.


School grades, I am told, date back to Harvard University in the late 1800; age based grouping refers to grouping students with their peers thereby allowing them build and share interests and experiences during their formative years; it’s part of a school’s culture.


The other use of “Grade” describes a level of achievement in a school subject. Confusion arises as gap between the two measures widens.:


  • Students who do well become bored.

  • The public loses confidence in the education system

  • The value of a graduation diploma falls.

  • Taxpayers lose trust in the public school system.

  • Keeping peers together has become more important than meeting standards.


There are alternatives; there is precedent. The most common alternative is to create a new term for achievement; the common term is level. There is no magic number of subject levels but five to seven seem common. As a result a Grade 11 student could be studying Level 7 math, Level 4 physics and Level 6 literature. It shouldn’t be a difficult transition and I believe that students would benefit from the change.

Basic Skills

Education 20/20 has been exploring the matter of basic skill levels among our youth: while we are still in the very early stages of our exploration, it appears that:


  • There is no minimum list or level of basic skills needed to graduate from high school.

  • There is no single Province-wide definition of what constitutes basic skills.

  • Many recent high school graduates do not possess the skill levels needed to enter postsecondary institution or secure and hold jobs.

  • Most people consider basic skills to include communication ( reading, writing, listening and speaking), practical mathematics, personal management skills and sometimes a computer language.
