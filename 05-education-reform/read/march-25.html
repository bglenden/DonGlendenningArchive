During the COVID-19 pandemic, Don challenges the term "remote learning," arguing that only learners can learn and learning happens where the learner is. Support for learning can be remote, but learning itself is not. He references the World Bank's observation that educators confuse "going to school" with "getting an education," and advocates for focusing on function rather than place. This piece reflects Don's philosophical precision and his effort to correct conceptual confusion even in common parlance.

March 25, 2021


Dear Editor:

Is Remote learning possible?


My Globe and Mail this morning carried an item to the effect that remote learning is likely to continue in Ontario long after the pandemic ends; in checking locally I also find reference to remote learning on PEI. As a colleague would say, “it’s a puzzler”. Let me explain.


Learning is about citizens changing the way they think, feel or act; only a learner can learn and learning can happen most any place, any time or under any circumstance. We can help learners learn, we can provide information, encouragement, demonstrations or even give an award but  only a learner can learn – the learner is always where the learning takes place.


On the other hand, support for learning can be with the learner or miles away, can be personal or virtual, one shot or ongoing, but it is the help not the learner that is remote. The reference to remote learning tends to confirm the World Bank view (Report on Education, May 2019) that Educators  are prone to confuse going to school with ‘getting an education”.  


It’s time to focus on the function not the place.


Sincerely


Don Glendenning.
