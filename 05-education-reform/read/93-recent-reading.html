No. 93 August 20, 2020

When schools and ministries are managed well, learning happens-World Bank


RECENT READING

Some recent items that caught my eye:


Hechinger Report of September 25, 2020 reports that “Colleges have an ethical responsibility to prepare all students for the world of work.” Colleges (in the US) are using a number of approaches to ensure career readiness including engaging faculty as career advisors, arranging for students to observe employment and meet employers and preparing career maps. These services are especially important for disadvantaged students.

The Globe and Mail, October 2, 2020 carries an item on Paul Bennet’s book, The State of the System: A Reality Check on Canada’s Schools. Bennet proposes a new set of priorities including: putting students first, democratizing school governance, deprogramming education ministries and school districts and listening more to parents and teachers.”


The Sydney (Australia) Morning Herald reports that “Almost one in 10 trainee teachers fails to meet literacy and numeracy standards required for graduation, prompting an internal government report to call for the tests to be held at the beginning of the degree so students don’t waste their time.”



The Washington Post of September 23, 2020 carried an article about Paul Quinn College in Dallas, Texas, partnering with Guild Education, a Denver-based firm that works with companies such as Walmart and Lowe’s to provide education benefits to employees.


The Globe and Mail of Aug 22, 2020 features “hammering without a nail” at the British Columbia Institute of Technology but also does a quick scan across Canada as to how colleges and institutes are adjusting their approaches to accommodate Kovid.


Canadian Vocational Association Newsletter ( CVA/ACFP <info@cva-acfp.org) carries international items on vocational and employment training. An item from the EU on “Skills for industry curriculum guidelines 4.0” Their strategy includes taking a proactive rather than a reactive approach by (1) Preparing students for lifelong learning, (2) Offering big-picture education, (3) considering needs of market, society and individuals. (4) shifting from knowledge to competencies and (5) offering personal and personalized learning.


Don Glendenning, Editor
