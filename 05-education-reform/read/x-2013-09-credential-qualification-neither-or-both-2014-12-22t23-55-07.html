Letter distinguishing between credentials (diplomas/degrees certifying program completion) and qualifications (attesting demonstrated competency). Triggered by a University of Moncton professor controversy, Glendenning argues transcripts inadequately assess citizenship/employability skills and proposes redesigned transcripts or gold seal endorsements reflecting basic skills competency needed for modern economic participation.

September 29, 2013


Editor, Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

Credential or Qualification?

The recent publicity about the qualifications of a professor at the  University of Moncton raises issues about the usefulness of using diplomas and degrees as screening devices. 

Diplomas are issued by educational institution to let people know that the holder has completed a programme of studies; degrees are granted on a similar basis.  Degrees and diplomas are considered credentials in that they may provide entrance into an arena but are not an indicator of how well the holder will perform once there.  Diplomas and degrees are too large a mesh for screening employees; too many good workers are missed.  

Employers can quickly assess technical skills; neither transcripts or diplomas provide information about 

basic skills; I call them citizenship skills, other call them employability skills, generic skills or even life skills.  

Perhaps a redesigned transcript would provide the information

A second transcript dealing with skills needed in every occupation but also in life generally

Perhaps even an endorsement, a gold seal, to identify not only that a person has graduated but as reached a level in basic skills to allow him or her to participate in the modern economy.  As a first step, a conversation with your local high school principal  It seema s as though both employers and teachers want to , place to start is with a dialogue

Sincerely



Don Glendenning

In the case of the professor, his field of study and recognized expertise were quite different fields.   

A long-time student of education

A qualification, on the other hand, describes what a person can do; the issuer of a qualification attests to the view that the holder has demonstrated the attitudes, skills and knowledge required to successfully perform in an occupation, role or task.
