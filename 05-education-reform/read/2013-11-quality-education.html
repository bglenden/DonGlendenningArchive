Don calls for shifting education measurement from "time in seat" to quality outcomes. He defines learning as developing attitudes, skills, and knowledge to navigate life roles (individual, family member, learner, citizen, consumer, producer). Using UNICEF's 2000 quality education framework, he notes progress on healthy learners and safe environments but identifies content relevance, child-centered processes, and holistic outcomes as needing attention, suggesting these may not yet be on the provincial agenda.
Education Philosophy & Reform

November 21, 2013


Editor, Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:


It’s time to change the education agenda.  Counting the length of time we have “bums in school seats” is no longer an adequate measure of education, if it ever was.  It is time to switch the focus to quality.  First, however, a couple of comments about what education is and what it is for.


Learning is a process of changing, gaining new or different attitudes, skills and knowledge to help us along the road of life.  The road of life is what we do and what happens to us between birth and death. It’s been said that the road of life involves a number of life roles, that of an individual, family member, learner, citizen, consumer and producer.  Based on this view, a quality education is one that prepares us well to cope with, embrace, enjoy and contribute while on that road.  


 In a 2000 report, UNICEF identified the characteristics of a quality education as:

Learners who are healthy

Environments that are healthy and safe

Content that is relevant

Processes that are child centered

Outcomes that include attitudes, skills and knowledge


We’ve made great strides in the first two; much remains to be done with the other three.  Sometimes I think that the last three aren’t even on the agenda.
