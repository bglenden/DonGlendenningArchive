This is a follow up to your request for additional information in support of our application for registration as a charitable organization with Canada Revenue Agency. Rather than rewrite the original application, I am providing supplementary information in response to questions raised in out telephone conversation of October 6, 2015; I have also added information about activities undertaken or completed since the application was presented.


A) Response to questions:

  1. Additional details about previous workshops: Workshops are designed to maximize engagement, they normally commence with an opening speech or panel presentation; all allowed for questions from the audience and some included facilitated discussions, flip chart recording and reporting back to participants. Rooms are usually arranged to accommodate 6-8 people at tables; some sessions, but not all, included IT support. For early workshops, we provided coffee and juice but neither proved popular so now we limit meetings to the provision of bottled water. Whenever possible, we find facilities available courtesy of a government, local NGO or volunteer agency; handouts are distributed at some sessions.


  1. Employers:

Exploration of the “preparedness gap” continues. Following considerable informal discussion, the first formal meeting was held on Wednesday, November 25 in the Board room of Ellis and Birt, 27 Brackley Point Road, Charlottetown. Our President chaired the session (copy of agenda enclosed) which included representatives of the Department of /education English Language School Board, Holland College, UPEI, PEI Literacy Council the Community foundation and employers. We are currently gathering information for the next meeting to be held at the call of the chair.





  1. Other education providers:

We recognize the contribution to education in PEI of agencies other than schools; we have been in touch with a number of institutions, have become aware of some of their needs and added them to our mailing list.


  1. Assessment: We continue our exploration of the role of assessment in the education process and concluded that, while formative assessment is a legitimate role for educational institutions, summative evaluation is not. Our activities to date have been exploratory in nature: we find, for example (1) at the international level, neither Canada nor the USA have “qualification authorities, (b) Canadian education authorities tend to rely on input measures (what is taught, rather than output measures (what has been learned) for purposes of comparison and (3) there appears to be no widely recognized graduation document available that provided the information that employers need or want when making their decisions to accept or reject applications. We are still in the “information gathering” stage but expect to become more active in the coming months.


  1. Creativity/Imagine Sessions:

The charting of a learning path was an “imagine activity”; the initiative started with a few students, blank flip chart and the question “what kind of help do you want/need to support your learning? Out of it came a number of discussions, a learning path and a clearer understanding of the conflict that arises when teachers also grade. We are The next will likely be a session to chart the role of teachers followed by “greenfield” projects for in which groups would be encouraged take on the design of a community “learning centre” Participants would be given a minimum set of criteria outlining safety and legal requirements and left to use their imagination as to a design.

  1. Webinar: While still believing in their value, we have put the activity on hold until we find additional resources.


  1. Emerging fields: Reorganization is consuming most of the energy of educators in PEI at the moment, however, the Minister has announced new funding in support of technology in schools and even the introduction of coding into schools One of our Directors serves on the Minister’s advisory committee. In addition, we see a growing interest in providing young children with computer skills outside the school system. Much the same could be said about robotics. Other than that, we are maintaining a watching brief. We certainly have no plans to provided credit courses in any of these fields

B) Other events:


  1. Handle with Care: (Planned) a workshop for parents and caregivers of young children is being planned for Hunter River, PEI, however. Ms. Taylor, session leader, is recovering from surgery and a date has not been finalized. The workshop in Hunter River will be hosted by Rochelle Shephard and the Early Learning Centre.


Handle with Care is a national program designed to help parents and other caregivers promote the mental health of young children every day! It is useful for everyone who cares for or works with children; especially parents, grandparents and teachers, and consists of sets of interactive strategies that build on the strengths of adults in their daily lives. The program is based on four building blocks: Trust and Attachment, Building and Enhancing Self- Esteem, Expressing Emotions and Building Relationships with Others. Each block builds upon the one before and contains the following components: Key message, Research, Activities, Bringing it Home and Bringing it Together, other resources and a Summary Handout. The key to a successful program is trained facilitators. Each participant is required to purchase a text book

Handle with care is a national program developed by Hincks Delcrest Centre in Toronto and the Canadian Mental Health Association and with funding from public Health Agency of Canada. It was piloted with 439 participants in four centres, two in Ontario and one each in Prince Edward Island and Manitoba. The developers of the program promote and allow its use but insist on trained facilitators. Ms. Taylor was a member of the original team supervised the testing process in PEI and is involved in its implementation in Nova Scotia; PEI has at least two fully trained facilitators and a number of others with some training.

2 Learning Path: Our original submission describes one of our workshops, “From a Learner’s perspective” in which we invited a panel of students to identify the kinds of help learners found useful. The initiative generated considerable interest Following some informal discussion, and an additional session was held on May 14 at the Haviland Club with about 35 participants present; those present included senior educators, President of PEIHSF< retired educators, parents and the public at large. An enlarged copy of”A Learning Path” was placed on a tripod as a focus of the discussion and the Chair facilitated a round table discussion. Following the meeting, minor adjustments were made to the document; it was posted on our website and published as an occasional paper. (See enclosed documents)

3 A Bridge to New Careers: was a presentation by Mr. Gerry Seaward, Director of Adult Basic Education Program at Holland College. Adult Basic Education is an inclusive term covering instruction to students who, for the most part, are high school graduates but who lack credits needed for entrance to university or Holland College. Perhaps the most interesting part of the presentation was the statistics showing that students not only had increased their grades from previous years but had actually reduced the number of days to complete the course.
