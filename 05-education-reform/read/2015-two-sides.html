Don distinguishes between professional and organizational problems in school systems, arguing that education typically addresses professional issues (resources, training, pilots) while ignoring organizational ones. He advocates for organizational solutions: flexible scheduling, alternative delivery models, after-school tutoring, extended school years for struggling students, challenge-for-credit options, online courses, school autonomy, and self-directed study. Without addressing organizational barriers, professional interventions lose effectiveness.

January 28, 2015


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

Two sides to reform


A school system is a complex organization; issues that arise may be either professional or organizational.

 

When problems arise, there seems to be an automatic assumption that the problem is a professional one; as a result, changes may be made in resource materials, equipment, training for teachers and principals, run a pilot, develop new courses, appoint a committee or give more tests.


Even when problems are clearly organizational, seldom it seems, do we consider changing the way we schedule or the way we deliver, offering after school tutoring sessions, extending the school year for those who need it, allowing students to challenge for credit or graduate early, encouraging students to take on-line courses, giving more autonomy to schools and teachers, or allowing and rewarding self- study.


Until we tackle the organizational issues, many of the professional interventions lose much of their effectiveness.


Sincerely



Don Glendenning

A long time student of education
