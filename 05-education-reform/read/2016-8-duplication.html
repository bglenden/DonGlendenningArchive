Don identifies apparent duplication in PEI's education system: the Department of Education teaches essential skills (reading, numeracy, writing, communication, thinking, computer use) to school-age learners, yet the Department of Workforce and Advanced Learning must teach identical skills to unemployed youth. He questions whether graduates already possess these capabilities and suggests taxpayers are "paying twice for the same service." He advocates for achieving educational goals effectively the first time rather than remedial spending.

August 1, 2016


Editor,


Dear Sir: 

Paying twice for basic education


The Guardian of July 27, 2016 (“New program launched for unemployed youth”) describes a national initiative to provide young people with essential skills and work experience.  The program includes the teaching of essential skills, skills which the PEI Literacy Alliance lists as: reading text, document use, numeracy, writing, oral communication, working with others, thinking skills, computer use and  continuous learning.  I’m glad to see such a program but I wonder why we didn’t teach those skills while the young people were in school.  I’d also be curious to know how many of the people requiring upgrading already have a graduation diploma. 


To the layman, it looks as though taxpayers are paying twice for the same service, once to the Department of Education and a second time through the Department of Workforce and Advanced Learning.  If so, that is a “waste” given the importance of education and the scarcity of dollars.  If it is true, let’s change our practice and do it right the first time.


Sincerely,




Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
