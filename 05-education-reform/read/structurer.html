Dear Editor

Restructuring called for

D-10-B - extended

Many years ago, a PEI school trustee spoke of the need for structural reform in our education system. Recent snowy weekends have given me a chance to investigate; he was right. Here are my notes.

  1. Vision - a public statement of an organization’s goals , purposes. ideals, core values and long- term objectives; a vision statement usually also lays out beliefs and guiding principles. Although PEI has a philosophy statement, it iis short on detalls neded to build educational programs.

  2. Conceptual clarity – education documents tend to leave out important detail of what is intended or planned. For example, although PEI documents are about education, they actually treat “education and going to school” as though they are one and the same. A school, like a hospital or shopping plaza, at best is only a resource rich location; education is influenced by student experiences while there.

  3. Structure - ”Form follows function” is still the standard when structuring any venture and one would expect the management structure to reflect functions or services offered. In addition, front line workers in a service organization need both the skills and flexibility to respond to learner needs.

  4. Accountability - The OECD tells us that PISA results are better from schools with autonomy in their day-to-day operation; the World Bank advises Ministries of Education to delegate. Mathew Bedard of the Montreal Economic Institute points out that “autonomy and accountability jointly applied” would cost practically nothing and allow education to better adapt to the diversity of student needs and aptitudes. Yet the education sytem in PEI appear to have little freedom to tailor services to meet individual learner needs.

  5. Collaboration refers to educators working with the community to increase student learning: Evidence suggests that the more people that are invested in a student’s education process, the greater the chances of student success. Perhaps unfairly, teachers are not viewed by the community as collaborators or communicators in the education process.


I am not suggesting that these issues are unique to education in PEI, are new, that they were deliberate nor implemented knowingly. Quite the contrary, each was introduced to solve some problem. Collectively, however, they hobble education’s ability to serve and should be modified or discarded.


Sincerely

Don Glendenning
