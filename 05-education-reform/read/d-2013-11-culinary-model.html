Dated November 21, 2013, Don draws parallels between culinary education's evolution and needed educational transformation. He recalls PEI's shift from quantity cooking (for institutional food service) to refined culinary arts with professional chefs, driven by changing market demands and entrepreneurial vision. He argues schools similarly need to evolve from delivering standardized content to fostering creative, rewarding learning experiences with teachers as skilled craftspeople. The letter reveals his belief that education, like the culinary sector, benefits from industry leadership, professional autonomy, and responsive innovation.

A Culinary Lesson 

The evolution of the culinary sector on PEI during the past 30 years is, I believe, instructive for education. 

I remember well the discussions around the establishment of the Culinary Insttute of Canada; they were exciting times but they were times of great learning.  And one of the lessons learned was the difference between the “quantity cooking” program then being offered and the culinary program envisaged.  We need to haave that conversation about education  In 1969 lunch time for most Islanders were eaten at home; most restaurants were small and family operated.  Restaurant workers were mostly trained on the job, trained through short preseason extension courses and graduates of cooking courses offered by PVI and PCVHS.  The focus of the available training was “quantity cooking” and was intended for people employed in hospitals, school cafeterias and the ferries.
