Achievement levels misnamed


The Sydney Morning Herald of Saturday, July 22, 2015 carried an item based on a report by the Grattan Institute on the wide range of achievement levels in a typical classroom in Australian schools. In Math, for example the range could be as much as 8 years. I’m told that the same applies to classrooms in PEI and results in both frustrated teachers and students. The article concludes that, and I quote, “it is not possible to remove the spread of grade levels and, therefore teaching methods must be adjusted.” That’s thinking inside the box and come from being wedded to grouping by age level for all activities and at all costs. That’s the “factory model” referred to in a recent issue of the Washington Post.


School grades make it easy for those who administer programs to count who is doing what, when and for how long; in addition to adjusting teaching methods, we also should seek ways to regroup students, especially in the basic skills, so that they have better access to assistance when needed:

  • I visited a high school once where all students studied math at 9:00 am, English at 10:00 am and science at 11:00; for the remainder of the day students were with their peers.

  • Peer teaching.

  • Application of more, better or different technology.

  • Clinics and tutoring services

  • Independent learning programs and activities


St the moment, we are focused on direct delivering help in a school setting; tomorrow we need learning managers/advisers who will help students access the help they need whether that be in school or elsewhere in the community.

Education Excellence

The BC Minister of education, after consulting widely with parents, teacher, students and other pesters plan to transform education

Reform is based on five key elements: personalized learning for every student, quality teaching and learning, flexibility and choice, high standards send Learning empowered by technology

The BC Minister of Science and Universities announced in early July that BC would recognize “excellent teaching” and “make good teaching better.” While details remain to be worked out, one idea being circulated is that universities that qualify, be given approval to raise tuition.


Curriculum review

A new Kindergarten to Grade 12 curriculum will be phased in by the 2017-2018 school year. The curriculum emphasises collaboration and critical thinking and will focus on reading, writing and arithmetic. The announcement include a figure of $1m support for training sessions for teachers, learning resources and district level teams, setting aside time for collaboration and professional development.
