"A Switch to Numeracy" proposal articulating Don's educational philosophy that numeracy (practical mathematical application) better serves citizen needs than traditional mathematics instruction. Distinguishes between math (study of numbers/space) and numeracy (applied ability in contexts). References PEI curriculum goals, Scottish Education definitions, and proposes platform development through community analysis. Shows Don's commitment to shifting educational focus from theoretical to applied competencies.


A SWITCH TO NUMERACY

Pro/Num-2

Purpose:

Develop a universal platform to support instructional services for Islanders wanting/needing to increase their numeracy levels.


Definitions:

  • Numeracy refers to the ability to use mathematics in a variety of context, including the workplace.

  • Platform is a standard structure for the presentation of instructional services in support of learners and learning.


Background

All schools, it seems, teach math, the study of the science of number, quantity and space. it is considered a key skill in all life roles; The Prince Edward Island Government public-school math curriculum guide lists the goals of mathematics as being to “prepare students to solve problems, communicate and reason mathematically, appreciate the value of mathematics, make connections between mathematics and its applications, commit themselves to lifelong learning and become mathematically literate adults using mathematics to contribute to society.”


Numeracy, on the other hand, refers to the ability to use “mathematical approaches” in everyday life; it complements literacy and together they are needed to thrive in modern life. Numeracy skills are needed to some degree by all citizens and in all life roles, can be learned by all or practically all Islanders. Having strong numeracy skills means having the ability to:

  • Interpret data, charts and diagrams

  • Process information

  • Solve problems

  • Check answers

  • Understand and explain solutions

  • Make decisions based on logical thinking and reasoning


Educators are faced with a dilemma; how much math does one need to be numerate? Or to be more accurate, mathematics, numeracy or a combination of the two? The dilemma may be likened to that of driver training. The objective is to teach safe and responsible driving. How much does one need to know about an automobile to handle normal driving. The literature suggests that, for most of us, numeracy is the answer.




The Oregon Department of Education contends that math provides “a foundation for the study of science and technology as well as the interpretation of quantitative information in other subjects.


Curriculum

The identification, description and organization of materials and services in support of instruction is called curriculum; principles of curriculum development include:

  • The primary purpose of developing curriculum is for the benefit of students

  • Curriculum outcomes/competences should be clear and measurable.

  • Outcomes/competencies should be drawn from every say life


Conclusions

Based on a search of the literature, discussions with colleagues and the writer’s own experience:

  1. Competencies are better than outcomes as a basis for instruction

  2. Numeracy will best serve the math needs of most Islanders

  3. Mathematics instruction is fragmented

  4. Improving achievement requires cooperation by providers at all levels.

  5. There is a need for a common set of definitions for key development concepts.

  6. Cooperation among providers is hampered by the lack of a common development platform.

  7. Evaluation of student performance should be based on the same criteria at all levels of instruction.

  8. A professional organization for math/numeracy instructors should be encouraged

  9. The need to improve math application skills is a key issue with employers


Proposal

To develop a platform for the delivery of numeracy skills
