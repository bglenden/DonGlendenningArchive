Letter to Charlottetown Guardian (February 12, 2017) examining implicit contracts between educational institutions and students. Education Philosophy & Reform

February 12. 2017


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir: 

The education contract

A few days ago, I came across an article about a student Guarantee Program at the University of Regina under which students, who do not find a full time job within six months of graduation, may return tuition free for an additional year of undergraduate study. I was reminded of a Canadian College that introduced a warranty program a number of years ago under which students  from the college who lacked skills for employment or certification in a field for which they had trained, could return to the College free of charge to fill the gaps.  And then there are the conversations I overhear about Island students who plan their high schools program in a way to take advantage of upgrading courses at Holland College – and perhaps even receive an allowance while doing it.  All of which raises issues as to the role of schools and postsecondary institutions and their accountability.


One may argue that education is either a product or service; when I think of teachers helping students to read, calculate and operate equipment, I think of education as a service.  On the other hand, when an institution hands a student a diploma or a degree, the relationship changes; the institution is treating graduates as products. It also seems to me that when an employer or postsecondary its entrance requirements like “high school graduation” must have completed Math 621 reinforces the product analogy


All this arises out of trying to sort out the accountability of schools and the school system.


Sincerely,


Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
