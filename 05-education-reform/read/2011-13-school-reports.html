Don critiques school report cards designed for post-secondary institutions rather than employers, noting that help-wanted ads emphasize skills (problem-solving, communication, teamwork, project management) largely absent from academic transcripts. He observes schools organize instruction around disciplines while colleges/universities do likewise, creating university-focused reporting systems. Don proposes schools and employers collaborate with colleges/universities to design comprehensive report cards including both subjects and transferable skills. He argues employer engagement would be valuable even without format agreement, advancing dialogue among stakeholders about educational deliverables.

Dear Sir:


School Reports


Each level of schooling should prepare students to enter the work force or to move to the next level of

education.  Schools, however, seem to tailor report cards to the needs of postsecondary institutions; I

am not sure why the information needs of employers are overlooked.


Schools organize instruction around disciplines or subjects. Teachers assess and report student progress

in the form of a letter or number grade on each subject whether it be history, biology, music,

electronics, biotechnology, or any other. Since colleges and universities also organize instruction in the

same way, the high school reporting system appears to provide the information they need to accept or

reject students into further studies.


"Help wanted ads", on the other hand, list skills needed or practiced in the workplace, such as the ability

to solve problems, perform analysis, communicate with others, work independently, manage projects,

negotiate contracts, operate heavy duty equipment, or install windows and doors. (The Grant Thornton

advertisement in the Guardian of Wednesday, August 3,2011 is a good example.) School report cards

that l've seen are silent on the matter of skills, leaving employers without the benefit of ratings or

comments.


It's time, it seems to me, and for employers, colleges and universities to get together with schools to

design a report card that includes both subjects and skills. lt is in employers interests to take the first

step; such a dialogue would be useful even if the parties involved cannot agree on a common format.


Comments welcome at standards2020@smail.com


Sincerely




Don Glendenning
