Don examines the disconnect between high school requirements and post-secondary admission practices. Referencing Australia's experience where universities admit up to 70 percent of students below minimum ATAR scores, he questions why post-secondary institutions spend resources remediating material taught months earlier in high school. He poses critical questions: What is high school's actual role? Do diplomas mean anything? Why don't under-prepared high school graduates return for upgrading? Should post-secondary institutions develop independent entrance assessments instead of lowering standards? He suggests financial analysis could reveal educational system truths.

January 20, 2016


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:


The matter of postsecondary institutions accepting “unqualified” students has come up in a number of recent discussions about education on PEI; I found it also in my Saturday reading (The Sydney (Australia) Morning Times of January 31, 2016 - Regional NSW universities are also flouting minimum marks cut-offs for many admissions). Australia uses ATAR (Australian Tertiary Admission Rank) results to assess and compare the school-leaving applicants applying for entrance to university. It appears that universities have taken it upon themselves to flout agreed upon standards for a high percentage of their students, perhaps as high as 70 percent. I have not found figures for PEI but, with the importance of post secondary training, one wonders why scarce postsecondary dollars are being spent to re-teach what was taught in high school a few months earlier.


The questions I hear most frequently include:

  • What is the role of a high school anyway, and who decides?

  • What pattern of school subjects best prepare young people for the next stage of life”

  • Do high school diplomas mean anything anymore?

  • Why shouldn’t a high school graduates who do not meet college entrance requirements be able to return to a home school to make up credits?

  • Why should postsecondary institutions use scarce dollars to upgrade students to meet entrance requirements?

  • If high school documents cannot be trusted, why don’t postsecondary institutions develop or adopt their own entrance tests?


An examination of the financial side of education could reveal some interesting educational truths.


Sincerely,




Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
