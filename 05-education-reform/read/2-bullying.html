Glendenning addresses school violence and bullying by first asserting that schools are not responsible for causing the problem but may have a constructive role. He identifies multiple intervention points: school policies (establishing safe learning environments), curriculum additions (conflict resolution, teamwork, communication), organizational models supporting individual learning and responsibility, and facility design/scheduling. He emphasizes the need to address fundamental questions about schools' role before implementing reactive programs, and calls for attention to environmental factors affecting behavior rather than treating symptoms alone.

The Government provides opportunity, encouragement, teachers and other resources to assist students

to obtain a quality education but only learners can learn. lt is a responsibility of learners, or their

parents, to ensure that learners take advantage of the resources. Teachers certainly play a role in

motivating students" Students make individual decisions about if what, when, how and how much they

learn; even when we are teaching, learners may be learning something quite different from what is

intended"


As for transportation, there are at last two separate points of decision-making. People providing the bus

service, or the operators themselves, should decide whether or not it is safe to drive. lf conditions are

unsafe, buses should stay off the road and students along the route should be informed of the change of

plans. Even when buses are operating, parents are the ones to decide each day whether or not their

child gets on the bus. lf bus transportation is not available, a parent may arrange alternative

transportation if they know the school is open.


Frequently I am asked if the above policies will not result in some students being ahead or behind others

and if the gap will widen. This implies that students in the same grade are at the same level of

achievement and move forward in a lock-step fashion. This is far from the case. ln any class, some

students are ahead and others behind the average - whatever average means. The system copes with

students who lose a day due to illness, why can't it also cope with a student who loses a day due to bad

weather? Granted the number of absentees may be larger on a storm day, but the problem remains

essentially the same.


Closing schools on stormy days is administratively convenient but I believe is disruptive to students and

learning. The decision to attend or not attend on any given day belongs to the student and/or parents. I

am quite satisfied that teachers are both willing and able to deal with school absences in a less

disruptive fashion. .

Dear Sir:


Bullying


Bullying and violence in our schools seerns to be on the increase and I am greatly disturbed by the trend.

Schools cannot and should not be blamed for this development; neither are they responsible for fixing

the problem. They ffi?y, however, have a role to play, and I know that some schools have implemented

activities and programs to deal with the issue.

It seems to me that we need to deal with some fundamental questions around the issue: Do schools

have any responsibility for reducing or eliminating violence on school premises? lf the answer is yes,

school policies may be required or need review. Some schools, maybe all, already have such policies. lf

not, they can be developed. The policy may be as simple as "Each school will provide an environment

that is safe for learners and conducive to learning". Some changes may be required in school rules and

regulations. Ask your principal about policies for your school.


Some curriculum changes may also be needed. Perhaps more attention should be given to studies in

conflict resolution, team work, communications, etc. lf we decide that safe schools are important and

the curriculum is full, some of the current subjects may have to be replaced.


We should examine also how schools are organized to see if there are alternative delivery models which

better address the issue of violence. There are some who would argue that the current model is not the

most supportive of individual learning, team work, responsibility, self-discipline, etc. There certainly are

other models which can provide equivalent academic results. Results from schools operating on other

models should be readily available.


Finally, one must ask whether the facilities and schedules contribute to violence. Certainly rigid

scheduling and the development of individual responsibility are at odds but that is not to say that rigid

scheduling causes violence. A different approach, however, may help.


I do not know the answer to the question of school violence but I believe that we owe it to our young

people to provide the best and safest learning environment that we can. And we should start now.


Sincerely
