Achievement levels


As I write, I’ve just finished a conversation, the third in three days, about reporting student achievement; to be more specific about the confusion arising from the practice of applying two meanings to the word “Grade”.


Labelling students by Grade has been a feature of schools since the beginning, with the great majority of students moving each year from grade to grade with peers. Such an arrangement allows students to build and share interests and experiences during their formative years; it’s part of a school’s culture.


But “Grade” has another meaning, this one a level of achievement in a field of study – Grade six Math, for example. We know that students learn at different rates so it is not surprising that teachers report subject matter gaps of 4, 6 or even 8 years in an age group. It is impossible to close that gap; as a result:

  • Students who do well become bored.

  • Students who fall behind become discouraged

  • Teachers become frustrated

  • Parents wonder what is going on

  • The value of a graduation diploma falls.

  • The public loses faith in the public school system.


It’s time to select a different word to identify levels of achievement; “level” appears to be the preferred word. Changing the term would allow a student to study any subject at any level. As a result, a grade 11 student could be taking Level 7 Math, Level 4 Physics and Level 6 Literature. The IB program does it, the Canadian Red Cross does it for swimming, and some qualification authorities do it. So should the public school system.

Basic Skills

Education 20/20 has been exploring the matter of basic skill levels among our youth: while we are still in the very early stages of our exploration, it appears that:

  • There is no minimum list or level of basic skills needed to graduate from high school.

  • There is no single Province-wide definition of what constitutes basic skills.

  • Many recent high school graduates do not possess the skill levels needed to enter postsecondary institution or secure and hold jobs.

  • Most people consider basic skills to include communication ( reading, writing, listening and speaking), practical mathematics, personal management skills and sometimes a computer language.
