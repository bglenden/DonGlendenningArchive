A lingering doubt returns


High schools need to rediscover their “chief reason for existence”. That was the basis for a major study of US high schools in the 1930’s; it applies to many high schools in Canada today. The study concluded that every student had a right to optimal development but also noted that individual development also contributes to the common good.


A “lingering doubt” about the relationship between high schools programs and success in college has returned to the minds of educators. As a result, findings of the Eight Year Study are re-emerging in conversations and the media, most recently a few weeks ago in the New York Times. After following students from high schools that broke the mold through four years of college, , the Study found that the best way to organize education at the high school level. Students in schools that broke the mold did as well, and oft times better, in college than those who graduated from traditional programs, the kind you still find in most Island schools today.


Over a period of tight years, hence the name, Eight Year Study, thirty secondary schools, some private and some public, participated in the study and 250 colleges and universities agreed to suspend their normal entrance requirements; students journey through college life was tracked and analyzed. The only subject where high school study seemed to make a difference was music.
