Don explains why education faces more reform pressure than health or justice services, identifying four key problems: public schools lack market incentives for improvement, operation follows formulas (ratios, hours, salaries) rather than standards, scripted curricula ignore individual needs, and services are bundled through single providers. He articulates government's appropriate roles: providing leadership, funding, access, learning culture, quality monitoring, and accountability reporting—notably excluding direct school operation. This letter synthesizes his governance philosophy, emphasizing market mechanisms and accountability within government-funded systems.
Education Philosophy & Reform

July 12, 2014


Editor, Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

Role of Government 


There is growing pressure for change in education, more pressure than I’ve seen on any other government department.  One cannot help but wonder why.  Compared to health a and justice services, There are many reason; my initial examination suggests the following:

Public schools are not market sensitive; no financial incentive to produce a better product or to do it more efficiently.

Operation of schools is largely formulae driven (student teacher ratio, hours of instruction rather than standards achieved, salaries based on credentials rather than nature and level of position) 

Schools deliver a scripted curriculum to all regardless of individual interests and needs

Services (instruction, credentials and assessment) are bundled and available through only one supplier.







Public or private

Choice or determined

Employees or self employed 

Education or knowledge

Education or school based

Bundled or diverse



I understand why a government believes that education is important and why it should have a Department of Education; what puzzles me, however, is why government wants to, or thinks it should, actually operate schools.   Schooling is only part, and a declining part at that, of what constitutes an education system, yet it appears to consume most of the Department’s time and energy.  So why does the Department operate our public schools?  I’ve asked the question of many people and no one has been able to provide a plausible answer.  


Government is preoccupied with schools to the detriment of education; it needs to step back from the daily operation and assume a leadership role.  Education would benefit from less bureaucracy, more school autonomy,  greater engagement and and increased accountability for student progress.  Delegating operational responsibility should also depoliticize most educational issues.


My thinking is that Government’s role is to:

Provide leadership

Provide funding

Ensure access

Create a learning culture

Monitor quality, consistency and fairness.

Report to its citizens

Sincerely


Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
