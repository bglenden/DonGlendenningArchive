Article No. 90 (April 15, 2020) addresses post-COVID educational impacts, anticipating long-term changes rather than "return to normal." Don notes Australian post-secondary institutions facing decimated budgets from lost regional students and uncertainty about their return, while endowments supporting disadvantaged students recover slowly. He predicts employers may shift from credentials to qualifications and from education products to services. The piece outlines seven structural systemic elements needed: overarching beliefs framework, service-based organization (listing leadership, information/counselling, funding, products, instruction, human resources, quality assurance), distinguishing education from schooling, service-industry culture, autonomy-accountability joint application, professional independence, and de-politicization. Don criticizes conflating education with schooling, noting this distorts schools' role, increases costs, and bureaucratizes services.

1. Over-arching beliefs - that set a framework for planning, developing and implementing educational services. Such a document would inform the public, set the tone, increase consistency and reduce red tape throughout the system.

Organization of services - Education is a service industry; typical functions or services include leadership and governance, Information and counselling, funding, products and technologies, instruction, human re-sources and quality assurance. Typically, the structure of organizations is based on the dictum “form follows function” as the basis for of the organizational structure. The Department seems to have ignored this rule. As a result, some services appear to have been left out.

3. Tendency to use education and schooling interchangeably Failure to distinguish between education and instruction; Seems strange to suggest that that educators are sloppy in their use of words. I say this because of the practice of using education and instruction (schooling) interchangeably. We do not so confuse agriculture and farming or health and hospitalization. The practice results in a serious distortion of the role of schools, adds to the cost, bureaucratizes the service and is a stumbling block to improvement. It is unlikely that education will become the service we deserve until we learn to define and describe it more clearly.
