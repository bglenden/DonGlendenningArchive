Don outlines five low-cost indicators of educational improvement requiring only administrative change: appointing community members to curriculum committees, restoring student exemptions and challenge-for-credit options, updating education philosophy beyond school-age focus, addressing basic skill deficiencies across stakeholders, and granting schools and boards greater autonomy with accountability measures. He emphasizes these changes need no significant budget impact, demonstrating his pragmatic approach to systemic improvement.


August 10, 2015


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

What to look for


Recently I was asked what one should watch for as a sign that Government is  listening to Islanders about the need for change in education.  Here are five from my short list:

The appointment of community members to curriculum committees

A return of exemptions and an expansion of opportunities for students to challenge for credit.

An updated philosophy of education that applies to all Islanders not just to young people in schools

A coming together of stakeholders to solve the problem of basic skill levels

Some new measures of autonomy and accountability for school boards and schools.


I offer these first because all can be achieved at the stroke of a pen and with little or no actual cost.

Sincerely,


Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
