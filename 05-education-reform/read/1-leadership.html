Editor letter to Charlottetown Guardian (July 27, 2018) arguing that education, particularly public education, remains in "constant turmoil" with growing gap between needed and provided services. Analyzes three key issues: government education role overly narrowed to school operations (criticizing as equivalent to delivering all health through hospitals); "fear factor" and politicization limiting professional autonomy; and structural misalignment preventing adequate services. Notes structural change unlikely without systemic reorganization, though commends ministerial efforts.
Education Philosophy & Reform


July27, 2018


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI

D-1

Dear Sir: 

Education leadership


It seems to me that education, especially public education, is in constant turmoil.; it’s been thus for many years; from my perspective, the gap between the education services  needed and education services provided gets wider and wider.  While the current government is not the cause of the gap, successive governments seem unable or unwilling to effect required changes.  Some roadblocks are considered structural. 

Mission

Most government departments adopt a role of “encouraging and supporting” their sector; not so for Education.  It is focused on running schools.  It’s the equivalent of delivering all health services through hospitals. It’s a narrow focus and deprives students of some important educational experiences    

Fear factor

Books have been written about the “Fear Factor” in education.  It’s found to some extent in any large organization but it is compounded when that organization is government.  I attribute it to politization arising from the large measure of government intervention and control.  Autonomy and accountability for helping Islanders learn should be the domain of the professional  and those who practice education should have the same level of freedom and accountability as those who practice medicine or law.  

Structure

An education system is an array of programs and services put in place by a community to encourage and support the education of its residents; given the complexities of serving so many people, designing and operating an education system is no easy task.  Unfortunately, the current structure does not reflect the services normally required.  It appears that the desire to run schools overwhelms the system and and drowns out a number of other functions.   


While I commend the Minister and Department for their efforts to improve public education, the likelihood of meeting their objective is slim until structural issues are resolved.


Sincerely, 

t


Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
