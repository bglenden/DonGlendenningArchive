An occasional paper published through Education 20/20 Inc. arguing that grade-based assessment in mathematics perpetuates myths about student learning and competency. Glendenning proposes an independent assessment system based on proficiency levels rather than age-based grades, citing models like apprenticeship certification and the Royal Canadian Conservatory of Music as working examples. The paper advocates for separating the teaching function from the ranking/grading function to improve educational outcomes.

Student Proficiency Levels


Occasional Paper D-1-C July 25, 2015


Education, like life, is a journey and like any journey needs recognizable checkpoints that inform us of progress towards our goals, however, the current system of school grade levels and diplomas have lost much of their value as a measure and report of what people know and are able to do when they complete their high school studies. There may be many reasons for the discrepancy but let me share a couple that I have encountered recently.

  • Grade nine math, or any other subject, is a myth – There are a number of ways to group students. Most schools group by age; that works fine for the purpose of counting but as a measure of educational growth it’s another story. One does not measure the steepness of a hill or the length of a walk by the age of the hike, nor can describe a breakfast by the age of the diner. Neither should one describe the level of a subject by the age of the person learning it. nor can describe a breakfast by the age of the diner. Standard practice suggests that each subject be analyzed, levels be identified or established and a student’s proficiency be assessed and reported accordingly. Teachers know that students in any grade are at a number of levels. The system of establishing subject levels simply recognizes legitimizes the reality of life.

  • Ranking and grading students are not teaching functions – a teacher’s role is to teach, to help students learn new skills and to increase levels of performance in existing skills. As part of the instructional process, teachers gather information from all sources and, based on the findings, design new strategies to help learners with their learning. That is a normal part of the teaching process. But in fact, teachers are also expected to assess students for the purpose of assigning levels of proficiency and rank. The latter, although frequent and acceptable functions, are not considered part of instruction; they represent a conflict of interest and, interfere with a teacher’s ability to teach.


Postsecondary institutions, employers, parents and the public at large deserve a better approach to measuring and reporting what they want or pay for, what students know and are able to do at any stage of their studies. This paper argues that that assessment function should:

• Be independent of people who teach

• Certify individual’s proficiency level

• Be open to individuals regardless of age or prior formal education

• Not a competition; establish a person’s level not rank

• Be confidential


The components of such a system should include:

  • A comprehensive analysis of the competencies of subject fields – our search for a comprehensive competency or outcome analysis of school subjects drew a blank. As a result, students are presented with a list of outcomes or competencies that adults think that students can learn. This, in our view, suggests limits to their learning. We would prefer an open analysis of a field with levels identified and options put in the hands of the learner rather than proscribed by a teacher.

  • A criterion referenced rating scale – Outcomes based Education requires criterion referenced rating scales. Some were found but our search did not turn up any in use on any of the provincial public school systems. It is urgent that we modernize the rating scale at the earliest possible date.

  • Recognizable levels of proficiency – people differ from each other in terms of proficiency based on two measures, the range of competencies and level of achievement on each.

  • Benchmark

  • An assessment model

  • An independent body to carry out individual assessments.



An independent model described above has a number of benefits including:

  • Let’s students know where they stand

  • Reduces chances of bias

  • Improves consistency

  • Reduces teacher workload

  • Supports independent learning

  • Increases opportunities for innovative learning and teaching

  • Well-described levels of achievement are an aid to learning


Examples of operating models that include at least some of the proposed features include:

  • The Apprenticeship Red Seal program allows qualified tradespeople to practice their trade anywhere in Canada

  • The Canadian Red Cross Swim Program allows sswimmers to work or continue training anywhere in Canada.

  • The Royal Canadian Conservatory of Music offers examinations in music that ARE Recognized across the country.

  • Competitive sports separate the function of coaching and referring.
