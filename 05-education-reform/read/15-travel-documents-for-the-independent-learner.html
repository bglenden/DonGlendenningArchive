Travel documents for the independent learner


We are a family of travelers.  As I write this, I am preparing for a trip to South East Asia.  I’m meeting my daughter in Bali and we will back-pack for a week or so on the Island of Java.  My son has just returned from a week of hiking alone in the mountains of Colorado. My wife will travel to New Mexico to visit our son and his family.  We start from different locations, have different interests and expectations, bring different backgrounds and use different modes of transportation.  My son was an independent travel3er for the entire eight days; the rest of us are involved in some group travel, some independent travel, and some independent travel but in a group setting.


In preparation for my trip, I have been pouring over maps, airline schedules, land transportation schedules, insurance coverage, visa requirements, currency regulations, travel guides, the Internet, etc.  most of the information so far I have gathered myself although I am now consulting my travel agent.  She has access to more, different and, in some cases but certainly not all, better information which she can use to help me plan and execute my journey.


I’m about ready now to make some binding commitments, lay down my hard earned cash and begin reading in detail about my anticipated new adventures.


While my travel objectives have never been to obtain recognition or certification, on occasion my travel experiences have been recognized.  Certificates have been awarded but not by my travel agent.  My passport certifies me to be a Canadian; visas have been require to enter some countries but increasingly this barrier is coming down; my health record, more of a transcript than a certificate, has never been shown.  I have certificates to show that I have crossed the equator, “kissed the cod”, spent more than 48 hours as a tourist in one province; and there are others.


Parallel in education


There is a parallel with education; it is often referred to as a journey.  And learners, parents and taxpayers certainly lay down great amounts of hard earned cash in support of our individual and collective educational journeys.


As learners, we start from different points, travel in a variety of ways, have differing interests and expectations, have differing amounts of time available for the journey, and have differing degrees of interest, energy and capability to travel independently.


The travel industry can handle both group and independent travelers; the education industry, in spite of the rhetoric, cannot.  In education, most of the travel literature is designed for the “travel” advisor.  The one exception is the availability of information about places in interest.


All learners, but especially independent learners, could benefit from more, better and different travel documents.  Here are the first ones I would prepare.  This is not a complete list; the possibilities are many.


An atlas which would provide learners with a quick visual overview of the learning world,


Curriculum maps to show learners the scope of the curriculum, how the curriculum is structured and how various subjects are related,


Learning models which show learners a variety of paths which they may follow in order to meet their educational objectives,


Learning management models which  professionals the various intervention points and the variety of roles required to support independent learners,


Learning guides to explain in some detail what is involved in reaching specific outcomes and how a learner can assess his level of achievement,


Learning plans or contracts between a teacher and a learner to assist both in the management of the learning activity.


Most, but not all, of these travel documents can be found in some of the more innovative schools.  Others would like to introduce them.  Many teachers would like to know how to prepare these travel documents.  Learners could profit from them.  In-service teacher education is the place to start.
