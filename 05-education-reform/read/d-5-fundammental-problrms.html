Glendenning summarizes core educational problems as structural and organizational rather than substantive. Drawing on insights from a former trustee, he identifies five fundamental issues: lack of clarity about purpose and function, absence of autonomy at operational levels, unclear accountability systems, insufficient freedom to act, and absence of normal market forces that drive quality improvement. He contends these problems cannot be resolved through adding resources—more professional development days, new courses, additional technology, changed graduation requirements, or higher achievement standards. These are matters government controls and should be capable of managing through better organizational practices. The letter synthesizes his systematic analysis, arguing that systemic redesign, not incremental resource allocation, is essential for educational improvement.


July 7, 2015
