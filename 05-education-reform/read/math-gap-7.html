This document outlines the Mathematics Gap Initiative, a steering committee effort led by Don Glendenning to address the low level of basic mathematical skills among Islanders. The committee identified the gap as real and having significant economic and social impact, arising from differing perceptions of educational institutions, silo mentalities, and politicization of education. The proposal seeks to establish an independent assessment service certifying proficiency in everyday mathematics skills with multiple performance levels, fee-based access, and employer consultation.
Education Philosophy & Reform


March 7, 2015

Mathematics Gap Initiative


Background


It is widely recognized that education is a key factor in determining one’s quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of our citizens will determine to a significant degree the future of our community and country. One of the most frequently mentioned concerns by Islanders is the low literacy levels among adults as reported via PISA results, recent CFPEI survey of Island Youth, presentation to the Standing Committee by the Charlottetown Area Chamber of Commerce, presentations to Government by both the Summerside and Charlottetown Chambers of Commerce, a 2014 survey by the PEI Home and School Federation and a series of workshops on Preparedness held throughout PEI by Education 20/20 Inc. The most frequently mentioned concern among Islanders, and especially among employers, is the low level of basic skills, especially mathematics, writing, communication and personal management skills. The Mathematics Gap initiative is a further step in our exploration of math skills needed to function in a modern economy.

Conclusions:


Briefly stated, the committee believes:

  1. The gap is real

  2. The result of the gap is a reduced quality of life for Islanders, reduced productivity for Island businesses, high than necessary unemployment rate, lower quality of PEI products, lower wages and poorer customer service.

  3. The gap is having an economic and social impact on both the Island economy and the future well-being of our young people

  4. The nature and extent of the gap has not been fully described

  5. The gap is unlikely to get dealt with by existing stakeholders





Causes of the gap appear to arise from the following:

  1. Differing perceptions of role of educational institutiona

  2. Silo mentalities among education providers and users

  3. Current institutional model of curriculum development, grading and reporting progress.

  4. Politicization of education


Proposal:

To explore the issue as it relates to basic numeracy skills with a view to:

  1. Defining the skill gap

  2. Finding or developing an assessment instrument that provides employers and institutions with information they need to know about math levels of applicants

  1. Test with job applicants and employers
