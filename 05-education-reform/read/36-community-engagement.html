BACKGROUND:

  • When families, schools, and communities work together, positive results happen: 1) Student achievement improves; 2) Teacher morale rises; 3) Communication increases among learners, teachers, parents and the community at large; 4) content becomes more relevant, and 5) feedback more meaningful.

  • Preliminary results from a series of community workshops organized throughout Prince Edward by Education 20/20 and the Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island revealed the lack of communication between schools and their communities. The workshops were focused on concerns of young people about gaps in their education. (Taking the Pulse of Island Youth, www.cfpei.ca)

  • An examination of a number of reports prepared for and by the PEI Department of Education and Early Childhood Development also reveals the communication gap between the Department and the public it serves.

  • Even positive steps toward school improvement criticized because of poor communication


PURPOSE:

The project proposes to improve the quality of education in Prince Edward Island through raising the level of engagement of communities with their schools


PROPOSAL:

To undertake a series of community based activities designed to provide parents with information and skills needed to initiate and maintain engagement activities with their local school

ACTIVITIES:

The following activities are planned:

  • Using our website as a focus, develop an information stream on items of particular interest to parents.

  • Organize sessions in communities for parents and other members of the public to increase their knowledge of issues of interest in education

  • Organize sessions for parents and other members of the public on best practices in initiating engagement techniques

  • Provide ongoing encouragement and advice to community groups involved in engagement practices.

It is widely recognized that the level and quality of education is key to our future as individuals, families, communities and countries. But this view is tempered with a concern that the education currently available is not preparing, nor is it likely to prepare, our people for the life that lies ahead. As an example, for at least a decade now The Conference Board of Canada has been urging greater emphasis on the fundamental skills of literacy and numeracy, the skills of self-management and the ability to work as a team member. And yet, literacy rates in PEI have not risen and we still find that 42 percent of our young adults lack the skills needed to function in a modern economy.


Some people confuse education and schooling; this is unfortunate and learners suffer as a result. Many schools continue to operate on a time based, subject centered, delivery model with little interaction between learner and teacher or school and community. The system must change but it is unlikely that it will make the desired changes without some outside help. We say this based on the view that the education enterprise lacks the usual market measures that ensure change. This need is becoming increasingly obvious. It is our view that, both in PEI and elsewhere, that shortfall is increasingly becoming recognized, brought on by a number of events including international competition, on-line-delivery of courses, student demand, large numbers of young people without sufficient basic skills, declining postsecondary enrolments, funding shortages, changing student demand and job skill requirements. To provide this kind of education will require a system that is flexible, transparent and , customized with clearly identified standards


Change is fuelled by Information, however, we know that having information itself doesn’t guarantee change – but information certainly supports it and must be accompanied by engagement of all concerned. Providing relevant information is the focus of this proposal.


In sectors other than education, there are community groups that play an important role in providing relevant information to the public. For example, think of the information that we receive day in and day out about cancer, diabetes, Alzheimers, the dangers of smoking as well as many other matters relating to our health services. Similar organizations do not exist in education. It is our view that information about matters of education, such as early detection of reading problems, helping with homework, standards or independent study would have a significant impact on the education of our young people.



  • Independent information: Most of our information about education comes from the people who operate the system; to address the shortage, we currently produce a monthly electronic newsletter; in addition, our website has also just gone live and we plan to use it as an information hub for surveys, announcements, occasional papers, social media, etc.


  • Engagement: When families, schools and communities work together, student achievement improves. For this reason, we are hosting a series of discussions throughout the Island focused on concerns of young people about gaps in their education. The first workshop was held in the Stratford Town Hall on January 20, 2014; attendance was good and discussion was lively; it also went on long after the meeting closed. The workshop is being repeated in Montague on February 18, Summerside on March 11 and Wellington on March 25; in addition, workshops are planned for Souris and O’Leary; and we are preparing for a second series, this one likely on educational quality.


  • Recognition and incentives: The education sector lack incentives – students cannot finish early; there is little encouragement to innovate, principals have little autonomy to make local adjustments; each school is a cookie cutter of all other schools, and parents have little choice in choosing a school and none in choosing a teacher. As a way to foster greater engagement, and thanks to a generous donor, Education 20/20 will soon announce an educational award for a school or learning centre that best demonstrates engagement in its everyday activities; other incentives will follow.

During the past year, our cities witnessed a number of occupations; the reasons may be obscure and open to interpretation but the unease between governors and the governed, between citizens and institutions, is evident. Some would say that the two are estranged.



During the past few months, the writer has engage many Islanders in discussions about education and here, also, exists a significant divide between providers and users of educational services, especially in the public school system. And, while I have not read widely on the subject, I find it to be of concern among governments in other countries as well. Even when decision makers seek the views of others affected by their decision, those consulted feel that they have been manipulated.



But people want more than simply being consulted, they want to engage in the making of the decision. The current term for this kind or level of participation is “public engagement”. It results in putting more decision-making powers directly into the hands of ordinary people – it is really grass roots democracy in action. The key to public engagement, I believe, is readily available unbiased information, community conversations and participation in policy development. Public engagement starts with the setting of the agenda, working from shared information, a transparent agenda, and care to insure inclusiveness, control and transparency



My recent reading uncovered the following:

  • State of Kentucky has made engagement a cornerstone of its educational change process.

  • Birmingham University in England has appointed a Professor of Public Engagement.

  • Wikipedia reports a couple of very large public engagement activities. One involved the gathering of some 5,000 people to discuss the future of Lower Manhattan. The other included thousands of people through a series of telephone interviews, workshops and town halls.



It experiment in public engagement is unique, however, it seems to me that any public engagement involves at least three components:

  • Information

  • Discussion

  • Policy making





Enhanced public participation in education in PEI could reap benefits as well.
