1-3-2-A

Education 20/20 Inc.

Basic Skills Project

Introduction:

In late 2013, The Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island (CFPEI) undertook a survey of over 200 Island youth to identify issues that directly affect them; the report, Taking the Pulse of Island Youth, available on the Foundation’s website at www.cfpei.ca, provides a snapshot of the findings. Bright spots include a positive sense of community, emerging entrepreneurial spirit, volunteerism and new postsecondary offerings. The main concerns include a lack of jobs, surviving on low-paying jobs, educational deficiencies and drug and alcohol addictions. On the subject of education and learning, the study reports a high rate of completion of high school and postsecondary education, a high level of inclusivity and relatively affordable tuition costs; young people also expressed concerns about their readiness for the next stage of life including:


“preparedness gaps with regard to readiness for

postsecondary study and the workplace” – Vital Signs, CFPEI, 2013


Following the publication of the CFPEI Report, Education 20/20 organized six public discussions for the purpose of informing the community of the report and its findings, learning more about youth issues in our communities, exploring issues of education and learning, and to identify actions to be taken to close gaps. Discussions were lively and wide ranging, and carried on well after the formal session ended. The final report included a recommendation that.



Further study be undertaken to clarify the gaps in preparation for further study and for work - (Preparedness, Education 20/20 Inc., Occasional Paper No. 1)

Education 20/20 has continued its exploration under the title, “basic skills”, meaning those skills required of any and all citizens, and considered foundational to all other learning. Basic skills are an issue because:

  • Content and levels are not consistently defined

  • Low basic skill levels drag down the economy of PEI

  • There is an added expense of re-teaching them at the postsecondary level.

  • The skill identification process appears to be repeated at each level of education

  • Responsibility for education is scattered across a number of agencies.

  • Basic skills do not appear to be a priority

  • A low level of basic skills may not only block entrance to postsecondary education but may also lower skill levels of people who graduate.



Education 20/20has has searched the literature, examined curriculum materials from a variety of institutions and Provinces and at various levels, reviewed achievement levels and assessment procedures for the OECD sponsored PISA assessment, Canada’s PCAP assessment, TOWES and others, seeking a foundation document, an analysis identifying competencies to be learned. One would think that, with all the curriculum development activities in Departments of Education across the country, a suitable foundation document would exist; based on our examination, it doesn’t. Provinces develop curriculum based on outcomes and, it is our considered view, that outcomes are too broad a level and do not lend themselves to common understanding and measurement lack readily identified assessment criteria ; the format of outcomes used by schools is at odds with the original as conceived by William Spady and later proponents of Outcomes Based Education. An outcome is a clear statement of expectations at the end of a learning experience; a competency is one form of outcome but one that focuses more directly on what happens to learners. By chance, we discovered a competency based analysis of Practical Mathematics prepared some time ago for Holland College and the PEI Department of Education; we propose to convene a committee of citizens to update this analysis rather than start from Scratch.


As a point of departure, we further narrowed our view to mathematics in the belief that it carries less cultural baggage, was a key field of student education for the information age and is of interest to people from a variety of institutions.

Proposal:

Undertake a comprehensive analysis of competencies in the field of mathematics used or needed by citizens. We are calling this document a ”curriculum map” that will serve as a master list of competencies for different users. Teachers may use the list to identify what is to be learned, students to track what has been learned, and curriculum developers as an analysis of the field, employers to identify the expectations of a new employee, and credentialing and qualifications bodies as a basis for their work. The analysis process will be based on DACUM (Developing A CUrriculuM), originally developed as part of a Nova Scotia NewStart project on Yarmouth, Nova Scotia but pioneered by Holland College in Charlottetown


Finally, and absolutely critical to our project, is a rating scale based on externally referenced criteria; we are very familiar with this component and confident in our ability to design this element of the project.


On completion, the map will be made available to any interested party including:

  • Employers as a master list of competencies from which to draw their requirements’

  • Tutors of mathematics as a guide to what is needed and a record of what has been learned.

  • Students as both an academic map and tracking tool

  • Certification bodies for whom mathematics is felt to be an important component

  • S common document on which to base communications about the subject of basic mathematics content.


Steps:

  1. Establish Steering Committee

  2. Gather background information

  3. Complete analysis

  4. Develop math rating scale - criterion referenced and reflective of Bloom’s taxonomy

  5. Identify profile(s)/levels

  6. Develop and test an assessment process

  7. Prepare final report


Timelines: The project will commence as soon
