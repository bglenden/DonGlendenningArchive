Letter dated May 21, 2018, analyzing PISA (Programme for International Student Assessment) results and OECD recommendations. Don notes Canada and PEI performed well on 2015 PISA tests measuring 15-year-olds' essential knowledge and skills. However, he highlights OECD's 2011 finding that schools with greater autonomy over curriculum and assessment outperform others. References Quebec's success attributed to combined autonomy and accountability (Montreal Economic Institute) and Japan's approach (98% autonomy on evaluation policies, 89% on course content). Concludes PEI principals and teachers have minimal autonomy, calling it a "puzzler" why the province doesn't implement these proven strategies.

May 21, 2018


Editor,

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir: 

It’s a puzzler


I’ve just reread the 2015 PISA results. Canada did pretty well; so did PEI.  PISA is the administered to 15 year- old’s to measure the extent to which they have mastered the knowledge and skills that are considered essential in a modern world.  Obviously, we would like to and could do better – In fact, the OECD itself tells us how to do better.  To quote a friend, why we don’t follow OECD recommendations is a” puzzler”.  


OECD did a study of PISA results in 13 countries; in 2011 OECD reported that students from schools with greater autonomy about what is taught and how it is assessed perform better than others.  Quebec seems to do exceptionally well on such tests; the Montreal Economic Institute (MEI) attributes success in Quebec to a combination of autonomy and accountability of teachers and principals.  The MEI article also points out that in Japan, a top-ranking country in PISA rankings, principals and teacher are able to choose evaluation policies in 98 percent of cases and course content in 89 percent of cases.  The issue is not one of Provincial governments changing standards but rather of giving schools flexibility in how they reach them.


On a number of occasions, I’ve enquired as to the level autonomy and accountability of PEI teachers and principals; from both what was said and what was avoided, it appears curriculum comes from the Department with little flexibility for change at the school level.  The Principals’ Council, at least in its first report, implies principals have little decision-making authority.


Implementing policies of autonomy and accountability at the school level sounds like a winner.  Why we don’t do so, like my friend says, is a puzzler.

 

Sincerely,



Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
