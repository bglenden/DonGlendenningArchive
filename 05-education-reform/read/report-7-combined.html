THE CITIZEN MATH PROJECT

Math - the ability to understand and work with numbers -Unknown


In late 2013, The Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island (CFPEI) undertook a survey of over 200 Island youth to identify issues that directly affected them; the report, Taking the Pulse of Island Youth, available on the Foundation’s website at www.cfpei.ca, provides a snapshot of the findings. Bright spots include a positive sense of community, emerging entrepreneurial spirit, volunteerism and new postsecondary offerings. The main concerns include a lack of jobs, surviving on low-paying jobs, educational deficiencies and drug and alcohol addictions. On the subject of education and learning, the study reports a high rate of completion of high school and postsecondary education, a high level of inclusivity and relatively affordable tuition costs; young people also expressed concerns about their readiness for the next stage of life including:


“preparedness gaps with regard to readiness for

postsecondary study and the workplace” – Vital Signs, CFPEI, 2013


Following the publication of the CFPEI Report, Education 20/20 organized six public discussions for the purpose of informing the community about the report and its findings, learning more about youth issues in our communities, exploring issues of education and learning, and to identify actions to be taken to close gaps. Discussions were lively and wide ranging and carried on well after formal sessions ended. Education 20/20 retained an interest in the subject and continued its exploration through informal discussion with educators, employers, students and the community at large.In the course of discussions about education, mathematics was often mentioned as a topic of concern


As part of our exploration of math services on PEI, Education 2020 Inc. gathered information, convened discussions with representatives of the Department of Education, Holland College, UPEI, employers, reviewed curriculum materials from a variety of sources and sought the views of many citizens. It also included an unreported informal survey of a dozen friends using a PEI math outlie for project integration


MATHEMATICS EDUCATION

Mathematics is considered essential in every day life and, for the most part, is organized around the conventional structure of Arithmetic, Algebra, Geometry, Trigonometry, Calculus, Probability and Statistics. One of the main goals for students of PEI Grade 9 mathematics is to “become mathematically literate adults”. The PEI math program is based on some 382 Specific Curriculum Outcomes (SCO). Outcomes are developed by the Northwest Education Protocol operating in Western Canada and coordinated by the Alberta Department of Education; their analyses and curriculum materials are used by a number of Provinces including the four in Atlantic Canada. Citizens in all walks of life participated in the identification process, however, it appears to be survey of skills thought to be important rather than a survey of skills actually used in every day life






Numeracy is also mathematics, the application of math, and is receiving increased attention in education circles; Numeracy is described as “having the confidence and skill to use numbers and mathematical approaches in all aspects of life.” The National Centre for the Study of Adult Learning and Literacy did a major study and of numeracy skills and identified four major context components – Family and Personal, Workplace. Community and Further Learning. A Report by the PEI Department of Education on the Early Numeracy Intervention Program (ENIP) found that numeracy “gives students the tools and the skills to solve problems logically, and to developmental flexibility, thought, self-efficacy, and the ability to persevere” According to the Conference Board of Canada, “Overall, Canada earns a “C” grade on inadequate numeracy skills." With regard to instruction, educators are faced with a dilemma; teach mathematics, teach numeracy or teach both?


The National Numeracy Organization (UK) proposes:

  1. Every teacher become a teacher of numeracy. That does not mean that every teacher becomes a math teacher but that numeracy, just like literacy- is recognized as an intrinsic part of every subject across the school curriculum.

  2. A new measure of numeracy proficiency should be introduced for all young people at the age of 14. This would establish a national benchmark for the level of numeracy needed for further study in all subjects between the ages of 14-19

  3. An additional and universally respected qualification in numeracy (or core math) is needed alongside GGCSE math. Politicians and curriculum and qualifications regulators must give serious and urgent consideration and introduction of dual GCSSEs (General Certificate pf Secondary Education) in math and numeracy



It is our conclusion that citizen concerns about mathematics can best be addressed through the teaching of numeracy with instruction bases on math skills used in every day life.


CITIZENS MATH STUDY

It was our view that the math curriculum for public school students should begin with a list of skills used in their every day lives; our search for such a list was not successful. Given the above, Education 2020 Inc. secured funding from an Island donor (who wishes to remain anonymous) to identify math competencies used by Island citizens as part of their every day life. Steps included:


  1. Identifying Competencies: Education 2020 Inc. convened a panel of citizens, ten in number, and, over a three-day period, (Appendix 1) and with the aid of a facilitator trained in DACUM facilitation process, identified a list of math skills practised on a regular basis by Island citizens. The session started with a discussion of citizen roles and was quickly followed by brainstorming mathematics skills involved in their doing. As each task was identified, it was written on a 5 by 8-inch card and posted on a blank wall. Some 84 math competencies emerged from the process. Education 2020 chose this approach because well known and widely used, reflects the terminology of the work role, measurable, and uses the language of the community. (Appendix 2)


  1. Develop Rating scale: Given that the analysis will likely be used as a basis for instruction, Education 2020. a separate panel of citizens (including educators), was convened (Appendix 1) to Identify criteria for rating performance; those criteria were then used o create a dynamic rating scale reflecting the rating of competencies in real life situations. (Appendix 3)

  2. A Reformat brought together the skills and rating scale into a traditional DACUM format for use by learners, (Appendix 4)

  3. Plot Employment profile: With competencies in hand, Education 2020 convened employer representatives who were intimately involved with hiring and/or supervising new employees, especially new employees taking up employment for the first time without benefit of technical training. Panellist were encouraged to focus on skills actually performed rather than skills they wanted new hires to have. Panellist were frequently asked” Do you perform this task and how often?” “When is you last time you performed this task? A number of sessions had to be postponed due to work schedules and weather; The employer profile actually emerged from two sessions, one in Charlottetown and one in Alberton. A list of Panellists for both sessions are included as (Appendix 5)

  4. Comparison

Cosolved Report: an educational consulting firm, was asked to compare the math competencies identified by citizens with curriculum outcomes used in the school system with a view to identifying gaps, if any. In summary Cosolved reported that:


“There are 84 skills identified by the Citizen Math panels in the areas of Estimate and Calculate Whole Numbers; Measurement, Fractions Decimals, Percentages, Rations and Rates; Use Spatial Reasoning; Recognize and use Patterns and Relationships; and Financial Literacy. Of those 84 skills, 56 were covered to some degree by the PEI Mathematics Curriculum.

In the Prince Edward Island Math Curriculum, there are 382 specific curriculum outcomes (SCO) covered from Kindergarten to Grade 12. These are organized into the areas of Number Sequence; Patterns and Relations Sequence; Shape and Space Sequence; and Statistics and Probability Sequence for Grades Kindergarten to Grade 9. The specific curriculum outcomes for Grades 10 to 12 are organized into the categories of Measurement; Geometry; Number; Algebra; Relations and Functions; Statistics; Logical Reasoning; Mathematics Research Project; Trigonometry; Probability; Financial Mathematics; Permutations, Combinations and the Binomial Theorem; and Calculus. 120 of the SCOs are matched to Citizen Math Skills. 262 of the SCOs are not covered in the list of Citizen Math Skills.” Appendix 5 and 6

CONCLUSIONS

In summary, it is our view that math education is important and would benefit from:

  1. Clear statements of educational philosophy, goals and principles

  2. Adoption of competencies as basis for program and curriculum development

  3. Development and adoption of a comprehensive set of competencies to serve the math interest of all grade levels

  4. A math advisory committee drawn from the community served

  5. Greater communication and teamwork among math professionals at all levels

  6. Adoption of a dynamic community based rating scale applicable to secondary school and above

  7. Reporting student progress on a competency by competency basis.

  8. Instruction based on skills practised in sector or community served

  9. Transition would benefit from a detailed description of each competency

  10. High school studies include a credit course in numeracy.


.

BENEFITS

Implementing the above would:

  1. Improve support for independent learning

  2. Improve communication among stakeholders

  3. Increase relevance

  4. Increases engagement of learners

  5. Improves consistency across the system
