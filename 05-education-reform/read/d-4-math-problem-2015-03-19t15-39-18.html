Education

It is widely recognized that education is a key factor in determining one’s quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of our citizens determines to a significant degree the future of our community and country. Our education system developed over decades and served us well, however, education now finds itself in a changed and rapidly changing world, and seems unable to adjust.


Education system

Education in Canada is a provincial responsibility and, for the most part and in spite of protestations to the contrary, is either run directly or closely controlled by Provincial Departments of Education. Over the years, education has become school centered, politicized, bureaucratic, focused on the delivery of knowledge, with control moving fro teachers in the classroom to bureaucrats in their offices.


Issues:

There is a gap between what Islanders have and what they want in the way of education, and Islanders want meaningful engagement in closing the gap. Evidence for this view arises from a number of public reports such as PISA results, CFPEI survey, Charlottetown Area chamber, Home and School, Education 20/20 workshops, PEILA statistics – views, interestingly, that have neither been debated nor refuted. While schools cannot be expected to teach all students everything, it seems reasonable to expect schools to ensure that each graduates be able to demonstrate the range of skills required to cope with everyday living hence the gap referred to in the opening sentence.


Skills of everyday living operate under different names and each has its own shading; recently they were described as citizenships skills, however, basic skills, essential skills or even employability skills are commonly used. This paper focuses on mathematics although some consider it too grand a term to for this skill level and refer to it as arithmetic or numeracy instead. The purpose of the paper is to lay out some views, ideas and even alternatives to be taken into account in our search for ways to improve the basic math skills of Islanders


While schools cannot be expected to teach all students everything, it seems reasonable to expect schools to ensue that its students have a range of skills at a level to function in a modern economy


PEI Commitment

According to the PEI Philosophy of Education, the purpose of “the public education system is to provide for the development of children so that each may take a meaningful place in society”. On the same website, the PEI Curriculum Guide for Grade 9 mathematics includes a goal for students to “become mathematically literate adults, using mathematics to contribute to society.” Obviously the intention is for high school graduates to have the skills needed to participate

in a modern economy but, apparently many do not and it is the latter group that sparks this paper.


My own search of the literature suggests that the differences in the level of performance between two people in math is based on a combination of two factors:

  1. Range and nature of skills of each person, and

  2. Levels of performance on skills learned


School Variables

We know that individual learners carry many variables into the learning situation such as “personal conditions, previous experiences and perceived futures”; these make teaching difficult. But schools also have a range of variables to bring to bear in the teaching learning situation including:

  • Allocation of time

  • Nature of help to supply

  • Amount of help to supply

  • Quality of help to supply

  • Clearly stated assessment criteria

  • How progress is reported

  • Quality of diagnosis and plan for corrective action

  • Clearly identified levels of achievement

  • Amount and quality of information

  • Progress reports

  • Clear expectations

  • Availability of quality learning resources

  • Referral to additional help

  • Assignment of priority of resources allocation

We are of the view that:

  • The education system is unlikely Educational achievement can and should be measured in terms other than time spent on a subject or in school.

  • Credentials are no longer an adequate instrument to describe depth or breadth of study in a subject or field.

  • The practice of having teachers act as both coach and referee of their own students is unfair to student

System as now structured is unlikely to produce students with the skills needed to function in a modern economy

  • Math is studied at all grade levels, and in both College and UPEI; It would be advantageous to students, therefore, if all institutions and accreditation agencies operated from the same master list of competencies.

Proposal


Establish a qualification authority, a separate authority for the purpose certifying individual levels of achievement. The role of teaching is designed to help others learn; when a teachers rank their students they are putting their ability to teach at risk. This includes the matter of assigning student grades, selecting students for prizes and awarding diplomas and degrees. We propose a separate qualification authority for the purpose certifying individual levels of achievement. Examples of a proper relationship between the two functions is that of apprenticeship and music.






Why study Math

John Paulos, mathematician, believes that we should study math because it is:

  • “Useful:

    • Mathematical problems abound in daily life

    • Mathematical proficiency is required for many jobs

    • Mathematics is essential for science, engineering, and research

  • Important:

    • A mathematically informed citizenry will make better economic and political decisions about risk, policy, and resource allocation

  • Interesting:

    • "Mathematics, rightly viewed, possesses not only truth, but supreme beauty" (Bertrand Russell) and should be studied in its own right

    • The landmark accomplishments of mathematics stand alongside the masterworks of art and music as cultural triumphs that all educated persons should be able to appreciate

    • Doing mathematics teaches patterns of problem-solving and insight that transfer to other knowledge domains

    • Mathematical proof teaches skills in rigor, argumentation and persuasion that transfer to other knowledge domains”

Conclusions

Education 20/20 .Inc. proposes a concerted effort to improve math skills for Islanders of all ages by identifying inherent stages in the” math mountain”; four-step process to include:


  • Comprehensive analysis of math competencies

  • Development of rating scale or scales that can be applied to each competency

  • Establishing identifiable levels of performance (combination of competencies and levels); ideally, the school system should organize stage based student instruction)

  • Establish a testing service to assess achievement in mathematics for any Islander who needs or wants it.
