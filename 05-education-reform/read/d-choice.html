Don argues that choice and autonomy are critical to well-being, yet school systems offer minimal choice compared to other services (doctors, lawyers, stores). He advocates for choice of both schools and individual teachers, noting that finding teachers suited to students' needs is as important as finding appropriate medical or legal professionals.

April 12, 2016


Editor

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Editor:

Choice as an agent of change

Paradox of choice tells us attinomy and freedom of choice are critical io our well being

It is interesting to note that all all organizations that provide services to the public, our school system, Our family moved to Prince Edward lsland in 1969. lt was a new community to us so we had to find a

new place to live, new places to shop, new doctors, new plumbers and other servicemen, new baby

sitters, etc.


Each time we needed a service, we asked our neighbors, colleagues at work, or new found friends for

recommendations. The system worked and soon we settled into the community with a family doctor, a

lawyer, a dentist, and an appliance serviceman to name but a few.


As for shopping, we soon learned which stores carried which line of products; once at the store, we also

soon found which sales clerks had the best product knowledge and which stores provided the best

service. Many readers will have had the same experience when they moved to P.E.l.


The exception to our decisions, of course, was that of schools and teachers. Our decision as to home

location was made, at least in part, on the basis of the school which our children would attend. Once a

school was chosen, we had no choice of individual teachers. (And P.E.l. is no different from other

provinces in this respect.)


I realize that there has been a change in this regard and today parents do have choices as to the school

which their children will attend. This is a good start and should be viewed as a normal operating

practice.


It should also be possible to select teachers. On moving to a new community, it should be possible to ask

around and to identify teachers for our children. lt is not so much a good or poor teachers, or doctors

for Thai matter, as it is of finding ones that suit your needs or the needs of your children at that time.

Should-l decide on a teacher who is three miles away rather than next door, the required transportation

is my problem.


And it should be possible to change teachers; we should be able to do it as easily as we can change any

other service provider. Students and their parents are consumers of the services which schools supply.

When the consumer wants to change, for whatever reason, it should be possible to do so; not only

possible, but relatively easy. I understand the limits to choice of any service in a small community but

choice of teachers is a concept foreign to the public school system. lt should not and need not be so.


We have had the same family doctor, lawyer and other service providers for the last two or three

decades. We know them and they know us. lt helps to ensure that the service we need is the service we

get. The school system does not allow" the same relationship between students and teachers; and our

students suffer as a result.


A change of teachers may make a positive impact on a student's learning; it should be possible for any

number of reasons; it shouldn't be a big deal. But we have made it that. But it is the student, not the

school, who should decide that a change would be beneficial for the learner.


To allow choice to occur, a changed staffing pattern will likely be required. Each learner will likely

need a teacher/adviser who fills that role for a number of years - perhaps even for the entire L2 years of

public education. An adviser will work with a child, and likely all children of a family. Sometimes the

adviser will be able to provide the required instruction; in other cases, the child will be referred to

another teacher for help. This is not unlike the present medical model.


lnjecting choice into the system would, I believe, have a salutary effect on all stakeholders. Parents and

students would feel that they have a major say in decisions about the education of their child. Choice

would, I believe, also improve accountability and recognize excellence in teaching. And more

responsibility for the education of individual children would be placed with the family where it belongs.


Many readers will react by saying that this cannot be done, that the scheduling and transportation

problems would be horrendous. I am convinced that considerable choice is possible. The first question

we must ask is: do we want it?
