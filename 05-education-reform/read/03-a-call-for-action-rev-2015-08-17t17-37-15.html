A Call for Action

Occasional paper No. 3 April 9, 2015



Preparation for life’s roles is called education and the better prepared we are the better we are able to cope with all that we encounter, or create. Educating is a complex matter made so by the fact that each person is unique in many ways, unique in basic physical, mental, emotional make-up; unique in the range and nature of previous experience, unique in perceived futures, but also unique in terms of learning pace, style and needs. Education is often described as any experience that has a formative effect on the way we think, feel or act; it may also be thought of as what we can do with what we have learned.


It is widely recognized that education is a key factor in determining each citizen’s quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of our citizens will determine to a significant degree the quality of the communities in which we live. In recognition of its importance, governments provide massive support for public education. The investment is justified for two reasons, one to ensure a high level of personal growth and development for every citizen; the other is to ensure the common good.


Based on media reports over the past few months, one can conclude that there is a gap between what Islanders have and what they want in the way of education. Literacy levels alone have come to the fore through PISA results, recent CFPEI survey of Island youth, presentation to the Standing Committee by the Charlottetown Area Chamber of Commerce, views expressed by the Summerside Chamber of Commerce, a 2014 survey by the PEI Home and School Federation and a series of workshops on Preparedness held throughout PEI by Education 20/20 Inc.


Indicators of the gap include: the limited engagement between educators and the community they serve; the lack of a philosophy of education that encompasses all our citizens; government’s focus on the young and schooling; service gaps such as the availability of part time instruction; politicization of decision making, and limits on professional autonomy and accountability. Citizens see most of the issues as organizational, created by government decision-makers and, therefore, capable of being changed by those same government decision-makers.


In recent years, Prince Edward Island has instituted a number of initiatives to improve education including new and upgraded facilities, additional investment in technology, the East Prince trade and technology centre, embedded professional development (PD) days, but much more needs to be done. It is our view that the impact of these changes has been blunted by an outdated delivery organization and, in support of this view, we recommend that PEI:


  1. Develop a “clear and compelling” vision of education for all Islanders. Although much has been written about education, a comprehensive vision statement does not currently exist for PEI. We believe that having such a statement is required, especially for a service as large and complex as education, however, we are not suggesting another study but rather a document prepared by a small working with opportunity for citizen input.


  1. Develop a learner centered service model that provides support in all domains to learners as, when and where needed. Doing so would involve student centered outcomes, criterion referenced assessment, flexible scheduling, challenge for credit arrangements, and greater autonomy and accountability for teachers.


  1. Assume a leadership role in creating a learning culture and providing a quality education for every Islander. Government should focus on matters of planning, setting priorities, funding, monitoring, and quality control, ensuring the availability of professional resources, home support for preschool children, information and continuing education.


  1. Delegate the role of providing instruction. The actual provision of instruction should be expanded beyond schools, colleges, universities to include community agencies, professionals in private practice and learners themselves.


  1. Establish a qualification body for the purpose certifying individual levels of achievement to persons wanting or needing such documentation. The role of teachers is to help others learn; when teachers rank their students they are putting their ability to teach at risk. Assigning student grades, selecting students for prizes and awards and awarding diplomas and degrees is a sorting process and should not be part of a teacher’s role. Apprenticeship with certification through a Provincial Department of Labour and music through the Royal Canadian Music Conservatory are examples of the recommended arrangement.


  1. Establish a professional model of service delivery where professionals are directly accountable to clients for the nature, extent and quality of service provided. Such a model must include a direct and close professional-client relationship, the potential for a family education adviser for those who want it, and professional autonomy and accountability for all.


  1. Put in place a strategy to encourage and support innovation, such a strategy to include freedom to experiment, an innovation fund and an external decision-making committee.
