A substantial working paper comparing school systems with education systems and arguing PEI operates the former, not the latter. Don establishes that education's future impact depends on citizen education quality, that education is an umbrella concept encompassing all impact-bearing experiences, and that early home/family experiences plus teacher influence are key. He notes education is constitutionally provincial responsibility but provinces should "promote, support and regulate" services—yet services are rarely identified separately or promoted, remaining embedded in schools/colleges/universities for enrolled students, creating a fragmented system. The paper includes British Columbia's education development goals (critical thinking, creativity, independence, skill, productivity, cooperation, civic awareness), defines education systems through multiple sources (Business Roundtable standards, components, Georgia Vision Project elements), and catalogs comprehensive services. Don identifies accountability types (compliance, professional norms, results), observes PEI lacks clear role descriptions for teachers/principals and performance reports, and recommends readings (OECD Education 2030, PISA, World Bank Education Crisis 2019, CMEC PCAP 2019). This paper represents Don's most comprehensive systematic analysis.

Dear Editor:

An Education System

______________________________________________________________________________________

Our future as individuals and as a community will be determined, in large measure, by the nature and level of education demonstrated by our citizens. Education is an umbrella concept and includes all experiences that have had an impact on the way we think, feel and act wherever those experiences occur and however they are triggered. The key to a person’s education, the literature tells us, are early experiences triggered in and through the home and family, followed by the influence of teachers


Education in Canada is a provincial responsibility; the role of a Province is to promote, support and regulate services; Although education services are not separately identified or promoted, they appear to be mainly delivered through schools, colleges and universities and limited to students who are enrolled in the various institutions. thus, giving rise to a fragmented system. As a result, the writer argues, PEI is serviced by a school system rather than an education system. This paper reports on the writer’s exploration of the subject.



Government (BC) is responsible for ensuring that all of our youth have the opportunity to obtain high

quality schooling that will assist in the development of an educated society. To this end, schools

in the province assist in the development of citizens who are:

  • thoughtful, able to learn and to think critically, and who can communicate information from a broad knowledge base;

  • creative, flexible, self-motivated and who have a positive self image;

  • capable of making independent decisions;

  • skilled and who can contribute to society generally, including the world of work;

  • productive, who gain satisfaction through achievement and who strive for physical well being;

  • cooperative, principled and respectful of others regardless of differences;

  • aware of the rights and prepared to exercise the responsibilities of an individual within the family, the community, Canada, and the world.

  • The Business Round table - components of an education system: Standards, Assessment, Accountabilities, Professional development, School autonomy, Parent involvement, Learning readiness, Technology and Safety and discipline
