This letter diagnoses fundamental cultural misalignments between school practices and real-world functioning. Don identifies six structural problems: task completion timing (schools interrupt; work requires task completion), self-direction (schools restrict autonomy; society needs self-starters), service mentality (schools lack accountability; society demands it), collegial organization (schools remain hierarchical), accountability obscurity (both students and teachers lack clear responsibility), and compensation based on credentials rather than performance. Don contends these are cultural, not curricular issues requiring systemic redesign rather than course additions.

May 4, 2011


Editor

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Editor:

The Culture of schools


Schools help young people prepare to function in a variety of life roles, that of an individual, a

family member, a learner, producer, consumer and citizen. To the extent possible, therefore,

schools should not only teach relevant subjects but also model the society into which students

are being graduated.


For example:

At work, one finishes a task and then moves to another; in school students move to another task after a set time whether or not the first task is finished,

 Students begin work when the teacher enters the room and finish when the teacher leaves the room; society needs people who are self-directed

Schools lack a service mentality; they have a guaranteed clientele regardless of the quantity and quality of their work,

Schools operate as old-time industrial organizations even though the work structure has become collegial.

The system obscures accountability of both students and teachers

Teacher pay is based on education and experience; it should be based on job size and performance.


l'm glad to say that one hears, but not very often, of exceptions to the above practices, but l'd

like to see those exceptions become the rule.


These are cultural issues; they cannot be resolved by simply adding new courses. lnstead, we

need to change the way schools operate. Not only will these changes make life more satisfying

for teachers and students, but it will better reflect the community into which our students.


Sincerely




Don Glendenning
