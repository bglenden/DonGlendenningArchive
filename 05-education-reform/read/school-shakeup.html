Using the same struggling mathematics student case, Don criticizes how schools handle academic difficulty through structural barriers rather than academic support. When students move to lower-level courses, schools create detours that reduce post-secondary options, delay advancement, decrease employment prospects, and potentially damage lifetime earning potential and personal development. Don emphasizes that education's purpose is to help students develop their fullest capacities, and that alternative options—extra instruction, specialized support, remediation—better serve this goal than institutional detours that effectively limit futures.

October 20, 2014

Editor, Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

Structural change



The role of a school is to open doors, to create opportunities, for young people; but not all schools do that.    Door closera often come up in conversations but for some reason, this time  the impact struck home. Granted, I do not have all the facts but here’s my understanding.


A high school student, with hopes, even plans to go to college or university, was having difficulty with math; that’s not unusual; I am told that struggling with math is not uncommon.   So what are a school’s options?

  • Let the a student struggle and likely fail a grade, 
  • Suggest an upgrading course after graduation
  • Provide extra instruction
  • Transfer the student a lower level course
  • Recommend private tutoring


I’ve heard many good stories about tutoring but it is out of reach for many parents; if a student could benefit from tutoring and the school cannot provide it, the cost should be covered by the education budget; why should a parent be expected to pay both taxes and the cost of tuition, especially for something as basic as math?  In this case the school chose option 4; the student is now in a lower-level math class. In effect, the school created a detour, no a barrier, in his path to education.  At best, this option delays entrance to postsecondary studies, reduces the likelihood of that student going to college or university, it reduces the chance of a smooth transition to employment and  likely  to a significant reduction in his lifetime income; at worst, it may divert him from being the citizen he is capable of being, the citizen we want him to be.


Education documents tell us education, and presumably schooling, is intended to help students to develop to their fullest capacities.  The option chosen for this student fails to fulfil that goal. Personally, I am more concerned by cases of this kind than by low PISA and PCAP scores.


Sincerely


Don Glendenning

A long time student of education
