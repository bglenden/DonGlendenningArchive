A new look at grading


This column overlaps with the last newsletter, however, it reflects some new learning during the past few days, more specifically, learning about assessment and evaluation


My last column dealt with formative and summative assessment. Since then my search on “grades and grading” turned up an item by Robert Paul Wolff (Wolff: A discourse on Grading, 1969). In it he identifies three elements or kinds of grading:

- Criticism which includes analysis and

feedback about current learning; criticism is

at the heart of learning and involves the give

and take between teacher and student about

what happened or should happen.


- Evaluation to establish a person’s level in a

subject and is, or should be, criterion

referenced, similar to levels in music

or swimming.

- ranking to see how one stacks up against

others; as for ranking, schools could take a

lesson from sports with both coaches and

referees. Ranking results from competitions;

students should be encouraged to

participate and teachers and students can

use the results but teachers should play no

part in the ranking process.


I find this interesting concept as I reflect on the education process.

Obama hosts teachers

The Washington Post of July 10 reported on a lunch meeting at the Whitehouse organized by the President for four teachers; the teachers are reported to have told the President:

1- There’s nothing wrong with the kids

2- Responsibility and delight can coexist

3- It’s not about good and bad teachers, it’s

about good and bad teaching

4- If we want students to innovate,

collaborate and solve real world problems,

we need to make it possible for teachers to

do the same things.”


Teachers’ Workchoice Day


The New Zealand Herald, of August 15, 2014 reports a pilot project designed to reduce youth unemployment. As a PD day, teachers in Christ Church and Auckland will spend the day visiting the types of work places where their students are likely to work after they finish school.
