Student Engagement


Background

Student engagement is considered a positive indicator of successful schools and considered important enough to be incorporated into a schools instructional strategy. While an actual definition is elusive, engagement is usually described in terms of a feeling of belonging, their depth of interaction with school work, a willingness to participate in school activities, a positive attitude and even a “stick-to itiveness”. Engagement seems to march hand-in-hand with achievement. It seems to flourish where there is a strong disciplinary climate, good teacher student relations and high expectations. Improved student engagement is a frequent outcome of school reform that includes school autonomy, parental involvement, strong core program, curriculum reform ND authentic assessment.


Benefits

The literature reports that, when families, schools, and communities work together, positive results happen:

  • Student achievement improves;

  • Teacher morale rises;

  • Communication increases;

  • Content becomes more relevant

  • Feedback more meaningful.

  • Viewpoints broaden

  • Community is better informed

  • New ideas are more likely to be generated and adopted

  • Helps gain public understanding and trust

  • Contribute to student health, wellbeing and quality of life

  • Students are more likely to continue their formal education and find satisfying employment


There are a number of ways to describe the engagement framework; we’ve chosen first, a student’s perspective and secondly, that of the school:

  • Students and peers:

The development of relationships based on respect, caring, courtesy, tact, honour, cooperation and collaboration alongan opportunity to work in small groups, large groups and the freedom to pursue their own interests.


  • Student and teachers:

An environment; one that is safe and comfortable physically and emotionally and based on the assumptions of respect, trust, care, optimism and intentionality’ Teacher encourages critical inquiry, writing, research, collaborative learning and other skills that develop students' intellectual and practical skills. Views student as central to Teacher ensures he/she is on leading edge of current teaching practices and technology so as to better prepare students' for 21st century learning.



  • Student and instructional process:

Teacher helps students engage actively in learning and make meaning of the information for application in the real world. Some examples would include collaborative projects, i.e. learning to work and solve real problems in groups by sharpening their own understanding through listening seriously to insights of others especially those from different backgrounds and cultures. Students could also be encouraged to record examples of their best work through a portfolio or video and discuss their learning with other classmates and teachers.

  • Student to school:

. encourages integration of learning across courses and invites students to discuss “big questions” that matter beyond the classroom.


  • School to Parent:

Study after study show the dramatic impact parental involvement makes to a child's success in school. In fact, engaging families in their children’s education improves school achievement, attendance, behaviour and graduation rates.


  • School Community Partnerships:

Engagement of schools in the wider community also brings benefit. A UNICEF report in 2000 points out the importance of engagement both for the relevance of the curriculum in meeting both individual and community needs and also the relevance to national goals as well. This involves direct experience with concepts and issues they are studying in the curriculum. The opportunity to learn, a analyze information, and be a participant in community setting also reinforces the idea of good citizenship, i.e. giving back to the community. Other examples of student/community partnership may include fundraising for various community projects and involvement in diverse community service


Measures/criteria

  • Level of communications with community, parents, students

  • Frequency of school events

  • Community based student activities

  • Parent involvement in curriculum development

  • Parent involvement in planning

  • Staff participation in community activities

  • School attendance
