Newsletter article (Open Education No. 25) by Don Glendenning examining the decline of high school diplomas as hiring criteria and the rise of third-party testing and skills assessments. He argues that schools should abandon ranking functions (which create conflicts of interest), scrap graduation requirements, and instead provide transcripts of learning with the option to help fill gaps later.

Alternative to High school diplomas


While I have note done an actual survey, it seems to me that high school completion has slipped from view as a requirement in most help wanted advertisements. In fact the same applies to degree with some large employers in the US. At the same time, there appears to be a growth in hird party testing. In fact, I am aware of workers heading west carrying with them, not their high school diploma, but test results from the Holland College Assessment Centre. I’ve even suggested that local employers consider a standardized test as part of their hiring process.


This development raises a number of issues for our school system:


  • Do diplomas have a future? Diplomas are credentials; they attest to what a person studied rather than what a person knows and can do. Obviously they carry some weight and prestige, and are a marketing tool for the institution.

  • I often ask employers why the y continue to ask for diplomas and degrees when they find them to be of so little predictive value. I’m told that they serve as a screening device, od a way of reducing the number of applications to be examined.When employers, but lack the specific information that employers want.

  • It is becoming increasingly clear that the public school system that relating Increasingly I am of the view that places one goes to get help with learning. Asking institutions and teachers to sort or rank their students constitutes a conflict of interest. Teachers may use the results of ranking but are in conflict when they participate in the ranking process.

  • There is no evidence of which I am aware that supports one pattern of courses over another as the best preparation for postsecondary education or for life. With social promotion, it doesn’t make any differerence what one studies.

Simply put. We could scrap most high school graduation requirements, encourage students to learn a variety of things, provide a transcript of what they learned and, if they find out they have not learned enough of the right things, invite them back and help fill the gaps.
