This concise letter proposes reconsidering how we measure educational attainment. Glendenning makes an analogy: we don't measure health by hospital stay duration, wealth by years worked, or spirituality by hours in church—so why measure education by time spent in school? This aphoristic approach challenges the fundamental metric of educational success: enrollment duration rather than learning outcomes. The brevity and conceptual elegance of this argument demonstrate Glendenning's ability to distill complex organizational problems into memorable principles. The question suggests education should be measured by demonstrated competence and learning achievement rather than institutional attendance.


July 7, 2015


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir: 

Measuring education

We don’t measure our health by how long we spent in the hospital, our wealth by  the number of years worked, or our spirituality by hours spent in church, why then do we measure our level of education by the time we spent in school.




Sincerely,

Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
