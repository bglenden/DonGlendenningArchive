Using vehicle alignment as metaphor, Don identifies misalignments requiring attention in school systems: between school practice and research, outcomes and life roles, assessment and real-world standards, reporting and stated outcomes, timetables and individual learning speeds, and benchmarks and community standards. He advises parents concerned about student progress to review course outcomes with their child before parent-teacher meetings, then bring these outcomes to discussions. Don notes that course outcomes are sometimes provided during course orientation and are available on the Department of Education website, positioning knowledge of outcomes as essential to productive parent-school conversations.


January 6, 2015


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

School alignment   


It’s not unusual at this time of year to have our cars checked for alignment; we may not realize it, but school systems also struggle with alignment issues.  While educators usually think only of curriculum alignment, we need to also check alignment of:

School practice and research

Outcomes with life roles

Assessment criteria with those in life outside school

School reporting with specified outcomes

Timetables and individual learning speeds 

 Benchmarks and community standards

priorities


Improving alignment may require changing priorities, rewriting resource material, adding community members to committees, making surveys, If you have concerns about your child’s progress, I suggest that start with the course outcomes, review them first with your child then take them to the parent teacher meeting.  Doing so will help focus the discussion.  In fact, sometimes copies of relevant outcomes are given to each student as part of the orientation process at the beginning of each new course.  If not, they are readily available on the website of the Department of Education

Sincerely,


Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
