Don responds to federal-to-provincial transfer of workforce training funding and responsibility. While supporting provincial control in principle, he expresses concerns about politicization, reduced worker mobility, and potential loss of federal support continuity. He advocates for employer and employee groups to lead public discussion on priorities and procedures, emphasizing that training funds must reach actual workforce development rather than administrative purposes. The piece reflects concern that provincial-level decision-making might prioritize local political interests over merit-based worker preparation.

Dear Editor:

Labour Market Training


Recently the federal government has agreed to the transfer of "labour market training" to provincial

governments. The change involves both the transfer of responsibility and dollars. This amounts, I

believe, to several million dollars. The change also involves the transfer of staff from HRDC to the

Province. Some of the money comes from Ul premiums - money contributed by both employers and

workers. On the surface, it makes sense that a Province have control of trainer of adults for and in the

labour force, however, I have some concerns. These concerns arise, at least in part, from the fact that

there has been little public discussion of this matter on PEl.


While talk of the technological age frequently turns to the need for more science and engineering

graduates, little is heard of the crucial role which will be played by our skilled workforce. That is why

the use of these funds is so important to our future. Even now we read that a neighbouring province is

having difficulty finding technically trained people. The transfer of responsibility to a province, by itsell

makes the training of skilled workers neither better nor worse. The transfer, however, provides an

opportunity to review the activity and consider changes which will serve us even better.


The Government of Canada has been supporting adult training for more than B decades; when federal

dollars dried up so did the training. By and large Provinces, especially the smaller ones, spent few

provincial dollars on adult training. Provincial dollars were allocated to universities, post-grade 72

programs in community colleges, and the public school system. With all its faults, federaL funding did

ensure that workforce preparation received both attention and support. lt is important that those

assuming the new responsibility give the matter at least equal attention.


We must ensure that the dollars transferred for training will be spent on the training of adults either

entering or now in the workforce. Funds could well end up paying for pavement.


The loss of federal direction may well result in decreased worker mobility as individual provinces train

only for local needs. And this at a time when mobility of a trained labour force is essential. I believe

that lslanders should receive training which will allow them to compete fcr work anywhere in Canada

or even internationally, we must take also that worthy candidates, especially those not drawing

benefits do not fall through the cracks.


Decision making by local counsellors may be even more susceptible to pressures to give priority to

supporters of the party in power rather than the most deserving adults. A provincial public servant,

regardless of how professional, is vulnerable to pressures from a Local MLA in the decision-rnaking

process. Federal public servants are much further removed from politicians than are provincial ones.


It seems to me that this whole matter needs a great deal of public discussion as to appropriate policies,

procedures, priorities, etc. Since the money is intended to prepare people for the workforce, for the

workforce, it is logical that the public discussion be led by employer and employee groups. I think that

they, plus individual lslanders, have the most to gain - or the most to lose.


Sincerely


Don Glendenning
