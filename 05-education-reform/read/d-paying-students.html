From September 18, 2010, Don proposes a thought experiment: if school is "work," should students be paid? He questions the basis for payment (attendance vs. achievement) and proposes tying compensation to accomplishments—credits earned, courses completed, diplomas awarded. He further suggests eliminating attendance requirements entirely, allowing students to challenge any course via examination with a fee, and awarding credit upon success with potential refund as reward. This radical proposal reflects his challenge-for-credit advocacy.
Education Philosophy & Reform

September 18, 2010


Editor 

Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

Paying Students

 

We pay people to work and it is often said that going to school is a student’s work.  But if we pay, what should be expected of the student in return?


Should we pay simply for showing up, for participating in school activities, for attending a set number of hours, for following school rules and not being disruptive?  At what age or grade level would the practice begin?  Would the pay rate be the same for all?  Under what circumstances would a student be docked pay?


Given our fixation on grades, courses, credits, diplomas and degrees, another option would be to pay for accomplishments, to assign a value to each year successfully completed, each credit earned and diploma or degree awarded,   This approach would certainly be much easier to administer.

 

To go a step further, why not consider allowing students the option of challenging any and all courses for credit.  Drop all attendance requirements, establish criteria for each credit, levy an examination fee and award credit to successful candidates.  To reduce administration, we could enclose a cheque along with the notification of a successful completion of each course.  Perhaps, on the other hand, a refund the examination fee would be sufficient reward.



Sincerely


Don Glendenning
