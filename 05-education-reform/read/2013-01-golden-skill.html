Addressing widespread concern about reading proficiency, Don cites alarming statistics: PEI 15-year-olds ranking below other provinces in reading/writing, 30% of Canadian Grade 6 students unable to read adequately, 30% of PEI adults lacking functional literacy. He notes interventions show promise and calls for sustained public engagement. Rather than treating reading as a discrete subject, Don suggests establishing independent reading clinics operating outside schools. He emphasizes public engagement as key to change, urging conversations with principals, school trustees, MLAs, and community members.

January 8, 2013


Editor, Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

The Golden Skill


I’ve been reflecting on what I’ve heard and learned about education during the past few months and without doubt, reading tops the list of concerns.  Reading, “the golden skill”, is key to our individual and collective futures.  Statistics, at least the ones in my file, are not very comforting.  Having said that, however, I must say that current interventions in our school system seem to be producing results – although I do not yet have the supporting numbers.


As evidence of public concern, here are some media comments:

The Guardian, Sept 28, 2006:  International tests place 15 year-olds in PEI lower than other Provinces in reading and writing.

Globe and Mail Sept 15, 2009: “Children who aren’t reading well by the end of Grade 3 are at risk of dropping out or failing to graduate

Globe and Mail Sept 15, 2009: estimates that by the end of Grade 6, at least 30 percent of Canadian students can’t read or write well enough to succeed in school

PEI Literacy Alliance - The reading skills of 30% of PEI adults are so limited that they cannot deal with most printed  material.

 Brink, Literacy in PEI: “If we keep doing what we do now, the number of people (in Canada) with low literacy will increase at the rate of about 100,000 a year.”


Readers interested in the numbers should view a report by Satya Brink, Literacy in PEI: Implications of Findings from IALSS, 2003; it is available on-line.


I write this letter at this time because I am not sure that decision makers in education realize how concerned Islanders, including educators, are about the issue; having heard their concerns, I feel a responsibility to pass them on.  I know that reading is not a school subject, not like biology or not even like physical education, but that may be part of the problem. Or maybe reading  would be better handled through reading clinics, operating outside schools, like dental or eye clinics.  


A key to change is public engagement, asking questions and making your views known. Don’t wait for someone else to start the conversation.  Talk with the principal of your school, then talk to your friends and neighbours, ask the WI to put it on the agenda for their next meeting, talk to a school board member, your MLA, and anyone else who will listen – and do it now and often.   And after the conversation, send me a note at  HYPERLINK "mailto:standards2020@gmail.com" standards2020@gmail.com 

Sincerely


Don Glendenning
