According to Robert Paul Wolff (A Discourse on Grading, 1969) there are three elements or kinds of student grading:

  • Criticism, which includes analyses and feedback, about current learning; criticism is at the heart of learning and involves give and take between teacher and learner about what happened or should happen.

  • Evaluation, which should be criterion referenced, to establish a person’s level in a subject or field

  • Ranking to see how one stacks up against others of the same age, number of years of study, in other countries, etc.

Issues

  1. Establishing levels of difficulty

Levels of difficulty or achievement in a subject arises from an examination of the subject. In fact, one of the reasons Spadey proposed OBE was to make it easier for learners to travel at their own pace. It is a long time since I read xxx by William Spadey but my recollection is that organizing outcomes, ant the curriculum arising therefrom, cannot nor should not, be linked to the age of the learner.


  1. Reporting achievementLinking achievementlevels to ages of learners

The logic of using the same terminology to label both administrative and grading purposes escapes me; I’m sure that it must puzzle students and parents as well. The two should be separate. Have 12 levels of s subject if you must, although that seems excessive, but refer to them as levels not grades; only use Grade for administrative groupings – “My daughter is taking Level 9 Math”.


  1. Combining sorting with teaching

The role of a teacher is to teach, to help students learn new skills and to increase levels of performance in existing skills. As part of the instructional process, teachers gather information from all sources and, based on the findings, design new strategies to help learners with their learning. That is a normal part of the teaching process

The above has led the writer to seek an alternative approach to measuring and reporting what students know and are able to do at any stage of their studies. This paper argues that that assessment function should:

• Be independent of people who teach

• Certify individual’s proficiency level

• Be open to individuals regardless of age or prior formal education

• Not a competition; establish a person’s level not rank

• Be confidential


Proposals:

  • Standards be set for key citizenship skills – our search for a comprehensive competency or outcome analysis of school subjects drew a blank. As a result, students are presented with a list of outcomes or competencies that adults think that students can learn. This, in our view, suggests limits to their learning. We would prefer an open analysis of a field with levels identified and options put in the hands of the learner rather than proscribed by a teacher.

  • A criterion referenced rating scale – Outcomes based Education requires criterion referenced rating scales. Some were found but our search did not turn up any in use on any of the provincial public school systems. It is urgent that we modernize the rating scale at the earliest possible date.

  • Recognizable levels of proficiency – people differ from each other in terms of proficiency based on two measures, the range of competencies and level of achievement on each.

  • Benchmark

  • An independent body to carry out assessments of key skills.



An independent model described above has a number of benefits including:

  • Let’s students know where they stand

  • Reduces chances of bias

  • Improves consistency

  • Reduces teacher workload

  • Supports independent learning

  • Increases opportunities for innovative learning and teaching

  • Well-described levels of achievement are an aid to learning


Examples of operating models that include at least some of the proposed features include:

  • The Apprenticeship Red Seal program where apprentices take instruction in institutions, gain experience in real work situations, levels of proficiency are certified by a provincial agency and are free to practice their trade anywhere in Canada.

  • Canadian Language Benchmarks, which has benchmarked a number of proficiency levels in both official languages and tests and certifies proficiency levels of new Canadians

  • The Canadian Red Cross Swim Program allows swimmers to work or continue training anywhere in Canada.

  • The Royal Canadian Conservatory of Music offers examinations in music that are recognized across the country.

  • Competitive sports separate the function of coaching and referring.
