Glendenning reviews the newly created Learning Partners Advisory Council's terms of reference with qualified enthusiasm. While partnerships can sometimes obscure fuzzy thinking, he sees potential merit. However, he identifies structural concerns. When organizations nominate representatives rather than individuals being selected for their personal expertise, representatives must advocate organizational positions and report back for instructions rather than thinking creatively beyond institutional boundaries. Half the council's members represent organizations, potentially limiting "outside the box" thinking. He questions whether such structures encourage participants to think independently about systemic problems or whether they default to protecting organizational interests. The letter raises fundamental questions about how advisory councils can genuinely function as collaborative problem-solving forums versus becoming extensions of existing bureaucratic structures.
