Newsletter (August 20, 2020) proposing revival of WWII-era J-Courses (Job Instruction Training, Job Relations Training, Job Methods Training) updated for 2020 (including Job Technology Training). Don describes these brief, sharply-focused skill courses created during Canada's agrarian-to-manufacturing transition, which provided workers with business-card-sized job reference cards. He draws parallels to modern challenges: construction trade shortages, employer complaints about graduates' work skills, and employment readiness gaps. Don sees these mini-credentials as models for modern community college offerings and cost-reduction for workforce entry. This demonstrates his appreciation for historical solutions to contemporary challenges.

What knowledge is of most worth? – Spencer 1860


J-COURSES SHOULD BE

BROUGHT BACK

WW2 saw Canada transition from an agrarian to a manufacturing nation; training of all kinds and in great measure was required for people enterin or already in the workforce and uniform. One of the innovations was the introduction of J-Courses – short courses focused on doing the Job.

  • Job Instruction Training (JIT) taught front line supervisors how to instruct new workers in their jobs.


  • Job Relations Training (JRT) dealt with relationship issues in the workplace


  • Job Methods Training (JMT) dealt with helping supervisors deal with managing the work process.


Presumably J-Courses in 2020 would include JTech.


Courses were sharply focused on skills needed by people thrust into a whole new work situation. Courses were a week or two in length and included essential skills of the job. workers who completed a J-Course were given a card, about the size of a business card, which carried the main points for their review. if needed. Somewhere among my souvenirs I have an original J-card but haven’t seen it for years.


I was reminded of J-Courses when I read of the need for basic training for new entrants into the labour force today – the hundred of people needed in the construction trades, comments by an employer about inadequate work skills of summer employees, and the general lack of

employment readiness in graduates. Although very basic, in some ways they may be likened to the mini-credentials offered by community colleges.


Remnants of the J-Coure movement lingered well into the 1960’s and were promoted in both Ontario and Quebec through their Industrial Training Societies. My personal experience with J-courses came into play when teaching new instructors in our trade and technical institutes - fore-runners of our community colleges. J-Courses may also help reduce costs to people entering the workforce for the first time.
