Presentation to

Standing Committee on Education and Innovation

Framework for decision-making


Education 20/20 is a national, not-for-profit education organization, located in Charlottetown, PEI. We are governed by a volunteer Board of Directors, comprised of people who have demonstrated an interest in and knowledge of education, but are not representative of an educational constituency. Our mission is to improve the quality of education for every Canadian. Our activities to date have included newsletters, workshops, Facebook, a website and an award to an Island school for demonstrating a high level of activities in its day to day operation..


First a few words about education. We see education as one of the most important issues of the day. An issue that confronts parents, students, teachers, teaching and learning experts and everyday Canadians concerned with the challenges we encounter within our system and beyond. Education is the force that shapes who we are as individuals; shapes our communities and our country. It is our view that with a quality education, many of our other problems would be reduced and some even eliminated


To start our presentation, we’d like to make a few comments about education itself. Education is a complex matter and it is important that we all start from a common base; or perhaps more accurately, that you know where we are coming from. The brochure we handed out provides some working definitions; they may not be the ones you see in text books or dictionaries but we consider them working definitions for every day use.


In a nutshell,

  • education is any experience that has a formative effect on the way we feel, think and act;

  • Its purpose is to help each of us gain the attitudes, skills, knowledge needed help us cope with, enjoy and contribute to the situations we find ourselves in for the rest of our lives.

  • Education starts in the home with family being the main source of instruction, help and influence

  • Our community invests heavily in schools for two purposes, to help individual Islanders develop their full potential and to ensure that our community has the skills needed to grow and prosper

  • When we learn, we change, and those changes may include changing the way we think, feel and act

  • The role of a teacher is to help us change from what we are to what we want to be – to gain new information, gain new skills or change the way we feel


So education and schooling are not the same, one becomes educated whether or not one attends school, education is more than the acquisition of knowledge, and being educated is a process of changing behaviour.


But the world outside schools is changing. While core citizenship skills are as important as ever, the emphasis on the transfer of knowledge has been upstaged by information technology; the core of current school activities is available unfiltered at our fingertips, while the number of skilled workers in our plants continues to decline those in the field continue to grow, the importance of the early years and the right start is now recognized as never before, the global workplace demands people with interpersonal and cultural sensitivities as never before. As we speak, events in the Middle East give an urgency to developing the skills of citizenship.


There is a widespread call for change although perhaps not a clarion call – at least not yet. It demands a return to education not just a revitalization of schools. At the broadest level, we need to rethink education, its components, its services, and how best to make them available to every citizen. Obviously, schools will continue to be needed but education tomorrow will likely be community not school based. What do we want schools to do? How do we engage and harness other community agencies in the education process. But we also need to rethink about who does what and for whom.


The community is calling for change; so far, organized education appears not to have heard the call. Or if it has heard, is ignoring the call, or decided not to engage the community in the change process. The question is where should change start. We believe that the place to start is with a set of broad principles. If we get them right, individual decisions are easier to make and helps reduce inconsistencies.


Change can begin anywhere; the kind of change required, however, demands a review and restatement of our guiding principles. We’ve found a number of overarching statements about education; the one below draws heavily on a couple of OECD and UNICEF reports. They are not a perfest set of criteria but provide a good jumping off point for further discussion.



A quality education system is one where:

  • Learners who are healthy, well-nourished and ready to participate

  • Environments that are safe and respectful

  • Content that is relevant to functioning in a modern society

  • Outcomes are linked to personal and community objectives

  • Support is focused on learners and learning.

  • Information and advice are both timely and independent.




If we were to make a specific recommendation to this committee it would be that you engage the community in a direct but simple exercise of reviewing and restating a set of beliefs about education to guide us until our 200 th birthday followed by a similar activity on which we base our schools.


We thank you for the invitation, we wish you well in your deliberations, we offer our services to help in your examination, and we’d be pleased to continue the discussion or respond to questions.

In Newsletter No. 10, we reported on characteristics of a quality education published in a 2000 UNICEF report. We’ve since reviewed some OECD and UNESCO document and have expanded the list. A quality education system is one Teachers are professional, autonomous and accountable



To put them in perspective, these characteristics would apply to a province, any province; the extent to which any or all would apply to a school district or to a school will depend upon how a province organizes its education support services..



The strength of these characteristics, over others I have seen, is their focus on learners and learning. Havin
