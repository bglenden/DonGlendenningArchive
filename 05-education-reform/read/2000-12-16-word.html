Please allow me an opportunity to present the concept of challenging for school credits.


As part of a degree requirement, I was required to have a reading knowledge of two languages. I

could meet the requirement through enrolling in language courses or taking a language exam. If

I was successful on the exam, I would be exempted from taking the courses. Language programs

often (usually ?) use tests to determine a placement level in the program; why can't the same

approach be used to award credit ?


I have often wondered, furthernore, why the same approach cannot, or should not, be applied tb

other university courses. For that matter, why not let people challenge any course for credit

whether that course be in school, college or university ?


If one examines school, college or university calendars, one finds that each course is quite clearly

described. In addition, I am sure that teachers have even more detailed course descriptions which

could be provided to a learner. Why, then, shouldn't learners be given the detailed course

description and allowed to study on their own, if they wish ? They could also have the option of

either taking the course or writing an exlm. If they pass the exam, why not give them credit ?

Students could use the time saved to take advanced studies or courses in other areas.


I have taken courses and am reasonably proficient in carpentry although, I must admit, I am a bit

rusty in some of the skills. In addition to the skills, I believe I have a reasonably good grasp of

the relevant background information. Certainly, I would need to bone some of the skills and

information especially in relation to to new construction materials. I could do some, if not all of

the required study on my own. I am sure that there are many other Islanders in similar positions.


My formal qualifications do not include an arts degree although I have listened attentively, read

widely, discussed vigorously and written occasionally on some of the subjects included in such a

program. Should I, and many like me, be allowed to challenge for credits leading to a Bachelor of

Arts ?

Dear Sir,


In recent days, I have been part of several discussions about school closings. The most interesting

was a brief conversation with a stranger while waiting in line at a store. It turns out that he was a

teacher who found his way to a store at 8.00 am but was upset because school opening was only

delayed instead of school being cancelled. He was not looking forward to the day; the students

would not be in a good mood; they also wanted/expected schools to be cancelled.


School cancellation is based on a number of assumptions. One is that schooling and education are

the same; they're not. Schooling is an important part of education but it is only a part. A second

assumption is that groups learn; they don't. Only individuals learn although they may learn in a

group setting. In fact, some things can best be learned with others; but certainly not all. Finally,

we have assigned schools the responsibility of, not only what takes in place in school, but also

with transporting learners from their homes to a school of our choice.


I would like to comment on three elements of the school- closing issue. First is the matter of the

school buildings and physical resources within them. A schedule of school and resource

availability should be established and maintained. Yes, there will be instances when a building

may be closed because of emergency situations but only in genuine emergencies. A snowfall

and/or high winds is not sufficient reason, to my way of thinking, to close a school building unless

it is felt that the structure is likely to collapse causing danger to the people inside.


A separate decision needs to be made about which resources within a school should be open to

students when numbers are small or teachers are absent. Most study areas including

library/resource centres, classrooms, and computer labs could be opened to most students most of

the time; some study areas may have to be restricted.


With schools generally open, teachers will have to decide whether conditions allow them to reach

their place of work. It is an individual decision. Teachers living nearby, should have no problem;

those living at a distance from the school may miss some days. When teachers cannot keep to a

schedule, they should let their clients, the students, know. When a teacher accepts a teaching

post, there is a contract to provide a service and that contract should be honoured at all

reasonable cost. A staffing policy may be required to deal with teacher absences but closing a

school so that a teacher gets paid should not be an option.


The Government provides opportunity, encouragement, teachers and other resources to assist

students to obtain a quality education but only learners can learn. It is a responsibility of learners,

or their parents, to ensure that learners take advantage of the resources. Teachers certainly play a

role in motivating students" Students make individual decisions about if what, when, how and

how much they learn; even when we are teaching, learners may be learning something quite

different from what is intended"


As for transportation, there are at last two separate points of decision-making. People providing

the bus service, or the operators themselves, should decide whether or not it is safe to drive. If

conditions are unsafe, buses should stay off the road and students along the route should be

informed of the change of plans. Even when buses are operating, parents are the ones to decide

each day whether or not their child gets on the bus. If bus transportation is not available, a parent

may arrange alternative transportation if they know the school is open.


Frequently I am asked if the above policies will not result in some students being ahead or behind

others and if the gap will widen. This implies that students in the same grade are at the same

level of achievement and move forward in a lock-step fashion. This is far from the case. In any

class, some students are ahead and others behind the average - whatever average means. The

system copes with students who lose a day due to illness, why can't it also cope with a student

who loses a day due to bad weather? Granted the number of absentees may be larger on a storm

day, but the problem remains essentially the same.


Closing schools on stormy days is administratively convenient but I believe is disruptive to

students and learning. The decision to attend or not attend on any given day belongs to the

student and/or parents. I am quite satisfied that teachers are both willing and able to deal with

school absences in a less disruptive fashion. .


Sincerely


Don Glendenning
