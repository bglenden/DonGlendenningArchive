Reframing the small schools debate, Glendenning argues the question should not be whether to close small schools but whether they can deliver quality education cost-effectively. He suggests better questions are "Can we design cost-effective models through small schools?" and "What system changes would make small schools work?" He contends that current system inflexibility may be responsible for small school difficulties rather than size itself, and that educational rather than administrative-convenience decisions should guide closure determinations. This represents his consistent strategy of reframing debates toward system redesign.

Dear Editor:


Small Schools


l'm glad to see that the "small schools" issue is still alive. I believe in small schools, however, it

is unlikely that they can be sustained given the way the system is now organized. The fault may

lie with the system rather than with the small school. New thinking could result in different

conclusions. "should small schools be closed?" is not the right question - at least not yet.


Let's rephrase the question to ask "Can we design a cost effective model that will deliver a

quality education through small schools? Or perhaps an ever better question is "What changes

in the system are required to make small schools work?" The outcome of the discussion may be

the same but the decision would be an "educational" one rather than one based on

administrativd convenience and costs.
