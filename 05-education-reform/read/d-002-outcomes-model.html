BACKGROUND


Education is a lifelong process involving experiences that have a formative effect on the way we feel, think and act; this effect is usually expressed in terms of a person’s attitudes, skills and knowledge. Major contributors to one’s education include one’s self, one’s home and extended family, schools and the community at large. Schools play a unique role in the provision of education in that they are the only provider of educational services allowed to award credentials, the folded paper, so esteemed by our society.

It is widely recognized that education is a key factor in determining one’s quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of our citizens will determine to a significant degree the future of our community and country. Because of this, we are among the world leaders in our investment in ensuring that our citizens, especially our youth, have access to a variety of educational activities and experiences. Our education system developed over decades and served us well, however, there are significant changes occurring around us that demand some different responses; our education system is unlikely to make the desired changes by itself. But there is a gap between what Islanders have and what they want in the way of education. Not only is there a gap, but Islanders want meaningful engagement in bringing about the change. There is no panacea in sight and there is not likely to be one, however, change must be immediate, significant and continuous, but planned. Most comment is focused on changes to the school system, however, the issue is broader than that, it must encompass not only schools, but the broader education including the role of the home and community; any review must also include a variety of issues including expectations, the need for collaboration and engagement, credentials and standards, personal freedom and accountability.

ISSUES

Issues are topics, problems or concerns of importance to groups of people; they are the big questions. Issues are not simple problems for which definitive answers can easily be found. On the contrary, they are complex and of significance. Once understood, the simpler questions can be dealt with and progress resumed. It’s not always easy to describe an issue in precise terms; sometimes we confuse issues with symptoms and incidents. An issue may be current, that is a problem of today; emerging and potential issues, on the other hand, focus on those threats and opportunities that are like to impact on life tomorrow. But the field is messy. Issues arise in a variety of ways but often become of concern when they impact on personal interests, activities or events, or are related in some way to concerns of friends or family. When trying to sort through issues, it is often useful to begin one’s analysis with the basic questions of who, what, when, where and why. It may also be useful to browse or skim broadly in the field, such as browsing the internet, to get a feel for the issues. In the case of education, while the organization is mine

There are many ways to frame and describe issues, for the purpose of this paper, I’ve decided to focus on the following six:



Curriculum

Most educators would agree that curriculum development should commence with clearly defined outcomes. Starting with outcomes changes the focus of education from content to learners and organizes teaching and learning around outcomes and measures change based on performance regardless of when the learning took place. Outcomes may be written with “traditional, transitional, or transformational goals in mind”. It appears that the leadership in OBE dealt with broad principles only; as a result there are no recognized standards for identifying out comes or implementing OBE. As a result, there are many versions of OBE, most focusing on outcomes only.

Qualifications

The acquiring of an education and the certification of qualifications are two separate functions although the two are closely linked and frequently one is thought to be the other. The fact that the delivery of both functions is, to a large degree, provided or driven by educational institutions often works to the individual’s disadvantage in terms of both education and certification. While separate certification bodies exist, they appear to place undue emphasis on results of study and too little emphasis on what has been learned outside the formal education system.



Most would agree that education is broad, lifelong and all encompassing, yet in practice we implement a more limited vew including:

  • Inflating the role of schooling and education by using the terms interchangeably,

  • Failing to support students in their search for qualifications.

  • Ignoring the need for independent advice to learners and their parent

  • Narrowing the curriculum focus

  • Schoolification of early childhood development

  • Limited support for Independent and life long learning

  • Bias towards postsecondary education at the expense of employability skills and skpll training
