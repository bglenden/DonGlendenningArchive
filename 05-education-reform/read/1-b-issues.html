Second draft editor letter providing Don's analytical framework for education. Distinguishes learning as individual behavioral change, teaching as helping facilitate change, and education as the cumulative result of all formative experiences. Argues government's role should be "encouraging and supporting" rather than direct operation; notes state must ensure skilled workforce for community sustainability. Dear Editor:

Draft 2

Issues in Education


For some time now I have engaged with Islanders in conversations about education; topics included learners, learning, learning paths, continuing education, math, politicization, transition to employment, professional autonomy, accountability, and the list goes on. My conversations reveal a desire for information and dialogue, not just about school matters, but also about education and what we can do to make it better


At its simplest, learning refers to engaging in an activity intended to change the way one thinks, feels or acts; teaching, on the other hand, is a process helping others change the way they think, feel or act. One’s education, on the other hand,  is the result of all the experiences that have altered the ways that a person thinks, feels or acts.   Of course, we also consider education as a sector of the economy.  Having an education or being educated may be thought of as a person’s stage of development; a person with a good education is better able to deal with circumstances that confront us throughout their lives than are people with a limited education.  


With regard to the education process, most would agree, I think, that the State has an important role in education that of  “encouraging and supporting citizens in their quest for education”; the State also regulates the education sector.  I believe, however, that the state has another interest and that is to ensure that it has the skilled people to make our community a place residents  will continue to want to call home.


Education is a large and complex service; the literature suggests that education services include governance, instruction, information, counselling, funding, learning products, quality assurance, and human resources. yet the mission statement describes its role as “developing and delivering  high quality programs and curriculum.”  


 I  find ithe Government description of role to be a narrow role, indeed and wonder:  

  • Shouldn’t all Islanders be encouraged to further their education?
  • Doesn’t Government have a regulatory role?
  • Who monitors  quality and equity?
  • that PEI should be encouraging and supporting young people increase or improve their level.
  • Shouldn’t the Department encourage people over age 18 to continue their education

  

I believe that it is time for a conversation  about education on PEI – not a study but an open exchange as to the nature and kind of services that will best serve our interests into the next centuryneeded and wanteby Islander in the n










  • Switch from education as a service to education as a product; from helping Islanders learn attitudes, skills, knowledge and values to one of delivering education products – lessons, courses, programs, diplomas and degrees. 
  • Relevance: desire for content reflects life roles of the 21st. century rather than traditional fields of study.
  • Autonomy: education is a helping profession; practitioners need freedom to deliver services of a kind, in a mix and at a time that best suit needs of clients.
  • Accountability: practitioners should be accountable for helping learners reach an objective and not just  for delivering a  service.
  • Politicization: embedding education services in a political system increases the fear factor and interferes with the system’s ability to deliver services to clients.
  • Confusing getting an education with going to school


Islanders would appreciate and education would benefit from a public conversation about what education is trying to do – and why.


Sincerely,



Don Glendenning
