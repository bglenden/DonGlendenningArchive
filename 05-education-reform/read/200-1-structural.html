Newsletter No. 95 (August 20, 2020) analyzing structural impediments to PEI education quality. Don defines structural issues as policies, procedures, culture, teaching approaches, and physical dimensions interfering with quality. He identifies seven key impediments: political filtering through government departments, narrowed vision compared to 50 years prior, lack of professional autonomy (both principals and teachers "non-existent"), inadequate accountability focused on school operations rather than skill development outcomes, "fear factor" discouraging open discourse, product-oriented (credentials-focused) rather than service-oriented approaches, and undervalued vocational/technical education. Don cites OECD PISA research and proposes "clear, compelling" vision as starting point. This synthesizes his systemic critique.

Open Education

No. 95 August 20, 2020

When schools and ministries are managed well, learning happens-World Bank


STRUCTURAL IMPEDIMENTS

A few years ago, I chaired a half dozen public sessions across PEI about the education concerns of youth. Sometimes, discussions continued long after sessions closed. After one session, a side discussion between two trustees turned to “structural impediments” to better education. A recent reading of a World Bank report on education, although not using the term “structural”, reminded me of trustee comments and prompted this letter.


By structural issues, I refer to “policies, procedures, culture, teachers, and physical dimensions” of the educational framework that interfere with educational quality.


Examples:

  • Political filter: Provincial Governments have a leadership role in encouraging, supporting and regulating education. But embedding the delivery process in a government depa rtment seem to simply add to the bureaucracy. Our health system, I suggest, has been able to support professional service with lesser government interference than is the case for education.

  • Vision: compared to my arrival on PEI fifty years ago, PEI education documents indicate a narrowing view of education; in fact it seems that the Department now views going to school and getting an education as one and the same. Not so says the World Bank, a large funder of public education throughout the developing world.

  • Professional Autonomy: the OECD (who gives us PISA tests) tell us that students in schools where the school has autonomy for a school’s day to day operation, do better on PISA tests than olther students. Informal evidence suggest that neither principals nor teachers in PEI have much freedom even in professional matters.

  • Accountability: is a “duty to report (give account of) on events, tasks and experiences. The education task task is to help Islanders gain skills, knowledge and attitudes of value to their and our futures. Reporting, however. Is mostly about the operation of schools.

  • Culture: Although not widely mentioned, “the fear factor” is alive and well on PEI. In fact it has It’s come up in discussions in recent days. To paraphrase one administrator “anyone who plan to live and teach on PEI will not speak openly about desiring change” . Some time ago, one small businessowner declined an invitation to participate in an education activity because he “gets small contracts with the Province.”

  • Product or service: Dictionaries that I consult consider education to be a service to people who want/need to learn. My sense of what’s happening, however, is that instead, education has become a product. As a result, much of focus is on years, days hours of schooling, credits, courses, diplomas and degrees. Employers use degrees and diplomas as a screening device. Credentialling may be likened to a huge Board game. In the word of one writer, employment does not require degrees but employers do.

  • Work ready education for employment – public educators have not been active supporters of technical and vocational education; leadership in education for work rested with the government of Canada. Unfortunately, the federal government has vacated, or been squeezed out of its leadership role.

  • Colleagues often said, training for employment is not valuedUnfortunately, however, the Federal government has vacated the leadership role and simply transfers money to the Provinces. The change is now being felt. Still encounter Islanders who decry the loss of PVI and PCVHS as training grounds for business entrepreneurs


In 2015, Education 20/20 called for a “clear and compelling” vision of education. If we want to improve our education system, that is still the place to start.

Don Glendenning, Editor
