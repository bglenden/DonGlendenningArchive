This comprehensive compilation contains multiple draft letters exploring education partnerships, education definitions, conceptual clarity, education services, structural changes, and system vision. Key themes include: (1) education is a service requiring skilled, autonomous providers; (2) education and schooling must be distinguished; (3) education services (information, counselling, instruction, quality assurance, products/technology, funding, human resources) should be explicitly organized and universally available; (4) structural issues include communication gaps, unclear purpose, service identification, autonomy/accountability, and reporting student progress; (5) professional development and qualification authorities require attention. Don critiques treating going to school as equivalent to education and advocates for community participation in education governance. He addresses the "service" vs. "product" confusion and proposes structural reforms including delegation, detailed reporting, and qualification authorities.

Dear Editor

Education Partnerships


The letter (Monday, August 29, 2022) on community partnerships was both timely and interesting. According to te letter. the benefits of learning together include strengthening community ties and strengthening parenting skills; it also provides an additional opportunity to share information about the education services and the education process.


My own conversations with community members are seldom about schools; parents and grandparents wonder about such things as basic or essential skills, relevance of content to real life, assessment and personal development – subjects over which schools appear to have little control.


Education is a service. People who provide the service need to be highly skilled but they also need considerable autonomy. The OECD (who administers PISA tests) tells us that students from schools with autonomy for their day to day activities do better on PISA tests. Seems like an idea worth trying.


Sincerely


Don Glendenning

Our educational purpose

Purpose provides the fundamental reason for establishing an organization and is the key element in the structuring process; yet it is not quite clear to me just what we are trying to accomplish through public education in PEI.

Department literature speaks of “creating opportunities for student success”, ‘helping students secure a place in society” and a role of “developing and delivering high quality programs.” None of these seem to capture the role and spirit of what education is doing or needs to be done. Neither do they provide sufficient detail on which to design programs or measure results. There are likely more definitive statements but I did not find them.


Education is a field of service; it is intended to help people gain knowledge, skills, attitudes and other attributes important in life. Life itself, we are told, includes a number of roles including that of a citizen, family member, individual, student, consumer and producer. All deserve support. Based on my conversations with Islanders, citizen and employment “destinations” are suggested most often as preferred contexts. Either or both would provide an appropriate focus or context for public education.


A clear purpose not only directs the organization. it provides a focus for instruction and assessment; Instead of simply teaching a subject or a grade level, teachers would be helping students gain skills needed to function or thrive in life roles.


In writing this letter, I am not saying that the Department doesn’t have a purpose for what it does but rather as now described, the purpose is not clear. To quote one of my Sunday School teachers of yesteryear, not only must we have “purpose firm”, we must also “dare to make it known”.


A clear purpose is urgently needed and now is the time to make it known.


Sincerely


Don Glendenning

Education services - Education is a service; typical ducation functions or services include: information, counselling, instruction, products and technology, quality assurance. governance, funding and human resources. A school, like a hospital is only a resource- rich location; education results from experiences gained while there.

Dear Editor:

Education Services

People describe education in many ways; most seem to think of education in terms of elementary, secondary and postsecondary students, programs and institutions. The writer, on the other hand, considers educational services as the kind and level of help organized by a community to “support the education of its citizens”. Not much has been written about educational services, however, the following is based on a report of the National Business Council:

  1. Infomation - Recent and relevant information needed by Islanders in the making of decisions about their own education or the education of others.

  2. Governance and Leadership - in the form of a body and organization with vision and responsibility to put in place and monitor educational services for the benefit of its citizens,

  3. Instruction - The provision of direct help to others in their learning and includes diagnosis, counselling, presentations, practice, assessment and access generally to learning resources

  4. Quality Assurance - a system that assures Islanders that their learning experiences, including providers of those services, meet community standards.

  5. Learniing products and technology - access by Islanders to educational materials, technology and other resources to help learners develop skills needed to meet their responsibilities and objectives.

  6. Funding - Funding to a level and in a manner that is equitable for all Islanders.

  7. Human Resources - Teachers, administrators, counsellors, advisors. Assistants and other specialists to help learners and students.


Given our Covid experience, skyrocketing costs of postsecondary education , individual learner profiles, skills based hiring, potential for independent learners, emergence of mini credentials, it may be time to rethink how we deliver education services. Rethinking could include rebuilding our education system rather than only delivering through schools

Sincerely


Don Glendenning

Structure/ Structural Changes


Structure


The literature speaks of the need for educational reform. I agree; the first steps, I suggest, should be structural. Here are some structural issues.

1. Communication – the education story is not well told; people often speak of education silos; I’ve encountered the fear factor. Education efforts need to be better described. See results of a study by the University of Sussex.

2. Purpose sets the stage for what we do and how we organize to do it. The PEI education literature refers to helping people find a place in society; we need to better define that “place”. Life roles have been described as that of a individual, citizen, a producer, a consumer, family member and student. All roles can benefit from education but all may not need the same mix at the same time. That’s part of what makes service complicated.

3. Services - Education is a service. Typical education services include: information, counselling, instruction, products and technology, quality assurance. governance, funding and human resources; Learners may need one or all services at any point in their education journey and services should be front and center in our offerings.

4. Schools are a “resource-rich” facilities where teachers and learners meet to deliver and receive education services but much of our education is community and not school based. Services need to be clearly identified and available to both independent and school- based learners.

5. Structure -” Form follows function” is still the standard when structuring any venture; one would expect, therefore, that the basic education structure would reflect services provided. Instead, the Department appears to be structured around clients served.

6. Autonomy – as mentioned earlier, education is a service. Front line service providers (our teachers) need both the expertise and autonomy to respond to learner needs. The OECD tells us that PISA results are better for students in schools with autonomy for their day-to-day operation; the World Bank advises Ministries of Education to delegate. PEI should also delegate.

7. Accountability -. Mathew Bedard of the Montreal Economic Institute points out that “autonomy and accountability jointly applied” would cost practically nothing and allow education to better adapt to the diversity of student needs and aptitudes. Yet teachers and principals in PEI appear to have little freedom to tailor services to suit individual learner needs.

8. Reporting student progress - grades and grading represents an attempt to apply standardized measurement to levels of achievement on the education journey. Grades also serve as a communication device between a provider and users of a service. Conversations with parents and employers suggest that report cards do not tell much about what learners can do with what they know; a further level of detail on subjects studied and level reached would be helpful. Most developed countries have established qualification bodies to deal with the rationalization of credentials and qualifications; Canada should consider the same.

9. Inservice professional development -


These structural issues are not new; no doubt readers have others to propose. All should be considered. They need to be dealt with soon, and should be dealt with in a very public manner. Education 2030 by the World Bank may prove helpful.
