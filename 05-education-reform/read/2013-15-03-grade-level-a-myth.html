Don challenges the validity of grade levels as meaningful measures of student achievement, noting that within any grade students may be several "grade levels" ahead or behind. November 3, 2013


Editor

Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

The myth of grade levels


School grade levels tell us little about what students know or can do; I say this because, I am told in any grade, students may be several “grade levels” ahead or behind in the subject being taught. School grades are an administrative tool; in their place, we need a system that reflects learning, and even encourages it.


One approach is to simply divide every subject into a number of levels, with clear exit outcomes for each level, and testing available to students on demand. Students, regardless of age, would enroll in the appropriate level, receive instruction as they do now, take the exam when ready, and based on the results, either return for more help or move on to the next level. The emphasis on grade levels would be replaced by “what level of math are you in?” Diplomas would be based on successful completion of selected subjects and levels regardless of age.


The Royal Conservatory of Music actually goes a step further and separates the teaching and testing functions. Instruction is provided by local music teachers but levels of performance are established and awarded by external examiners appointed by the Conservatory.


Obviously, this model is designed for the higher grades. Teachers would benefit from a sharper focus on the level being taught, and the grouping of students at or about the same level in each class. I believe this approach would reduce some of the stress on teachers.


The option would, I think, make for an interesting pilot in one or two schools.


Comments are welcomed at standards2020@gmail.com




Sincerely


Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
