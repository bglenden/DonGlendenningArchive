Education:

Structural impediments

The structure of public education includes some impediments to improvement. I heard this first from a PEI school trustee; later exploration confirmed it. Education is complex; learners have different interests, capabilities, experiences and needs; so does a community. Islanders fill a variety of life roles; graduation requirements and essential skills don’t match; the education system is embedded like no other within the government structure. And the focus on services has waned along with professional freedom. Quality improvement demands change.

In 2015, Education 2020 Inc. called for a clear and compelling vision of education for all Islanders; we are still waiting. Department documents tell us that Government interest is primarily in helping young people “find a place in society”; that is not a clear enough purpose on which to build an education system. The Department of Education gives its role as that of “developing and delivering programs and curriculum” to young people – what Islanders need is skills, knowledge, attitudes and values to help them along life’s road. In a 20i9 report, The World Bank cautioned about the confusion of treating “education and going to school” as though they are one and the same; a review of PEI education documents suggests that PEI has also fallen into that trap. A school, like a hospital or shopping plaza at best, is only a resource rich location; education is the result of changes that occur in those who participate.

“Form follows function” is still the standard when structuring any venture, and successful service organizations require significant freedom at the point of service delivery. In support of this view, the OECD tells us that PISA results are better in schools with autonomy in their day to day operation; the World Bank advises Ministries of Education to delegate. Mathew Bedard of the Montreal Economic Institute points out that “autonomy and accountability jointly applied” would cost practically nothing and allow education to better adapt to the diversity of student needs and aptitudes. Functional restructuring would highlight instruction, counselling, information, learning materials and technology rather than yje running of schools.Restructuring is overdue and should highlight its services rather than its clients.

Organizational accountability is about defining a mission, values and goals and everyone’s role in accomplishing them. Accountability starts with clarity and includes a high level of trust, collaboration and connections. The writer’s search failed to retrieve information that describes teacher and principal duties and responsibilities; I’m left with the impression that teachers invariably public conversation and reports focus on system shortcoming my perception, however, is that teachers lack both freedom and encouragement to take action on their own. changes that occur with people. Accountabiliies in the health field provide an interesting alternative.

Readers interested in structure will find a number of interesting items on the internet.

Sincerely

Editor-D-8
