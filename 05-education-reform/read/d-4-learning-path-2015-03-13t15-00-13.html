Learning path      

No. B-2                 November6, 2014


The first step in understanding education is to understand learning and the learning process.  So, what do we know about learning.  First of all learning is an individual matter; only the learner can learn.  To learn is to change behaviour; a person who has learned has new knowledge a new skill or a different attitude. Learning involves engaging in an experience that has a formative effect on a person.


To better understand the learning process, Education 20/20 invited a panel of young people to tell how they went about learning and what support they found most helpful.  Key words were listed on a flip chart; the list was long, however, on further discussion the panel members settled on the following steps they took the learning path:

  • Gather Information
  • Attend lecture
  • Seek counselling
  • Observe demonstration
  • Attempt task
  • Undergo assessment
  • Motivate self
  • Observe others
  • Use resource material
  • Reflect on experience
  • Take diagnosis
  • Obtain feedback
  • Take instruction
  • Self evaluate
  • Coach others
  • Practice


Next, students were asked the order in which they needed help; not surprisingly, students followed quite different paths.  As an example, one student said his first step would be to Google the topic; another said if it involved a new skill, he would probably attempt it first, while others liked the traditional lecture as a place to start.  Since panel members were in school or recent graduates, much of the discussion revolved around gaining new knowledge rather than skills or attitudes.  Our  first chart was linear, however, it soon became evident that learning was anything but linear.


It also became evident that different purposes for learning did not substantially change the help needed, although different purposes may make the path longer.  Although not part of the original discussion, we’ve added the manner of recognition likely to occur at the close of the learning cycle.  Consultation is ongoing, however, consider this as an interim progress report.
