A detailed Windsor Foundation proposal from Education 20/20 Inc. proposing to explore alternatives to current achievement grading methods, specifically titled "Mapping the Math Mountain." The proposal argues that using "grade" for both age grouping and achievement levels is confusing and proposes establishing criterion-referenced achievement levels in mathematics. It outlines specific steps including establishing a steering committee, developing competency lists, and creating rating scales based on Bloom's taxonomy.

BACKGROUND


Mission/purpose of organization:  Education 20/20 Inc. is federally incorporated as an independent, broad based, national, not-for-profit education organization, located in Charlottetown, PEI and is governed by a volunteer Board of Directors.  Our mission is to encourage and support a quality education for every Canadian; Education 20/20 Inc. is unique among educational organizations in Canada in that it is unaligned with any educational institutions, grade level, subject field or cause.   The organization operates with ten Directors, a network of some fifty people, and a mailing list of almost 300.  Enclosed is a list of activities by year since our incorporation and a brief plan of activities for the coming year.  Although most Directors have participated in externally funded projects in their other roles, this is the first such project for Education 20/20 Inc. and certainly our first request for funding from the Windsor Foundation.


Proposal: to explore alternatives to the current method of establishing levels of achievement in school studies.


Issue being addressed

The term “grade”< so common in education, has two meanings; one use refers to a grouping of students by age while the other is used to identify a student’s level of achievement in a subject. It is especially confusing when we know that people of all ages learn at their own pace and,  what one knows and can do has little to do with age.  The practice of using the same word for two purposes is confusing and the results are misleading.  A better practice would be to simply organize material in some order of difficulty and encourage and help students to reach the level they need or want.  This project proposes to explore that issue. 


Relationship to Windsor Foundation Priorities

The project actually addresses both major interests of the Windsor Foundation.


Higher education initiatives:   

The project is being designed to provide university and college admission officer, HR personnel and others who need to know, with reliable and consistent information about achievement levels of applicants so that they, in turn, can make better decisions about the both recruits and students they accept.


Employers, postsecondary institutions and qualification bodies ask for “graduation” as a basic entrance requirement then try to figure out how to use it.  We want to remove the inconsistencies currently found in that term; although high school graduation involves a fairly rigid set of school courses as determined by Departments of Education, it is an accepted fact that posted requirements may be significantly shaded first by the culture of the school providing the grade and secondly by the “shading” applied during the selection process. As a result, many college and university students struggle with course work and, it is felt, practice of accepting less than asked lowers the achievement level of college graduates and professionals in later life.  As well, postsecondary dollars are being used for non- postsecondary purposes.


Disadvantaged or vulnerable youth: Securing a recognized proficiency level should help addresses the issue of basic skills, those skills needed to gain entry to the next phase of life and, without which many are branded as failures.  We are aware that sometimes this may result from poor decisions by  students  themselves; on the other hand, we are also aware that the gap may from incorrect information, biased counselling, streaming, outdated course content, poor teaching, inflexible scheduling, lack of encouragement and mentoring, and the list goes on


PROJECT NARRATIVE


Following the publication of the CFPEI Report in late 2013, Education 20/20 organized six public discussions for the purpose of informing the community of the report and its findings, learning more about youth issues in our communities, exploring issues of education and learning, and to identify actions to be taken to close gaps.  Discussions were lively and wide ranging, and carried on well after the formal session ended.   The final report included a recommendation that.   


Further study be undertaken to clarify the gaps in preparation for further study and for work

-(Preparedness, Education 20/20 Inc., Occasional Paper No. 1

Since then Education 20/20 has continued its exploration, both formally and informally, with postsecondary institutions, employers, teachers and education officials and concluded that:

The use of “Grade” to designate both age level and achievement level is confusing; we recommend that “Grade” continue to be used for this purpose.

A separate classification (we recommend “Level”) be used to mean achievement level in a subject. 

New achievement levels should be struck

Mathematics be chosen for the pilot project 

An independent body be struck to conduct assessment for the purpose of assigning levels of achievement.


Weaknesses of the current system include:

The sheer number of institutions with authority to grant grades.

Inherent conflict when those who teach also grade

Different approaches to curriculum development

Confusion arising from the use of “Grade” to both classify the age of the learner and as a level of achievement

Growing use of social promotion

Changing style of student reporting

Lack of benchmarks
