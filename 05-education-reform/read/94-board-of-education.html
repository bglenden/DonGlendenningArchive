In this October 2020 piece, Don proposes reimagining school governance on PEI through a "Board of Education" rather than traditional school trustees. His vision involves local residents ensuring community members access 21st-century educational services including online courses, counseling, and personal development. No. 94 October 15, 2020

Education isn’t something you can finish – Isaac Asimov


Board of Education

I was pleased to read in recent days that the matter of school trustees has moved higher on the government agenda; whether or not this will have a positive impact on education will depend on what trustees (1) believe about education and (2) what they are allowed and mandated to do. Before settling on trustees, however, I suggest that we consider other options.


Readers may remember that Education 2020 Inc. carried out a study of continuing education in Souris a few months ago. (Occasional Paper No. 10). The study identified some needs and proposed some solutions.


What surprised me was, not the interest in further training, but rather the lack of a mechanism to handle the matter.


So I’m wondering if, instead of school trustees, we should consider a Board of Education, a group of local residents not to run a school, but to see that local residents receive the educational services needed for the 21st century – services such as information, on-line courses, personal development, essential skills, counselling, instruction, education products, etc.


This morning, my Washington Post carried an article about candidates for the Board of Education in Washington, DC. Not surprising, there is some controversy about their role but, what caught my attention was the following:


“But while these nonpartisan positions wield little power, they have become symbolic battlegrounds over the future of public education — and the members of the board have emerged as visible education advocates in the city.”


That kind of discussion is needed on PEI.


Educators provide an important service to individual Islanders and to our community.; that professional voice deserves to be heard. But the voice of those who use and pay for the service is equally important. Collaboration is; a Board of Education may help.




Don Glendenning, Editor
