Education Philosophy & Reform

October 14, 2014

Editor, Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

The crux of the problem


I didn’t have a happy Thanksgiving, in fact most of the past month has been stressful.  The cause of my stress is an event in the life of a student, a person I have never met but whose family I know and future has been limited because of the way we organize schools and operate schools.  It is not the I’ve hear similar stories before. 


A high school student, with hopes, even plans to go to college or university, was having difficulty with math; that’s not unusual, I am told, many people struggle with a subject and struggling with math is not uncommon.   So what are the options?

  • Let the a student struggle and likely fail a grade
  • Provide extra instruction
  • Transfer to a lower level of the subject
  • Recommend that they continue and take an upgrading course after they graduate
  • Allow them to drop the class but not graduate
  • Provide each teacher with an aspirin allowance
  • Blame the home


The correct action is obvious; provide more help.  A system that allows other options is out dated to say the least and extremely limiting to the future of our young people and our communities.


During the past few weeks, I’ve had a number of conversations about math, conversations with people in all walks of life.  Arising out of these conversations are a number of recommendations; two are worthy of being passed on:

  • Create a math ladder that sets out a number of clearly identified levels
  • Report achievement in terms of which rung a student has reached
  • Establish a math assessment service, apart from teachers, for assessing each students level.


Correcting situations like this, although not as sexy as PCAP and PISA, are at the heart of securing our future.


Sincerely


Don Glendenning

A long time student of education
