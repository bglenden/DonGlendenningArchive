DRAFT LETTERS


MeaningA clearer Focus


I write this letter because I believe it to be important. I also write because I believe that education has been over powered by the influence of schools


Education is important. It is a major factor in determining both our individual and collective futures. It is said that we fill six life roles, that of an individual, family member, student, citizen, consumer and producer. Education helps us thrive in any and all these roles. Most important, it gives us choices in our everyday living.


So, what is education? I consider education to be experiences that changes what we know, what we can do, what we believe, what we value and much more; education experiences that change us are arranged by parents, by teachers, but may be arranged by friends or even experienced by accident. Our education is the result of the give and take of all our experiences, not just those in school.


What one learns in school is certainly part of one’s education; what gives school experiences the edge in ones education is the manner in which experiences are measured and recorded.

Sincerely

Don Glendenning



In preparing to write this letter, I visited a number of education websites (Departments of Educatio, Canadian Education Association, Council of Atlantic Ministers of Education and Training, Council of Ministers of Education and others; I was curious to see for myself the topics dealt with. Turns out they dealt mostly with matters of schooling and not education.


If we want better education we should focus on education – improving essential skills, employment readiness, personal finance, life skills and interpersonal relationships. Improving the rink is unlikely to improve your grandson’s hockey.


We should also engage the community in the discussion, but that is a subject for another letter.


Sincerely


Don Glendenning

Education or schooling


Dear Editor:

The World Bank is the world’s largest financial contributor to public education. In an annual report some time ago, the Bank cautioned developing countries about using “education and going school” as though they are one and the same. Good advice.


Education is an umbrella concept. It involves experiences that impact on what we know, feel, value, do, believe and think. A school, on the other hand, is a space or building designed, equipped, staffed and organized to provide groups of learners with help.

A school may be likened to a hospital where patients go for assistance with health matters. Being hospitalized means that you are getting help; getting out of the hospital suggests that you are on the mend but not necessarily well.


I urge all who deal with education matters choose their words carefully.


Sincerely


Don Glendenning

The Purpose of Education

Dear Editor:

There have been some interesting items in the media recently about the state of basic education; I’ve encountered similar comments in my conversations.


Such issues, I believe, arise in part, because our objectives are not clear. References to “purpose” that I encounter in the PEI literature refer to helping “Islanders find a meaningful place in society”, “developing and delivering programs”, and helping people reach their dream. While all are positive none are sufficiently clear as a basis for designing programs or assessing progress.


Purpose and goals guide our activities, our organization, what we teach and how we measure. As readers may know, New Brunswick has undertaken a recent review of public education; one of their goals is that “every child will leave Grade 5 having mastered the tools to learn – reading, writing and numeracy.” PEI needs an equally clear statement. It gives focus to curriculum development, assessment, transcripts and report cards and the meaning of grade levels and graduation.

.

This is not to argue that we do not know what we are doing but rather to argue for clarity in describing it.


Sincerely


Don Glendenning

EDUCATION SERVICES

Education is one of the helping professions; it helps other who want or need to learn. It is an unusual helper in that it seldom identifies the services offered. My search of the literature suggests that an education system typically includes the following services:

• Information - recent and independent information

• Counselling – help to people making decision about their education needs

• Governance - a body with vision, experience and responsibility

• Instruction – providing individuals direct personal service through diagnosis, presentations, practice and access to other resources

• Quality Assurance – to assure Islanders that their learning experiences meet community standards.

• Products and Technology – direction and access to educational materials and technology

• Funding - to a level and in a manner that is equitable for all Islanders.

• Human Resources - teachers, administrators, counsellors, advisors. assistants and others


We need a system that allows citizens to select the services needed without having to enroll in a school. We do it in health; why not in education?

Clarity


Education is complex; information plays a key role. Learners have different interests, experiences, capabilities and needs; learners also fill a variety of life roles. Those who teach, or other-wise serve learners, must be very clear in their communication. Conceptual clarity heightens our understanding of key words and concepts; lack of clarity is a significant issue for public education. It must be addressed directly so that the concept of education won’t become diluted and potentially ineffective


. Lack of clarity may arise form an idea itself, from the person promoting the idea or from the person receiving the idea; Lack of clarity could also signal conflicting ideas and levels of discomfort or disagreement from within the education family group itself; some say “clarity” is also related to self confidence. Conceptual clarify is also a must in planning and assessing performance.


I first thought that the lack of clarity resulted from a style of writing, an attempt to be inclusive , an attempt to not leave out important items, but I’ve changed my mind. I now realize that I comes from educational antiquity, from classifying education first as elementary, secondary and higher rather than helping others gain personal, citizenship or employment related experiences. Going to school is the focus rather than helping people change the way they think, feel and act.

Improving the education content of school curriculum


A recent World Bank report ( ,2018 ?) cautioned developing countries about confusing “going to school” with ”getting an education”. Schools focus on helping young people learn subjects, usually traditional ones, such as language, technology, mathematics, history, science, etc.


Education, on the other hand, includes knowledge of traditional subjects but includes the development of character, creativity, problem solving, personal skills, analysis, self-assessment, discussion, assuming responsibility, self care, planning, care of others, etc. Schooling is a part or stage of learning that takes place in facilities specifically designed for education purposes (schools). Experiences in school are an important part of education, but only a part.


Educational tweaks to school studies include such things as learning plans, holistic rating scales, learner self- assessment, individual learner profiles, debate, ownership, context, use of community based assessment criteria and project based assignments .

DEFINITIONS NEEDED


The Dictionary of Education (Good) defines education s “the aggregate of all the processes by means of which a person develops abilities of positive value in the society in which he lives”. Given this, readers will understand, therefore, why I stuttered when asked by a colleague where I “got my education”. Education is a “meaning laden” subject; those of us who work in the field have an obligation to be clear in our use of words.


But there are other and simpler description as well.

  • Any experience that has an impact on the way we think, feel or act

  • Bringing about change in ourselves or others
