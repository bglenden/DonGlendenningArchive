Policy paper addressing mathematics education following Community Foundation of PEI's youth survey (2013) revealing preparedness gaps in postsecondary and workplace readiness. Documents Education 20/20's six public discussion series responding to survey findings. Frames mathematics as essential citizen-level skill for all Islanders based on Oregon Department of Education principles. Identifies instruction providers spanning public schools (comprehensive Grades 1-12 with testing and streaming), postsecondary (UPEI programs and School of Mathematics), SkillsPEI (federal funding coordination), private schools, and independent learners. Analyzes proficiency determinants: skill number, performance level, and clustering. Distinguishes formative assessment (interactive teacher-student process) from summative assessment (regulatory independent function). Notes competencies as "gradeless" (learnable across all levels with varying achievement). Documents related developments including MPHEC competency adoption, Queens medical program conversion, holistic rating scales, independent field analysis, ungraded schools, separate grading authorities, and streaming disappearance. States eleven system conclusions emphasizing non-school-alone solutions, fragmentation, grading non-educational role, inconsistent teacher authorization, definition barriers, coordination absence, and provider cooperation willingness. References "Taking the Pulse of Island Youth" Community Foundation survey.


MATHEMATICS EDUCATION

March 12, 2018

____________________________________________________________________________


In late 2013, The Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island (CFPEI) undertook a survey of over 200 Island youth to identify issues that directly affect them; the report, Taking the Pulse of Island Youth, available on the Foundation’s website at www.cfpei.ca, provides a snapshot of the findings. Bright spots include a positive sense of community, emerging entrepreneurial spirit, volunteerism and new postsecondary offerings. The main concerns include a lack of jobs, surviving on low-paying jobs, educational deficiencies and drug and alcohol addictions. On the subject of education and learning, the study reports a high rate of completion of high school and postsecondary education, a high level of inclusivity and relatively affordable tuition costs; young people also expressed concerns about their readiness for the next stage of life including:


“preparedness gaps with regard to readiness for

postsecondary study and the workplace” – Vital Signs, CFPEI, 2013


Following the publication of the CFPEI Report, Education 20/20 organized six public discussions for the purpose of informing the community of the report and its findings, learning more about youth issues in our communities, exploring issues of education and learning, and to identify actions to be taken to close gaps. Discussions were lively and wide ranging and carried on well after the formal session ended. Education 20/20 retained an interest in the subject and was encouraged to resume discussions with providers and users of educational services following the report of the Council of Ministers in July of 2016


“A student’s ability to transition from secondary school to post-secondary education and the labour market is a key measure of the education system’s success for students” –

CMEC, July 2016


Transition through education has been the subject of a number of informal discussions with educators, employers, students and the community at large. It is the writer’s view that, at the minimum, there is an issue of communication among parties involved or interested in the subject, a communication gap resulting from the number of service providers, lack of joint planning and ongoing coordination.


Mathematics is the study of the science of number, quantity and space; it is in demand to some degree by all citizens: employers, institutions and the community at large. The Oregon Department of Education contends that math provides “a foundation for the study of science and technology as well as the interpretation of quantitative information in other subjects. Mathematics may be structured in a number of ways but structure does not appear to have a particular influence on the quality of instruction.


The writer is of the view that math is an essential skill needed to some level by all citizens and can be learned to the citizen level by all or practically all Islanders. Furthermore, helping citizens acquire math skills for citizenship is one of he key roles of our public-school system but found no agreement, however, as to the nature and level of citizen math and how it should be determined.


MATH EDUCATION

A person’s education begins early; the ability to count is often quoted in reference to a child starting school and is often used by parents to support a view that their child is “bright”. At the same time, many parents, unintentionally I am sure, encourage the view that math is difficult and the literature tells us that a person’s beliefs about mathematics can have a substantial impact on their interest, their enjoyment and their motivation in math classes.


Providers of math instruction: in PEI include:

  1. The public-school system plays a key role in increasing and formalizing each student’s knowledge of math. The school system accepts all children and not only provides but requires math instruction each year through to graduation. Math to be learned is identified for each grade and achievement in the time available is reported to parents and the public alike. Student results are used to stream students depending on the perceived view of the student’s likely career path. Performance across course offerings has become a screening mechanism in the transition process.



  1. Postsecondary institutions provide math instruction in support of programs or majors generally; in addition, UPEI offers a math major and most recently created a School of Mathematics and Computational Science.



  1. SkillsPEI is a federally funded agency that purchases training under federal criteria. The writer is unclear as to how instructional content is determined or achievement measured Levels of Instruction in math may be provided by provincial schools and institutions, by employers and their organizations or NGO’s on behalf of clients. Content is determined or agreed upon at time of purchase. Except for adult upgrading, there appears to be little coordination of math content across courses. It doesn’t appear that NGO operators can or do tap into available provincial instructional resources prepared elsewhere in the system

  2. Private schools offer instruction with content being aligned with the relevant provincial curriculum.

  3. Individuals may study and improve their math skills on their own or with the help of family, friends, tutors, on line courses and other personal learning strategies. Independent learners appear not to access institutional guides and instructional materials and lack a simple mechanism for demonstrating their proficiency levels


PROFICIENCY LEVELS

As to proficiency levels, there appears to be some agreement that differences in math levels may be attributed to three factors:

  1. The number of skills that can be demonstrated

  2. Levels achieved on skills demonstrated, and

  3. Nature or degree of clustering of skills learned.


Grade levels are established by the Department of Education; individual student proficiency levels are awarded by teachers.

• Formative assessment is an interactive process between student and teacher designed to inform both parties how well students are learning what teachers are teaching. Formative assessment will likely be course or incident based and sometimes anonymous and not even connected to passing or grading.


• Summative assessment (evaluation) is a regulatory function responding to society’s demand for minimum levels of performance in certain life roles and, therefore, should be carried out by an independent body. may take into account more than course content and academic success depending on its purpose including discussion, cooperation, attendance, presentation, etc.


Outcomes and competencies are “gradeless” meaning that, at least theoretically, that any competency can be studied in public school, college and university. What changes is the level achieved on each outcome,


RELATED DEVELOPMENTS

The following developments have the potential to impact math instruction in PEI:

  • The MPHEC adoption of outcomes or competencies when describing institutional programs.

  • Apparent general trend to adopt competencies as a basis for curriculum development

  • Emergence of dynamic/ holistic rating scales,

  • Field analysis as an independent step in the curriculum development process.

  • Ungraded senior high schools.

  • Separate grading authorities.

  • Disappearance of streaming

  • Local informal survey suggest that vectors, trigonometry and simultaneous equations are not used by Islanders in everyday life.


CONCLUSIONS

Based on the literature, the above noted discussions and the writer’s experience, the writer concludes that:

  1. The transition issue cannot be solved by adjustments to the public-school system alone

  2. Math instruction in PEI is fragmented

  3. Grading students is not an instructional or school function

  4. The large number of teachers authorized to grade students increases the likelihood of inconsistencies across the system.

  5. Cooperation among providers is hampered by the lack of agreed- upon definitions of a number of key concepts.

  6. Broadly speaking, education lacks an overarching statement of philosophy and direction;

  7. There is no formal mechanism for coordination among math providers

  8. Evaluation criteria for math should be the same for all levels of education.

  9. The way content is described differs from provider to provider.

  10. Improving transition requires cooperation of all math instruction providers.

  11. Current instructional model getting out of date – alignment, curriculum map and rating

  12. There is a willingness, if not an interest, among math providers in in cooperating on a pilot project
