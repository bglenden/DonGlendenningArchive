Open Education

No. 92 August 20, 2020

When schools and ministries are managed well, learning happens-World Bank


TIME FOR CONVERSATION

i’ve been in education all my life; I believe education to be important and never more so than at the moment. I view education as a service; a service of helping people gain new skills, information, attitudes and values. Many argue that the system has changed and is now focused on delivering products – credits, diplomas and degrees. – rather than services. The Department’s description of role as one of “ develop(ing) and deliver(ing) high quality programs and curriculum in English and French to Island children from birth to grade 12” does nothing to allay these concerns.


Certainly, my conversations reveal a gap between services expected and services delivered – a gap that is increasingly referred to as structural – meaning not caused by people failing to act but rather by a framework that limits opportunities to do better: Examples include:

  • Unclear or unstated definitions and guiding ptinciples

  • Use of seat-timein school as a measure of accomplishment.

  • The failure to acknowledge findings of respected international organizations (OECD and World Bank) with regard to autonomy and accountability.

  • theOptics of a Department having authority to operate schools, require attendance, require graduation requirements, issues diplomas, icertifies teachers,

  • Meagre reports on student progress.

  • In ability or unwillingness to accommodate indiviual needs, interest and timetables.

  • Lack of coordination at community level

  • To what extent is education improvement deterred by its embedment in government structure?

  • Recognized qualification

The time has come for an open conversation about the likely future of PEI and the role of education in its unfolding. o engage educators in conversation about the future needs of education in PEI; they have much to offer for conversation and collaboration

Low profile of respected educators in presenting and discussing education policies

Don Glendenning, Editor
