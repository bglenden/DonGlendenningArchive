Education 2020 Numeracy Award Application

A Proposal to Improve Data Literacy in Prince Edward Island Students

Submitted by the Outreach Committee of the School of Mathematical and Computational

Sciences (SMCS), University of Prince Edward Island

Contact: Dr. Shannon Fitzpatrick, Associate Dean, SMCS, sfitzpatrick@upei.ca,


ISSUE

Digital literacy has become an important issue for a number of reasons:

• Personal data is being collected at unprecedented rates. It is important for all of us to

understanding how data is stored and used.

• Data is used to advocate for change, inform policy and justify decisions. Many of us do

not have the tools required to understand and question the data that is presented to us.

• In business and industry, quantitative models have become a vital part of decision

making, and the need for skilled employees is ever increasing. The ability to work with

and analyze data is becoming a vital skill in our knowledge-economy.

As the amount of data being created and collected explodes, improving data literacy in all young

Canadians has become increasingly important.

BACKGROUND

Data literacy is the ability to collect, interpret, analyze and use data in a critical manner.

However, we should not view data literacy as being distinct from numeracy and other

mathematical skills - all require the same problem-solving and critical thinking skills. By

developing educational tools that are built around data analysis, we expand students’ numeracy

skills through concrete examples that inform students about the world around them.

A key tool in increasing data literacy is inquiry-based learning. Inquiry-based learning, which

has gained significant popularity in recent years, encourages students to take an active role in

the design and implementation of their own experiments. Many studies support the

effectiveness of such an approach. [1]

While inquiry-based learning may be typically associated with science education, this approach

can also be applied to mathematics education. The PPDAC approach (Problem, Plan, Data

Collection, Analysis and Conclusion) [2] applies the inquiry-based model to statistical analysis.

Where the PPDAC approach differs from typical inquiry-based model is that the traditional route

of posing a question, collecting data and a drawing conclusion is not always followed. With

PPDAC, students can begin with an existing data set and, by analyzing the data, decide what

relevant questions can be answered.

Along with learning basic statistical concepts and principles, student would participate in

• Collection of data

• Selection of data (querying existing data sets)

• Creation of data visualizations

Of course, when dealing with large data sets, the use of technology also plays a significant role.

One example of how statistical reasoning has been brought into the classroom is the Census at

School project (censusatschool.ca). Through various surveys and activities, students can not

only collect and analyze data from their own class, but examine data collected both nationally

and internationally. Learning activities are available for grades 4 through 12.

Another example is the approach outlined in [3], where researchers created lesson plans based

on large data sets related to solar energy generation and use in the UK. In this study, a group

of 10-11 year-olds were presented with visualizations of the data and tasked with answering a

number of questions. (In the PPDAC model, students were starting with the conclusion.) It was

found that “students demonstrated that they were able to make valid interpretations and tell nice

stories from the relatively sparse information given in visualizations”.

While data literacy can be obviously integrated into traditional mathematics and science

education, we should not ignore the need for data literacy in the arts. Data can be used to

engage people and encourage change; data can be used to create a narrative. Some examples

of data-driven arts projects can be found datatoolkit.org,uk. Data Toolkit encourages arts

organizations to combine art and data to examine social issues, and provides the framework for

creating such projects.

GOALS

We are interested in providing tools for teachers that will encourage them to integrate data

inquiry into their curriculum. In particular, our goal is to provide teachers with concrete learning

activities that can be used in conjunction with Census at School to teach specific components of

the PEI curriculum. We will hire two undergraduate students to collect, organize, and update

existing Statistics learning activities for use with Census at School in Mathematics classrooms

on Prince Edward Island. These students will identify specific numeracy components of the PEI

curriculum at each grade level that are well suited for Census at School projects, and they will

provide teachers with collections of self-contained learning activities that will explicitly address

each curriculum goal.

Specific outcomes for the project are:

1. To complete a survey of available materials, identify the best materials for each grade level,

and indicate how they align to PEI Curriculum outcomes.

2. Update and modify available materials to work within the Census at School framework and

the newly-created Canadian Census at School R Shiny interactive web apps.

3. Create an online repository of the best resources related data literacy.

4. Create a brochure to be distributed to schools that provides teachers with a quick reference

for locating data-driven activities.

5. To develop activities that use the PPDAC approach to learning, utilizing data sets that are

relevant to island students.

Such activities could be used by teachers, or become part of a data literacy outreach initiative.

Once resources are developed, the Outreach Committee of the School of Mathematical and

Computational Sciences will offer training to teachers and other educators on the available

materials.

LOCATION, DATES, and PERSONNEL

This is part of an ongoing initiative of the Outreach Committee at UPEI’s School of Mathematical

and Computational Sciences. The committee consists of Dr. Michael MacIsaac (Statistics), Dr.

Christopher Power (Computer Science), Dr. David LeBlanc (Computer Science) and Dr.

Shannon Fitzpatrick (Mathematics).

Two undergraduate students will be hired for a 14-week period between May and August, 2020

to contribute to this project. These students will be mentored by Outreach Committee members.

Funding from the Education 2020 Numeracy Award would be put toward student salaries.

The rate of pay at UPEI for students with specialized skills is $15.75 per hour. Based on a 35-

hour work week, the total cost of personnel is $15,435. We have applied for funding from the

Canada Summer Jobs Program, which would contribute approximately $6,000. Remaining

funds would come from a gift to the School of Mathematical and Computational Sciences from

TD Bank.
