My notes

March 22, 2013

Some notes on reading

By

Don Glendenning


NOTE: These notes are put together to focus discussions on the subject of reading and are intended to reflect the subject from a learner’s rather than a teacher’s perspective.


Our futures, as individuals and as a community, depend, to a large degree, on the nature and level of education available to, and actually attained by, our citizens. Education helps us to develop our individual potential to the fullest and prepares us for our many life roles* that of an individual, family member, learner, citizen, producer and consumer. This means engaging in a variety of activities with a view to assembling a variety of skills, knowledge and attitudes supportive of functioning in those life roles. Based on this thinking, the quality of one’s education would be measured by how well one is able to function in the various life roles.


One’s education is the result of everything that we learn in our lifetime that has had a formative effect on us. It starts at birth with some of our most important learning occurring in the first few year of life; skills learned at that time, to a large degree, determine what we become in later life. Learning is a partnership and nowhere is that partnership more important than in improving the reading level of every citizen.


Key to success in education generally, is reading, the golden skill, and a tool for lifelong learning. Statistics indicate reveal the magnitude of the task. We are aware of the attention given by our Ministers of Education in Atlantic Canada to increasing literacy rates, including reading, and this increased attention appears to be paying dividend. We also know, however, improving reading is more than a school problem, it is a community problem, even a development problem, which can be traced in part to the early years and outside the influence of the school.


While we recognize that reading, and learning to read better, occurs in the context of other learning to communicate generally, including listening, writing, speaking, it is usefu, I suggest, to examine each component separately, and that we should commence with a common understanding of what it means to read.



Definition:


The matter of definition is likely a good place to start our exploration. A quick perusal reveals a number of definitions:

  • The act of turning writing into meaning.

  • The ability to use printed information as meaningful language (Source unknown).

  • Specific abilities which enable a reader to read the written form as meaningful language, read anything written with independence, comprehension and fluency and to mentally interact with the message. (Source unknown)

What you do when you read?

So, what is involved in reading? To answer the question I searched the literature for a comprehensive list of competencies, outcomes or tasks. I did not find one but, from what I found, I pulled together the following list of what a reader is able to:

  • Choose words appropriate to the subject

  • Comprehend what has been read

  • Speak fluently

  • Exhibit a wide vocabulary

  • Read and Interpret Prose

  • Read and interpret graphs

  • Read and interpret drawings

  • Read and interpret manuals

  • Read and interpret tables

  • Read and interpret symbols

  • Read and interpret instructions

  • Read and interpret labels

  • Distinguish fact from fiction

  • Interpret authors intent

  • Skim and scan for information

  • Read and interpret computer print outs

  • Read text messages

  • Read rapidly for the main points

  • Read rapidly to find specific information

  • Read signs, labels or lists.

  • Complete forms

  • Read tables, schedules and other table-like text

  • Obtain specific information from graphs or charts.

  • Recognize common angles such as 15o, 30 o, 45 o and 90 o.

  • Interpret scale drawings (e.g. blueprints or maps).

  • Obtain information from sketches, pictures or icons

  • Sketch and interpret drawings

  • Read and interpret manuals

  • Read and interpret tables and graphs

  • Read and interpret symbols

  • Read and interpret instructions

  • Read and interpret written information

  • Utilize dictionaries

  • Utilize Thesaurus

  • Utilize style manuals

Key Performance Criteria:

How well one reads compared to another, is usually measured in terms of:

  • Range of material one can read

  • How well one comprehends what has been read

  • Fluency

  • Degree of assistance required

  • Speed

  • Vocabulary

  • Flow
