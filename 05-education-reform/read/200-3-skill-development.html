No. 200-3 August 20, 2020

When schools and ministries are managed well, learning happens-World Bank


SKILL DEVELOPMENT

The future of Islanders and the Island will depend in large measure on the nature and extent of education gained by our citizens – young and old. Educators, therefore, should be front and centre working with community leaders, providing services and shaping education outcomes.


Islanders have had access to quality skill training for the past 100 years, first under the Agricultural Aid Act of 1912, and under replacement legislation regularly thereafter. Quality was assured through stipulations on standards, staffing, curriculum, inspections, national conferences, newsletters and visits.


Contacts tell me that the leadership role no longer applies; agreements have been concluded with each province under which Provinces are reimbursed for agreed upon expenses. As a result, many technical courses are technical rather than skill development.


For the past fifty years, PVI and PCVHS have supplied work entry workers for the local market, people who often went on to become small business entrepreneurs. The gap is often mentioned by the business community..









and has been replaced with a simpler role of The decision Instead, offerings are narrowing; gone are PVI and PCVHS, gone is the Vocational education division, gone are the roving adult education courses and continuing education is greatly reduced. in fact it seems that the Department now views going to school and getting an education as one and the same. The education issue in PEI, and likely in the rest of Canada, is a narrow mission with the focus on schools. As evidence I refer readers to the statement on our Department’s current website to the effect that ”the role of the Department is t\develop and deliver programs” to youth; District Councils jurisdictions are also limited to schools matters. Education matters seem of little interest.


The role of education is to help people gain new information, attitudes, skills, values, etc. Islanders believe education to be important and, through their government, pay for services that are both good for people and good for the community. A problem arises, however, when Government decided to l deliver all services itself rather than simply pay for them and embedded the delivery system in the government structure.


As a result of this decision, education lost both autonomy and accountability. Government simply runs schools rather than providing educational services to its people. Education has become a product marked by courses, credits, diplomas, degrees and events rather than a simple service.


Don Glendenning, Editor
