The letter outlines structural problems in the education system and describes tools for change including newsletters, engagement activities, recognition programs, and awards. It emphasizes the role of independent information as fuel for systemic educational improvement.

Since we last spoke, Education 20/20 Inc. has continued its work in support of change, especially change in public education. It is our view that much remains to be done.


Our objective is to ensure a quality education for every Canadian; that means bringing about  change, not an easy task  for an external organization.  Most of the initial impediments to change in public education are structural; by that, we mean a school system masquerading as an education system, an out-of-date and out-of-tune philosophy of education, an out dated management system, goals and outcomes too vague for meaning and measurement, a teaching model that delivers instruction developed and packaged in the Holman Building in Summerside rather than a  developmental model that responds to learner needs, floating standards light on externally referenced criteria,  a monopoly that survives primarily because it is mandatory, a system tha values conformity over competence and is kept alive it seems by politicians, senior bureaucrats and the teachers’ unions.

The nature and quality of education will, to a large extent, determine our future as individuals, communities and countries. Education First is convinced that education is important, that improvement is needed and that there is wide support for change.  Although education systems are instituting changes, the changes fall short of what is actually required; changes fail to deal with a number of underlying weaknesses.  Furthermore, the required changes are not likely to happen unless the public demands it. Traditional efforts to bring about change have not worked.  Some innovative, perhaps even bold, measures are required, but we think that a better education system is worth it and we’d like to play a leading role in making change happen.


While we have identified many entry points into the change process, we propose to commence with the following:

  • A monthly electronic newsletter, copy attached.  
  • A public participation survey using recently developed Canadian community engagement software.
  • A series of occasional papers on subjects of interest to the public such as reading, standards, school autonomy, etc., and
  • Educational leadership awards for individuals, institutions and organizations that demonstrate best practices.


Information is the fuel for change; it doesn’t guarantee change but certainly supports it.  Our health, for example, is the subject of frequent media articles about cancer, diabetes, Alzheimers, the dangers of smoking as well as matters relating to our local health services.  In comparison, information items in the media about education tend to be operational matters only, bus service, school closings, school construction and professional development days.  There is nothing in the local media equivalent to the monthly section on matters of health. 


In gathering and presenting information, we propose to engage people, especially teachers in training, in the preparation of occasional papers – on topics of our choice.  We’ve had some informal discussion with the UPEI Dean of Education about the matter and are hopeful that something can be worked out.  And there are other ways of approaching the information deficit including debates, competitions, presentations, etc.   


We also are pleased to announce that the Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island supports our educational initiatives.  As a result, contributions to Education First Canada Inc. will be receipted and administered by the Foundation.


Our group cannot make this happen alone; we need your help – both encouragement and financial support and preferably over a three year period.  I would welcome an opportunity to meet, to provide more details about our plans.   I’ll be in touch within the next few days seeking an appointment.
