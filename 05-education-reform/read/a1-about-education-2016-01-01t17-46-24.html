The meaning of education

No. 01 Date



Sometimes conversations about education are confusing; I attribute the confusion, not to the varying points of view on a subject but rather to the failure properly identify the meaning of words in a consistent manner. That impreciseness may be acceptable from the public but there is an onus on those in the specialty to be more precise.


The word “education” may be used in a variety of contexts.

  • In terms of what one knows and is able to do – he can fix anything

  • Number of years of formal schooling and/or credentials held – she has a Master’s degree in Nursing.

  • Formal qualifications – an “iron ring” meaning a professional engineer.

  • A department of government that has been designated to oversee education in a political jurisdiction

  • An economic sector


When used in terms of an individual, I tend to use the following definitions:

  • Education - any experience that has a formative effect on the way a person thinks feels or acts.

  • Education, purpose – to develop one’s capacities as participating member of a community or society

  • Education system - services put in place by a community to encourage and support the education of its citizens When applied to a department of government, education had the following components: instruction, information, human resources, governance, finance, delivery




While formal study in schools, colleges and universities are important, so too is what we learn at home, at church, in the various community groups we belong to, on the job and through self study. In fact, in the broader scheme of life, formal education, or at least the part we measure, is one of the sectors of least importance.
