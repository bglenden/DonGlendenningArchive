The published March 2018 version of Occasional Paper No. 7, this document presents Don's definition of quality education and analysis of international frameworks. He distinguishes between "education" as formative life experiences and "schooling" as formal instruction, arguing many educators conflate these terms. The paper examines quality education from perspectives of UNICEF, UNESCO, OECD, and others, emphasizing that quality requires more than test scores. Key themes include the importance of school autonomy, accountability, learner-centered approaches, and professional teacher development. Don's contribution includes critiques of traditional grading systems and calls for independent qualification bodies. The published version refines arguments from the draft revision.

Quality Education


Occasional Paper No. 7 March 1, 2018

________________________________________________________________________________



There is a on-going concern about the quality of our education services and little agreement as to what constitutes a quality offering. To open the subject, I propose the following definitions:


  • Education: any experience that has a formative effect on the way a person thinks, feels and acts

  • Quality: “a customer’s perception of the value of a supplier’s product or service”

  • Quality education: “one that provides all learners with capabilities they require to be come economically productive, develop sustainable livelihoods, contribute to peaceful and democratic societies and enhance individual well-being”-New Zealand, (I am intrigued by this definition-Ed)


Part of the issue, I would argue, is the practice of educators not differentiating between education and the instructional services offered by schools, colleges and universities. Until we differentiate, there is little likelihood that the quality will improve.


Past considerations of quality have been based on education as “cognitive understanding and development”, current discussions of quality reflect the view of education’s contributions to each persons quality of life. The emerging view of education reflects a focus on sustainable human development, universal values including peace and security.”




recent and brief scan of the subject.

UNICEF (Defining Quality-2000)

A quality education is one where:

  • Learners who are healthy, well-nourished and ready to participate and learn, and supported in learning by their families and communities;

  • Environments that are healthy, safe, protective and gender-sensitive, and provide adequate resources and facilities;

  • Content that is reflected in relevant curricula and materials for the acquisition of basic skills, especially in the areas of literacy, numeracy and skills for life, and knowledge in such areas as gender, health, nutrition, HIV/AIDS prevention and peace.

  • Processes through which trained teachers use child-centred teaching approaches in well-managed classrooms and schools and skilful assessment to facilitate learning and reduce disparities

  • Outcomes that encompass knowledge, skills and attitudes, and are linked to national goals for education and positive participation in society


UNESCO 2017

General quality education system is one that:

  1. is effective for purposes,

  2. has enduring/sustained development relevance or responsiveness,

  3. is equitable,

  4. is resource efficient and

  5. translates into substantive rather than symbolic access’




OECD (PISA in Focus-2011)

Reports that:

1) Where schools have greater autonomy over what is taught and how students are assessed, students tend to perform better.


2.) In countries where schools account for their results by posting achievement data publicly, schools that enjoy greater autonomy in resource allocation tend to show better student performance than those with less autonomy.“


GLAZE REPORT (NS-2016)

An independent assessment service reporting directly to the public.



BEDARD, Mathieu, (2015, MEI Educational Think Tank), Pedagogical Autonomy)

Bedard quotes some international studies to the effect that “accountability and autonomy” jointly applied, are key factors in school achievement. Elsewhere in the article, Bedard writes that changes to accountability and autonomy would cost government nothing, would entail better academic results and allow education to better adapt to the diversity of student needs and aptitudes
