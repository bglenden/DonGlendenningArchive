During a visit to Royalty Centre and observation of the Learning Materials Distribution Centre (LMDC), Don poses critical questions about educational resource distribution and access. He questions whether shelves of materials are only available to teachers, whether differentiated resources exist for students ahead or behind grade-level peers, whether individual student/parent browsing and purchasing options exist (as in college/university bookstores), and whether education product production should be a private-sector function. The letter critiques the centralized, teacher-controlled, standardized distribution model as potentially limiting to student choice, differentiation, and consumer engagement. Reflects his broader concern that monopolistic, centralized systems inherently limit innovation and individual responsiveness.
Education Philosophy & Reform


July 7, 2015


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir: 

The educational pharmacy


I visited Royalty Centre a few days ago; it brought back many memories; after my meeting while strolling the corridors, I happened upon the LMDC (Learning Materials Distribution Centre).  I realize that it is a wholesale distribution centre but let me share som of my first thoughts:

Are the shelves and shelves of material only tor teachers

Given the spread in most grades, is it possible that there are resource materials  better suited for the child that is ahead or behind others in his or her class

I like to browse college and university book stores; I wonder if I’d be allowed to browse the LMDIC and purchase things I might like?

Isn’t the production of “education productsa natural role for the private sectorWould   

Sincerely,

Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
