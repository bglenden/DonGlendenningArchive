Don advocates for school autonomy based on PISA test evidence showing students in autonomous schools outperform those in centrally controlled systems. He proposes specific autonomy provisions: principals should control staffing decisions (recruitment, hiring, assignment, supervision), budget allocation reflecting local priorities, and professional development tailored to school needs. He argues autonomy improves not just test performance but school culture, engagement, and spirit. Don's position reflects his conviction that educational improvement requires devolving decision-making authority from central bureaucracies to school-level professionals closest to student needs.


January 2, 2013


Editor

Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

School autonomy



Recent studies show that students from schools with a high degree of autonomy do better, at least on PISA tests, than students in schools where the day to day operation is externally controlled. My own experience supports this view. I happen to believe that increased autonomy will result in a better education for students, even beyond that measured by success on tests.


Greater autonomy for schools could apply to many decisions but, as a minimum, should allow principals to;

  • Recruit, hire, assign, supervise and remove instructional staff.

  • Allocate funds within a budget to reflect the needs and priorities of students and the community served.

  • Organize professional development days that are tailored to meet the needs and activities of staff for which he/she has responsibility.


Placing the control of day to day activities with a school, would not only improve PISA test results, but, depending on the management style of the principal, would likely be a major energizer of school spirit and activity.


Your views on the matter of school autonomyare welcome at standards2020@gmail.com


Sincerely


Don Glendenning
