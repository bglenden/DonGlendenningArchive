Don defends teachers against widespread criticism, arguing that teachers are constrained by bureaucratic systems rather than inherently inferior. He catalogs restrictions: curriculum control, standardized testing schedules, prescribed texts, and undefined additional duties beyond teaching. He argues teachers are enveloped in "a cocoon of bureaucracy" that only employers and unions can change together, yet neither group demonstrates commitment to improvement. He advocates for professional development structures separate from union negotiations, as he observed in other provinces. This letter shows his empathy for teachers and focus on systemic constraints rather than individual blame.

April 2011


Editor 

Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

In Defence of Teachers


I was questioned recently about my support of teachers.  Although teachers bear the brunt of criticisms about schooling, as far as I am concerned, they are neither significantly better or worse at their jobs than any other occupational group.  


I might be less lenient if:


What, when and where they teach is controlled by their employer along with the texts to be used, testing schedule, content and nature of reports.

Their employer sets their entrance qualifications and certifies that they know how to teach, In addition to teaching, for which they trained and for which they were hired, teachers may be called on for playground duty, hall monitoring, fund raising and other duties.  

I’ve tried to locate a statemet of duties, a position description, so to speak but have not flound one yet.

It is likely that individual teachers have considerable control of how they  teach; this is determined, to a large degree by the nature and extent of their training.  How long they have to teach it, however, is a different question in that it all must be squeezed into an instructional period less any interruptions by the administrative staff.  



Individual teachers are enveloped in a cocoon of bureaucracy that can be changed, it appears, only when the employer and the union agree.  It is no wonder that we have frustrated and burned out teachers, and that instruction suffers as a result.  I see little evidence that ether party supports improvement.  While the teachers and thiir union could may support change, the responsibility for improvement rests with the employer.
