Open Education No. 68 (December 17, 2016), authored by Don Glendenning documenting emerging education innovations globally: British Columbia gradeless report cards; Ontario teacher math competency requirements; Canadian medical schools adopting competency-based education; university student advocates (especially for international students); tech companies hiring without degree requirements; single-student schools in NYC; school-without-walls models; independent qualification authorities (most developed countries except Canada/USA); democratic schools (Hanover High, NH); holistic rating scales; work colleges; full-service schools; independent student models. Don solicits additional Island innovations from readers, positioning Education 20/20 as a knowledge clearinghouse for education reform ideas. This demonstrates his commitment to learning from global experiments and adapting promising models to Canadian contexts.

WHAT’S GOING ON?


Picked up in passing:

  • Gradeless report cards in British Columbia

  • Teachers who cannot pass Grade 7 math exams in Ontario

  • Adoption of CBE in Canadian medical programs – Queens University being one of the most recent

  • Paid agents and advocates for students -primarily for foreign students at universities

  • Tech companies that do not require new hires to have diplomas and degrees – reported recently in the Washington Post

  • Schools for one in New York City

  • Schools without walls in a number of US cities

  • Independent qualification authorities – most developed countries other than Canada and the USA

  • Schools operating on democratic principles, Hanover high, New Hampshire

  • Holistic rating scales, PEI

  • Work colleges in the southern USA

  • Full service schools in Toronto

  • Independent student t the BC SAIL Academy

  • Incoming students at Jason Lee school join multi-grade advisory group and stay as a group through school.

  • Students at Bishop Carrol High School in Calgary have an academic advisor throughout their high school years.


Would be pleased to add other Island items to future lists






EDUCATION AWARD


A right to literacy


Do Islanders have a right to reasonable level of literacy? Do Islanders have a right to a reasonable level of instruction in literacy skills? Should helping Islanders reach a reasonable level of literacy be a goal of the Government of Prince Edward Island? On average, how long does it take an Islander to raise their reading level by one grade level? Should the first call aginst public education funds be in support of helping Islanders become literate?


The above questions arise from reding about court challenges but, more importantly, to understand the ”contract”. actual or implied, to each Islander.
