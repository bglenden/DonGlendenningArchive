Dear Editor:

Draft 3

Issues in Education


For some time now, I have engaged with Islanders in conversations about education; topics include learners, learning, continuing education, math, politicization, transition to employment, autonomy, accountability, and the list goes on. My conversations reveal a desire for information and dialogue, not just about school matters, but also about education - and how it can be improved.


At its simplest, learning refers to a person engaging in an activity intended to change the way they think, feel or act; teaching, on the other hand, is a process helping others change the way they think, feel or act. One’s education is the result of the experiences that have altered the ways that a person thinks, feels or acts. Having an education or being educated may be thought of as a person’s stage of development; people with a good education are better able to deal better with circumstances they encounter throughout their lives than persons with a limited education.  


Education is also a sector of the economy; a large and complex set of programs and services intended to help people learn; services such as information, instruction, counselling, finance, learning products, technology, quality assurance, governance and people.  Given a population of nearly 160,000 people, a Department of Education has potentially a large pool of clients needing or wanting help.


It appears to me that the meaning of education has narrowed since my arrival on PEI some 50 years ago; education is now thought of and treated as a product rather than a service. The emphasis has shifted to getting “a high school, college or university diploma or degree” rather than getting a teacher’s license, journeyman’s standing or engineering qualification; PISA results have replaced community ` `standards. Islanders frequently comment on the loss of PVI and PCVH.  One should also note the passing of a federal-provincial partnership in terms of the education of adults.


The results of my explorations call for review of the system – a glossary of common definitions, clearly stated program purposes, conceptual clarity, defined autonomy and accountability of individuals and institutions, increased collaboration between providers and users of education services, holistic or universal rating scales, more informative student transcripts and greater recognition of the importance of context.  


But let’s start with public conversations about our needs.  Let’s engage Islanders in conversation about what they need and want by way of education, develop a clear statement of the ideal education for both individuals and the Island Community, and the overarching principles that will guide its operation. Everything should be on the  table. The process should be led by local leaders assisted by outside specialists if needed.  Members of the Legislature perhaps?


Once the report is tabled, we should review the implementation process’


Sincerely


Don Glendenning
