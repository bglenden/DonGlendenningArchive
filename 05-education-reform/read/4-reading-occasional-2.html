EDUCATION 20/20 INC.

April 20, 2013

Learning to Read


NOTE: This paper was pulled together to provide background information for a discussion on reading. The document is based on the view that improving learning levels should start with a clear understanding of the nature of reading and the process of learning to read.



Our futures, as individuals and as a community, depend, to a large degree, on the nature and level of education available to, and actually attained by, our citizens. Education helps us to develop our individual potential to the fullest and prepares us for our many life roles (Oregon Education Act-1990) that of an individual, family member, learner, citizen, producer and consumer. Educating ourselves requires engaging in a variety of activities with a view to assembling a range of skills, knowledge and attitudes supportive of functioning in the various life roles for the remainder of our lives. Based on this thinking, the quality of one’s education would be measured by how well one is able to function in the various life roles.


One’s education is the result of everything that we learn in our lifetime that has had a formative effect on us. It starts at birth with some of our most important learning occurring in the first few year of life; skills learned at that time, to a large degree, determine what we become in later life. Learning is a partnership and nowhere is that partnership more important than in helping every citizen learn to read well. Reading is the one skill that undergirds and even unlocks life’s challenges and treasures.


Concern about reading is widespread. During the past few months I’ve talked about education with Islanders, teachers, retired teachers and the public at large, from Souris to Alberton and, invariably, they expressed concern about reading. And literacy is a frequent subject in our local media whether it be the Raise A Reader initiative, the work of the PEI literacy Alliance, initiatives by the Atlantic Ministers of Education, or the need for skilled workers. Reading is a community problem; it cannot be solved by schools alone. While informal reports suggest that reading levels in schools are improving, much remains to be done. Raising the reading levels of our citizens is a major undertaking and one worthy of our support.


When one learns, one changes behaviour; in simple terms, learning results in a change in attitude, skill and knowledge and often a combination of the three. We recognize that learning to read, and learning to read better, occurs in the context of learning to communicate generally and includes learning to listen, to write and to speak. We also believe, however, that it is important to understand each component separately, and have limited this paper to an examination of reading from a learner’s perspective.


Definition:

  • The ability to use printed information as meaningful language (Source unknown).

Statistics


The following articles say yes:

  • The Guardian, January 17, 2013 reported that “40 percent of Islanders haven’t aquired a level of literacy or numeracy to allow them to complete their high school studies.”

  • The Guardian, Sept 28, 2006: International tests place 15 year-olds in PEI lower than other Provinces in reading and writing.

  • The Guardian, Sept 28, 2006: Forty percent or 40,000 Islanders have skills so limited that they cannot deal with most printed materials such as newspapers or telephone books. Most can only read and understand simple language.

  • The Guardian, Sept 28, 2006: reading skills of many seasonal workers is lower than other workers. This restricts their ability to gain permanent employment.

  • The Guardian, Sept 28, 2006: About 80% of PEI seniors cannot read well enough to deal with everyday tasks, such as reading prescriptions

  • Globe and Mail Sept 15, 2009: “Children who aren’t reading well by the end of Grade 3 are at risk of dropping out or failing to graduate

  • Globe and Mail Sept 15, 2009: estimates that by the end of Grade 6, at least 30 percent of Canadian students can’t read or write well enough to succeed in school

  • New York Times, March 4, 2011: “About three-quarters of the 17,500 freshmen at the community colleges this year have needed remedial instruction in reading, writing or math, and nearly a quarter of the freshmen have required such instruction in all three subjects. In the past five years, a subset of students deemed “triple low remedial” — with the most severe deficits in all three subjects — has doubled, to 1,000.”

  • The New York times of January 3`, 2012 :”Nearly 80 percent of its 11th grade students (University City High School, Philadelphia) read below grade level in statewide tests this year, while 85 percent failed to make the grade in math.”

  • IALSS Study: Literacy rate in Canada did not change between 1994 and 2003

  • While literacy numbers are improving, literacy rates are not


What do readers do?


Based on the view that reading ability reflects what one can do and how well one can do it, I searched for and found a number of lists of reading outcomes, competencies, skills or expectations; none were as complete or comprehensive as I had hoped. The Atlantic Provinces cooperate on curriculum and the website for the Department of Education and early Childhood Development publishes curriculum guides for all high school subjects. I am not sure which guide best suits our purposes but, on the assumption that reading competencies or skills at the PEI Grade 12 level and those of adults would be roughly the same, I opted to include in this paper the reading and viewing component of the Language Arts graduation expectations. (Readers are also encouraged to visit the PEI Department of Education website).







The Atlantic Canada English Language Arts Curriculum

for Grades 12

  • read widely and experience a variety of literary genre and modes from different provinces and countries, and world literature from different literary periods

  • articulate their understanding of ways in which information texts are constructed for particular purposes

  • use the cueing systems and a variety of strategies to construct meaning in reading and viewing complex and sophisticated print and media texts

  • articulate their own processes and strategies in exploring ,interpreting, reflecting on sophisticated text and tasks

  • access, select, and research, in systematic ways, specific information to meet personal and learning needs, use the electronic network and other sources of information in ways characterized by complexity of purpose, procedure or subject matter, evaluate their research process

  • make informed personal responses to increasingly challenging print and media texts and reflect on their responses

  • articulate and justify points of view about texts and text elements

  • critically evaluate the information they access

  • show the relationships among language, topic, purpose, context and audience - note the relationship of specific elements of a particular text to elements of other texts, describe, discuss, and evaluate the language, ideas, and other significant characteristics of a variety of texts and genres

  • respond critically to complex and sophisticated texts, examine how texts work to reveal and produce ideologies, identities, and positions- examine how media texts construct notions of roles, behaviour, culture, and reality, examine how textual features help a reader and viewer to create meaning of the texts

Increasing reading levels


There are many possible causes of poor reading skills, some neurological, some environmental and some may be both but, without doubt, a rich environment in early life is a key to later reading success. Ideally, reading levels are improved when:

  • Education is important

  • Parents are readers

  • The community is engaged in the education process

  • Reading is stressed at school

  • Quality of schools

  • Reading skills are valued

  • Children have access to computers


Some Early signs of difficulty:

Parents or grandparents may be the first to notice signs of reading problem; early signs include trouble with:

  • Recognizing words that start with the same sound.

  • Letter and number symbols

  • Repeated ear infections

  • Associations between sounds and letters.

  • Pronouncing new words

  • Breaking words apart into sounds

  • Remembering the names and sounds of the letters

  • Skipping words in a sentence and doesn't stop to self-correct

  • Guessing rather than sounding out unknown words

  • Remembering names and sounds of letters

  • Rhyming

  • Reversing letters–such as writing "d" for "b" or "u" for "n"

  • Reversing words, such as "nap" for "pan"

  • Being reluctant to read, or read aloud
