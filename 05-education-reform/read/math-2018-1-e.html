Formal occasional paper (No. 8, June 1, 2018) addressing comprehensive mathematics education reform framework. Begins with Community Foundation of PEI youth survey (2013) identifying preparedness gaps and Education 20/20's subsequent engagement. Emphasizes mathematics as essential citizen-level skill learnable by all Islanders. Details instruction providers: public schools (mandatory annual instruction through graduation with grade-based achievement reporting and streaming), postsecondary institutions (UPEI programs and School of Mathematics and Computational Science), SkillsPEI (federally funded training), private schools (province-aligned), and independent learners. Establishes basic educational beliefs: learning as individual matter, group setting constraints, behavior-change resultants, teacher enablement roles, self-correction lack, education independence from school attendance, achievement over time emphasis, and non-grading educational functions. Details instructional support services including governance, information, learning products, competency maps, assessment criteria, rating scales, profiles, instruction, human resources, and learning opportunities. Documents field developments: MPHEC format changes, competency-based education trends, holistic rating scales, independent field analysis, school ungrading, independent qualification authorities, streaming disappearance, and lifelong education services. Presents ten system conclusions and comprehensive nine-part recommendation set addressing belief statements, competency-based outcomes, curriculum platforms, education term lexicons, profile identification, and learning guides. Details extensive benefits including employer tools, SkillsPEI platforms, job-seeker transcripts, counseling tools, provider communication, instructional relevance, worker upgrade guidance, and professional organization references.
Education Philosophy & Reform


MATHEMATICS EDUCATION

In PEI

Occasional Paper No 8 June 1, 2018

____________________________________________________________________________


In late 2013, The Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island (CFPEI) undertook a survey of over 200 Island youth to identify issues that directly affected them; the report, Taking the Pulse of Island Youth, available on the Foundation’s website at www.cfpei.ca, provides a snapshot of the findings. Bright spots include a positive sense of community, emerging entrepreneurial spirit, volunteerism and new postsecondary offerings. The main concerns include a lack of jobs, surviving on low-paying jobs, educational deficiencies and drug and alcohol addictions. On the subject of education and learning, the study reports a high rate of completion of high school and postsecondary education, a high level of inclusivity and relatively affordable tuition costs; young people also expressed concerns about their readiness for the next stage of life including:


“preparedness gaps with regard to readiness for

postsecondary study and the workplace” – Vital Signs, CFPEI, 2013


Following the publication of the CFPEI Report, Education 20/20 organized six public discussions for the purpose of informing the community of the report and its findings, learning more about youth issues in our communities, exploring issues of education and learning, and to identify actions to be taken to close gaps. Discussions were lively and wide ranging and carried on well after formal sessions ended. Education 20/20 retained an interest in the subject and was encouraged to resume discussions with providers and users of educational services, especially following the report of the Council of Ministers in July of 2016, which said


“A student’s ability to transition from secondary school to post-secondary education and the labour market is a key measure of the education system’s success for students” –

CMEC, July 2016


Since then, transition through education has been the subject of a number of informal discussions with educators, employers, students and the community at large. It is the writer’s view that, at the minimum, there is an issue of communication among parties involved or interested in the subject, a communication gap resulting from the number of service providers, lack of joint planning and limited ongoing coordination.


Mathematics is the study of the science of number, quantity and space; it is in demand to some degree by all citizens, employers, institutions and the community at large. The Oregon Department of Education contends that math provides “a foundation for the study of science and technology as well as the interpretation of quantitative information in other subjects. Mathematics may be structured in a number of ways but structure does not appear to have a particular influence on the quality of instruction.


Math is an essential skill needed to some level by all citizens and can be learned to the citizen level by all or practically all Islanders. Furthermore, helping citizens acquire math skills for citizenship is one of the key roles of our public-school system but there appears to be no agreement, however, as to the nature and level of citizen math and how it should be determined.


MATH EDUCATION

A person’s education begins early; the ability to count is often quoted in reference to a child starting school and is often used by parents to support a view that their child is “bright”. At the same time, many parents, unintentionally I am sure, encourage the view that math is difficult, and the literature tells us that a person’s beliefs about mathematics can have a substantial impact on their interest, their enjoyment and their motivation in math classes.


Providers of math instruction: in PEI include:

  1. The public-school system plays a key role in increasing and formalizing each student’s knowledge of math. The school system accepts all children and not only provides but requires math instruction each year through to graduation. Math to be learned is identified for each grade and achievement in the time available is reported to parents and the public alike. Performance across course offerings has become a screening mechanism in the transition process.



  1. Postsecondary institutions provide math instruction in support of programs or majors generally; in addition, UPEI offers a math major and most recently created a School of Mathematics and Computational Science.



  1. SkillsPEI is a federally funded agency that purchases training under federal criteria. The writer is unclear as to how instructional content is determined or achievement measured Instruction in math may be provided by provincial schools and institutions, by employers and their organizations or NGO’s on behalf of clients. Content is determined or agreed upon at time of purchase. Except for adult upgrading, there appears to be little coordination of math content across courses. It doesn’t appear that NGO operators can or do tap into available provincial instructional resources prepared elsewhere in the system

  2. Private schools offer instruction with content being aligned with the relevant provincial curriculum.

  3. Individuals may study and improve their math skills on their own or with the help of family, friends, tutors, on line courses and other personal learning strategies. Independent learners lack a simple mechanism for demonstrating their proficiency levels


Outcomes and competencies themselves are “gradeless” meaning that that any competency can be studied in public school, college and university or independently. What changes is the level achieved on each outcome. As to proficiency levels, there appears to be some agreement that differences in math levels may be attributed to three factors:

• The number of skills that can be demonstrated

• Levels achieved on skills demonstrated, and

• Nature or degree of clustering of skills learned.


Grade levels in the public-school system are established by Department of Education; individual student proficiency levels are awarded by teachers. Levels in postsecondary institutions are set in the institution. This in spite of the fact that awarding levels is a regulatory and not a teaching function. Furthermore, in the public domain regulatory functions are usually the responsibility of a separate body.


BASIC EDUCATIONAL BELIEFS

Consistent decision making requires a set of basic beliefs about learners and learning; this paper is based on the view that:

  1. Learning is an individual matter

  2. Although groups do not learn, individuals may learn in a group setting.

  3. Learning results in a change of behaviour.

  4. The role of a teacher is to help students change behaviour;

  5. The school system lacks a self- correcting mechanism

  6. People are educated whether or not they attend school

  7. Education should emphasize achievement not time served

  8. Grading is not an educational function


TYPICAL INSTRUCTIONAL SUPPORT SERVICES:

  1. Governance and leadership

  2. Current and independent information

  3. Learning products and technology

  4. A map, overview or inventory of competencies that collectively describe the life role

  5. Assessment criteria

  6. A rating scale that can be applied to each competency.

  7. Profiles that show the range and nature competences and level of performance associated with satisfactory performance in one’s life roles.

  8. Instruction

  9. Human resources

  10. Opportunities and Incentives to learn at higher levels or in a shorter period of time


DEVELOPMENTS IN THE FIELD

The educational literature reveals a number of changes or trends likely to impact math instruction in PEI:

  • Format changes being adopted by the MPHEC.

  • General trend towards competency-based education

  • Emergence of dynamic/ holistic rating scales,

  • Field analysis as an independent step in the curriculum development process.

  • Ungrading of schools.

  • Independent qualification authorities.

  • Disappearance of streaming

  • Education as a life long service.







CONCLUSIONS

Based on the literature, the above noted discussions and the writer’s experience, the writer concludes that:

  1. The transition issue cannot be solved by adjustments to the public-school system alone

  2. Math instruction in PEI is fragmented

  3. Grading students is not an instructional or school function

  4. Cooperation among providers is hampered by the lack of agreed- upon definitions of a number of key concepts.

  5. Broadly speaking, education lacks an overarching statement of philosophy and direction;

  6. There is no formal mechanism for coordination among math providers

  7. Evaluation criteria for math should be the same for all levels of education.

  8. The way content is described differs from provider to provider.

  9. Improving transition requires cooperation of all math instruction providers.

  10. There is an interest among math providers in in cooperating on a pilot project


Recommendations:

  1. Develop an over-arching statement of beliefs and mission on education in PEI as a basis of decision-making

  2. Replace Curriculum Based Outcomes with a Competency Based outcomes.

  3. Adopt a common platform for curriculum development in mathematics to include field analysis, evaluation criteria and rating scale

  4. Prepare a lexicon of relevant education terms – outcomes, competencies, rating scale, etc.

  5. Identify key profiles i.e. citizenship, employment entry and entry to post secondary institutions

  6. Develop learning guides for each competency.

BENEFITS:

The benefits of adopting the above changes include:

  • An additional tool for employers when planning, hiring and promoting

  • A platform for negotiations between SkillsPEI and providers

  • A transcript for job seekers

  • Counselling tool for employers and institutions

  • Improved communication among providers and users

  • Increased relevance of what is taught

  • Clearer understanding of math and instruction process

  • A tool to assist in the planning customized training

  • Reduction in confusion off changing schools

  • A tool for institutions when planning math courses

  • Guidance for workers/learners needing/wanting to upgrade smath skills

  • A reference for trade and professional organizations when organizing upgrading courses
