A Quality Education System


INTRODUCTION



Education is a key factor in determining one's quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of our citizens will determine to a significant degree the future of our community and country.  Because of its importance,  provincial governments have invested heavily in the provision of educational support services for all our citizens but especially for our youth.  The system has served us well, however, as a result of the many changes occurring around us, not the least being the ever expanding information technology, there is a growing gap between what Islanders have and what they want in the way of education - and lslanders want meaningful engagement in narrowing the gap.


This paper is focused, not on the philosophy of education but rather the characteristics of an education system that ensures quality; it draws heavily but not exclusively on recent reports published by the OECD and UNICEF. 



Quality in education refers to the fitness of education for proposed purposes; quality is important in helping PEI achieve economic goals, maintaining a level of trust, protecting consumers, attracting students, attract industry, meeting increased expectations and in attracting and retaining a mobile workforce.

CHARACTERISTICS OF A QUALITY EDUCATION SYSTEM



1 Learners who are healthy, well-nourished and ready to participate


We show concern for our children’s health through providing nutritious food, plenty of rest, safe surroundings and medical attention when needed; in so doing, we are not only ensuring good health today but helping to develop good health habits for tomorrow.  The same applies to education; good physical, mental, emotional health and social wellness make a positive contribution to learning.

2 Environments that are positive, safe and respectful


Both teachers and students, need to feel socially, emotionally, intellectually, and physically safe. People need to feel safe to be respectful to others. If we feel unsafe, we will naturally focus on protecting ourselves; we won't be able to listen to and appreciate others.

respectful schools are, by definition, democratically informed learning environments where people feel safe, supported, engaged, and helpfully challenged.—respectful in the context of our social, emotional, civic, and intellectual interactions.

A sustainable, positive school climate fosters youth development and the learning necessary for a productive and satisfying life in a democratic society. In such a climate, 

  • People are engaged and respected.
  • Students, families, and educators work together to develop and contribute to a shared school vision.
  • Educators model and nurture an attitude that emphasizes the benefits and satisfaction of learning.
  • Each person contributes to the operation of the school and the care of the physical environment (National School Climate Council, 2007).

3 Content that is relevant with an emphasis on the skills of citizenship.


With a focus on rigourous and relevant curriculum: 

teaching materials and assessment strategies are aligned with the curriculum’s rigour and relevance 

assignments focus on the application of concepts and skills in a real-world context 

teachers support and encourage students to meet the high standards set for them 

teachers keep track of and share strategies that work.

7 Teachers who are professional, autonomous and accountable

Sherren, a columnist and specialist in management effectiveness who was born in PEI, says that employees are “responsible for their actions, behaviours, attitudes, decisions, work and performance.”  Responsibility means taking ownership and corrective actions.  Accountability, on the other hand, is described by Sherren as “the process of making, keeping and managing promises and expectations… being liable or answerable for the overall outcomes.”  Although the article makes no mention of schools, from his article one can conclude that teachers are responsible for seeing that students are taught.  On the other hand, principals, as instructional leaders, are accountable for students getting what they came to school for, the skills needed in order to gain entrance to postsecondary studies or employment. Of course, students and parents also have responsibilities for success in school.



8 Assessment that is independent and criterion referenced

Teachers assess student performance for two reasons; the first is to determine the extent to which learned what they were taught and, secondly to provide the basis for planning what to do next.  How well one student does in comparison to another is generally is only of educational concern if it can be used to plan the next step. Student performance is assessed tor two reasons, one iAssessment and Critical to criterion referenced assessment is an evaluation framework that includes are the analysis of what is to be learned, specification of performance levels, nature of testing, approach to evaluation, benchmarks and reporting. 

Training programs developed in CRI format tend to be self-paced courses involving a variety of different media (e.g., workbooks, videotapes, small group discussions, computer-based instruction). Students learn at their own pace and take tests to determine if they have mastered a module. A course manager administers the program and helps students with problems. 

CRI is based upon the ideas of mastery learning and performance-oriented instruction. It also incorporates many of the ideas found in Gagne's coditions of learning (e.g., task hierarchies, objectives) and is compatible with most theories of adult learning (e.g., andragogy, experiential learning) because of its emphasis on learner initiative and self-management


  • Instructional objectives are derived from job performance and reflect the competencies (knowledge/skills) that need to be learned. 
  • Students study and practice only those skills not yet mastered to the level required by the objectives. 
  • Students are given opportunities to practice each objective and obtain feedback about the quality of their performance. 
  • Students should receive repeated practice in skills that are used often or are difficult to learn. 
  • Students are free to sequence their own instruction within the constraints imposed by the pre-requisites and progress is controlled by their own competence (mastery of objectives).
