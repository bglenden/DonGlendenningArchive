From October 14, 2014, Don recounts a student's life-altering decision to accept a lower-level math course recommendation, dramatically reducing college/university prospects and likely lifetime income. He argues schools should open doors, not close them. Presented as a concrete case study, the letter shows Don's emotional investment in individual student outcomes and his conviction that well-intentioned teacher decisions can have devastating consequences when they narrow rather than expand student possibilities.
Education Philosophy & Reform

October 14, 2014

Editor, Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

Closing doors


I didn’t have a happy Thanksgiving, in fact most of the past month has been stressful.  The cause of my stress is an event in the life of a student, a person I have never met but whose family I know and whose future has been limited because of a teacher’s professional decision.  I’ve heard similar stories before, but this time I actually “experienced” it.  Granted, I do noty know the whole story but here’s my ubderstanding.


A high school student, with hopes, even plans to go to college or university, was having difficulty with math; that’s not unusual, I am told that struggling with math is not uncommon.   So what are a school’s options?

  • Let the a student struggle and likely fail a grade, 
  • Offer an upgrading course after graduation
  • Provide extra instruction
  • Transfer to the student a lower level course
  • Blame the home


The teacher recommended number 4; the student took the teacher’s advice.  This is a life-altering event in that it dramatically reduces the likelihood of that  student going to college or university, reduces the chance of a smooth transition to employment and  likely significantly reduce his lifetime income.  


Schools should open doors not close them. 



Sincerely


Don Glendenning

A long time student of education
