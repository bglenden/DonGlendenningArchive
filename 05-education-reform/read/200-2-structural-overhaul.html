Newsletter (August 20, 2020) proposing specific structural overhauls following institutional structural analysis framework. Don advocates: granting schools operational autonomy (per OECD PISA research showing better outcomes), organizing departments around functions (governance, instruction, information, counselling, products, human resources, quality assurance) rather than hierarchies, distinguishing education from schooling (World Bank perspective), applying Mathieu Bedard's "accountability and autonomy jointly applied" principle, and separating professional from administrative roles (hospital model). These targeted recommendations reflect his systematic, evidence-based reform approach grounded in international comparative research.

Open Education

No. 95 200-2 August 20, 2020

STRUCTURAL OVERHAUL

A few years ago, I chaired a half dozen public sessions across PEI about the education concerns of youth. Sometimes, discussions continued long after sessions closed. After one session, a side discussion between two trustees turned to “structural issues”. A recent reading of a World Bank report on education, although not using the term “structural”, reminded me of trustee comments and prompted this letter.


Structural includes policies, procedures, organization, culture, and arrangements within the education framework. Many structural impediments, it seems, result from embedding the educational delivery process in a government department rather than designing a framework that allows education services to flourish.


That’s not to downplay the important leadership role of government but rather to confirm that the structure accommodates and supports education services:

To be specific:

  1. The OECD tells us that students from schools with autonomy in their day to day operation, do better on PISA tests. So why not give schools the autonomy they need?

  2. I was taught that, when organizing, form follows function. My reading suggests that education functions include governance, instruction, information, counselling, education products, human resources and quality assurance. The longstanding guideline seems not to apply to education.

  3. Recently the World Bank called attention to the fact that Ministries of Education often confuse going to school with getting an education. It appears that our Department has fallen into the same trap.

  4. Mathieu Bedard of the Montreal Economic Institute attributes autonomy and accountability “jointly applied” as key factors in school achievement; is this something we should try?

  5. Position titles should reflect the task. It is interesting that hospitals separate professional and administrative roles; perhaps it's time for education to do the same.


See Newsletter No. 13 for other suggestions.


Don Glendenning, Editor
