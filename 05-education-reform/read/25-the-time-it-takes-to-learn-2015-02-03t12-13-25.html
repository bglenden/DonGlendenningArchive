Newsletter article (Open Education No. 25) by Don Glendenning revisiting the issue of credential devaluation and arguing for alternatives to traditional diplomas and degrees. He contends that schools function as learning support institutions and should avoid ranking students (creating conflicts of interest), instead providing transcripts and allowing gap-filling when needed.

Open Education

25 February 15, 2015

…employers lack good objective information on the academic competencies

of young job applicants - Bishop


Alternative credentialing


Alternative credentialing is replacing school and college diplomas and degrees, and yes, it’s happening on PEI. In fact, I am aware of workers heading west carrying with them test results from the Holland College Assessment Centre and I’ve even suggested that employers do the same when hiring locally. This development raises a number of issues for institutions.


  • Diplomas and degrees are credentials; they attest to what a person studied rather than what they know and can do. Obviously they carry some weight and prestige but personally, I cannot ever asking an employee if he had one; transcripts are another matter altogether.

  • Schools, colleges and universities are places one goes to get help with learning. Asking institutions and teachers to sort or rank their students constitutes a conflict of interest. Teachers may use the results of ranking but they shouldn’t rank.

  • There is no evidence of which I am aware that supports one pattern of courses over another as the best preparation for postsecondary education or life.


Simply put. We could scrap most of the requirements, encourage students to learn a variety of things, provide a transcript of what they learned and, if they find out they have not learned enough of the right things, invite them bac and help fill the gaps.
