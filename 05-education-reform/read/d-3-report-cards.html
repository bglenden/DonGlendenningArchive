Don reflects on his old report cards and proposes that report cards should focus on future-oriented information exchange between school and family, similar to a follow-up doctor's visit. Rather than merely reporting past achievements, report cards should identify what needs to happen next to help learners meet their objectives. He advocates for parents to access course outcomes from the Department of Education website and suggests students should receive copies of learning outcomes. The letter addresses parent concerns about jargon and proposes that criterion-referenced reporting, with proper explanation, can serve families better than simple grades. His example of doctor-patient communication reflects his inclination toward professional service models.

June 24, 2013


Editor 

Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:


What should a report card report?


I came across some of my old report cards recently;  by the way, they weren’t as good as I remembered (or as I told my children).  They were the traditional report cards of the day with a numerical grade for each subject, record of absences and a mark for deportment.  They must have provided  the information my mother needed because she signed them  and I took them back to the teacher.   But what really triggered this letter was a report about changes proposed for reporting systems in BC.  I also recall some media coverage last year about report cards in Nova Scotia. 


Reports should be viewed as a part of information exchange between two of the key players in the education process – that is, other than the learner, of course.  It is like a return visit to your doctor’s office to report on progress, exchange information,  and identify new strategies, if any.  


The report cards I’ve seen tend to, as the name implies, report on what has happened.  While that is important, the real value of the report and the ensuing discussion, is to focus on the future.  What needs to be done next to ensure that the learner will meet his/her objectives?  To move on to the next grade?  To obtain an acceptable level of general education?  To graduate?  To meet college and university entrance requirements/  to meet job entry requirements? 


Parents should be encouraged to view course outcomes available on the Department of education website; one article I read even suggested that parents, and eveh high school students, be given copies of actual outcomes for each course as an aid to learning.


In the meantime, if you have trouble understanding your child’s report card, have a conversation with your principal; if you  are not clear on the mening of criterion referenced, I’m sure that your principal could help with that also.


In the meantime, your reports are always welcome at  HYPERLINK "mailto:standards2020@gmail.com" standards2020@gmail.com. 


Sincerely


Don Glendenning
