CONCEPTUAL CLARITY

The literature speaks of the need for educational reform; I suggest that we ee it as a plea for clarity. it’s time for a new focus on the teaching- task, a clarification and realignment of the focus and framework that engages us. It’s a recognition that the task is changing and so must our approach in supporting it. Here is my take on the matter:

  1. Terminology – The education literature needs to be read carefully. No word has a simple meaning; every word seems to have multiple meanings or, at least multiple shadings of the same meaning. In an endeavour as large and complex as education, It is likely that shades of meanings exist. A PEI lexicon would be very helpful.

  2. Education services - Education is a service; typical ducation functions or services include: information, counselling, instruction, products and technology, quality assurance. governance, funding and human resources. Learners may need one or all services at any point in their education journey. Services need to be clearly identified and available to all Island citizens not just to citizens enrolled in schools and programs. How services are organized and paid for is a separate issue..

  3. Education versus Schooling – The World Bank, the largest funder of public education, cautioned Ministries of Education about treating “education and going to school” as though they are one and the same. I also encounter this view in my conversations and in the PEI education literature. A school, like a hospital is only a resource- rich location; education results from experiences gained while there.

  4. A Vision is a public statement of an organization’s purposes. ideals, core values and long-term objective and goals; a vision statement usually also lays out beliefs and guiding principles. Although PEI has statements of philosophy and goals, they lack sufficient detail to set direction or measure progress.


The above issues are not new but improvement in education rests on their resolution.
