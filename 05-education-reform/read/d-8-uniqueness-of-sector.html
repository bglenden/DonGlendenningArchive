Don references Donald Savoie's book and a New York Times article about French schools' "stifling hierarchy, micromanagement, centralized decision-making," noting similar dynamics in Canadian education. He identifies education as uniquely problematic: it is a mandatory monopoly (required participation ages 0-18/20), functions as a formula-driven service-delivery system designed at provincial level, and lacks mechanisms for checking, balancing, or responding to community needs. Lists systemic barriers: focus solely on knowledge, payment based on qualifications, rigid grading systems, insufficient skills emphasis, predetermined learning time, and absence of checks and balances. The letter treats education's structural characteristics as distinctive problems requiring systemic reform beyond typical service-sector issues.
Education Philosophy & Reform


July 7, 2015


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir: 

Uniqueness of the education sector

A recent article in The New York Times referred to the “stranglehold on French Schools” resulting in a rapid decline in academic performance and growing social equality, all stemming from a “stifling hierarchy, micromanagement centralized decision making”.   These are strong words but the sentiment applies to education in Canada as well.

The article caused me to reflect on some of the features in our own education system that have become barriers to change. Too large an issue for a letter to the editor but at least, open the conversation.

Not only is education a monopoly but every one is required to participate, especially in the first 18 ton 20 years.

While educating is a developmental activity, the education system has become a tightly controlled system of delivering services developed in the Holman building in formuls drivenSummerside.

Focus on knowledge, Pay by qualifications, grading system, lack of skills, exams

Predetermining time available for learning

Grading system

No checks and balances



Sincerely,

Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
