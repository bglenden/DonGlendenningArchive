December 2019 letter to The Guardian signed by Don Glendenning reflecting on school board re-establishment in PEI. References Patrick Watson's 'The Day the Schools Disappeared' and advocates for autonomy, accountability, and rethinking education beyond institutional schooling models—consistent with Don's lifelong education reform philosophy.
Education Philosophy & Reform

December 11, 2019



Editor:

Guardian and Journal Pioneer

Trustees 



I was delighted to read that plans are afoot to re-establish community based school boards; the political benefits are obvious; the likely improvements to education are less clear and will be determined by what Boards are mandated and allowed to do.  The OECD, which gave us PISA assessments, tells us that PISA results are better when schools have autonomy and accountability for their day to day operation.  So, if improvement is the reason for establishing school boards, not only should we delegate to School Boards, we should also delegate much of the day to day operation of schools to schools themselves.  While I generally agree, such a move by itself is unlikely to improve the quality of public education. 


Some 50 years ago, I sat on the top step in the Confederation Centre theatre during a PEI Teachers’ convention and listened to Patrick Watson describe what happened “The Day the Schools Disappeared”. Fifty years later, my mind is still stirred by the possibilities.  Watson led his audience through a series of reactions and events of citizens as they grappled  with events and set  course of action.  We, like Watson, should revisit the question; unfortunately, however, I was unable to secure a copy a copy of Watson’s text.


Education in Prince Edward Island could benefit from some structural changes.T




Education is messy, institutions are not enough; squeezing education into schools distorts education services and may be likened to delivering all health services through hospitals.  Communities suffer. It’s time, even past time, to rethink our practises.



Given all the changes in the world we inhabit, it’s time to revisit the subject.  Doing, may reveal new roles, even for trustees


Sincerely


Don Glendenning

A long -time student of education
