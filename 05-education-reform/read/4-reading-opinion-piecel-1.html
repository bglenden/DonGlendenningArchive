Dated May 22, 2013, this letter to Charlottetown Guardian editor expresses Don's public advocacy for reading improvement as key skill. He articulates concern about reading's state through parents paying both taxes and private tuition, children reading below grade level, employers seeking skilled workers, and seniors endangered by inability to read medicine labels. Don challenges complacency, noting 42 percent of Canadian adults lack sufficient literacy for modern employment. Rather than assigning blame to systemic causes (literacy silos, staffing, resources, curriculum), he advocates for expert problem-solving, citing British Columbia's appointment of a Superintendent of Reading. Don calls reading a pandemic problem and literacy "the most valuable of school subjects," emphasizing most people can learn to read with proper support. He positions himself as inviting feedback (standards2020@gmail.com) and advocates trusting educators' expertise while addressing coordination gaps among providers (school system, post-secondary, community organizations). The piece reflects Don's public engagement on education issues and practical problem-solving orientation.

Reading: the key skill



Concern about the state of reading is widespread. Parents and grandparents paying both taxes and private school tuition, parents with children reading below grade level, employers seeking or just having hired skilled workers and seniors whose health is endangered by their inability to read labels on medicine bottles. Then there are the hard statistics; 42 percent of adult Canadians not sufficiently literate to hold employment in a modern economy and a static literacyrate for the past decade in spite of significant investment.


Some people ask where the fault lies; I don’t know and even if we knew, fixing the blame doesn’t solve the problem. The fault may lie in the literacy silos, that is the variety of agencies providing literacy training but re;porting through different agencies, the Department of Education, Department of advanced education, Holland College, UPEI, and several other agencies. Or it could be staff, learning resources, class time, counseling. Or it could be vocabulary, fluency, ability to decode or any one of a number of causes.


I believe the reading problem is pandemic; if it were a health problem we would call out or chief medical officer, In education? Announce a new initiative and hope people will forget about it. British Columbia has appointed a Superintendent of reading.A more effective approach, I suggest is to ask the people in the trenches; or betterstill, give them the task and get out of their way.


I believe that most people can learn to read, literacy generally and reading in particular is the most valuable of school subjects, and well honed reading skills are tools of lifelong learning.


Literacy of our citizens; let’s put it on our priority list and read our way into a brighter future.


And, as usual, I’d welcome your feedback at standards2020@gmail.com

Sincerely

the mother ouring the past few months I’ve talked about education with Islanders, teachers, retired teachers and the public at large, from Souris to Alberton and, invariably, they expressed concern about reading. And literacy is a frequent subject in our local media whether it be the Raise A Reader initiative, the work of the PEI literacy Alliance, initiatives by the Atlantic Ministers of Education, or the need for skilled workers. Reading is a community problem; it cannot be solved by schools alone. While informal reports suggest that reading levels in schools are improving, much remains to be done. Raising the reading levels of our citizens is a major undertaking and one worthy of our support.


When one learns, one changes behaviour; in simple terms, learning results in a change in attitude, skill and knowledge and often a combination of the three. We recognize that learning to read, and learning to read better, occurs in the context of learning to communicate generally and includes learning to listen, to write and to speak. We also believe, however, that it is important to understand each component separately, and have limited this paper to an examination of reading from a learner’s perspective.

What do readers do?


Based on the view that reading ability reflects what one can do and how well one can do it, I searched for and found a number of lists of reading outcomes, competencies, skills or expectations; none were as complete or comprehensive as I had hoped. The Atlantic Provinces cooperate on curriculum and the website for the Department of Education and early Childhood Development publishes curriculum guides for all high school subjects. I am not sure which guide best suits our purposes but, on the assumption that reading competencies or skills at the PEI Grade 12 level and those of adults would be roughly the same, I opted to include in this paper the reading and viewing component of the Language Arts graduation expectations. (Readers are also encouraged to visit the PEI Department of Education website).

Increasing reading levels


There are many possible causes of poor reading skills, some neurological, some environmental and some may be both but, without doubt, a rich environment in early life is a key to later reading success. Ideally, reading levels are improved when:

  • Education is important

  • Parents are readers

  • The community is engaged in the education process

  • Reading is stressed at school

  • Quality of schools

  • Reading skills are valued

  • Children have access to computers


Some Early signs of difficulty:

Parents or grandparents may be the first to notice signs of reading problem; early signs include trouble with:

  • Recognizing words that start with the same sound.

  • Letter and number symbols

  • Repeated ear infections

  • Associations between sounds and letters.

  • Pronouncing new words

  • Breaking words apart into sounds

  • Remembering the names and sounds of the letters

  • Skipping words in a sentence and doesn't stop to self-correct

  • Guessing rather than sounding out unknown words

  • Remembering names and sounds of letters

  • Rhyming

  • Reversing letters–such as writing "d" for "b" or "u" for "n"

  • Reversing words, such as "nap" for "pan"

  • Being reluctant to read, or read aloud
