Characteristics of public education:

In putting forth this project, the proposer is aware of the following characteristics of the education enterprise, including:

Silo nature of schools:  The nature of the education enterprise reinforces silo practices, time based instructional segments, age based grouping, clearly defined Institutional boundaries, single teacher led classrooms, and laboratory based curriculum development, The most readily identifiable educational silos on PEI include the public school system, the university, private schools, colleges (2), literacy training, continuing education, and the many “NGO” education and training projects.  Government documents clearly show that the role of the Department of Education is limited to public instruction thereby making the Premier the actual Minister of Education.  


Inconsistencies on curriculum alignment:  refers to the practice of ensuring that each element of education supports the program as a whole. Alignment starts with a clear mission statement, attainable and measurable goals, relevant curriculum, appropriate instructional methodology, unbiased assessment and accurate reporting.




Subject centered resources and instruction.  Education is a very personal and individual developmental experience; school instruction, on the other hand, is a product delivery system with the product development by one professional and the delivery by another. Neither does the system give sufficient attention to student development in the affective and psychomotor domains. There is little inclination or opportunity for customizing at the point where student and teacher meet.  The literature, leaders and the community call for change but, at least to date, there is little evidence that the call is being heard.


Recognized standards and assessment reveal confusion between grades and standards, the lack of criterion referenced standards and some confusion as to the role of teachers in the evaluation process.  A review of alignment is long overdue.

To develop a simple curriculum map that identifies a set of skills as being those required to function as a citizen, to enter postsecondary institutions or enter employment; such to become a common master lists for use by students, teachers, employers and postsecondary institutions.  Our proposal has several innovative features including (1) a curriculum map to be used by teachers and learners alike, it also ensures that students and teachers have the same view of the content and process, to ensures that common map to be used by learners and teachers alike so that students and teachers have the same view of the process, (2) it to a single analysis with multiple profiles instead of the current approach of creating a map for each grade, (3) engaging s broad range of users and providers in the curriculum development process and (4) encourages  all educators to see themselves as a team.

Education 20/20 Inc.

Preparedness 2- Skills of Citizenship

Introduction:

In late 2013, The Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island (CFPEI) undertook a survey of over 200 Island youth to identify issues that directly affect them; the report, Taking the Pulse of Island Youth, available on the Foundation’s website at  HYPERLINK "http://www.cfpei.ca" www.cfpei.ca, provides a snapshot of the findings.  Bright spots include a positive sense of community, emerging entrepreneurial spirit, volunteerism and new postsecondary offerings. 


The main concerns include a lack of jobs, surviving on low-paying jobs, educational deficiencies and drug and alcohol addictions.  On the subject of education and learning, the study reports a high rate of completion of high school and postsecondary education, a high level of inclusivity and relatively affordable tuition costs; young people also  expressed concerns about their readiness for the next stage of life including: 

“preparedness gaps with regard to readiness for

post- secondary study and the workplace” – Vital Signs, CFPEI, 2013

Following the publication of the CFPEI Report, Education 20/20 organized five public discussions for the purpose of informing the community of the report and its findings, learning more about youth issues in our communities, exploring issues of education and learning, and to identify actions to be taken to close gaps.  Discussions were lively and wide ranging, and carried on well after the formal session ended.   The final report included a number of conclusions and recommendations of which the recommendation below triggered this proposal. 

Further study be undertaken to clarify the gaps in preparation for further study and for work - (Preparedness,  Education 20/20 Inc., Occasional Paper No. 1)

Education 20/20 has continued its exploration under the title, “citizenship skills” meaning those required of any citizens and considered to be a basic foundation for life changes that are likely to occur including skills needed for entrance into employment and expected for acceptance into postsecondary studies.  As evidence I refer to PISA test results for Prince Edward Island; a 2013 survey of issues of Island youth conducted by the Community Foundation; a presentation to a Standing Committee of the PEI Legislative Assembly by the Charlottetown Area chamber of Commerce; How Canada Performs, a Provincial and Territorial Ranking, Education and Training; Education and Skills, and a series of workshops on Preparedness held throughout PEI by Education 20/20 Inc.  The PEI Department of Education has not taken exception to the findings nor, to the best of our knowledge, assumed a leadership role that includes corrective action.  Based on our consultations, a pilot in the area of math has the most likely chance of success.

Based on our explorations, it is the view of Education 20/20 that:

The math gap is real

Low basic skill levels on PEI are an issue

Low basic skill levels are dragging down the economy of PEI

Basic skill levels are not consistently defined

Skills thought to have been learned while in high school, are being taught again in Adult Basic Education program at Holland College. 

PEI lacks a master list of recognized basic skills

PEI lacks a single body to give direction to its education efforts

Basic skills do not appear to be a priority
