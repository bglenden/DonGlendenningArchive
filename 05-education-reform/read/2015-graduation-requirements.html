Don commends the Department's review of graduation requirements and the Chambers of Commerce for advocating literacy standards, but questions whether research supports rigid graduation requirements. He notes that the Secondary Literacy Assessment requires students to write but not pass the exam, uses results for teacher improvement rather than student accountability, and doesn't solicit feedback from employers or postsecondary institutions despite their expressed concerns. The letter shows Don's attention to policy details and his belief that requirements should be evidence-based and stakeholder-informed.
Education Philosophy & Reform

January 6, 2015


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

Graduation requirements   


First I want to commend the Department for its ongoing review of graduation requirements; I also commend the Charlottetown and Summerside Chambers of Commerce for their literacy needs to the Departments attention.  As for the course changes, I understand that courses need to be revised and updated from time to time but my reading of the literature suggests that research does not support our ridgid graduation requirements. 


I find the wording of the section the Secondary Literacy Assessment to be very interesting.  It says that students must write the exam  but does not say they must pass it, results will be used to help teachers rather than students, and there is no mention of soliciting suggestions from the Chambers or postsecondary institutions although their concerns seem to have triggered the change. 

Sincerely,


Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
