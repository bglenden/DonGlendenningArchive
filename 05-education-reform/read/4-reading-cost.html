Research document on reading instruction economics and comprehensive reading improvement strategy. Analyzes per-student schooling cost ($10,000/year) as insufficient metric without understanding cost breakdown. Notes research suggesting 35-40 hours tutoring needed to raise reading level one grade at $2,000 cost per level. Cites statistics on magnitude of problem: 42% of adults below level 3 literacy, Canadian students performing below national average on reading tests, 30% of Grade 6 students unable to read adequately, community college students requiring remediation. Proposes comprehensive multi-stage initiative to improve reading through public engagement, educator training, standards development, system support (reading clinics, qualification authority), and community information. Identifies reading as "golden skill" foundational to all learning and key to individual opportunity and community development. - **Key People**: Jessie Lees (reading advocate); Don Glendenning (author) - **Key Organizations**: Kingston reading clinic, Brock University, Wilson Reading (Moncton), Bermuda reading services, Department of Education, schools, libraries, communities - **Tags**: EDUCATION, PROFESSIONAL, COMMUNITY - **Eulogy Relevance**: HIGH - Shows Don's focus on reading as foundational to education equity and economic opportunity; demonstrates his strategic thinking about institutional change; reflects his advocacy for evidence-based, systematic improvement initiatives

Cost


The cost of schooling runs about $10,000 per student per year.  That figure is interesting but not very useful because I do not know what costs are included.  There are certain direct costs such as teacher salaries which, I assume, are attributable to the students that a teacher serves; the costs of technology lab would be spread across all students who use that lab.  school transportation costs could be be charged against all students or only those who use the service, then there snow removal and parking lot improvement costs that are oftenborne by  the Department of Public Transportation?  So, although I find the figure useful, for planning purposes it is not very useful


While the above is interesting, the important figure, however, is to know the cost for each student receiving a diploma:  Or the cost for each person who doesn’t.  Since reading is such a key skill, I’d be interested in knowing what it costs to teach a person to read.  I recvall reading somewhere,but unfortunately cannot find the article now, that it takes on average 35-40 h0urs of tutoring to raise a person’s reading level by one grade.  If that is true, 40 hours of tutoring at $50 per hour would mean a cost of $2,000 per grade level.  I/m sure my figures are way  off base, but I’d be interested in the real ones.  It seems to me that obtaining such figures would be a relatively easy task.  If I find them, I’ll certainly let you know.


















Findings and conclusions


Many outcomes not measurable

Not all statements aew outcomes

Language needs to be simplified

Need for a master list of outcomes that would apply at all levels; tracking learner progress throughout life;  for planning, to show progress, allows for circular curriculum, 


Rating scale needs to be developed that includes performance criteria

  • No common set of reading competencies
  • Little exchange of information among teachers of reading across the system
  • Early childhood needs customized support
  • Students and families need an advocate
  • What does it costto raise a person’s reading performance by one level

Recommendations:  

  • Replacement of outcomes with competencies
  • Provide all learners with a personal reading record – a list of competencies and indicating their levels on each
  • A minimal level of performance in order to graduate
  • Clearly identified assessment criteria such as vocabulary, comprehension and speed.   
  • Providing a written description for each performance level – not to exceed seven.
  • Identifying a benchmark such as “Level three denotes a person who can read and discuss articles found in the daily newspaper”.
  • Making all of the above information available to learners, parents and the public.
  • Including the outcome with the level of performance on report cards.
