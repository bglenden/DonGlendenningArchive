A Call for Action

July 16, 2018



Preparation for life’s roles is called education and the better prepared one is the better one will be able to cope with all that we encounter, or create. Educating is a complex matter made so by the fact that each person is unique in many ways, unique in basic physical, mental, emotional make-up; unique in the range and nature of previous experience, unique in perceived futures, but also unique in terms of learning pace, style and needs. Education is often described as any experience that has a formative effect on the way we think, feel or act; it may also be thought of as what we can do with what we have learned.


It is widely recognized that education is a key factor in determining each citizen’s quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of our citizens will determine to a significant degree the quality of the communities in which we live. In recognition of its importance, governments provide massive support for public education. The investment is justified for two reasons, one to ensure a high level of personal growth and development for every citizen; the other is to ensure the common good.


Based on media reports over the past few months, one can conclude that there is a gap between what Islanders have and what they want in the way of education. Literacy levels alone have come to the fore through PISA results, recent CFPEI survey of Island youth, presentation to the Standing Committee by the Charlottetown Area Chamber of Commerce, views expressed by the Summerside Chamber of Commerce, a 2014 survey by the PEI Home and School Federation and a series of workshops on Preparedness held throughout PEI by Education 20/20 Inc.


Indicators of the gap include: the limited engagement between educators and the community they serve; the lack of a philosophy of education that encompasses all our citizens; government’s focus on the young and schooling; service gaps such as the availability of part time instruction; politicization of decision making, and limits on professional autonomy and accountability. Citizens see most of the issues as organizational, created by government decision-makers and, therefore, capable of being changed by those same government decision-makers.


In recent years, Prince Edward Island has instituted a number of initiatives to improve education including new and upgraded facilities, additional investment in technology, the East Prince trade and technology centre, embedded professional development (PD) days, but much more needs to be done. It is our view that the impact of these changes has been blunted by an outdated delivery organization and, in support of this view, we recommend that PEI:


  1. Review and update Develop an overarching, clear and compelling vision of education for all Islanders -

Although much has been written about education, a comprehensive vision statement does not currently exist for PEI. We believe that having such a statement is required, especially for a service as large and complex as education, however, we are not suggesting another study but rather a document prepared by a small working with opportunity for citizen input.


  1. Establish an independent qualification service

The role of teachers is to help others learn; when teachers rank their students they are putting their ability to teach at risk. Assigning student grades, selecting students for prizes and awards and awarding diplomas and degrees is a sorting process and should not be part of a teacher’s role. Apprenticeship with certification through a Provincial Department of Labour and music through the Royal Canadian Music Conservatory are examples of the recommended arrangement. PEI, perhaps Canada, needs an independent h a qualification body for the purpose certifying individual levels of achievement to persons wanting or needing such documentation

  1. Provide schools with autonomy in their every day activities


  1. Develop a delivery model that provides support in all domains to learners if, as, when and where needed. Doing so would involve student centered outcomes, criterion referenced assessment, flexible scheduling, challenge for credit arrangements, and greater autonomy and accountability for teachers. Establish a professional model of service delivery where professionals are directly accountable to clients for the nature, extent and quality of service provided.
