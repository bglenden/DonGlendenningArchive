Need for clarity


Education is about helping people change their ways - the way they think, feel and act and even what they value. It is complex in that education is a personal matter and people have such a variety of interests, capabilities, experiences and needs. The key contributor to one’s education is one’s self and one’s family followed by the community. Then, I’m told, comes teachers and schools.


People play a variety of roles in their community including that of an individual, citizen student, family member, producer and consumer. This makes education important to our community as well as to the its citizen. Because education is important to both individuals and the community in which they live, governments set up special departments to “promote, encourage and regulate education matters” for its citizens.


Not surprising, given the size and complexity of the service, many people do not understand or are not satisfied with services provided; usually teachers and the PEITF get the blame. My exploration, however, leads me first to problems of conceptual clarity, that is the way in which educational matters are presented or explained. For example:

  • Purpose

  • Many people think that going to school and getting an education are one and the same; actually a school is like a hospital, only a purpose built facility where instruction takes place

  • Accountabilities for the provision of services are frequently unassigned, unassumed or otherwise not clear. The OECD, for example, studied PISA results from thirteen countries and found that students did better in schools where the school hadt significant control of its day to day operation

  • The community, through its government, invests millions of dollars annually in public education; a clear purpose would help keep us on track.

  • Educating is a service; effective service organizations are decentralized thereby allowing front line staff both autonomy and accountability to deal with individual differences. The OECD has been recommending this for some years.

  • The role of education is to help people develop skills, knowledge and attitudes; the Department seems to limit itself to developing and delivering curriculum to youth.

  • Education deserves and would benefit from public conversation with the community it serves; the literature emphasizes the importance of collaboration; educators seem reluctant to engage.

  • The management structure of a service organization should focus on services provided rather that clients served.


These issues are not limited to PEI nor to the impact of the pandemic. Neither is it a call for outside experts. We simply need to task a few Islander to lead us through it.
