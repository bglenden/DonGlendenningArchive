An analytical exploration of educational standards that distinguishes between credentials (diplomas/degrees) and qualifications (demonstrated skills and competencies). Don argues that public confusion between these concepts generates complaints about school standards, advocating for separation of educational and certification functions. He proposes establishing an independent Qualification Authority (AQUA) to assess and certify competencies, allowing schools to focus purely on teaching while a separate body handles qualification assessment.

While sorting stuff in my basement recently, I came across a number of diplomas, degrees and certificates.  Although I remember getting them, I cannot remember ever using even one.  So, one wonders what purpose or purposes they serve.  

Diploma, according to Wikipedia, is of Greek origin meaning  "folded paper", and is a document issued by an educational institution indicating that the holder has completed a certain programme of studies; degrees are also granted by educational institutions on a similar basis.  Degrees and diplomas are considered credentials in that they may provide entrance into an arena but are not an indicator of how well one will perform once there.

A qualification, on the other hand, speaks to the issue of attitudes, skills and knowledge required to successfully perform in a role or at a task.  Employment advertisements often identify both a credential, such as a high school diploma, and a set of qualifications usually including training and or experience.  In order to teach, one also needs a credential, a university degree, and a qualification, a teaching licence, the latter attesting to one’s ability to teach.

Public complaints about school standards arise, at least in part I believe, because we confuse credentials and qualifications.  A school’s “folded paper” is a credential; the public, and employers in particular, think that the “folded paper” is a qualification – and that’s the rub.

Given responses to my recent letter on credentials and qualifications, some additional comments may be in order.  It is my view that:

  • Credentialling is about what one knows whereas qualification is about what one can do with what one knows 
  • Diplomas and degrees are credentials not qualifications.
  • Employers latch onto credentials thinking they are qualifications
  • Schools help people help people qualify, gain the skills, knowledge and attitudes required for qualifications, but schools should not award qualifications.  
  • If qualifications are necessary, and I believe some are, the function should be assigned to a body separate from educational institutions. 
  • Many people who are, through training and experience, qualified to fill positions, do not have a piece of paper to prove it.
  • The key when hiring is to spell out details the work to be performed or competencies required, and ask applicants to cast their application in matching terms.
  • I also believe that changing the focus from credentials to qualifications would be a much better way to deal with immigrants seeking to meet the requirements for work in Canada. 








It may well be time to consider a certification body or qualification authority, perhaps an Atlantic one, to undertake the task of assessing and confirming qualifications  – but first we should sort out more precisely the skills to be measured, assessed and reported.
