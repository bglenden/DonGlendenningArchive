Don addresses the incoming premier with constructive proposals for education reform. He encourages updating PEI's Philosophy of Education document, arguing the current version addresses only K-12 schooling and ignores lifelong learning, early childhood education, upgrading programs, and self-directed learning. He recommends striking a committee with public members to develop a comprehensive, forward-looking philosophy and revision of government's role in education. Don positions education transformation as "Job 1" for the new government, noting that clear educational vision would significantly improve outcomes.

First I want to thank all the people who accepted a nomination for the coming election.  We may or may not agree on positions, policies or actions but our communities are richer because you care.


But this letter is directed to the new Premier and I write it because you have a rare opportunity to make a difference to our community and especially to future generations of young Islanders



I am grateful to  the many Islanders who put their names forward in the coming electionFirst, I want to thank you for your willingness to serve

Organizational changed of this kind can only be made by government; it is my hope that , when the election dust settles, our new Premier will do two things:

  • Strike a committee, that includes public members, to layout the mission, mandate, principles and goals of education for Prince Edward Island (The current philosophy deals only with the public school system
  • Take a fresh look at the role of government in ensuring that Islanders receive the quality of education that they deserve within the resources available.   



Every Islander who is interested in education should read the “Philosophy of Education” found on the website of the Department of Education and Early Childhood Development. It’s a short document about purposes, basic principles, goals and the role of schools in society.  It’s a basic document and one that reflects the beliefs and values and of the people who operate our schools – but it’s incomplete.  


A philosophy of education should encompass the lifelong nature of education and take into account the needs of all Islanders; not just those in schoolsthe one referred to above deals only with young people and only that part of education provided in a school setting.  Little or no mention of services needed by preschool children, by people who ned up grading or updating through their working years, or people who may need validation for self study or learning in bits and pieces throughout life
