Transition in Math

Education


Education is a provincial matter. Provinces agree that they have a responsibility to help their citizens develop attitudes, skills and knowledge at least to the level of good citizenship; Provinces also have a responsibility provide skilled people to care for the community in which they live. In PEI, that means encouraging more and better education for all residents and providing specialized training at least for those who serve us – not an easy task in a rapidly changing world.


It is widely recognized that education is a key factor in determining one’s quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of our citizens will determine to a significant degree the future of our community and country in which we live and work. While our system of education ranks well in the world, it falls short of the expectations of many citizens. Evidence of and concern about that gap can be found in relation to PISA results, recent CFPEI survey of Island youth, presentation to the Standing Legislative Committee, provincial and PanCanadian reports. While the concern applies to the state of education generally, this paper limits itself to the field of mathematics.


All provinces and Territories operate a public-school system devoted to serving the needs of young people, a relatively autonomous postsecondary sector, and a melange of services operated or administered through other provincial ministries. Add to this federal funding funding in support of economic development, to which education is a major contributor, and we are faced with some issues of coordination and communication. Providers of educational services in PEI include:

  • Families

  • The Department of Education, Early Learning and Culture delivers instruction through the public-school system

  • The Department of Workforce and Advanced Learning which oversees postsecondary institutions and but direct delivers through grants and contract wide range of educational services

  • Postsecondary institutions operating under Boards of Governors

  • Private schools and colleges

  • Tutors, educational consultants, NGO’s and associations

  • Employer and employee associations


Given the number of delivery organizations, it is not surprising that coordination and communication is an issue. In fact, the Council of Ministers of Education, (CMEC) drew attention to the issue of educational “transition” in a press statement on July 7, 2016 in which they highlight their concern, a concern that is shared by many educators, employers, certification agencies and the community at large. Our review of the matter suggests that transition issues likely arise from:

  • Number and variety of agencies involved in a Province

  • Differing foundations on which curriculum is developed - skills, competencies or outcomes

  • Absence of a common master list of math skills available throughout the education system

  • Approach to defining levels of achievement.

  • Differing assessment criteria

  • Absence of a common rating scale

  • Math assessments and reporting not perceived as being reliable

  • Large number of people engaged in the assessment process

  • Potential for conflict when teachers also grade

  • Limited, if any, consultation between and among those who teach at different levels

  • Limited representation on advisory committees

  • Mixed messages about entry requirements from postsecondary institutions


MATH INSTRUCTION

Math in PEI is based on the conventional structure: arithmetic, algebra, geometry, trigonometry, calculus, probability and statistics and is provided at all grade levels; its goal, as stated in the PEI Curriculum Guide for Grade 9 mathematics is for students to “become mathematically literate adults”. It is an achievable goal, at least for most students but, if math test scores and public comment are used as the measure, the goal remains a challenge rather than an accomplishment.


Not all math providers start from the same base; the public-school system use a Curriculum Based Outcomes model developed under The Canada North West Protocol the same curriculum is used in several other Provinces. Post secondary institutions tend to use a more traditional curriculum model although more and more they are turning to competencies. The US literature is witnessing a major swing to competencies with the best known likely being Common Core State Standards. No examples were found of “receiving” organizations, employers and postsecondary institutions, willing, able or, in some cases, allowed to articulate their needs using the standard list of ingredients. On the other side of the coin, “receivers” of students have a variety of math interests and needs not all of which are adequately documented. The writer’s search for a master list for either math as a whole or any of its major components.


Curriculum development involves laying out the education course for students, or more accurately, the courses for students. It is a key component of successful transition and involves four steps:

  • An analysis/description of the subject or field under consideration

  • Identification of skills required by learner to meet education objective

  • Availability of funds to provide instruction

  • Documentation/attestation of what has been learned


We believe that math instruction would benefit from a common set of underlying beliefs such as the following:

  • The ultimate beneficiary of curriculum development is the learner

  • A person’s performance level in mathematics is determined by the nature and extent of skills that a person can demonstrate, level of performance at which each skill can be demonstrated, and the extent or degree of clustering.

  • Outcomes and competencies are not age related.

  • Mandating those who teach to also “sort” constitutes a conflict of interest

  • Bloom’s Taxonomy provides an appropriate basis for establishing levels of proficiency

  • To the extent possible, outcomes, skills or competencies should be stated in terms that apply directly to learners and be part of a learner’s support material

  • Alignment between competencies offered, competencies studied, levels learned, levels demonstrated, levels achieved, credentials awarded and qualifications acquired.


Conclusions

Based on our collective knowledge of education generally of Education 2/20 Inc. and referring to education in mathematics in particular, and arising at least in part from the issue of transitioning raised by the Council of Ministers of Education Canada, we’ve reached the following conclusions:

  • An inconsistency in the way we describe math content through our education system - skills, competencies or outcomes

  • Confusion about levels of achievement and who defines them

  • Lacks common assessment criteria and rating scale used to assess student achievement

  • Reliability of math assessments and reporting

  • Potential for conflict when teachers also grade

  • Limited communication between and among those who teach and between those who hire the product of our teaching.

  • Need to involve all providers in the education framework


Recommendations:

Education 20/20 Inc. recommends that Prince Edward Island:

  1. Engage stakeholders in a pilot project in one math sector – arithmetic.

  2. Adopt competencies as a basis for curriculum development

  3. Issue an independently developed analysis of math competencies for use in the Island education system

  4. Adopt a standard list of assessment criteria

  5. Adopt a common rating scale for competencies

  6. Report student achievement in terms of competencies demonstrated and levels achieved

  7. Establish an ongoing advisory committee of providers and users

  8. Organize ab annual professional event for math teachers at all levels

  9. Make instructional resource material available throughout the system


Benefits:

  • Sharing materials

  • Increased communication

  • Reduces cost

  • More consistent interpretation

  • Increased accountability

  • Eases transition

  • Improves communication
