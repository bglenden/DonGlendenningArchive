Newsletter column examining grade-level myths in education, using construction and music training as examples of competency-based progression. Don contrasts this with age-based classroom grouping and discusses Robert Paul Wolff's framework distinguishing between criticism (feedback), evaluation (proficiency measurement), and ranking (competitive comparison). The piece advocates for moving away from rigid grade levels toward developmental, ability-based instruction.

The myth of grade nine math


One of my first paid jobs was in construction at the Chatham airport; day one was spent nailing floors and siding, rough carpentry, I think it was called; by noon on day two I was asked if I could cut a rafter (I could) and found myself working on the roof. A few days later, I was a “finish carpenter” installing trim. Apparently there were “levels” of carpenters and I was being tested to see where I fitted. The royal Canadian conservatory ranks levels in music that have nothing to do with the age of the performer; the same holds true, I am told in swimming. One is a beginner regardless of one’s age.






Not so in educationIn life generally, one may be a beginn

It is not unusual, I am told, to find students in a grade 10 math class who are operating at a level anywhere from Grade 8 to Grade 12. In fact, earlier this year I was told of a student in Grade 12 math class whose skills were at the grade four level Why then, do we call it a Grade 10 math class?

My last column dealt with formative and summative assessment. Since then my search on “grades and grading” turned up an item by Robert Paul Wolff (Wolff: A discourse on Grading, 1969). In it he identifies three elements or kinds of grading:

- Criticism which includes analysis and

feedback about current learning; criticism is

at the heart of learning and involves the give

and take between teacher and student about

what happened or should happen.


- Evaluation to establish a person’s level in a

subject and is, or should be, criterion

referenced, similar to levels in music

or swimming.

- ranking to see how one stacks up against

others; as for ranking, schools could take a

lesson from sports with both coaches and

referees. Ranking results from competitions;

students should be encouraged to

participate and teachers and students can

use the results but teachers should play no

part in the ranking process.


I find this interesting concept as I reflect on the education process.
