Supporting Keith Hogg's suggestion that high school students successfully challenge examinations could be exempted from taking courses, Don recalls similar exemption policies during his university studies. Exemptions would free students to concentrate on challenging subjects, explore new interests, or graduate early, eliminating wasted time re-learning previously covered material. Don identifies only positive impacts (student motivation, personalized pacing, exposure to new learning) and no downsides. He frames this as a practical incentive encouraging student achievement.

April 3, 2013


Editor, Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

High School Exemptions

Keith Hogg made the suggestion (‘Exams fail to prepare students for the workforce’, The Guardian, March 7, 2013) that high school students be allowed to write exams and, if successful, be exempt from taking the courses.  It makes sense and would be a real incentive to students.  I’ve had it happen twice during my university studies but I’ve also sat through courses where the material had already been covered in an earlier course or by another teacher; there was nothing wrong with the material, it was just that it was a repeat.  

Under an exemption policy, students could spend more time on subjects where help is needed, study a different subject, or even finish early.  There are so many new and exciting things to learn, it’s a shame to think that students must spend valuable school hours listening to things they already know.  

What would the impact of an exemption policy be on learners and learning?  All positive, I would venture.  Is there a downside?  I can’t think of any; at the least, it is worth a try.
