Islanders speak out on skill development


Ideas to improve skill development arrived by Email as well as in person at Wednesday’s evening’s session on skill development. But first, participants talked about what it is. It’s more than knowing something, “It’s being able to do things”, they decided.


Not surprising, thoughts turned to literacy and numeracy followed by the use of computers or coding; some would like to see carpentry, welding and auto mechanics for everyone, Do all programs have to be two years long? Why not some short courses in carpentry or mechanics, and they want them to be real courses taught by people who have worked in the field.


Schools front load training; we should make it easier for people to get training they need when they need it. There was considerable support for short bursts of upgrading courses for people now working, even skill upgrading weekends, perhaps in the form of vouchers to cover both tuition and lost wages; people should be able to challenge for credit, someone suggested segmented qualifications. Schools and colleges cannot be expected to provide all the training; some companies could also be designated as training centres.


Some wondered how graduation requirements were set, why there weren’t more emphasis on essential skills an why there are no employers on advisory committees.


Education 20/20, the organizer of the session, will make a list of the suggestions and

forward them to both the Federal and Provincial Governments.
