Don identifies what he calls "the great disconnect" in education—the disconnect between outcomes-based education theory and actual school practice. He notes that while extensive educational outcomes are published, report cards don't reflect performance on individual outcomes. Teachers cannot realistically assess and report on every outcome, creating a gap between stated educational philosophy and institutional practice. This letter reflects his attention to practical implementation challenges and the importance of alignment between policy rhetoric and classroom reality.


January 6, 2015


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

The great disconnect


We spend a great deal of money in developing outcomes for all subjects and for all grades; lists of outcomes are easy to find on the website of the Department of Education and Early Childhood Development.  Anyone interested in what their child or grandchild is learning should take a look.  And if your child is having a problem with a subject, you should make a copy and take it with you to the next meeting with your child’s teacher.

In Outcomes Based Education, outcomes are, or should be, the basis for developing curriculum, organizing teaching activities, tracking students and assessing progress.  While teachers cannot teach each outcome separately, they certainly can and should report progress on each outcome.  The report cards I’ve seen, do not report that way. Otherwise, And I’m not sure that postsecondary institutions and HR Departments could handle it if they did.

 - and that’s the great disconnect in education.   

Sincerely,


Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
