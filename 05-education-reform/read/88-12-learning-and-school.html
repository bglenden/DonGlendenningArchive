Article No. 88 (June 4, 2020) clarifies the distinction between "going to school" and "getting an education," citing a 2018 World Bank report. Don emphasizes that learning results in behavioral change and can occur anywhere, not just in schools, yet many Islanders (including educators) conflate education with schooling. He explains that communities encourage and fund learning they deem valuable (medicine, mechanics, policing) and that while schools are purpose-built facilities, attending school does not guarantee good education. The piece advocates for holistic rating scales, competency-based learning, self-paced programs, and democratic school governance while criticizing the current politically-filtered conversation focused on administrivia rather than meaningful educational change.

No. 88 June 4, 2020

CLARIFICATION NEEDED


A recent report by the World Bank called attention to the fact going to school and getting an education are different functions and should not be confused.(World Bank – 2018) I was actually asked a number of years ago on PEI, where I got my education; it turned out the person really wanted to know where I went to school. I suspect that that view is widely held; my personal experience suggests that many Islanders, including professional educators, fail to differentiate.


Learning results in a change in behaviour and is demonstrated through the ay we think, feel and act. Learning itself is neutral, whether good or bad for ourselves or rhe community, it is still learning.


Communities believe that some kinds of learning are good for individuals and good for the community in which they live. They not only encourage that kind of learning but they help pay for it. Because medical doctors, mechanics and policemen are needed to ensure people are safe, they often pay for that also.


Learning can happen anywhere; some of our most important learning takes place at home; sometimes because of numbers or special circumstances, communities build purpose built buildings and insist that learners go there when they need help; these places are called schools. But as the World bank points out, just because you’ve done some learning in schools doesn’t mean that you have a good education.


How well one performs in a subject depends to a large extent, on the reason for studying the subject. person or body doing the measuring. For example, one does not need to know a great deal about an automobile to be a good driver.

















I do not, we cannot, blame the current government, the previous government or any single government that went before for the current status; Government has a responsibility however, for instituting corrective measures.


Islanders want our students to be literate and numerate, to become independent learners, to take care of themselves and learn how to work and play together. Education’s business is to help Islanders gain new and better skills, knowledge, attitudes and values. We wonder if our students would benefit from holistic rating scales, competency based learning, self- paced programs self-assessment and schools operating on democratic principles.


We believe that education is central to the future of our citizens and our community. Educators need to engage and collaborate with the community they serve The current conversation, however, is politically filterd and often bogged down on matters of administrivia (school upgrading, credits, grades, noon meals, technology, etc.) at the expense of changes that help Islanders gain knowledge, skills, attitudes and values needed to help them to thrive in later life.


It is the writer’s view that much of the needed change is blocked (perhaps not encouraged is more accurate) by an outdated system


embedded in a body that can give it neither

The direction, freedom nor support needed to accomplish the task at hand. Government not only needs to change a number of existing policies and practices but also needs to allow, nay encourage, change to happen. The World Bank recently chided Ministries of Education for not upgrading their own operation and for with holding autonomy from schools.


PEI has some 150,000 citizens; all are potential users of educational services. Their need’ s, interests and capabilities are many and varied, but policy documents are focused on providing services to young people through schools. It’s as though the only education that counts is the part provided o young people in schools.


Education needs a plan, something simple, some definitions, some guiding principles, description of services to be offered, services covered by government, and an organization that reflects the task at hand. Such a statement should be short, sharp and developed with public input – but not by outside experts.


Education is a recognized profession, it is time to allow them to enter the fray as professionals in their own right and not to be subject to a political filter..


Don Glendenning, Editor
