STRUCTURAL CHANGES

A few years ago, I convened a half dozen public discussions about education concerns of youth. Sometimes, discussions continued long after sessions closed. After one session, a side discussion turned to “structural impediments” to better education. A recent reading of a World Bank report on education, although not using the term “structure”, reminded me of trustees comments and prompted this letter.


By structural issues, I refer to “policies, procedures, culture, teachers, and physical dimensions” of the educational framework that interfere with educational quality.

Examples:

  • Political filter: Provincial Governments have a leadership role in encouraging, supporting and regulating education. But embedding the delivery process in a government department may result in some unintended consequences.

  • Vision: PEI education documents indicate a narrow view of education; it seems that the Department views going to school and getting an education as one and the same. Not so says and the World Bank, a large funder of public education throughout the developing world,

  • Professional Autonomy: the OECD (who gives us PISA tests) tell us that students in schools where the school has autonomy in the school’s day to day operation do better on PISA test; informal evidence suggest that neither principals or teachers have much freedom on even professional matters.

  • Accountability: “duty to report (give account of) on events, tasks and experiences. The task is to help Islanders skills; reporting seems to focus on instruction in schools. increase One often hears comments about math deficiencies; it would be interesting to hear a teacher or specialist explain if it is real and why.


In 2015, Education 20/20 called for a “clear and compelling” vision of education. If we want to improve our education system, that is still the place to start.


Don Glendenning, Editor
