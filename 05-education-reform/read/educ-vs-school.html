Drawing on a 2018 World Bank report, Don emphasizes the distinction between "going to school" and "gaining an education." He provides three evidence-based reasons: (1) Time in school is unreliable as a learning measure; better to set standards and allow time to meet them; (2) Education comprises multiple services—information, instruction, counseling, assessment, learning products, technology—most offered anywhere, while "going to school" is not itself an educational service (analogous to hospital or shopping plaza); (3) PEI's department title "Education and Lifelong Learning" seems redundant since education includes lifelong learning. Don notes that instructional services benefit from centralized locations, while non-instructional services (information, counseling) may serve better if decentralized. He proposes a lexicon of common educational terms, supporting his broader call for definitional clarity.

Education or schooling

A World Bank  report in 2018 on education  reminded developing countries that going to school and gaining an education are not the same thing; the difference needs to be affirmed in PEI as well. Some reasons for that conclusion include:

  • Given that individuals learn at their own rate, the length of time one has been in school is not a very reliable measure of how much one has learned. The alternative is ti set a standard and allow students time to meet it.
  • Education is typically comprised of a number of services -   information, instruction, counselling, assessment, learning products and technology, etc; most can be offered most anywhere. Some services require special facilities and groups.  But going to school is not itself an educational service.  I liken a school to a hospital or shopping plaza. 
  • I’m puzzld by the title “Education and Lifelong learning” in the name of the Department; doesn’t education already include lifelong learning? 

Certainly instructional  services usually benefit from being in a single location; non-instructional services such as Information and counselling , however, may serve equally well or even better  if located off campus used by the Department of Education


A lexicon of common educational terms may be in order

Sincerely


Don Glendenning
