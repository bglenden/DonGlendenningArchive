From September 6, 2010, Don reviews Matthew Crawford's "Shop Class as Soulcraft," noting Crawford earned a PhD in political philosophy but operates a motorcycle repair shop. Don endorses the book's argument that tangible, hands-on work provides measurable accomplishment and problem-solving satisfaction. He recommends the work to educators, arguing practical skills and troubleshooting ability transfer to life problem-solving. This reflects his emphasis on skills-based, practical education over purely theoretical knowledge.
Education Philosophy & Reform

September 6, 2010


Editor 

Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:


A friend loaned me a book recently.  As a former Industrial Arts teacher and a lifelong handyman, I recommend it to others, especially to educators.    No, it is not a “how to” book for dummies but it certainly laments the loss of tangible work.  


Matthew Crawford has a Ph.D. in political philosophy from the University of Chicago and has held a number of prestigious positions in corporate America, but he operates a motorcycle shop in Richmond, Virginia.  His book, Shop Class as Soulcraft, tells why.


It is a personal story, an easy read, about the pleasure of craftsmanship, creating with your won hands, the satisfaction of measurable accomplishment,   I was intrigued with the description of the motorcycle shoop  skills of diagnosing, problem solving and immediate rewards of satisfying a customer and putting him on the road again.  


My view good skills that can be applied to solving problems in any in other areas of life.  The book may be a bit wordy; after all, hhe does have a PhD. But is an interesting and easy read.  Should be read by educators but also has an important message for employers.


Sincerely


Don Glendenning
