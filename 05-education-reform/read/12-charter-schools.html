Don Glendenning frames the charter schools debate, moving beyond simplistic arguments about public versus private education. He identifies five fundamental issues deserving public attention: the distinction between education and schooling, differing stakeholder expectations for schools, roles and responsibilities of various parties, the significance of credentials, and whether teaching and certification should remain separate functions. Rather than advocating for charter schools outright, he calls for thoughtful debate on these foundational questions that affect all educational models.

April 5, 1999

Charter schools


Recently we were presented through this newspaper with two quite different views on the issue of

charter schools. The reasons why one supports or does not support private schools are many and

varied and depend, to a large extent, on one's role as a stakeholder and experience as a student,

parent, teacher, administrator, employer, labour representatives or Minister. One's reasons also

depend on one's beliefs.


lssues raised in the articles include: local versus centralized control, reducing or containing costs,

school transportation, small and usually rural schools , public versus private schools, lack of

research on educational issues, innovation and change, choice and relevance. These issues are

worthy of public debate. I hope that the two recent articles will trigger it.


ln addition to the issues identified in the articles, however, I believe there are some even more

fundamental issues that deserve our attention. These include:


the difference between education and schooling. We use the words interchangeably. While the

latter is an important and makes a major contribution, education is more than schooling.

against which schools are measured.
