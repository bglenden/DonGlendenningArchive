Education Philosophy & Reform

February 12. 2020

Dear Editor:

Life is complex; people fill many roles; there is much to be learned - to communicate, to get along with others, to look after ourselves and .to look after the environment.  It’s more than parents can handle; parents need help; the rest of us need help too. It’s in the interests of the community to provide help. But providing help is complex. There are so many people, such a range of differences, disparate needs, etc. etc.  Government wants to help, so they set up a system of schools; decide what everyone needs and that all young people must attend  – but service got lost, education become a product described by hours, lessons, outcomes, grades, requirements, diplomas, degrees, years of schooling, etc.; responding to citizen needs is no longer part of the equation; educators seem to have withdrawn from public conversation and, stay inside their respective silos.  “Service” and education got lost in the process.

It’s time It’s time for a public conversation about education; to quote a long time education activist from Ontario:

“The public, all the people in an organized, preferably democratic, society/state should/must take ownership of how their young are educated. Everyone and especially parents should be partners in setting the responses to the following issues:  

  • why should the state educate it’s citizens?  
  • How should the state educate it’s citizens?  
  • Who should decide when a citizen is educated? 
  • What part of the state’s limited resources should be put towards educating every citizen?  Who decides how much?
  • Should schools be operated? As  a for profit business?  Like a community library? Like a not for profit hospital with a chief medical officer and a chief admin officer?

Currently there are so many players with different and conflicting agendas involved with public education it is no wonder “schools” are either dysfunctional or operational at a high social, psychological, emotional, organizational stress level that make them, in many instances, toxic work/learning environments.

The education literature is full of comment about schools but little about other education  services and schools  are unlikely  to improve until we understand their role within education.  Islanders deserve to be better informed but also to be consulted about education – what it is, who it’s for, principles on which it will operate, what government will pay for, etc.  Because of the fear factor, any conversation likely needs o be sanctioned, and probably led, by the Minister. In the spirit of political cooperation, perhaps an all- party panel could be struck to lead such a discussion.

One further point; education likes consultants.  If the Province decides to act, based on past history, they would likely hire an outside consultant. I recommend instead, that the person or persons  who are going to implement the change be asked to come up with a plan; hire consultants to help if needed, but make the project a responsibility of the peope who will implement the decision.


To add to the confusion, the Department sees its role as one of “delivering high quality programs” to youth, which was essentially the same role as the previous government. Again, a narrow role and one, I suggests, that leaves communities with gaps in continuing education, shifts costs of gaining ssential skills from secondary choolss to pot secondary institutions, and leaves Islanders unprepared for job vacancies that exist  in our home Province.
