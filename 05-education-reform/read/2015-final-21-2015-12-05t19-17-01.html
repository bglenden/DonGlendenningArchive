QUESTION 11A –PURPOSES AND ACTIVITIES

For application to CRA for charitable status


APPROVED PURPOSES

The purposes of Education 20/20 Inc., as included in our incorporation documents, are as follows:

1 To promote education and educational opportunity for every Canadian

2 To exercise leadership in the improvement in all facets of education

3 To foster cooperation between and among all stakeholders

4 Undertake and support research and innovation

5 improve public understanding of the education system


As part of the process of preparing this application, however, we reviewed and revised the objectives as found in our incorporation documents to better reflect our interests and plans; the objectives and activities included below result from that review.


A)  ADVANCE EDUCATION BY ORGANIZING LEADERSHIP DEVELOPMENT ACTIVITIES FOR PEOPLE WHO PLAN, ADMINISTER AND DELIVER EDUCATION PROGRAMS.   


Education 20/20 is well positioned to engage educators from across all sectors, to provide them with opportunities to learn about new education innovation from around the world, share best practices and with this new knowledge, advance education programming to the benefit of learners from birth through to the workforce.  To this end, education 20/20 has undertaken or planned the following activities.

Handle With Care

Handle with Care is a national program designed to help parents and other caregivers promote the mental health of young children every day.  It is useful for everyone who cares for or works with children; especially parents, grandparents and teachers, and consists of sets of interactive strategies that build on the strengths of adults in their daily lives.  The program is based on four building blocks: Trust and Attachment, Building and Enhancing Self- Esteem, Expressing Emotions and Building Relationships with Others.  Each block builds upon the one before and contains the following components: Key message, Research, Activities, Bringing it Home and Bringing it Together, other resources and a Summary Handout.   The key to a successful program is trained facilitators.  Each participant is required to purchase a text book.


Handle with Care was developed by Hincks Delcrest Centre in Toronto and the Canadian Mental Health Association and with funding from public Health Agency of Canada.  It was piloted with 439 participants in four centres, two in Ontario and one each in Prince Edward Island and Manitoba.  The developers of the program promote and allow its use but insist on trained facilitators.  Ms. Taylor was a member of the original team supervised the testing process in PEI and is involved in its implementation in Nova Scotia; PEI has at least two fully trained facilitators and a number of others with some training.


A Handle with Care workshop for parents and caregivers of young children is planned for Hunter River, PEI. however, Ms. Taylor, session leader, is recovering from surgery and a date has not been finalized.  The workshop in Hunter River will be hosted by Rochelle Shephard and the Early Learning Centre.

Webinars

There is a large unserved and even growing body of people engaged in education outside public institutions; I refer to a dozen agencies on PEI part of whose educational programs have been accepted by the Department of Education for external credits, some private schools, home schooling parents, employer based training and a number of organizations receiving funding to mount programs for underserved youth.  Education 20/20 Inc. plans to offer a series of webinar-style 3-5 minute video clips for use by educators either alone or in groups.  Our first series, “A Learning Framework” is under development and consists of four sessions (1) Learners which provides background as to why learners differ, (2) Learning: sheds light on what happens when a person learns, (3) Levels of Learning: presents options on the meaning of levels of achievement, and (4) The Learning Path which follows a learner through a learning cycle.  Involved in the first series are: Presenter Donald Glendenning, CM, PhD; Henry Purdy, CM, CRA, and Sara Underwood, Office of Alumni Relations at Holland College.  This activity is on hold until we find additional resources.




Work with employers:

On 2013, the Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island conducted a survey of over 200 young people to better understand their issues.  (www.cfpei.ca). On reviewing their report, Education 20/20 was struck by the “preparedness gap”, that is the statement that many young people find a gap in the extent to which their previous education prepared for further education and for work.   Early in 2014, Education 20/20 Inc. organized workshops in six communities across the Island to publicize the findings, determine the extent of the concern, and identify actions, if any, that could or should be taken. Perhaps the most significant finding was the lack of communication about educational matters between providers and users of educational services.   


On the other side of the issue, employers and post-secondary institutions use high school graduation as an entrance requirement to further study and to work, but they are finding that having such a high school graduation diploma is no longer adequate evidence of an acceptable level of basic or essential skills.  This is not surprising since teachers often report that in most classes, student grade levels range from 2-3 grade levels above to 2-3 grade levels below the nominal level of the class.  In other words, it is not unusual to have students whose math levels range by six to 8 years.  


On November 25, 2015 Education 20/20 convened a meeting that included Representatives of the Department of Education, Early Learning and Culture, the English Language School Board, Holland College, UPEI, PEI Literacy Council, the Community Foundation and employers in the Board room of Ellis and Birt, 27 Brackley Point Road, Charlottetown.  The purpose was to explore the issue of the gap referred to earlier in this document. It soon became evident that public school educators do not consult with the public they serve when planning school programs; furthermore preparing students for citizenship, let alone for employment is not a stated purpose of schools.  Employers, on the other hand, have not identified their needs especially in the area of basic education.  Further sessions have the potential as an instrument of educational improvement but to do so may well challenge the views of all parties represented.  The initiative is important and will continue.  

Other providers of educational services

We are familiar with schools, colleges and universities as providers of educational services; we are less familiar with, and often overlook, the many other providers such as youth organizations, cadet programs, sports development programs, the private sector and a range of NGO’s.  More to the point, staff in these groups is seldom included in conferences, workshops or other educational activities.  We have made contact with a number of community based training agencies such as Skills PEI, Workplace Learning PEI, Bridges and some private trainers; some already participate in current activities of Education 20/20.  With the initiative by the Department of Education to increase credit for external courses, we see an increased opportunity and an obligation to help.  Although the dialogue is open, we have not undertaken yet any programs specifically designed to meet their needs.  


Assessment and Evaluation

Schools not only certify they also sort; by sorting we mean issue credentials (Diplomas and Degrees).  Some see the issue an inadequate standard- setting and assessment model while others simply believe that schools have should not be in the business of sorting and grading people they teach. It appears that s need exists for assessment services. Education 20/20 is exploring the idea of a separate certification arrangement similar to that provided by the Royal Canadian Conservatory of Music under which teachers teach but the Conservatory certifies.  In the broader context, I should point out that most advanced countries have developed qualification bodies to deal with both, perhaps even rationalize national mobility and well as that of people who work internationally; Canada and the US are exceptions. 


The idea of an independent assessment service or qualification authority is gaining support and, we expect, will soon become centre stage in the movement to improve public education. We continue to develop the idea and see a feasibility study on the horizon.


B) ADVANCE EDUCATION THROUGH INCREASING THE AMOUNT AND FLOW OF INFORMATION AND IDEAS TO, BETWEEN AND AMONG EDUCATION PARTNERS


Society must draw from the shared knowledge, experiences and aspirations of citizens to maximize the achievement of learners and to better support them throughout their lives.  Achieving this goal requires greater connectivity among education partners that include students, parents, teachers, principals, government, government and education, community and business organizations.


It takes an informed and engaged community to educate a child; evidence shows that students perform better in schools with a high degree of autonomy but also in schools where parents and the community work together; in PEI, during the last few months, the Department of Education has dramatically expanded the number of external credits.  Education 20/20   believes that more needs to be done and that our organization can play an important role in (1) increasing the flow of information and (2) providing opportunities for “deliverers of educational services and “users of educational services” to dialogue.  The connectivity among education partners on PEI is currently limited due in large part to jurisdictional boundaries, for example among provincial government departments, between school boards, among educators in the early years, K-12 and postsecondary  systems and those involved in workforce skill development.  Education 20/20 is working to reduce these boundaries, to enable education partners to come together with a shared sense of purpose and direction on learning.  This information sharing should enable improved decision making within all education and learning sectors that will ultimately be to the benefit of all learners. To this end, Education 20/20 has undertaken or planned the following activities.

Working with the Community at Large

Parents play a pivotal role in the education of their children; children perform better when their parents and the community are engaged in the education process and yet, broadly speaking, the school system does not encourage deep engagement.  To encourage engagement, Education 20/20 Inc. has instituted an award of Excellence to a school or learning centre that best demonstrates a high level of engagement in its day to day activities, especially those activities over which the school has control.  It is anticipated that this award will encourage parents, teachers, students and principals to think about new ways to interact and engage one another to the benefit of learning and learners.   We will continue to engage employers and educators in projects and panels to increase communication; service clubs will be encouraged to invite educators, especially principals, as speakers, and will make a special attempt to produce occasional papers and media releases about items of interest to them.  


(C) ADVANCE EDUCATION BY PROVIDING SUPPLEMENTAL EDUCATIONAL SUPPORT SERVICES


Learning begins at birth, continues through the early years, through the K-12 school system, postsecondary education and into the workforce.  Every citizen is at one point or another, a learner.  Education is a lifelong process; most of our educational funding, however, goes to institutions and for full-time instruction of the young.  Even at that, schools cannot, should not be expected to, supply all educational services for youth; neither can governments be expected to pay all educational costs.  There is a need for part time instruction and short courses but, even where they are available, offerings tends be limited to courses being offered to others on a full time basis.  Although technology has put knowledge building within easy reach of many adults, maintaining and progressing in the workplace may also require new skills and attitudes that can best be dealt with in group settings.  Partners in the education process also include the family, community agencies, sports groups, service clubs, NGO’s, private sector, and individuals, to name but a few; future plans of Education 20/20 include:


Study Centre(s)

Many students need a place to study, to meet with a tutor, to access occasional help and to interact with other learners; Education 20/20 plans to provide such places.  As demand for services grow, we see the need for a full service study centre where youth can get customized help such as counselling, diagnosis, assessment and even referral. Our longer term plan is to expand the service to number communities as demand builds and resources allow.  At least at the beginning, tutors will be volunteers; initial space has been offered by the PEI Association of Professional Engineers; costs will be minimal and will be met through donations from individuals and businesses.


  • Emerging fields – 

Our world is dominated by technology.  Most computer users benefit from less than 10% of a program’s functionality.  There are contemporary computer languages that are learnable by virtually anyone.  Knowledge of a computer language not only helps us better use existing programs, but also enables us to start building some of our own tools thereby changing us from passive to active technology consumers.  Schools cannot be expected to meet the emerging demands by all students, in all new fields and in all locations.   We need to open doors to more people, especially young people. At the moment, interest appears to be centered on coding but robotics, 3D printing and other IT related areas are also in demand.


At the moment, school reorganization is consuming most of the energy of educators in PEI, however, the Minister has announced new funding in support of technology in schools and even the introduction of coding into schools  One of our Directors serves on the Minister’s Information Technology Advisory Committee.  In addition, we see a growing interest in providing young children with computer skills outside the school system.  At the moment, our role is limited to encouraging supporting such changes; however, other roles may emerge over time.


Success in school depends to a significant degree to the level of social and emotional development of people entering school; there is a need, we believe, to provide information and assistance to parents of pre-school students in ways to ensure young people are provided with appropriate experiences.  At the moment, we are in active discussion with a view to offering the Handle-with-Care program, mentioned elsewhere in this document.


  • Imagine experiences

In the hurly burly of everyday life, and especially in the context of school classrooms, there is seldom time for creativity.  We propose to provide such opportunities to Islanders of any age.  The charting of a learning path was an “imagine activity”; the initiative started with a few students, a blank flip chart and the question “what kind of help do you want/need to support your learning?  Out of it came a number of discussions, a learning path and a clearer understanding of the conflict that arises when teachers also grade.   The next step will likely be a session to chart the role of teachers followed by “greenfield” projects for in which groups would be encouraged take on the design of a community “learning centre”  Participants would be given a minimum set of criteria outlining safety and legal requirements and left to use their imagination as to a design.



D)  ADVANCE EDUCATION BY SUPPORTING RESEARCH AND INNOVATION, AND BY PROMOTING BEST PRACTICES IN THE FIELD OF EDUCATION AND TRAINING

Education 20/20 Inc. realizes the importance research and innovation and will encourage research and researchers through publicizing research findings, identifying and promoting underserved areas, and featuring researchers in our public sessions. 

Existing research, it seems to us, focuses almost exclusively on institution based education, early childhood, the young and literacy. We believe that other issues also could benefit from additional research such as the role of credentials and qualifications, determining levels of achievement in key subjects, choice in education, delivery options, alternatives to age level grouping and continuing education.


Survey of Interests and needs

Education 20/20 has initiated on-line public surveys to gather stakeholder views on the goals of public education as published on the Department of Education website. We start with goals because they are a basic direction- setting activity, and are the first step towards renewing the philosophy and vision of education for our Province.


Occasional papers

We plan a series of occasional papers, short papers on issues related to any aspect of education.  Papers may report research findings, best practices, innovations, and points of view.  Papers will be aimed at the general public and be widely available electronically.  Occasional papers provide information and views on a variety of subjects but also provide an opportunity, and even encouragement, to people to clarify their views and “grow’ in their chosen field. The first is a brief report on a series of workshops held throughout the Province earlier this year. Another, on Charter Schools, arises from a request following a public meeting, a third responds at least in part, to providing a framework making decisions about education.  (Copies of some papers, not yet released, are included with our supporting documents).  


Assessment

We continue our exploration of the role of assessment in the education process and concluded that, while formative assessment is a legitimate role for educational institutions, summative evaluation is not.  Our activities to date have been exploratory in nature: we find, for example (1) at the international level, neither Canada nor the USA have  “qualification authorities, (b) Canadian education authorities tend to rely on input measures (what is taught, rather than output measures (what has been learned) for purposes of comparison and (3) there appears to be no widely recognized graduation document available that provided the information that employers need or want when making their decisions to accept or reject applications. We are still in the “information gathering” stage but expect to become more active in the coming months.
