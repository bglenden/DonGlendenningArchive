Letter to Charlottetown Guardian (August 20, 2018) using a bus transportation analogy to frame education spending and accountability. Don argues that while government investment in education (like bus purchases and maintenance) is important, accountability should focus on outcomes—improvements in literacy, numeracy, self-management, and skills—not merely inputs. He expresses concern that recent graduates enrolling in upgrading courses and employer complaints about new hire skills suggest disappointing returns on education investment. This demonstrates his pragmatic, outcome-focused approach to education policy evaluation.


August 20, 2018


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir: 

What part of education should government pay for? 


When I purchase a bus ticket, I am seeking safe and comfortable transportation; I know that the price of my ticket includes many factors including the purchase and maintenance of the bus. bridge tolls, drivers’ wages, staff training, lease and maintenance of the bus depot, licenses and permits, etc.   My assessment of the quality of service, however is based on the journey being safe, on time, reasonably priced, courteous treatment and competent drivers.


The same applies when I pay my portion of the Education budget, I do so to help Islanders gain the skills needed to “thrive” in the years ahead. Yes, I know that the cost includes text books, salaries, staff development, snow clearing, buildings etc. etc. but my judgement of return on my tax investment lies in the new levels of literacy, numeracy, self- management and other skills Islanders need in future years.


All this by way of saying that, while I commend our government for providing additional staff, adding technology and the like, my real interest is in the actual and expected improvement in services rendered and levels achieved.  Other evidence would be aa decline in the number of recent graduated enrolling in educational upgrading or comments from employers about skill levels of new hires. I am not sure that I found the latest figures but the ones I found were not encouraging.


Sincerely, 



Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
