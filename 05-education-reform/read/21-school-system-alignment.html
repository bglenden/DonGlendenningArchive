School System Alignment

Occasional paper No. 100 July 14, 2014





There is a problem with basic skills; whether it’s real or imagined, one cannot be sure but whichever the case, it needs to be dealt with. It arises frequently and in a number of contexts; for some there is the concern about PEI test results, but it is also about getting into postsecondary studies or securing work, but I also encounter it from the public at large. It is not always easy to understand; it hides under different labels such as basic skills, essential skills, employability skills, BTSD, literacy and numeracy. I decided to try to learn more about it and have been talking with others and searching the literature to see what pops out.


And none of these have anything to do with the teaching process


My first conclusion is that exploring basic skills is more than I can handle so I’m focusing on mathematics I’ve been looking through the literature and talking with people. For my exploration, I decided to focus on the field of math, not that it is a subject of special interest or study, but because, at least in my experience, it is easier to keep the conversation on track. The chances are that, what I learn about math, will help me understand other subject areas as well. The following observations arise from my reading, discussing and reflecting on the subject:


  • One quickly concludes that, compared to other fields, there is a paucity of information about education, including mathematics education, available in the public domain, and the information that is provided is usually somewhat peripheral to the issue of improving education, let alone improving math: in addition, there is little dialogue between educators and the public they serve. This in spite of the fact that there is a significant improvement in the quality of education when teachers, parents and the public work together in fostering education. My own review of education studies, for example, did not reveal a practice of inclusion of community members as commission members; the same can be said for curriculum committees. And I cannot recall ever hearing off a discussion between a teacher or a parent with a focus on the improvement of the learner’s math rather than accommodating his or her shortcomings. While communiation is a two way street, the level of interaction called for is likely to remain low until teachers integrate it into their teaching style and schedule.


  • The education delivery process needs some realignment. By realignment, I mean that the various parts of our education service need review and likely some from fine tuning to ensure that we have a well-functioning operation. The statement of philosophy, for instance is twenty five years old and should be written to include current thinking, the outcome identification process should include consultation with the community it serves, community based benchmarks should be spelled out and conflicts removed from the assessment and grading practices.

Perhaps the first issue is the matter of whether or not there is a math issue. Most common examples the math of retailing, personal finance, entry level into employment, or skills of every day living. Others seem concerned about students lacking the math needed to enrol in postsecondary courses. I am not sure that these are one and the same. I find the first issue to be one of role and responsibility of schools when it comes to the teaching of math. Our schools appear to have settled their role to be that of “helping students develop to their fullest capacities. The OECD view assigns schools two roles, one of helping students develop their full capacities as citizens and the other is to ensure that our communities have the skills needed to ensure their future. To me, the inclusion of “citizens” in the statement sharpens both the focus of what is to be taught and the level of accountability of the system.

Math is not my field of study but in my search, I found two approaches to organizing math instruction:

  • The “logical” approach which sorts math into areas of numeracy/arithmetic, elementary mathematics, Euclidean geometry, elementary algebra, trigonometry, calculus, set and function, complex numbers, logarithmic functions and infinite series.

  • The “conventional” approach which sorts math into arithmetic, algebra, geometry, trigonometry, calculus, probability and statistics

It’s not unusual at this time of year to have our cars checked for alignment; we may not realize it, but school systems also struggle with alignment issues. While educators usually think only of curriculum alignment, we need to also check alignment of:

  • School practice and research

  • Outcomes with life roles

  • Assessment criteria with those in life outside school

  • School reporting with specified outcomes

  • Timetables and individual learning speeds

  • Benchmarks and community standards

  • priorities


Improving alignment may require changing priorities, rewriting resource material, adding community members to committees, making surveys, If you have concerns about your child’s progress, I suggest that start with the course outcomes, review them first with your child then take them to the parent teacher meeting. Doing so will help focus the discussion. In fact, sometimes copies of relevant outcomes are given to each student as part of the orientation process at the beginning of each new course. If not, they are readily available on the website of the Department of Education
