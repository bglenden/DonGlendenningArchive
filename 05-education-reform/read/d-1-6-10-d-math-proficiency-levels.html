Student Proficiency Levels


Occasional Paper D-1-6-1-D July 31, 2015


Education, like life, is a journey and like any journey needs recognizable checkpoints that inform us of progress towards our goals, however, the current system of school grade levels has lost much of its value as a measure and report of what people know and are able to do when they complete their high school studies. There may be many reasons for this development but let me share four:

  1. Quality of outcomes

Outcomes are widely used as a basis for developing curriculum; outcomes apply at many levels including program, course, unit, and lesson level; skills and competencies apply only at the level of the learner. Developing specific learning is a challenge and, unfortunately, there appears to be little in the way of ttrainingfor the task in Canada; neither is there a recognized developer’s guide. Based on the writer’s examination, the development of quality OBE based programming is still a work in progress

  1. Basis of determining levels

One does not measure the height of a mountain or length of a walk by the age of the hiker; neither should one describe the proficiency level of a person by the age of the learner. Levels of difficulty or achievement in a subject should arise from an examination and achievement at a level be assigned regardless of the age of the learner.

  1. Responsibility

The role of a teacher is to teach, to help students learn new skills and to increase levels of performance in existing skills. As part of the instructional process, teachers gather information from all sources and, based on the findings, design new strategies to help learners with their learning. That is a normal part of the teaching process. Assessing students for the purpose of assigning levels of proficiency and ranking are not teaching functions, in fact, may not even be an education function, and represent a conflict of interest between teacher and student, thereby interfering with a teacher’s ability to teach.

  1. Social promotion

The increasing expectation that every child will graduate from high school has led to the practice of social promotion, that is, promoting students to the next grade level whether or not they have met the established criteria. This practice poses significant problems for postsecondary institutions and employers who want, and usually need, a known level of proficiency on some skills to ensure entry and even safe and competent performance


The above has led the writer to seek an alternative approach to measuring and reporting what students know and are able to do at any stage of their studies. This paper argues that that assessment function should:

• Be independent of people who teach

• Certify individual’s proficiency level

• Be open to individuals regardless of age or prior formal education

• Not a competition; establish a person’s level not rank

• Be confidential


The components of such a system should include:

  • A comprehensive analysis of the competencies of subject fields – our search for a comprehensive competency or outcome analysis of school subjects drew a blank. As a result, students are presented with a list of outcomes or competencies that adults think that students can learn. This, in our view, suggests limits to their learning. We would prefer an open analysis of a field with levels identified and options put in the hands of the learner rather than proscribed by a teacher.

  • A criterion referenced rating scale – Outcomes based Education requires criterion referenced rating scales. Some were found but our search did not turn up any in use on any of the provincial public school systems. It is urgent that we modernize the rating scale at the earliest possible date.

  • Recognizable levels of proficiency – people differ from each other in terms of proficiency based on two measures, the range of competencies and level of achievement on each.

  • Benchmark

  • An assessment model

  • An independent body to carry out individual assessments.



An independent model described above has a number of benefits including:

  • Let’s students know where they stand

  • Reduces chances of bias

  • Improves consistency

  • Reduces teacher workload

  • Supports independent learning

  • Increases opportunities for innovative learning and teaching

  • Well-described levels of achievement are an aid to learning


Examples of operating models that include at least some of the proposed features include:

  • The Apprenticeship Red Seal program where apprentices take instruction in institutions, gain experience in real work situations, levels of proficiency are certified by a provincial agency and are free to practice their trade anywhere in Canada.

  • Canadian Language Benchmarks, which has benchmarked a number of proficiency levels in both official languages and tests and certifies proficiency levels of new Canadians

  • The Canadian Red Cross Swim Program allows swimmers to work or continue training anywhere in Canada.

  • The Royal Canadian Conservatory of Music offers examinations in music that are recognized across the country.

  • Competitive sports separate the function of coaching and referring.
