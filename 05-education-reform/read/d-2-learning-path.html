Learning path


Report No. D-2 March 16, 2015


To learn is to change behaviour, to gain new knowledge, new skills or new attitudes. Learning involves engaging in an experience that has a formative effect on the way we think, feel and act.


To better understand the learning process, Education 20/20convened a workshop, From a Learner’s Perspective, and invited a panel of young people to tell how they went about learning and what support they found most helpful. Key words were listed on a flip chart; the list was long, however, on further discussion the panel members settled on the following steps they took on the learning path:

HELP NEEDED

Gather Information Undergo assessment Motivate self

Observe others Attend lectures Seek counselling Observe demonstration Attempt task Undergo assessment

Practice Coach others Self evaluate

Take instruction Obtain feedback Take diagnosis

Reflect on experience Use resource materials Observe others












Students were asked the order in which they used help; not surprising, students followed quite different paths; one student said his first step was to Google the topic; another said if it involved a new skill, he would probably attempt it first, while others liked the traditional lecture as a place to start. Since panel members were currently in school or recent graduates, much of the discussion revolved around gaining new knowledge rather than skills or attitudes. Our first chart was linear and included the following steps:

  • Decide to learn

  • Explore options

  • Attempt task

  • Use help

  • Reach expectations

  • Exit


It soon became evident that learning is anything but linear, different purposes or styles of learning do not substantially change the range of help needed although it may change the order in which help is needed and the length of time on the path. What constitutes help and how it is made available is still under discussion. Our thanks to: Tanya Craig for convening and chairing the youth panel; youth panel members Callen Cudmore, Ryan Rioux, Iain Cudmore and Stephanie Mayne; Mike Chapman for facilitating discussion and charting the first path; Holly Moses and students of the Early Childhood Education Program at Holland College and the many others foe support, comment and advice.
