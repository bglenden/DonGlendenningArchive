Newsletter 200-5 (August 20, 2020) critiquing the conflation of schooling with education. Don notes World Bank research showing this confusion affects developing countries and Canada alike—comparing it to "going to the supermarket" versus "shopping." He observes people speak of "Grade 9 or Grade 12 education" as if duration equals education, and they ask "where I got my education" meaning school location. This conceptual confusion extends to curriculum and grading. Don proposes granular grading—rather than overall subjects, grade components like "number," "shape," "statistics" separately. He notes some nations have established qualification authorities (Canada hasn't). This reflects his precision-oriented approach to educational language and structures.

200-5 August 20, 2020

When schools and ministries are managed well, learning happens-World Bank


SCHOOL IS WHERE I WORK


I’ve mentioned earlier a World Bank report about education in developing countries; seems that education officials have a habit of confusing going to school and getting an education. Well guess what, it happens in Canada as well. It’s a bit like going to the supermarket rather than going shopping


People speak of a “Grade 9 or a Grade 12 education” as though we can measure education by how long one spends in school. I’ve also been asked “where I got my education” meaning where I went to school. ( I find this very confusing in that I was a fulltime student in four different institutions before I received my first degree)


The issue also arises in curriculum development and instruction; I understand how, in learning a subjett it makes sense to study some things first but what one teaches and another learns are separate issues.


Some countries have established qualification authorities to examine institutional grading systems – but not USA and Canada.




It seems to me also, that we could move our grading downward s notch. Instead of a math grade we could base grades on the next level of detail may work; onstead of grades on math, grade omnumber, sgappe, statistics, geometry, etc.e, speed and shapes











Don Glendenning, Editor
