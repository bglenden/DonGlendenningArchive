Article No. 88 (June 4, 2020) on "Some Structural Issues" outlines four starting points for change: clear compelling provincial education vision, functionally-based organization, learner-centered instructional model, and autonomy with accountability for professionals at delivery points. Don includes Epstein's Framework on Involvement, which details six dimensions of family and community engagement: parenting support, communication, volunteering, home-based learning, decision-making participation, and community collaboration. This framework emphasizes removing barriers to involvement and providing meaningful, flexible opportunities for families to engage with schools and support student learning.

Some structural Issues


aaaEducation services on PEI need an overhaul; they are narrowly focused, cognitively based, bureaucratic, lack accountability, and focus on program delivery rather than on learner development – they also lack a self-correcting mechanism.


I do not, we cannot, blame the current government, the previous government or any single government that went before for the current status; Government has a responsibility however, for instituting corrective measures.


Traditionally, blame for education’s shortcomings fall to teachers and schools, the World Bank points out the need for refoem within Ministries themselves. My reading, conversations and experience confirms this view and identifies the following as a starting point for change.


  1. A clear and compelling vision of education for the Province.

  2. A functionally based organization.

  3. A learner centered instructional model

  4. Autonomy and accountability delegated to the professionals at the point of delivery











Epstein’s Framework on Involvement


■ aaaParenting. Assist families with parenting skills, family

support, understanding child and adolescent development, and setting home conditions to support learning

at each age and grade level. Assist schools in understanding families’ backgrounds, cultures, and goals for children.

■ Communicating. Communicate with families about

school programs and student progress. Create two-way

communication channels between school and home that

are effective and reliable.

■ Volunteering. Improve recruitment and training to

involve families as volunteers and as audiences at the

school or in other locations. Enable educators to work

with volunteers who support students and the school.

Provide meaningful work and flexible scheduling.

■ Learning at Home. Involve families with their children

in academic learning at home, including homework,

goal setting, and other curriculum-related activities.

■ Decision Making. Include families as participants in

school decisions, governance, and advocacy activities

through school councils or improvement teams, committees, and other organizations.

■ Collaborating with the Community. Coordinate resources and services for families, students, and the school with

community groups, including businesses, agencies, cultural and civic organizations, and colleges or universities
