PUBLIC ENGAGEMENT


Summary of notes



See also “engagement” file and articles in “education” file

Background:


It is widely recognized that the level and quality of education is key to our future as individuals, families, communities and countries. There is also a widely held view that the education system we now have is unlikely to provide the education our people need, especially our young people. There is a widespread call for change from many quarters, including educators, employers, and the public at large. One of those calls is for increased involvement, no increased engagement, of the public in the development and delivery of education and educational services.

During the past year, our cities witnessed a number of occupations; the reasons may be obscure and open to interpretation but the unease between governors and the governed, between citizens and their institutions, is evident; some would even say that the two are estranged. During the same period, the writer has engage many Islanders in discussions about education and here, also, exists a significant divide between providers and users of educational services, while concern exists about all levels of education, most people engaged me in conversations about the public school system. And, while I have not read widely on the subject, I find it to be of concern among governments in other countries as well. Even when decision makers seek the views of others affected by their decision, those consulted often feel that they are being manipulated.

What is Public engagement?

Public engagement refers to the is the interaction of specialists with the public in in the decision making process. Typically, specialists consult, that is, they arrange opportunities for the public to make its views known about a matter under development. But the public has become quite cynical about the consultation process viewing it simply as an exercise in public relations.


But people want more than simply being consulted, they want to engage in the setting of policies and the making of decision. The current term for this kind or level of participation is “public engagement”. It results in putting more decision-making powers directly into the hands of ordinary people – it is really grass roots democracy in action. Public engagement starts with the setting of the agenda, working from shared information, a transparent agenda, and ensuring inclusiveness, control and transparency.

Principles:


Wikipedia has identified nine principles of public engagement:

  1. “Participants should join those organising the process in setting terms of reference for the whole exercise, and framing the questions that they will discuss.

  2. The group organising, or in overall control of, the process should be broad based, including stakeholders with different interests on the subject being discussed.

  3. There should be a diversity of information sources and perspectives available to participants.

  4. There should be space for the perspectives of those participants who lack specialist knowledge of the area concerned to engage in a two-way exchange with those possessing specialist knowledge.

  5. There should be complete transparency of the activities carried out within the process to those both inside and outside it.

  6. Those without a voice in policy-making should be enabled to use the consultation process as a tool for positive political change. This should be embedded in the process by sufficient funds being made available for follow-up work after their initial conclusions have been reached.

  7. The process should contain safeguards against decision-makers using a process to legitimise existing assumptions or policies.

  8. All groups involved in the process should be given the opportunity to identify possible strategies for longer-term learning, development and change on a range of issues relating to their conclusions.

  9. The group organising, or in overall control of, the process should develop an audit trail through the process, to explain whether policies were changed, what was taken into account, what criteria were applied when weighing up the evidence from the process, and therefore how the views of those involved in the participatory process may have made a difference. This should be explored together with as many those involved in all levels of the process as possible.”

Benefits:

The benefits of public engagement include

  • Fosters better communication

  • Generates broader viewpoints

  • Informs the community

  • Generates new ideas

  • Increasing the likelihood of new ideas being adopted

  • Helps gain public understanding and trust

Recent Examples


As one would expect, public engagement in education occurs in a great variety of ways. During a recent literature scan, the writer found these to be interesting:

  • The State of Kentucky recently reviewed and revitalized it’s education system using public engagement as a cornerstone of the change process.

  • Birmingham University in England recently appointed a Professor of Public Engagement.

  • One article reported the gathering of some 5,000 people in New York to discuss the future of Lower Manhattan.

  • The National PTA in the USA gives a family engagement award to schools that engage families in significant ways in school activities.

Increasing Public Engagement


  • Inform the public


At present, most information about education is initiated by and aligned with organizations that deliver educational services including Departments of Education, Home and School Associations, Canadian Education Association, Teacher Federations, etc. Writing about education for public consumption is practically non-existent; in our community, media coverage is generally focused on the operation of schools. One would find it difficult to find an article that delves into the intricacies of teaching, diagnosing, measuring progress, standards, curriculum, etc. Yet, such programs exist in many other fields especially health and the law.


  • Engage the public

Knowing, that is being informed, is one thing but being confident to participate in discussion and debate is another.



  • Involve the public

  • Raise public awareness of and involvement in educational matters

  • Bring issues to the fore
