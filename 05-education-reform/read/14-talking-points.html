This substantial policy document (dated August 23, 2022) presents a comprehensive framework for education system reform in PEI. Don develops detailed definitions of education, learners, learning, teaching, and schooling, emphasizing that education is a service distinct from schooling. He outlines six life roles (individual, family member, student, citizen, consumer, producer) that education should support. The document identifies structural issues including unclear purpose, inadequate services identification, misaligned organizational structure, insufficient teacher autonomy, and weak accountability mechanisms. It proposes OECD and World Bank research to support system redesign and addresses grading practices, noting that students should demonstrate competencies rather than receive grades based on effort or equity.

INTRODUCTION

It is widely recognized that education is a key factor in determining each citizen’s quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of our citizens will determine to a significant degree the quality of the communities in which we live. Government support is justified for two reasons, one to support personal growth and development for every citizen; the other is to ensure the common good. Educating is a complex service made so by the fact that each person is unique in many ways, unique in basic physical, mental, emotional make-up; unique in the range and nature of previous experience, unique in perceived futures, but also unique in terms of learning pace, style and needs. The decision to draft this paper arises from discussion with a variety of stakeholders and the conclusion that the role and functioning of our education system is unclear. In thinking about education, I’ve adopted re view of the Orgon Education that each person fills six life roles – individual, family member, student, citizen, consumer and producer. Education can support any and all

The division to draft this paper arises from discussion with a large variety off stakeholders with the hope of adding clarity to our educational endeavors.


EDUCATION


Education has several meanings:

  1. Services put in place by a community to encourage and support the education of its citizens

  2. A process of arranging experiences which result in others learning or broadening the knowledge, skills, attitudes and other attributes.

  3. The state or level of a person’s ability to deal with daily living

  4. A sector of the economy.


Key definitions:

  • Learner – a person who, for whatever reason, needs, wants or is trying to bring about change in his/her life – trying to change an attitude, gain new knowledge, or add skills. But learners are neither empty jars nor blank slates; each learner carries three pieces of luggage to every new learning situation (1) certain physical, mental and emotional conditions with which a learner is born, (b) prior learning resulting from home circumstances, formal study, travel, state of health, personal interests, culture in which learner operates and (3) aspirations and imagined future including where one will live, the work one will do and the lifestyle one will have, nature of the family, quality of life, etc. . Learners have different interests, experiences, capabilities and needs.

Learners also fill a variety of life roles, that of an individual, family member, student, citizen, consumer and producer

Although only the learner can learn, learning may occur in group setting

RESTRUCTURING CALLED FOR


The literature speaks of the need for educational reform. I see it as a plea for clarity. it’s time to review and restate the education task and to circulate the results widely throughput the system. Structural matts include:

1. Purpose – the literature refers to helping people find a place in society; that place is a role and should be named or described – a citizen, a producer, a consumer or? Both content and assessment demand it.

2. Services - Education is a service, typical education services include: information, counselling, instruction, products and technology, quality assurance. governance, funding and human resources. Learners may need one or all services at any point in their education journey and services should be front and center in our offerings. Services need to be clearly identified and available to all Island citizens not only to citizens enrolled in schools and programs. Whether or not all services are paid for by government is a separate issue.

3. Structure -” Form follows function” is still the standard when structuring any venture; one would expect, therefore, that the education structure would reflect services provided. Instead, the Department appears to be structured around clients served.

4. Autonomy – as mentioned earlier, education is a service. Front line service providers (our teachers) need both the expertise and autonomy to respond to learner needs.

5. Accountability - The OECD tells us that PISA results are better for students in schools with autonomy for their day-to-day operation; the World Bank advises Ministries of Education to delegate. Mathew Bedard of the Montreal Economic Institute points out that “autonomy and accountability jointly applied” would cost practically nothing and allow education to better adapt to the diversity of student needs and aptitudes. Yet teachers and principals in PEI appear to have little freedom to tailor services to suit individual learner needs.

6. Grades and grading, represents an attempt to apply standardized measurement to levels of achievement on the education journey. Grades also serve as a communication device between a provider and user of a service. Conversations with parents and employers suggest that report cards need a further level of detail on subjects studied. Most developed countries have established qualification bodies to deal with the rationalization of credentials and qualifications; Canada should consider the same.


These structural issues are not new; no doubt readers have others to propose. All should be considered. They need to be dealt with soon, and should be dealt with in a very public manner.
