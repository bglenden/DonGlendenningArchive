Don argues that early childhood development is critical, with families as first teachers. He proposes family-focused educational services similar to medical advice for parents with young children. He challenges the focus on preparing children for school, arguing schools should instead adapt to serve children's developmental needs.

February 3, 2011


Editor 

Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

Ready for School


Education is a life long process.  Pr5oviding each child with a good education is a worthy objective; school is a means to that end.  But, education begins long before we start school and after we leave.   


The early years are formative years; family members, especially parents, are the first teachers.   Much of what we become is the result of what we experience during our first five years.    Coaching Many parents need, or at least would like help; it would be money well estedinvspent.


When parents need professional advice on health matters they can consult a family physician or perhaps request a visit from a nurse.  Why not, therefore, a similar arrangement for advice on educational matters?  It would likely require a small team of practitioners with a background in early childhood development.  Such a service could well extend into family advice even for children in the early years of school.







Sometimes I think that we should pay more attention to preparing schools to deal with children rather than preparing schildren to deal with schools.  After all,  that is why we have schools 


We hear a great del about parents preparing children for school, but shouldn’t we really be talking about preparing schools for children?  Both have the same objective, preparing children for life.


.


After all, children are who they are.  it be the reverse, prparents talking about getting their children read for school




Sincerely


Don Glendenning
