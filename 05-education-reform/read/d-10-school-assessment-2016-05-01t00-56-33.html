Student Testing

Occasional Paper No. 10 May 15, 2016


Background


Testing, standards, passing, assessment, grading, evaluation are all words associated with schools, and they are all words that cause stress in various degrees to students, teachers, parents and the community; according to what I read, the level of stress is rising. We seldom discuss, however, what is being measured, how, by whom and how the results are to be used. The issue deserves attention; perhaps we can even find a framework to help our understanding. First some definitions:

  • Testing - an objective and standardized method for estimating the extent of achievement based on a sample of desirable achievement

  • Assessment - is a process of gathering information about a person’s performance on a task or

set of tasks for the purpose of initiating improvement

  • Evaluation - an overall judgement of what has been learned

  • Grading - the process of applying standardized measurements of varying levels of achievement in a course.

  • Standards - a readily distinguishable level of performance, demonstrated through the making of a product or provision of a service, which is deemed to be worthy of recognition.


Purposes

According to Robert Paul Wolff (A Discourse on Grading, 1969) there are three elements of student grading:

  • Criticism or formative assessment, sometimes called analyses and feedback, is at the heart of teaching and learning and involves the give and take between teacher and learner about what happened or should happen during the learning process; assessment focuses on learning, teaching, outcomes, competencies, skills or even the acquisition of knowledge. This kind of assessment provides information for improving learning and teaching and is an interactive process between student and teacher designed to inform both learner and teacher how well their students are learning what teachers are teaching. The information is used by teachers to make changes in what will be taught next and when, where, how and even why. It is considered feedback to students, shared fully and probably confidentially, unique to each learner, course or incident based and sometimes anonymous and not connected to passing or grading.

  • Evaluation or summative assessment is used to establish a person’s level or grade in a subject or field. Evaluation is criterion referenced, focused on establishing a level of achievement and may take into account more than course content and academic success depending on its purpose including discussion, cooperation, attendance, presentation, etc.

  • Ranking involves measuring hiw one person stacks up against others of the same age, number of years of study, in other countries; ranking serves a number of functions in an educational system including accountability, gate-keeping, instructional diagnosis and monitoring of student achievement.

Who measures what?

The role of a teacher is to teach, to help students learn new skills and to increase levels of performance in existing skills, to gain new knowledge or change attitudes. As part of the instructional process, teachers gather information from all sources and, based on the findings, design new strategies to help learners with their learning. It can be argued that that assessing students for the purpose of assigning levels of proficiency and ranking are not teaching functions, in fact, may not even be an education function, and represent a conflict of interest between teacher and student. For these reasons, most sectors and many educational organizations separate the teaching and the grading functions; apprenticeship, International baccalaureate program, qualification authorities, music and other are exmples of the latter approach.

The logic of using the same terminology to label both administrative and grading purposes escapes me; I’m sure that it must puzzle students and parents as well. The two should be separate.
