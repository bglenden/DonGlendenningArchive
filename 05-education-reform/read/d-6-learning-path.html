Early draft exploring how learners conceptualize their own learning process. Convened workshop "From a Learner's Perspective" with youth panel to identify learning support activities and steps. Generated 13 key activities including gather information, obtain feedback, take instruction, self-evaluate, coach others, practice, observe others, attend lecture, seek counselling, observe demonstration, attempt task, and undergo assessment. Found students followed different learning paths rather than linear progression. Identified five major steps: decide to learn, explore options, attempt task, use help, reach expectations. Acknowledges learning purposes vary and paths are non-linear. Learning Path

Occasional Paper D-6 March 28, 2015


To learn is to change behaviour, to gain new knowlsdge, new skills or new attitudes. Learning involves engaging in an experience that has a formative effect on the way we think, feel and act.

To better understand the learning process, Education 20/20convened a workshop, From a Learner’s Perspective and invited a panel of young people to tell how they went about learning and what support they found most helpful. Key words were listed on a flip chart; the list was long, however, on further discussion the panel members settled on the following steps they took the learning path:

  • Gather Information

  • Take diagnosis

  • Obtain feedback

  • Take instruction

  • Self evaluate

  • Coach others

  • Practice

  • Observe others

  • Attend lecture

  • Seek counselling

  • Observe demonstration

  • Attempt task

  • Undergo assessment

  • Motivate self















Students were asked the order in which they needed help; not surprisingly, students followed quite different paths. As an example, one student said his first step would be to Google the topic; another said if it involved a new skill, he would probably attempt it first, while others liked the traditional lecture as a place to start. Since panel members were in school or recent graduates, much of the discussion revolved around gaining new knowledge rather than skills or attitudes. Our first chart was lineaand included the following steps:

  • Decide to learn

  • Explore options

  • Attempt task

  • Use help

  • Reach expectations


But learning is anything but linear; different purposes for learning did not substantially change the range of help needed, although it may change the order and the amount of time on the path.different purposes may make the path longer. What constitutes help and how it is made available is still under discussion.


Our thanks to: Tanya Craig for convening and chairing the youth panel, Youth Panel membersCallen Cudmore, Ryan Rioux, Iain Cudmore and Stephanie Mayne; Mike Chapman for facilitating discussion and the many others who offered comment and advice.
