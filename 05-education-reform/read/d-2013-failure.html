A lengthy, exploratory draft examining whether schools should teach students to deal with failure. Don distinguishes between failure as a life experience and schools' instructional role. He argues that test results are diagnostic information for teaching improvement (formative), not measures of student failure. He critiques schools taking on ranking functions alongside teaching, proposing three metaphors: sport (with coaching and umpiring), race (with judges), and journey (with guides). This philosophical piece reflects Don's mature thinking about systemic purposes and the distinction between learning support and competitive ranking.

Failure in school

Should schools teach students how to fail?

The matter of student failure has come up in a number of recent discussions; there is a general view that failure happens to all of us, we need to learn how to deal with it.  Failure in school is an acceptable experience, perhaps even a desirable one. 

The role of teachers is to help others learn.   As part of the teaching process, teachers test students to find out what they‘ve learned.  Test results may show the student has learned  a lot or a little;  not having learned has many causes; it’s a diagnosis and you cannot fail a diagnosis.  Based on test results, teachers teach again taking the test results into account and, hopefully, the results will be satisfactory.

But we’ve asked our schools to do something else, to sort their students – to identify who gets prizes and diplomas – and who doesn’t.  What was intended as an aid to teaching has become a sorting process, a process that has nothing to do with teaching.  The guide and advocate has now become a judge and the teacher is no longerthe best, whatever that means, name the best, which ajtomatically identifies the poorest.  something

, and as parents, we make huge investments in providing Our children go to school to learn; At this point, I’m simply trying to sort out my own thinking.  Let me Friends tell me that young people, students in particular, need to learn to deal with failure.  The comment usually comes up in the course of conversations about schooling, and in particular about standards and social promotion.  I understand what’s being said, but the role of our schools in dealing with failure is still very murky, at least in my mind.

So, I look for or try to create a working definition.  A quick browse of the internet soon reveals such phrases as not achieving the desired end, falling short and non-performance of what is requested or expected.  This is helpful but, in the case of education, I am still left with questions about the desired end, whose expectations, and how the shortfall is determined.

I am not arguing against the lessons to be learned from failure; my concern is only about the role of schools and teachers.  Is failure a legitimate school activity?  Does failure belong in schools or should it be shared by other teaching agencies, the church, sports for example?  What role, if any, should a school play in creating activities that result in failure?  Should schools create circumstances where significant numbers of students are likely to fail?  Or is the role of a school to help students deal with the impact of failures that may occur in their lives?

One of the obvious contributors to failure is the tyranny of time (Readers may recall my letter on this subject in this newspaper shortly before Christmas); we might be amazed at the reduction in the failure rate by simply giving students more time to learn.  After all, learning is a journey, or perhaps a destination, not a race.  Reaching the destination is the objective not how far you travel each day.
