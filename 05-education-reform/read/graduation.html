HIGH SCHOOL GRADUATION



We need a lively debate about the meaning of high school graduation. What does it mean to be a high school graduate? What should we expect a high school graduate to be able to do? These are basic questions; perhaps that is why they are seldom asked. Until we deal with that question, answers to other questions about school matters will remain fuzzy. From time to time there is a flurry of interest in how well our students do in comparison to students in other countries but, such flurries are usually short lived. My thinking on the subject runs something like this.


The role of schools is to prepare young people to live life to the fullest, which includes enjoying life, making a living and contributing to their communities.


Schools, on the other hand, see their roles as preparing people for further education, they look to colleges and universities for direction as to what should be taught at the secondary school level. They have that focus even though a large percentage, perhaps even up to one-half, do not go to college or university but go directly into the community or to work. As evidence, publicity about graduates and graduations emphasizes high grades, academic awards and acceptance at universities; little is said about success of graduates going directly to work.


Given this orientation, it is natural that secondary schools look to colleges and universities to determine what should be taught or learned in high schools – subjects that meet university and college entrance requirements.. Universities organize around disciplines, therefore, secondary school organize around disciplines also.

Typically students are required to gain credits in the following subjects in order to graduate from high school:


Language Arts

Mathematics

Science

Social studies

Art

Phys. Ed and Health

Business/Technology

Social
Science

Life Skills

Electives



If schools were more focused on what students will do when they leave school, they would look to the community to determine what should be taught. For example, Oregon Education Act for the 21st Century identifies the following life roles we all fill: individual, learner, producer, citizen, consumer and family member. The state of Michigan, on the other hand, states their high school graduation requirements as (State of Michigan on August 22, 1990):

  1. Students will be proficient in listening, speaking, reading, writing, mathematics, and the use of technology.

  2. Students will apply knowledge, concepts, and processes acquired through the study of the arts, sciences, and social sciences.

  3. Students will demonstrate a healthy self-esteem.

  4. Students will select and use thinking skills, individually and in groups, to solve problems and to make decisions.

  5. Students will be able to find, evaluate and use information.

  6. Students will demonstrate respect and co-operation within a community of individual and cultural differences.

  7. Students will demonstrate personal, social, civic, and environmental responsibility.

  8. Students will be self-directed learners who are adaptable to change.

  9. Students will demonstrate a positive work ethic.


In my views, the latter is a clearer statement of what to expect of a high school graduate and a better reflection of preparation for life.
