Letter to the Editor of Charlottetown Guardian (October 14, 2014) titled "Closing doors," written by Don Glendenning. The letter critiques a school's decision to move a struggling high school math student to a lower-level course, arguing this "closes doors" to college/university options and lifetime employment prospects. Don advocates for schools to expand opportunities through extra instruction and support rather than restrict them through course placement. He emphasizes that school decisions fundamentally shape student futures and expresses concern about institutional practices that limit rather than enable student potential.

October 14, 2014

Editor, Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

Closing doors


I didn’t have a happy Thanksgiving, in fact most of the past month has been stressful.  The cause of my stress is an event in the life of a student, a person I have never met but whose family I know and whose future has been limited because of a school decision.  I’ve heard similar stories before, but this time I actually “experienced” it.  Granted, I do not know the whole story but here’s my understanding.


A high school student, with hopes, even plans to go to college or university, was having difficulty with math; that’s not unusual; I am told that struggling with math is not uncommon.   So what are a school’s options?

  • Let the a student struggle and likely fail a grade, 
  • Offer an upgrading course after graduation
  • Provide extra instruction
  • Transfer to the student a lower level course
  • Blame the home


In this case, the school chose number 4; the student is now in a lower-level math class. This is a life-altering event in that it dramatically reduces the likelihood of that student going to college or university, it reduces the chance of a smooth transition to employment and  likely  a significant reduction in his lifetime income.  


Schools should increase opportunities for students, not reduce them. 



Sincerely


Don Glendenning

A long time student of education
