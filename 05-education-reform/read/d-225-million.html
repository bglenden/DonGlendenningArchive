Letter to editor by Don questioning what PEI receives for $225 million education spending, defining education as formative experiences, and arguing government should fund citizenship skills development while reconsidering funding for ancillary services like transportation and childcare.
Education Philosophy & Reform


July 7, 2015


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir: 

What do we get for $225 million


Education is a key to our individual and collective futures; the question is “What part should taxpayers pay for?”  My usual background sources weren’t very helpful but I believe it to be an important issue so I’ll venture some initial thoughts.


By education, I mean those experiences that has a formative effect on the way we think, feel or act.  The difference between your education and mine is reflected, not in what or where we studied but in the range and levels of things that you can do with what you know.  


There is fairly broad agreement that government should pay for experiences that help develop good citizenship skills; that means help to develop a mix of appropriate or required attitudes, skills and knowledge.  The nature and level of help a student needs will depend, to a large degree, on  the previous experiences and imagined futures of each student, but everyone seems to agreethat students need a strong mix of basic skills.   Beyond that, there is not much agreement but self -management and relational skills appear to be high on the list.


There is also a view, and one that I share, that schools should produce, and government should help pay for, the skills needed to maintain and enhance this community we call home, that would include our artists, health workers, skilled trades workers, farmers and fishers  to name but a few.


On the other hand, government probably should help pay for school transportation but not through the school budget; transportation to a medicalclonic or to a hospital is a health care issue but, by and large, is not a hospital or clinic’s responsibility.  One could also ask whether hungry children is a school issue, an education issue, a health issue and, of course whether or not schools need to provide care when n education A second is quality care while parents work, an important issue but not one that should  be foisted on schools, no matter how willing they are to accept it. 

Sincerely,

Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
