Letter from Don describing his investigation of PEI mathematics instruction, undertaken not as a math specialist but from recognizing math as a frequently-cited community concern. Don details his research: interviews with math educators, curriculum examination, survey administration, and internet research. Key findings include citizens need math fluency not extensive knowledge, tutoring helps struggling students, good teaching overcomes home disadvantages, more practice time needed, teacher preparation requires strengthening, competencies better than outcomes for curriculum development, and emerging rating scales reflect real-life criteria with self-assessment components.
Education Philosophy & Reform

Dear Editor:


Some time ago, I decided to explore math instruction on PEI; I did so, not because I am a math major, but because math is the subject mentioned most often in conversations as a topic of concern.  Furthermore, I thought it might be interesting to explore one subject area rather than having rambling conversations across the whole field.

I talked with people who know more about math than I do, examined Grade 9. 10, 11 and 12 math curriculum outlines, browsed a math text, tried to obtain a curriculum development handbook, skimmed most if not all Provincial Education websites a number of times,  convinced a dozen people to complete math survey and, of course scanned   the internet a number of times using a variety of search words and phrases..  As a result, my little “office” is awash in loose papers.  My findings:

  • Citizens don’t need a lot of math but they need to be fluent in math facts.
  • Tutoring during and after school classes seems to help students who are having difficulty with math
  • Good teaching can offset the effect of “poor homes”
  • Students need more time to practice math applications; 
  • Teachers may need more application/practice as part of their preparation
  • Competencies reflect community applications than outcomes and, therefore,  make a  better foundation for curriculum development
  • Emerging rating scales tend to reflect criteria used in real life and include self- rating as part of the process.

I continue to explore the issue.   

 Sincerely


Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
