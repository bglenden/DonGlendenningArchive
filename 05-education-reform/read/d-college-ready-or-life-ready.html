Don critiques the emphasis on "college readiness," arguing that education's ultimate goal is preparing people for life beyond school. He reframes college preparation as an interim step, not the end goal, and emphasizes that schools should equip students with diverse skills for multiple life roles. He calls for substantive public debate about educational objectives and expresses frustration at the absence of community dialogue on fundamental educational questions, positioning citizens as stakeholders entitled to voice their perspectives.


January 5, 2011


Editor

Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

College Ready or Life Ready


The New York Times on Tuesday, February 8 declared that “Most New York Students are Not College Ready”; in fact, according to the article, the percentage of “college ready” high school students is decreasing. I am sure that many educators on PEI would feel the same about Island students. I have some problems with that line of thinking.


The ultimate goal of education is to prepare people for life outside school. For some, that will occur at the end of high school; for others, that comes after further study. Preparation for life outside school is not something to be added or completed just before graduating. It is an integral part of all one’s education


But the preparation is pPreparing students for college or university is an interim goal; the ultimate goal of education, including schooling, is to prepare for life outside school. For some, that will take place at the end of high school, for others That is not to exclude preparing for college but even preparing for college is also only an interim step; how well we do that is the ultimate measure of the success of our education system.


People in the education system now will ultimately move out of the “student role” in school, college or university and into other life roles. Schools should help with the transition and provide a variety of skills used in later life. There is little doubt in my mind about the importance of our schools, colleges and universities in helping students prepare for these life roles but the detail of what they should do and how the should do it would, I am sure, produce both a variety of views and some lively discussion. But it is a debate we should have.


I am struck by the absence of public debate about important educational issues and urge readers to enter the dialogue.



Sincerely


Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
