Don counters the assumption that small class sizes automatically improve education, citing a school that improved dramatically by trading seven teaching positions for fourteen teacher assistants. This evidence-based argument shows that the composition of instructional support matters more than simple class-to-teacher ratios. The letter demonstrates Don's willingness to challenge conventional wisdom with concrete examples and suggests that intervention strategies must be thoughtfully targeted rather than formula-based.


January 6, 2014

Editor, Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

Class size 


The minister is right (McIsaac: The facts on class size, Guardian, October 4, 2014); small classes and low student staff ratio by themselves are no guarantee of better education.  Interventions needed to be better targeted..


As an example, I visited a school a few years ago that, with the agreement of the teachers’ union, traded seven teaching positions for fourteen teacher assistants.  Four years later, that school had moved from last position in the district to Number Three spot.  School officials attributed the improvement to the change in adult to learner ratio.


Sincerely


Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
