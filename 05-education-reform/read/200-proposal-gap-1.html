July 28, 2014 proposal from Education 20/20 Inc. (with which Don was involved) addressing perceived gaps between school delivery and education quality. Document cites Community Foundation of PEI's youth survey revealing employment concerns, educational deficiencies, and preparedness gaps. Bridging the gap

Background


Education is a lifelong process of gaining experiences that have a formative effect on the way we feel, think and act; it is a process of changing attitudes, skills and knowledge. Education is a key factor in determining one’s quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of our citizens will determine to a significant degree the future quality of our community and country.


After years of focusing on the transfer of knowledge deemed to be needed to prosper and enjoy life, thanks to technology, schools find that their clients have access to the same knowledge as the teacher. In place of knowledge, is a call for help in the development of skills needed to live and work in in a human community, skills that cannot be left to schools alone. Furthermore parents and the community at large are not only calling for change but want to play an active and ongoing role in its unfolding. Schools and educators generally find themselves in unfamiliar territory and are having difficulty adapting to the change.


Concerns among Islanders have been widely reported: (expansion needed)

  • PISA results

  • Charlottetown Area chamber

  • Home and School


In late 2013, The Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island (CFPEI) undertook a survey of over 200 Island youth on a number of issues that directly affect them; the report, Taking the Pulse of Island Youth, available on the Foundation’s website at www.cfpei.ca, provides a snapshot of the findings. Bright spots include a positive sense of community, emerging entrepreneurial spirit, volunteerism and new postsecondary offerings. The main concerns include a lack of jobs, surviving on low-paying jobs, educational deficiencies and drug and alcohol addictions. On the subject of education and learning, the study reports a high rate of completion of high school and postsecondary education, a high level of inclusivity and relatively affordable tuition costs; young people also concerns about their readiness for the next stage of life including “preparedness gaps with regard to readiness for post- secondary study and the workplace” – Vital Signs, CFPEI, 2013





Findings/conclusions:



Change results from many forces but is fueled by relevant information; without Information, the change process is likely soon to sputter and fail. Stakeholders need current, relevant and consistent information about a wide range of subjects, but it must also be independent. Our exploration of the subject reveals major information gaps among students now in school, parents with children still in school, out of school youth who are and feel inadequately prepared for further education or for work, and adults whose lives could be dramatically improved through selected educational enhancements. But having information by itself, is not a guarantee of change. Change also thrives on public engagement, that is being part of the setting of policies and the making of decision. Public engagement results in putting more decision-making powers directly into the hands of ordinary people – it is grass roots democracy in action. Public engagement starts with the setting of the agenda, working from shared information, a transparent agenda, and ensuring inclusiveness, control and transparency.



Proposal:

To fill the information gap and to facilitate educational change in PEI, Education 20/20 proposes to:

  1. Clarify the gap

  3. Better identify the gap through surveys of youth, employedevelop a series of short papers on educational topics. Topics to be based on interests and needs as identified by stakeholders. (See example attached)

  • As part of the development process, to use each paper as a discussion guide with a group at the community level and revise accordingly.

  • Distribute each paper widely in both print and electronic form


Each subject:

  • Maximum 2 pages

  • Standard format

  • Tightly focused

  • Both languages

  • Hosted on website

  • Distribute widely

  • Focused on parents as the key educators


Steps:

  1. Steering committee

  2. Survey parents as to information needs, where they get their info, format, media, etc about their children’s education

  3. Identify resource people

  4. Prepare paper

  5. Test with community group

  6. Publish in both official languages

  7. Use as a basis for local discussions

  8. Use as a basis for media releases


Potential topics:

  • Essential skills

  • Emotional awareness/intelligence

  • Social promotion

  • Preparedness for work

  • Formative evaluation

  • Learner feedback


Budget notes:

  • Experienced editor

  • Photographs as appropriate

  • 500 copies for initial distribution

  • Logo of sponsor

  • Electronic copy in colour; print copy in b&w

  • Experienced facilitator
