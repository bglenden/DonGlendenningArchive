BACKGROUND

In the course of discussions about education, mathematics is often put forth as a topic of concern; this document describes an exploration of the subject by Education 20/20 Inc.


DEFINITIONS:

  • Mathematics – the study of quantity, structure and space. (UPEI)

  • Education – any experience that has a formative effect on the way a person thinks, feels or acts

  • Math level – the extent and nature of a person’s attitudes, skills and knowledge in the field of math.

  • Numeracy – the ability to understand and work with numbers

  • Math education – programs and activities put in place to help people improve math skills


MATHEMATICS EDUCATION

Mathematics is considered essential in every day life and, for the most part, is organized around the conventional structure of mathematics: arithmetic, algebra, geometry, trigonometry, calculus, probability and statistics. The goal for students of PEI Grade 9 mathematics is to “become mathematically literate adults”.


Numeracy is also mathematics, the application of math, and is receiving increased attention in education circles; Numeracy is described as “having the confidence and skill to use numbers and mathematical approaches in all aspects of life - at work, in practical every-day activities at home and beyond, as consumers, in managing our finances, as parents helping our children learn, as patients making sense of health information, as citizens understanding the world about us”. A report by the PEI Department of Education on The Early Numeracy Intervention Program (ENIP) found that numeracy “gives students the tools and the skills to solve problems logically, and to developmental flexibility, independence of thought, self-efficacy, and the ability to persevere” According to the Conference Board of Canada, “Overall, Canada earns a “C” grade on inadequate numeracy skills."


With regard to instruction, educators are faced with a dilemma; teach mathematics, teach numeracy or teach both? The National Numeracy Organization (UK) proposes:

  1. Every teacher become a teacher of numeracy. That does not mean that every teacher becomes a math teacher but that numeracy, just like literacy- is recognized as an intrinsic part of every subject across the school curriculum.

  2. A new measure of numeracy proficiency should be introduced for all young people at the age of 14. This would establish a national benchmark for the level of numeracy needed for further study in all subjects between the ages of 14-19



  1. An additional and universally respected qualification in numeracy (or core math) is needed alongside GGCSE math. Politicians and curriculum and qualifications regulators must give serious and urgent consideration and introduction of dual GCSSEs in math and numeracy

CITIZENS MATH STUDY

Given the above, Education 2020 Inc. received funding from an Island donor (who wishes to remain anonymous) to identify math competencies used by Island citizens as part of their every day life. Steps included:

  1. Analysis: Education 2020 Inc. convened a panel of citizens, ten in number, and, over a two-day period with the aid of a facilitator trained in DACUM facilitation process, identified a list of math skills practised on a regular basis by Island citizens. The session started with a discussion of citizen roles and was quickly followed by brainstorming mathematics skills involved in their doing. As each task was identified, it was written on a 5 by 8-inch card and posted on a blank wall. Some 82 math competencies emerged from the process. (Appendix 1)

  2. Profiles: Stage two involved the convening a panel of employer representatives, Panellists were intimately involved with hiring and/or supervising new employees, especially new employees taking up employment for the first time without benefit of technical training. Panellist were encouraged to focus on skills actually performed rathe than skills they wanted new hires to have. Panellist were frequently asked” Do you perform this task and how often?” “When id you last time you performed this task? Two groups were convened; the profile developed by the Charlottetown group are shown in Appendix 2 and the Alberton group in Appendix 3.

  3. Rating scale: Competency based instruction requires performance-based assessment. Education 2020 Inc. convened a separate panel of citizens (including educators), to Identify criteria for rating performance; those criteria were then used o create a dynamic rating scale reflecting the rating of competencies in real life situations. (Appendix 4)

  4. Specific Outcomes PEI: For purpose of comparison, PEI specific math outcomes found in High school Math curriculum guides for Grades 9, 10, 11 and 12 are included in this report. (Appendix 5)

  5. Math map: The components were brought together as a working math map to be used by island citizens complete with rating scale and employer profile (Appendix 6)


CONCLUSIONS

In summary, it is our view that math education would benefit from:

  1. Clear statements of over-arching philosophy, goals and principles

  2. Adoption of competencies as base for program and curriculum development

  3. Development and adoption of a comprehensive set of competencies to serve the math interest of all grade levels

  4. A math advisory committee to include community members

  5. Greater communication and teamwork among math professionals at all levels

  6. Adoption of a dynamic rating processes; the same scale could apply to all levels

  7. Reporting student progress on a competency by competency basis.

  8. Adopting a dynamic rating scale



BENEFITS

Implementing the above would:

  1. Improves support for independent learning

  2. Improve communication among stakeholders

  3. Increase relevance

  4. Increases engagement of learners

  5. Improves consistency across the system
