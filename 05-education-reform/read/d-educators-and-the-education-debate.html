Drawing on a New Zealand Herald article, Don highlights how schools sometimes expect children to be "ready for school" rather than schools adapting to meet children where they are. He argues for child-centered approaches emphasizing the critical importance of parent-teacher communication for successful school transitions. This piece reflects his belief that institutional inflexibility represents a systemic failure, and his openness to international best practices and evidence-based approaches.
Education Philosophy & Reform


January 6, 2015


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

Reverse thinking

According the New Zealand  Herald, children aren't getting all the help they need to make the "critical" move to school”  (New Zealand Herald, “Half of NZ kids not adequately prepared for school” Tuesday June 23, 2015). The study of 100 schools and 374 Early Childhood Centres  was carried out by the Education Review office and criticized poor-performing schools “for having rigid systems where the child ‘had to be ready for the school’ rather than adjusting to suit the child”.  The key to successful ‘jump” to formal schooling is the nature and extent of communication between parents and teacher.

Sounds familiar.

Sincerely,

Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
