Comprehensive statement of Glendenning's educational operating beliefs covering learning as personal experience, individual learner agency, variable time in education, and behavioral change through learning. Twenty-six distinct beliefs address learning philosophy, teaching roles, assessment approaches, institutional limitations, and systemic reform needs. Core beliefs emphasize learners as responsible agents, education's personal nature, distinction between education and schooling, and critique of current institutional inability to implement necessary changes. Argues school system lacks self-correcting mechanisms and external intervention is required for substantive reform.

Key Operating Beliefs

New.

NOTES:

  1. Belief - a principle, proposition or idea accepted as true

Key beliefs:

  1. Learning is a personal experience

  2. Most people can learn skills, knowledge and attitudes needed to live and work in a modern economy

  3. In education, time should be a variable

  4. The primary purpose of education is to help each person to develop skills needed to thrive as citizens.

  5. Assessment criteria should reflect those applied in the community served

  6. Each learner should have an advocate

  7. Learning results in a change of behaviour.

  8. Changes in behaviour are demonstrable through new skills, increased knowledge or changed attitudes.

  9. The role of a teacher is to help students change behaviour;

  10. Teachers design experiences to bring about desired changes in learners.

  11. Teaching is a developmental process

  12. People are educated whether or not they attend school

  13. Grading is not an educational function

  14. Credentials and qualifications are not the same

  15. Awarding prizes is not an education function

  16. Our future as individuals and communities depend, to a large degree, on the nature and extent of education services available to each citizen.

  17. Governments invest in education for two reasons (1) to help each citizen develop personal capabilities and (2) to ensure that the community has skills needed to service the community.

  18. Organized education has neither the will nor the capacity to bring about the changes required or desired by the public it serves.

  19. Cost of speaking out is too high for most Islanders; cost of silence is even higher

  20. Each learner has own combination of traits base on genetics, prion learning and aspirations

  21. Learners are responsible for their own education

  22. Although groups do not learn, individuals may learn in a group setting.

  23. Learning results in a change of behaviour.

  24. Teachers teach subjects and infer that students have the skills that parents asked for.

  25. Report cards should report on what a student has learned instead of what he/she has studied.

  26. The school system lacks a self- correcting mechanism

  27. Changes in education desired by the public are unlikely to occur without external interventions.

  28. Helps learners prepare for qualifications and not just credentials

  29. Role of a government is to encourage, support and regulate the delivery of education services

  30. Education system and school system are not synonymous

  31. Schools should focus on the development of learners not just the delivery of services

  32. The education system lacks incentives to respond positively for calls for change

  33. The Importance of institutions (schools, colleges and universities) in helping individuals acquire the education they want and need is on the decline

  34. There are no apparent benefits for the education system to improving the educational level of their students; neither is there a penalty for lack of improvement

  35. Should encourage and support independent learning
