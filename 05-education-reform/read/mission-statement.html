EDUCATION 20/20


EDUCATION 20/20 is an occasional newsletter published by Don Glendenning, a retired educator with a continuing interest in education. Newsletters are directed to the general public and cover a variety of topics in public education at all levels, Through EDUCATION 20/20, the author hopes to both raise questions and spark new thinking.


Each issue of EDUCATION 20/20 is limited to a single page and is available in electronic format only. Comments are invited and readers are welcome to quote with attribution.


A 21ST CENTURY MISSION


My personal point of departure is the following statement by John Dewey in School and Society, “Relate the school to life, and all studies are of necessity correlated."


People fill many life roles. The Oregon Education Act of 1991 identified six: individual, learner, producer, consumer, family member and citizen. Although the significance of each role varies from individual to individual and changes as we mature, the ultimate objective of the school system is to help each person to assume an acceptable level of performance in each role.


With or without schooling, people participate in all six life roles. It is generally agreed, however, that schooling helps in the preparation and, furthermore, the provision of schooling is a good investment by the community


The output of schools for each learner is the growth that is attributable to interventions made by school employees – teachers, librarians, guidance counselors, etc. The clearer we specify the desired exit performance, the more likely learners are to achieve it. The better we describe the difference schools make, the more likely we are to meet our community’s expectation and of the return on their investment. The ultimate objective is to prepare young people to function in adult roles.


The above being the case, schools should look to the community when they plan school programs. What mix of life roles should a school offer? What attitudes, skills and knowledge will best serve learners both while in school and in later life? What are the community norms? How do schools organize for the task?

Schools offer a safe and controlled environment to experiment, to try new things, even to fail. Society has placed few restrictions on what schools can or should do but neither has it described well its expectations.
