Engagement


Islanders want more than simply being consulted about education, they want engagement, The difference being that people want to participate in the making of decisions; this results in putting more decision-making powers directly into the hands of ordinary people – it is really grass roots democracy in action. Consultation usually involves decision makers seeking the views of others affected by their decision,; in such cases, those consulted feel that they have been manipulated. Engagement, on the other hand, atarts with the setting of the agenda, working from shared information, a transparent agenda, and care to insure inclusiveness, control and transparency. The key to engagement is readily available independent information. Education suffers from a lack of independent information; as a result, most of the information available to parents and the public is provided by our education service providers.

Benefits

According to the literature, when families, schools, and communities work together, positive results happen:

  • Student achievement improves;

  • Teacher morale rises;

  • Communication improves;

  • Content becomes more relevant

  • Feedback more meaningful.

  • Viewpoints broaden

  • Community is better informed

  • New ideas are more likely to be generated and adopted

  • Helps gain public understanding and trust

  • Contribute to student wellbeing



For its excellence Award in Education, Education 20/20 Inc. identified the following components:School engagement includes the following components:


Student to Student:

Opportunity for individual, small group and whole class interactions, along with the development of relationships based on respect, caring, courtesy, tact, honour, cooperation and collaboration.


Student to Teacher:

Establishment of a welcoming environment; one that is safe and comfortable physically and emotionally and based on the assumptions of respect, trust, care, optimism and intentionality.

  • Teacher encourages integration of learning across courses and invites students to discuss “big questions” that matter beyond the classroom.

  • Teacher encourages critical inquiry, writing, research, collaborative learning and other skills that develop students' intellectual and practical skills.

  • Teacher ensures he/she is on leading edge of current teaching practices and technology so as to better

prepare students' for 21st century learning.


Student/ Community Partnerships:

Experiential learning with community partners is an instructional strategy and can be made part of a course. This involves direct experience with concepts and issues they are studying in the curriculum. The opportunity to learn, analyze information, and be a participant in community setting also reinforces the idea of good citizenship, i.e. giving back to the community. Other examples of student/community partnership may include fundraising for various community projects and involvement in diverse community service.


Student to Learning Material:

Teacher helps students engage actively in learning and make meaning of the information for application in the real world. Some examples would include collaborative projects, i.e. learning to work and solve real problems in groups by sharpening their own understanding through listening seriously to insights of others especially those from different backgrounds and cultures. Students could also be encouraged to record examples of their best work through a portfolio or video and discuss their learning with other classmates and teachers.


Parent involvement in schools:

The topic of parental involvement in schools has more than 30 years of positive research. Study after study show the dramatic impact parental involvement makes to a child's success in school. In fact, engaging families in their children’s education improves school achievement, attendance, behaviour and graduation rates.


School Community Partnerships:

Engagement of schools in the wider in the wider community also brings benefit. The UNICEF report in 2000 points out the importance of engagement both for the relevance of the curriculum in meeting both individual and community needs but relevance to national goals as well.



Measures/criteria

  • Level of communications with community, parents, students

  • Frequency of school events

  • Community based student activities

  • Parent involvement in curriculum development

  • Parent involvement in planning

  • Staff participation in community organizations

PUBLIC ENGAGEMENT







My recent reading uncovered the following:

  • State of Kentucky has made engagement a cornerstone of its educational change process.

  • Birmingham University in England has appointed a Professor of Public Engagement.

  • Wikipedia reports a couple of very large public engagement activities. One involved the gathering of some 5,000 people to discuss the future of Lower Manhattan. The other included thousands of people through a series of telephone interviews, workshops and town halls.



It experiment in public engagement is unique, however, it seems to me that any public engagement involves at least three components:

  • Information

  • Discussion

  • Policy making





Enhanced public participation in education in PEI could reap benefits as well.
