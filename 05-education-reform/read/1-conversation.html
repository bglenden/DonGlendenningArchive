Don calls for democratic public dialogue about fundamental education questions: Why should the state educate citizens? How? Who decides when education is complete? This lengthy piece, structured around an Ontario educator's framework, emphasizes that education policy should be citizens' responsibility, not isolated to government. He critiques Department confusion between "education" and "schooling," notes community concerns about literacy, numeracy, and practical skills, and proposes structural solutions including a Board of Education to provide ongoing advice. Don argues that current education dysfunction stems from multiple players with conflicting agendas.
Education Philosophy & Reform

February 12. 2020

Dear Editor:

Conversation needed

It’s time It’s time for a public conversation about education; to quote a long time education activist from Ontario:

“The public, all the people in an organized, preferably democratic, society/state should/must take ownership of how their young are educated. Everyone and especially parents should be partners in setting the responses to the following issues:  

  • why should the state educate it’s citizens?  
  • How should the state educate it’s citizens?  
  • Who should decide when a citizen is educated? 
  • What part of the state’s limited resources should be put towards educating every citizen?  Who decides how much?
  • Should schools be operated? As  a for profit business?  Like a community library? Like a not for profit hospital with a chief medical officer and a chief admin officer?

The education literature is full of comment about schools. The World Bank cautions us about thinking that going to school and getting an education ae the same. The OECD ells us that PISA results are better when schools have autonomy in their day o day operation. The Montreal Economic Institute argues for accountability. Given the importance of family and community, Islanders deserve to be both consulted and informed about education as well as schools. Although I support the appointment of school trustees, I must point out that most questions and criticisms I encounter are about education not schooling – levels of literacy and numeracy, self development, knowledge of local community, personal finance, and the usual “ability to make chanfe”. It appears that day-to-day citizen skills” are not required to obtain a diploma.


Lt me suggest two changes.  First(1) a public conversation inform and listen to citizen views as to future education in PEI and (2) a Board of Education to provide on-going advice. 

Sincerely


Don Glendenning
