Don addresses a different aspect of alignment: the coherence between learning outcomes, curriculum, assessment, and reporting. He defines outcomes as a contract between teacher and student, each holding a copy. He outlines specific alignment challenges: curriculum disconnected from assessment, assessment disconnected from life outcomes, and reporting misaligned with actual student learning. Don provides practical advice for parents to use learning outcomes as discussion anchors in parent-teacher meetings, positioning outcomes documentation as a transparency and accountability tool. The letter suggests students and parents often lack access to these critical documents, identifying an institutional accountability gap.


January 6, 2015


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

School alignment   



Alignment in education has a number of meanings but for the purpose of this letter I refer to elements of a school system that have a significant bearing on coherence and efficiency.   three:

Outcomes, according to my definition, are statements of performance expected to occur at some point in a person’s learning.  I see them as part of a contract between a teacher and a student, and each should have a copy.

Curriculum development

Assessment

Reporting student progress



Fine tuning, replacement of parts


Examples include:


Alignment specialiats


It’s not unusual at this time of year to have our cars checked for alignment; we may not realize it, but school systems also struggle with alignment issues.  While educators usually think only of curriculum alignment, we need to also check alignment of:

Curriculum

Assessment criteria with those in life outside school

School reporting with specified outcomes

Timetables and individual learning speeds 

 Benchmarks and community standards

priorities


Improving alignment may require changing priorities, rewriting resource material, adding community members to committees, making surveys, If you have concerns about your child’s progress, I suggest that start with the course outcomes, review them first with your child then take them to the parent teacher meeting.  Doing so will help focus the discussion.  In fact, sometimes copies of relevant outcomes are given to each student as part of the orientation process at the beginning of each new course.  If not, they are readily available on the website of the Department of Education

Sincerely,


Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
