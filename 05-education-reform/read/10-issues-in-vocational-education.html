A comprehensive analysis identifying twelve critical issues in secondary vocational education: definition, image, versus general education, graduation requirements, articulation, curriculum, staffing, teacher certification, facilities, counseling, administration, and employment. Don criticizes academia's bias and the degradation of vocational education's image through renaming (technical, applied). He questions why fewer vocational educators occupy leadership roles and why comprehensive schools have lost vocational administrators. He notes the declining labor-intensive job sector requiring corresponding curriculum shifts toward service-sector roles. This article synthesizes decades of Don's practical experience with competency-based and vocational education.

Issues in vocational education


Introduction

Secondary vocational education is in a state of flux and many educators, academic and vocational, are concerned about its survival and, even more important, the negative impact its demise would have on the education of our young people.  Secondary education ahs a strong bias towards university preparation even though a relatively small percentage of students who enter school pursue that objective.  The recent trend across the country to increase the number of academic courses required for high school graduation reflects this view but appears to have little support in either research or logic.

Within this context, vocational secondary education itself is undergoing considerable change including a movement towards more generic courses, exploration rather than occupational specific training, and less streaming.

Against this background, the following are identified as some of the critical issues in secondary vocational education.


Issue 1 ─ Definition


Vocational education has traditionally been defined as that part of one’s education intended to help one choose an occupation, prepare to enter income-generating employment, hold employment, keep abreast of change in a specialty, secure promotion, or to change occupations. Is this an appropriate definition for the 21st century?  What is the relationship between vocational education and technical, adult, or continuing education?  If vocational is defined as a level, what are the criteria used to determine levels?  What is the role of “vocational educators” in defining vocational education?


Issue 2 ─ Image


The image of vocational education appears to have deteriorated and “vocational” is frequently replaced by such terms as technical, technological, applied education, etc.  Some people view vocational education as narrow and academic education as broad.  Some still define education as a level rather than a focus; other people believe that there should be a number of levels within any vocational subject in the same way that there are levels in academic subjects.  Should vocational courses be offered in secondary schools, and if so, what should be done to improve their image?


Issue 3 ─ Vocational versus General Education


There is a wide agreement that everyone needs a good general education.  Frequently this is interpreted as including ability to communicate, good human relations skills, ability to solve problems, to be a contributing member of a community and exhibiting responsible behavior.  These can be taught using vocational subjects as a vehicle.  Why do we continue to emphasize academic subjects when there is little or no evidence that they are the best vehicle for general education, especially when the academic offerings are restricted to a narrower career path?  In the view of some writers, vocational subjects are excellent vehicles for teaching general education.

Issue 4 ─ Graduation Requirements


In response to criticism of the public schools, the overall number of credits required for high school graduation has been increased.  As well, there has been an increase in the number of compulsory academic courses needed to meet secondary school graduation requirements.  At the local level, schools frequently require a higher number of credits that the provincial graduation requirements.  The additional courses also tend to be from traditional academic offerings.  The outcome of all the above is decline in the number of students who elect vocational courses.  One wonders about the logic of this.  Are the current graduation requirements appropriate for the next decade?  What should the graduation requirements be?  Are the graduation requirements consistent with the purposes of secondary education?


Issue 5 ─ Articulation


Students who take vocational courses in secondary school often find that those courses are not accepted for entrance into college or university.  A sequence of courses in traditional trades, on the other hand, frequently leads to advanced standing in a designated trade.  Is there any evidence that one pattern or sequence of courses rather than another pattern provides better preparation for college or university?  Is it reasonable to expect advanced standing in a college or university program when it is frequently not granted for an apprenticeship program?


Issue 6 ─ Curriculum


Are some new thrusts required in the secondary vocational curriculum?  More pre-technical courses?  Increased co-op education?  Entrepreneurship?  Does the curriculum reflect the current and future needs of the nation?  What are the implications for the curriculum of a move toward generic vocational courses in high school?  Should some pre-employment training be retained at the secondary level?  Who should participate in the development for vocational education courses?

Centers of excellence and quality assurance are key issues for vocational educators.  What program characteristics will carry vocational education into the next century?  What is the role of advisory committees?  Do we have clear and well-understood mission statements?  What about instructional methodology ─ teacher-directed or self-directed?  How are the programs being organized and managed?  Should secondary vocational programs be competency-based instead of time-based?


Issue 7 ─ Staffing


Staff for vocational programs traditionally are drawn from appropriate business and industry and are required to have journeyman status or equivalent in a single field.  All instructional staff are required to have occupational experience.  Given the movement towards generic vocational education, are the traditional qualifications still valid?  What qualifications are appropriate for secondary vocational instructors for the next decade?  What are the implications for the preparation or training of vocational education teachers?  Are salaries and benefits of vocational teachers comparable with those in business and industry?


Issue 8 ─ Teacher Certification


The current certification system appears to favor academic teachers in that the requirement for a university degree works an extra hardship on vocational staff.  Vocational teachers are required to have significant business and industrial experience: academic teachers do not (perhaps this requirement should be added to the criteria for academic teachers also).  Why should vocational teachers alone carry this additional burden of preparation time?  A teacher of history can take a history course during the summer and increase both certification level and salary.  Why shouldn’t vocational teachers be treated the same way?  Should teacher certification be designed to encourage teachers to keep up to date in their field?  What is the purpose of a certification system?  Do we have any evidence that it works for the betterment of education?


Issue 9 ─ Facilities


Traditionally, vocational facilities have been large, unit shops or labs, with equipment that replicated that found in business or industry.  Is this still appropriate?  Can generic vocational courses be taught in industrial arts facilities?  If the answer to this question is no, what kind of facilities do we need for secondary vocational programs?  How can the change of focus be best accommodated?


Issue 10 ─ Counseling

Guidance counselors appear to give most of their time and attention to the university bound.  Students are often discouraged from electing vocational courses and frequently learn about such options from peers rather than professionals.  Most guidance counselors are not well versed in vocational options.  The non-university bound student is frequently viewed as a “trouble-maker” and a source of embarrassment to school personnel.  Early drop-out relieves schools of a nuisance.  What is the role of the guidance counselor for a student who is not university or college bound?


Issue 11 ─ Administration and Organization


At one time, comprehensive schools had principals or vice principals with a vocational background.  Today, fewer administrators seem to have emerged from among vocational instructional staff.  People with vocational backgrounds are not emerging in leadership roles.  Even Departments of Education appear to be downgrading vocational expertise.  Are these statements true?  If so, why is this happening?  What, if anything, should be done about it?  Can quality vocational programs be led by academic educators?

Specialized vocational high schools appear to be on the decline and vocational programs are now being delivered as part of comprehensive school offerings.  What advantages does the comprehensive model of organization have for vocational education?  What disadvantages?


Issue 12 ─ Employment


The decline in labor intensive jobs as a result of technology advances will certainly have an impact on the design and substance of secondary vocational education program sin the future.  Will high tech create new jobs?  Or will the growth of high technology obscure the fact that the demand for workers now and in the future will be in the service sector – secretaries, nursing aides, sales clerks, home service workers.  Is this switch reflected in secondary vocational education programs?
