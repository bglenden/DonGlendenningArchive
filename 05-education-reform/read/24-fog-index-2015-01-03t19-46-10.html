Newsletter article (Open Education No. 25) by Don Glendenning on implementing educational change, identifying four essential elements: a change agent with freedom to challenge status quo, an "oasis" or protected space for innovation, patience for implementation, and advocacy from knowledgeable third parties. He emphasizes that systemic issues often prevent teachers from implementing known solutions.

Give change a chance


There is a great deal of talk about changes that would benefit our education system

For change to be successful in education, we need:


Change agent- someone to keep the focus on the issue, someone with freedom to ‘roam” from the old way of doing things, a person with standing in the organization


Oasis – it’s difficult to be an innovator alone; the innovators and the innovation need their own space, an oasis where their needs come first.



Patience -


Commitment by people up the line – when someone in an existing organization talks to me about launching a new initiative, I usually ask how his boss feels about the idea. From the answer, one can uusually judge the chances of success, if the boss wont run interference it greatly diminidhes your chance of success


An advocate – who is a knowledgeable but diiisinterested third party.

































It seems as though when there is a problem in education we rush to blame teachers. Now, I am not saying that teachers are never at fault but in most of the examples I hear about, teachers know what should be done.; The problem seems to be that the “system” is focused on delivery rather than development and neither allows nor encourages alternative ways of helping. It’s the old industrial model still at work
