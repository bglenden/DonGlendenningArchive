Don raises fundamental questions teachers cannot adequately answer: How can a student graduate unable to read? He distinguishes schooling from education and notes communication problems in school reporting. Report cards describe what was taught (subjects/grades) rather than what was learned (reading ability, writing skills, social competence). He critiques vague rating scales (A/B/C) undefined outside school contexts. He argues these communication gaps obscure actual learning and calls for improved reporting mechanisms aligned with learner outcomes. This piece demonstrates his attention to communication clarity and practical feedback systems.

School Questions


Recently, but on separate occasions, I was asked questions that I could not answer.  They were not idle questions and are likely in the minds of others, so I decided to make them public.


The first question was “How come a student can graduate and not be able to read?”  Because I do not have details about the student in question, I’ll restate the question.  d itknow about the case to It is not a new question but I’ve heard similar questions aabout reading beforethe question beforeconcern about reading is widespreadThe first was about a student who had graduated from high school and could not  readhear many complaints about education.  Some, I believe, may be blamed on poor communication.  Let me elaborate. 


We speak of schooling and education as though they are the same thing; in fact, they are not.  We speak of school buildings, school years, school days, school teachers, school books, school buses and school report cards.  To be consistent, in terms of improvement, we should limit our focus to schools.  


Report cards also cause of communication problems.  Parents want information about what their child has learned; report cards focus on what has been taught or studied.  The questions parents ask are quite specific.  How well can he read?  Write? Get along with others? The answers tend to be broad.  He got an A in English, a B in History and a C in music.


Rating levels need definition; they are at odds with rating swcales used in life outside school.  I cannot ever remember anyone ever referring to a colleague or coworker as an A, B or C.  When we say a person is an A student or a B student, what do we mean?  How do we describe the difference between a student with an “A” and one with a “B”?  I can think of at least three possibilities.  A student could be given an A because he or she has much broader information about a subject than the B student.  On the other hand, a student may be given an A because he is able to complete assignments more quickly, to a higher standard and with less assistance from fellow students or teachers.  Or has an A student actually reached a higher level of learning and is able to analyze or problem solve rather than simply identify and report?  


Improving communication with learners, parents and even the general public, would, I suggest, be a useful first step in improving school performance.


Sincerely


Don Glendenning
