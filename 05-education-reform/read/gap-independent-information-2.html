From December 1, 2013, Don identifies a critical information gap: insufficient independent, practical information for educational decision-making beyond school. He describes conversations with students unable to assess course implications, employers dismissing report cards as useless (proposing independent assessment bodies like Royal Canadian Conservatory of Music as models), and parents and graduates seeking better career information. He argues career counseling should relocate from schools to community access centers open evenings and Saturdays, making services available to post-secondary adults. This reflects systems thinking beyond traditional schooling.

Information gap


Although we are in an information age, there appears to be a shortage of independent information about education available to people making decisions about their education. The exception is young people still in school, is either available to in-school youth who are college and university bound or people who qualify for training under a federally funded program.

I’ve heard the plea for more and better information most recently from parents, students and young adults; their’s is a plea for more better and even independent information on which to make career choices.  (I’ve also heard a plea from parents about more informative report cards and from employers about educational levels, but I’ll save those for later letters.)


This need first became evident to me when charting  a learning diagram; the learner often needs information to give put learning in perspective but also to make choices about future learning; and teachers need information in order to plan an appropriate teaching strategy.  it seems, make important choices based on insufficient and perhaps even erroneous information.  The most frequent examples mentioned were decisions about career choices, about having chosen courses in school that resulted in not qualifying for some programs in college, university or apprenticeships, and general information about what to expect in your first full time job.  


Most of the information and counsel available and needed, it seems, is housed in a school and not readily accessible to parents or to students who have already graduated.  Furthermore, while newspapers carry items on education, they are seldom of the nature to be helpful in making educational decisions.  And as we know, important career decisions involve more than simply selecting appropriate mix an level of courses.


Career counselling is not a school issue.  One option is to relocate career counselling services from schools to Access Centres, or some other facility open to the public at large and open during evenings and on Saturdays.  Another option would be to house it with some of our service or youth clubs

A few days ago, I picked up a copy of the Vital Signs report published by the Community Foundation (www.cfpei.ca).  While the Report dealt with a number of issues facing Island youth, my particular interest is youth views on education.  Quite a few respondents to the survey were not satisfied with the quality, nature or extent of their education, especially in relation to preparedness for work.  Students expect their schooling help prepare them for employment not just for further education.
