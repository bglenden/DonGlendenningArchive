Don identifies a fundamental mismatch: government invests over $200 million annually in schools, yet employers report skill gaps and jobs go unfilled. Schools issue credentials describing what was studied; employers want qualifications indicating what was learned. He notes many countries (excepting US and Canada) have established qualification frameworks administered by independent authorities with standardized, nationally-recognized qualifications. He calls for Canada to establish similar framework, starting with basic skills standardization. This demonstrates his evidence-based approach and willingness to learn from international comparative practices.


July 7, 2015


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir: 

Employers want to know what’s been learned

I’ve been trying to understand the cause of the basic skills gap on Prince Edward Island.  On the one hand, taxpayers spend over $200 million on our public school system each year; on the other hand, employers tell us that jobs go begging for lack of skills.  It certainly sounds like a mismatch.  In addition to technical skills and knowledge, employees need a certain level of “basic skills” to function in the workplace and the lack of these basic skills has a negative impact on the employee, the employer and our economy. Schools issue credentials that describe what has been studied.  Employers want to know a person’s qualifications, what has been learned.  Therein lies the problem.  

Whereas, Canada depend on credentials issued by schools, many countries, the US and Canada being exceptions, have established a “qualification framework”  administered by a qualification authority; qualification authorities design and implement standards that reflect what has been learned, qualifications instantly recognized across the country.  Designs differ but each country can decide the nature and scope of qualifications system to suit its own purposes.

A qualification framework for Canada is long overdue and standardizing levels of basic skills would be a good place to start.  In addition to supporting worker mobility, such a system would reduce a school’s workload.

Sincerely,


Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
