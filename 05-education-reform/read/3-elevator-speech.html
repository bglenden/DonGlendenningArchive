Don advocates for educators to develop and consistently use coherent "elevator speeches"—brief, essence-capturing statements about their work. He references John Robinson's Community Foundation work and the importance of capturing shared purpose in a sentence or two. Don proposes educators collectively articulate how they're helping people develop skills, knowledge, and attitudes enabling them to manage life's obstacles and thrive. He emphasizes that consistent key terminology across educators signals unified direction, and invites colleagues to develop their own language variants while maintaining core coherence about education's transformative purpose.

Dear Editor:


Are we there yet?

I met John Robinson through our work together in the early days of the Community Foundation; he taught me the importance of elevator speeches, that is the first few words one says to a person you haven’t seen for a while, or a person you’ve just met,  when he or she asks “what are you up to these days? Elevator speeches are a sentence or two in length, capture the essence of what one does but not the detail.  All of us should learn and practice our elevator speeches.


PEI educators need an elevator speech.; we need to hear some key words, and the same or similar ones, each time educators speak.  They must be clear, project into the future and include measurable actions.  If asked the question, a person in the field of education might say something like the following:

  • I’m helping  
  • People, citizens, Islanders, others
  • To gain, develop, acquire
  • Skills, knowledge, attitudes, 
  • Needed, desired, that help them to
  • Cope, manage, conquer, overcome  obstacles, hurdles, barriers
  • That they encounter on the road of life
  • Thus helping them thrive
  • Wherever they find themselves in the years ahead.


Readers can select the word or words they want or choose entirely new ones.  When I hear an elevator speech containing words like this, from our educators, I’ll know that we are on the same journey.


Sincerely


Don Glendenning

A long time student of education
