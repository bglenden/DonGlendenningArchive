Detailed background document from October 29, 2017 establishing the context for proficiency level initiatives. Begins with the 2013 Community Foundation of PEI survey identifying youth concerns about preparedness gaps for postsecondary study and employment. Following this report, Education 20/20 organized community discussions and published recommendations. The document catalogs multiple concerns: PEI's unsatisfactory PISA scores, low literacy rates, declining enrollment in adult basic education, employer-reported skill shortages, and parent/grandparent disappointment. Key conclusions state that diplomas/degrees are unreliable achievement measures, grading is not a school function, and current organization likely duplicates adult education services. The document proposes skill-based curriculum development, independent proficiency bodies, community-based criteria, universal skill lists, and holistic growth tracking—all designed to reduce bias, improve consistency, reduce teacher workload, remove conflict of interest, and support independent learning.


Proficiency 1

2017-10-29

Proficiency levels in education

Introduction:

In late 2013, The Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island (CFPEI) undertook a survey of over 200 Island youth to identify issues that directly affect them; the report, Taking the Pulse of Island Youth, available on the Foundation’s website at www.cfpei.ca, provides a snapshot of the findings. Bright spots include a positive sense of community, emerging entrepreneurial spirit, volunteerism and new postsecondary offerings. The main concerns include a lack of jobs, surviving on low-paying jobs, educational deficiencies and drug and alcohol addictions. On the subject of education and learning, the study reports a high rate of completion of high school and postsecondary education, a high level of inclusivity and relatively affordable tuition costs; young people also expressed concerns about their readiness for the next stage of life including:


“preparedness gaps with regard to readiness for

postsecondary study and the workplace” – Vital Signs, CFPEI, 2013


Following the publication of the CFPEI Report, Education 20/20 organized six public discussions for the purpose of informing the community of the report and its findings, learning more about youth issues in our communities, exploring issues of education and learning, and to identify actions to be taken to close gaps. Discussions were lively and wide ranging, and carried on well after the formal session ended. The final report included a recommendation that.


Further study be undertaken to clarify the gaps in preparation for further study and for work - (Preparedness, Education 20/20 Inc., Occasional Paper No. 1)

Education 20/20 has continued its exploration by examining curriculum materials from a variety of institutions and Provinces and at various levels, reviewing achievement levels and assessment procedures for the OECD sponsored PISA assessment, Canada’s PCAP assessment, TOWES and others, The concern is widespread; PEI media report unsatisfactory PISA results, students report not receiving the help wanted, literacy rates remain low,the age of enrolees in Adult Basic Education at Holland College has dropped, the Greater Charlottetown Area Chamber of Commerce reports skill shortages, and parents and grandparents continue to be disappointed.


Conclusions:

  • Diplomas and degrees are not reliable measures of breadth or depth in fields of study

  • Establishing and awarding grade levels is not a school function

  • Linking achievement levels to school grades is misleading.

  • Employer interest, and perhaps even student interest, is switching from credentials to qualifications

  • Public education makes little apparent attempt to meet needs of local employers

  • Education has become a tool for enticing companies to locate on PEI

  • There is potential for grade inflation, social promotion, and conflict when an agency both teaches and grades

  • Employers find many job seekers lack adequate basic education skills

  • Current organization of education services likely supports a duplication of services for adults that were already provided in secondary studies.

  • Costs of education are rising faster than the cost of inflation

  • Content of programs at both secondary and postsecondary levels has become overly theoreticalis becoming very theoretical

  • Competency based education gives clearer direction to students than does curriculum based outcomes




Recommendations:

Education 20/20 considered a range of solutions to the issue of grades and recommwns that priority be given to: PEI

  • Adopt skill basis of curriculum development

  • Establish an independent body to set proficiency levels

  • Adopt authentic/ community based criteria

  • Undertake an analysis of citizen skills as a benchmarks for public school programming

  • Report achievement on basis of skills demonstrated

  • Prepare a universal list skills for each academic subject

  • Adopt a holistic rating scale that tracks growth in any subject


Benefits of adopting above recommendations:

  • Reduces bias

  • improves consistency

  • Reduces teacher workload

  • Removes conflict of interest

  • Supports independent learning

  • Eliminates grade inflation

  • Better reflect growth in learning
