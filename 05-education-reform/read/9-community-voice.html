This letter to the Charlottetown Guardian and Journal Pioneer (January 15, 2019, draft version) argues that Souris—or any community—should have a standing committee to ensure its residents receive needed educational services, without necessarily operating schools municipally. Don emphasizes that meeting community needs is foundational to education's purpose and that modern technology enables this without silos. He directs readers to a Souris report on the Education 20/20 website, positioning Souris as a natural experimental site for community-based education planning, reflecting his ongoing engagement with local educational advocacy.


January 15, 2019


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Journal Pioneer


Dear Sir:

Without a voice 

Draft

Seems strange that a community the size of Souris, wouldn’t have a voice in planning educational services for its citizens; after all, anything I read confirms that meeting community needs, while not necessarily a role of education, is one of the reasons why government provides financial support.  No, I’m not recommending that schools be operated by municipalities but maybe, just maybe, local communities should have a standing committee to ensure that its residents receive the education they need.  The same, of course, should be said for Stratford or any other community.  


Citizens in communities large and small need a quality education services for their citizens.  Fortunately, we have the technology to do so.  But it doesn’t work within silos.  Souris could make a natural home to experiment.


Readers will find the Souris report at  HYPERLINK "http://www.Eucation2020.ca" www.Eucation2020.ca



Sincerely, 



Don Glendenning 

A long-time student of education
