A Qualification Framework

Labour mobility is a fact of life; our ancestors practiced it, so have most of us. The difference is that circumstances around us have changed, numbers have increased, the pace has quickened and people travel further afield. Labour mobility refers to the movement of people from one part of the country to another but it also refers to the movement of workers both within and between occupational fields. One example may be seen in the regular commute of Islanders between their home in PEI and their workplace in Alberta, another is the retraining needed when a town is devastated by a plant closing, and a third example of mobility is the education and experience that allows young people to live and work around the world. Labour mobility is may be hampered geographic ties, lack of housing, unemployment benefits, licensing requirements, lack of portable qualifications, and lack of nationally recognized educational levels. If my reading of the literature is correct, Canada lags in its programs in support of still relies mostly on a myriad of academic credentials.

Part of the mobility problem is the confusion between credentials and qualifications. Diplomas and degrees are credentials; they are awarded to people who have successfully completed a prescribed number and pattern of courses in an institution; one has to know quite a bit about the institution to know the quality of the product. Qualifications, on the other hand, attest to the fact that the holder has demonstrated attitudes, skills and knowledge required to successfully perform in a role or at a task. A qualifications authority is a body established under law and given the authority to assess, evaluate and certify a person’s level of performance on a task or set of tasks, competencies, outcomes. The first, a credential implies that the holder knows something; the second tells what the holder can do with what he or she knows. It’s a significant difference.

To facilitate the movement of workers, both within a country and internationally, many countries have developed a qualification framework and a qualification authority to enforce it; Canada and the US are exceptions. A qualifications authority is a body established under law and given the authority to assess, evaluate and certify a person’s level of performance on a task or set of tasks or competencies. Qualification authorities operate under government legislation but often have their own governing structure. While the nature of their work varies from country to county, qualification authorities usually perform direct assessments of individuals, including setting, preparing and marking examinations; identifying levels of accomplishment in an occupation or field, setting standards and benchmarks; maintaining a databank, translating qualification documents; lliaising with schools and training institutions on assessment practices, issuing licenses and certificates and providing a recognition service for people with overseas qualifications .


Neither Canada nor the US has bought into the qualification concept, at least not yet. As a result, we have training institutions issuing transcripts and credentials of their own making; attempts that have been made, have focused on input measures rather output- longer days, more subjects, additional technology and more training for teachers and administrators. One exception is apprenticeship; although apprenticeship is a provincial responsibility, Provinces have found a way to both honour the jurisdictional boundaries and, at the same time, produce a qualification document recognized across the country. We’ve done this through common analyses, both provincial and national examinations and an endorsement, a Red Seal. Other exceptions include the work of the Royal Canadian conservatory of Music.


Canada needs a qualification framework and an independent body to run it. By that I mean an authority to assess and certify a person’s level of performance on a task or set of tasks such as found in an occupation or field of study. Such an authority would reduce if not eliminate the confusion between teaching and sorting now faced by teachers, focus certification more clearly on performance that matters, support lifelong learning and self-directed study, allow schools to focus formative evaluation that helping learners learn, and reduce major distractions for teachers caused by their involvement in the sorting process. A qualification authority would, in effect, serve two clients, organizations desiring an assessment and individuals wanting to confirm the level and suitability of their qualifications. While qualification authorities, at least the ones we have reviewed, focus on approving, courses, accrediting institutions and analyzing current offerings; we believe in a qualification authority that assesses individuals directly, not one that massages the minutiae of hours, outcomes, teacher qualification of education providers. A qualification authority could also play a useful role in helping people identify gaps in their preparation for a given field of work.


We support a qualification authority that is independent with its own governing structure, accepts clients on direct application or by referral and Issues certificates as appropriate based on demonstrated performance and, on request, provides applicants with information on gaps to be filled to meet certification requirements, a qualification authority would not, however, organize competitions, rank students or grant credentials. The main functions of a qualification authority would be to:

  1. Prepare analyses

  2. Establish levels and profiles

  3. Establish assessment criteria

  4. Assess individuals

  5. Report results

  6. Liaise with training institutions

  7. Operate a databank


A review of the literature certainly supports the value of a qualification framework; benefits include: easing the assessment burden on teachers and the school system, improving the consistency in reporting achievement, helpful in comparing foreign credentials, easing the mobility process, improving communications between and among education stakeholders, encourage self- study, reducing the problem of grade inflation, send an almost instant widespread recognition of what a person is able to do. Governments in most countries have taken the leadership in developing a qualification framework: in the US, however, it appears to be coming primarily from the private sector. Given that education in Canada is a Provincial responsibility, it is unlikely that the Government of Canada will move to fill the void. It is a niche waiting to be filled. That leaves it up either to Provinces to band together or the private sector. Unless someone initiates some steps soon, the US agencies will fill that role in Canada by default.
