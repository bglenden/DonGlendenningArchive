A letter to editors (Charlottetown Guardian, Journal Pioneer) prompted by a 1950s memory of a booklet titled "Whither Bound?" in a landlady's home at an American university. Don questions PEI's educational direction, noting the $600 million government investment lacks clear purpose. He advocates for the state to clarify why it educates citizens, how it should do so, and who determines when someone is educated. He emphasizes learning as behavioral change distinct from school attendance and calls for public conversation on education.

Whither Bound?


In the mid=1950’s, I spent a year at an American University.  I lived off campus in The basement of a private home and had cooking privilege in the regular kitchen.  My landlady was legally blind but also deeply religious.  On my bedside table she had placed a little booklet with the title “Whither Bound”.  I think of these words often these days as I reflect on the future of Education; I do so because PEI educational plans  are just not clear.


PEI is home to 155,000 people; all should be encouraged to learn.  By ”learn” I mean add to or further develop their knowledge, skill or attitudes base.  They may do it on their own or need help. The helper is usually referred to as a teacher and the person being helped is known as a teacher. Of all a person’s teachers, the home is most important.  A person’s education is the result of all our experiences in life.


Parents and communities generally think learning to be important and authorize government to arrange learning experiences. Government responded by setting up special places called schools where teachers and students meet and work together of a kind in in a manner set out by government.  Because having an education is so important, governments also require people to attend school until they are 18 years of age. The problem that arises, however, is that people learn at different rates In fact being in school for 12 years is not a very reliable measure of how much a person has learned. A second problem, as identified by the World Bank, is he confusion that arises from people, parents and educators alike, in thinking that going to school and getting an education are one and the same. 


I am told that the government of Prince Edward Island invests some 600 million dollars in educational services; the purpose is not clear.  Is it to help people what’s missing is a set of expectations in return for that investment. The literature suggests a number of possibilities (1) thrive as citizens in a democracy, (2) entry level employment  supply services needed to maintain PEI as a place we want to live – and I am sure there are others.
