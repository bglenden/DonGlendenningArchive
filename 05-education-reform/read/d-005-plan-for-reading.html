A Plan for Reading

Occasional paper No. 2 October 20, 2013


The myth of grade levels



School grade levels tell as much about one’s education as travel days tells about one’s vacation; grade levels correlate well with age, what the Department thinks, what teachers are teaching, even where to physically locate students while on school premises, but grades tell little about what students know and can do. School grades are an administrative tool, not an instructional one and should be drummed from our vocabulary. We need a better system, one that reflects learning, and even encourages it.



A number of years ago, I visited a school in England. At 9:00 each morning all students studied English, at 10:00 mathematics, and at 11:00 science; in the afternoon, the traditional school offerings and structure prevailed. Instruction in the morning was available in each subject at a number of levels; students selected, or perhaps were directed, to the level they needed, and could progress from level to level at their own rate. I like the idea but suggest that we start with a pilot project in basic skills.



Given the widely expressed concern about the level of basic education, I call them citizenship skills, among our students and young adults, let me propose as a modest beginning that we :

  • Select communication skills as a pilot.

  • Identify the competencies

  • Establish a recognizable levels of performance for each

  • Organize instruction around each level

  • Find or develop an assessment tools to measure student achievement against each level.

  • Prepare a rating scale.

  • Give the list of competencies and rating scale to each student

  • Set aside the first period each morning in all high schools for instruction in basic skills

  • Assign students to a level that reflects their needs and interests

  • Allow students move from one level to next based on demonstrated performance

  • Report on levels attained rather than age

  • Establish an assessment service separate from the school system

  • Student report cards would show Provide students and parents with a copy of student profile on each.

  • Provide students with a recognition of level of attained

  • Allow schools to continue instruction in their own fashion for the remainder of the day

  • Invite Holland College, UPEI, private schools and company based training programs to participate in the project.

  • Monitor results



There is no doubt in my mind that the above arrangement would go a long way towards improving the level of reading among Islanmdcers.
