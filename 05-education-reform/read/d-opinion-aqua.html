This is Don's most comprehensive articulation of his qualification authority proposal. Using passport/visa analogy, he distinguishes credentials (what has been studied) from qualifications (what has been learned/can do). He argues these serve different purposes and shouldn't be combined. He proposes an independent qualification authority separate from schools to certify demonstrated competencies, operating fee-for-service. Such authority would reduce teacher-sorting conflicts, focus schools on learning, support lifelong learning, and could serve all Atlantic Canada. This represents his signature contribution to education reform thinking.


Opinion Piece


A separate qualification authority


Passports are issued by one’s home country, provide basic information about the bearer and, if examined, provide some insight into the exciting places travelled. Visas, on the other hand, are issued by a receiving country, involve a more detailed level of scrutiny by the receiving country, specify the purpose of the visit and provide some detail as to activities to be carried on; often travelers require both a passport and visa.


Diplomas and degrees share many characteristics with passports while qualifications may be likened to visas. They are not the same, they serve different purposes, both may be needed and they should not be confused. At one time I thought the diplomas and degrees could be redesigned in order to attest to qualifications but I’ve changed my mind; I now realize that credentials tell what has been studied, qualifications tell what has been learned and, as a result, what the holder can do. Combining both functions in one document won’t work, frustrates the process and the people and, in the end, is doomed to fail.


Schools are teaching institutions; their role is to help learners learn. Each learner decides what he or she will learn, although others, including teachers, influence that decision. As part of the teaching process, teachers assess what learners have learned and, depending on the results, design and implement new teaching strategies. Unfortunately and increasingly, the emphasis in school has switched from helping learners learn to grooming them the education “race”for a race, and even selecting winners. The purposes are incompatible and should be separated. The answer is to separate the functions of teaching and sorting.


In spite of my earlier comments, I believe that both learners and the public should have and would benefit from the certification of skills such as literacy and numeracy. Perhaps such a service could be provided through the apprenticeship division or perhaps certification could be provided through existing program infrastructure.


Another option is to establish a new body, a qualification authority, whose sole purpose is to evaluate and certify a person’s level of performance on a task or set of tasks, competencies, outcomes. Such an authority should be independent of government and educational institutions, have its own governing structure, operate on a fee for service basis, accept clients on direct application or by referral, issue certificates based on demonstrated performance, and provide applicants with information on gaps to be filled to meet certification requirements.

It would, in effect, have two clients, the organizations desiring an assessment service and individuals wanting to aconfirm their qualifications. A qualification authority would take its cues from the destination of the learner, focus on what a client can do and how well, examine each skills carried and assess their suitability also identify gaps for the individual would not simply administer . A qualification authority could also play a useful role in helping people who take on line courses convert them to a qualification – or even the assessment of foreign credentials.


If establishing a qualification authority, it would make sense to develop a service capable of serving all Atlantic Canada; it would make an interesting new business venture and could be located on PEI.
