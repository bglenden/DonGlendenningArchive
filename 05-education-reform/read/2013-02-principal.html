Education Philosophy & Reform

January 22, 2013


Editor, Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:

Principals make a difference


I’ve always thought that a principal makes a difference in a school, and I still do; while a teacher sets the tone of a classroom, a principal sets the tone of a school. By their personalities, by their knowledge and experience, and by their management style, principals create an environment that either supports or discourages effective learning; if given greater autonomy, I am sure that most would do even better.  This view is supported by a recent OECD study that found autonomy and accountability at the school level is a key to how well students perform, at least on PISA tests.  And out of New Zealand this morning as I write comes further evidence about the importance of a principal’s leadership role, this time in setting and achieving student outcomes. 


This letter is a call for a change of emphasis in the role of a principal; it is a plea to allow principals freedom to:

Allocate operating funds to better support activities unique to each school.

Organize and fund professional development for school personnel.

Select and assign staff in a manner that reflects the needs and priorities of students in that school, and to

Be a leader in identifying outcomes and implementing programs 

There are other organizational models that I could support, however, given current organizational arrangements, principals play a key role in the quality of education that schools offer; greater autonomy for schools will not guarantee improvement but it will increase the odds that improvement will happen.
