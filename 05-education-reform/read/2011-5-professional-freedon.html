Following international education conferences emphasizing teacher importance, Don calls for expanded professional autonomy for educators. He argues teachers should control key instructional elements—content, time on task, textbook selection, assessment methods, and reporting—which he categorizes as professional rather than administrative matters. Granting teachers similar freedoms afforded other professionals could dramatically improve instruction quality. The letter reflects Don's consistent theme that systemic constraints rather than teacher capacity limit educational quality.


April 21, 2011.


Editor

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Editor:

Professional Freedom


In recent days the media, including this newspaper, reported on a major international gathering in New

York to exchange information and ideas about improving education. Our minister of education

participated in the discussions and, as reported in this newspaper, found the gathering to be worthwhile. Coverage in other media supports this conclusion.


According to reports l've read, there was agreement that teachers are the key to bringing about changes

in schooling. This certainly speaks to the issue of teacher education but, beyond their training, teachers

need more professional freedom than they have at present. The system must be opened up to allow

teachers more control over the teaching function including content, time on task, textbooks,

assessment and reporting; these are professional and not administrative matters.


lf we allow teachers the same freedom that we give other professionals, we may be surprised at the

impact of this change alone on the quality of instruction on our public schools.


Sincerely




Don Glendenning
