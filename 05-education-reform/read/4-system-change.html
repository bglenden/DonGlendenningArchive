An occasional paper recommending comprehensive educational system reform in Prince Edward Island. Glendenning calls for a clear provincial vision of education, learner-centered service models, delegation of instruction to diverse providers, establishment of a qualification authority, and a professional service delivery model with greater educator autonomy and accountability.

Occasional Paper No. 1

March 20, 2015



A Call for System change

Preparation for life’s roles is called education and the better prepared we are, the better we are able to cope with all that we encounter, or create. And education is made more complex by the fact that each person is unique; unique in basic physical, mental, emotional and social make up, unique in range and nature of previous experience, unique in perceived futures, but also unique in terms of learning pace, style and needs. Our education consists of what we have learned or perhaps even more accurately what we can do as a result of what we have learned


It is widely recognized that education is a key factor in determining each citizen’s quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of our citizens will determine to a significant degree the quality of the community in which we live. Our government supports education for two reasons, one is to ensure a high level of personal growth and development for every citizen; the other is to ensure the common good.


There is a gap between what Islanders have and what they want in the way of education. Indicators of the gap include: the limited engagement between educators and the community they serve; the lack of a philosophy of education that encompasses all our citizens; government’s focus on the young and schooling; service gaps such as the availability of independent information on which to make educational choices; availability of part time instruction; lack of market conditions normal for a sector; politicization of decision making and limits on professional autonomy and accountability. Citizens see most of the issues as organizational, created by government decision-makers and, therefore, capable of being changed by those same government decision-makers.


To close the gap between what Islanders want and what the Province provides; Education 20/20 Inc. recommends that:


  1. As an immediate step, Government engage in the development of a short, clear, overarching, public statement about education, who it is for, its guiding principles and basic beliefs. Although much has been written about education, a comprehensive vision statement does not currently exist for PEI. We believe that having such a statement is required, especially for a service as large and complex as education, however, we are not suggesting another study but rather a document prepared by a small working with opportunity for citizen input.


  1. Develop a learner centered service model that provides support in all domains to learners as, when and where needed. Doing so would involve student centered outcomes, criterion referenced assessment, flexible scheduling, challenge for credit arrangements, and greater autonomy and accountability for teachers.

  2. Government reserve for itself a leadership role in creating a learning culture and providing a quality education for every Islander. Such a role is important but currently lacking, and education suffers as a result. Government should focus on matters of planning, setting priorities, funding, monitoring, quality control, ensuring the availability of professional resources, home support for preschool children, information and continuing education. Under such an arrangement, the Department may well play an increased role in delivering support to families with pre- school children, more active role in promoting and offering continuing education classes and even in providing information and services to children and their parents.


  1. Government delegate the role of providing instruction; actual provision of instruction to be expanded beyond schools, colleges, universities to include community agencies, professionals in private practice and learners themselves.


  1. Establish a qualification authority, a separate body for the purpose certifying individual levels of achievement to persons wanting or needing such documentation. The role of teaching is to help others learn; when a teachers rank their students they are putting their ability to teach at risk; assigning student grades, selecting students for prizes and awards and awarding diplomas and degrees is a sorting process and should not be part of a teacher’s role. Apprenticeship with certification through a Provincial Department of Labour and music through the Royal Canadian Music conservatory are examples of the preferred arrangement.


  1. Take steps to establish a more professional model of service delivery such as in health and justice services, where professionals are directly accountable to clients for the nature, extent and quality of service provided. Such a model must include closer professional-client relationship than now exist, the potential for a family education adviser for those who want it, and professional autonomy and accountability.
