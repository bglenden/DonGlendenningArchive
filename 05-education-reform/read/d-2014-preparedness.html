Don addresses reported gaps in student preparation for employment and further education, referencing the Conference Board of Canada's framework of employability skills: fundamental skills (communication, information management, numeracy, problem-solving), personal management (attitude, responsibility, adaptability, safety), and teamwork. He argues these skills are primarily developmental and experiential rather than content-based, making school classrooms poorly suited for their acquisition. Don proposes community-based pilot projects with local leadership involvement, positioning community as the classroom and transforming skill development into participatory experience rather than classroom instruction.
Education Philosophy & Reform

January 6, 2014

Editor, Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:



Student preparedness   

Young people report gaps in their preparation for the work force and further education; members of the public agree.  Examples that are given in support of the view usually involve skills needed in every day living.  I think  that the best description of the missing skills is provided by the Conference Board of Canada.   

The Conference Board has identified three clusters employability skills: (1) fundamental skills of communication, managing information, using numbers and solving problems, (2) personal management skills of demonstrating a positive attitude, responsibility, adaptability, working safely and being a lifelong learner, and (3) teamwork skills of working with others and participating in projects and tasks. While labelled employability skills, I believ that they are broader than that and view them as the skills needed for ever day living.

Can these skills be learned?  Absolutely.  Should these skills be learned by young people? Again, absolutely and learned by all people of all ages.  Are they a legitimate part of our educational system?  Again, absolutely.  Can they be learned in school? Maybe; probably not.   I say this for  a couple of reasons.  Schools are organized to deliver content and, in fact, are measure on how well they do it.Employability skills are largely developmental or experiential; they are processes to be developed and cannot be packaged into 50 minute periods.  They don’t fit well into a school environment.  

The improvement of personal skills, I suggest, could be well served from a community based setting with the community  as the classroom, content identified by community leaders and local schools as partners.  It would be a winning combination.  Yes, it would require a modest investment but the return to the community would be substantial.

I suggest a pilot project, running in a couple of communities, under local control.  Based on the recent workshops conducted throughout the Island by Education 20/20, I am certain  a number of communities would be interested and leaders would come forward 

.  

Sincerely,

Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
