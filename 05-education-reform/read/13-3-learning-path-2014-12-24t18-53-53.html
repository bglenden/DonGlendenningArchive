Learning path

No. B-2 November6, 2014


To learn is to change behaviour, to engage in an experiences that have a formative effect on the way we think, feel and act. A person who has learned has new knowledge, a new skill or an different attitude. To help us better understand the learning process, Education 20/20 invited a panel of young people to tell us how they went about learning and what kinds of support were most helpful. After considerable discussion panel members settled on the following steps on the learning path:


  • Gather Information

  • Attend lecture

  • Seek counselling

  • Observe demonstration

  • Attempt task

  • Undergo assessment

  • Motivate self

  • Observe others

  • Use resource material

  • Reflect on experience

  • Take diagnosis

  • Obtain feedback

  • Take instruction

  • Self evaluate

  • Coach others

  • Practice


Students were asked the order in which the help was needed or provided; not surprisingly, individual paths differed. For example, one student said his first step would be to Google the topic; another said if it involved a new skill, he would probably attempt it first, while others liked the traditional lecture as a place to start. Since panel members were in school or recent graduates, much of the discussion revolved around gaining new knowledge rather than skills or attitudes. It also became evident that different purposes for learning did not substantially change the help required, different purposes may well make the path longer although different purposes may affect the time spent on the path.


The chart on the back of this page reflects an attempt to chart the suppoer that learners found useful and to do so in a manner that respects all learning styles, subjects and locations. It’s a work in progress. We think that it is a good start, but it is only a start and a challenge rather than an accomplishment. We welcome your help as we take the next step
