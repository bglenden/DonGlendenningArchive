Letter praising government's consolidation of education services under one department while expressing concerns about workforce training quality. References Good's Dictionary on definitions. Criticizes loss of federal leadership role in training standards and warns that without protection from academic bureaucracy, vocational training will become theoretical with narrowed standards and reduced practice time.


Dear Editor

Careless with our words


Educators have an obligation to be careful with their words; while I m a frequent user of the internet, when in doubt, I still turn to Good’s Dictionary of Education to check on meanings. All this by way of saying that I find I find the fluidity of meanings disconcerting.  


Education is an umbrella word, education services may be organized 

  • by age of learners – early childhood or continuing education
  • subjects being studied – history, IT, math, chemistry, 
  • perceived degree of difficulty, 
  • School year, summer, part time, evening,  
  • purpose for which person enrols – general, vocational, workforce development 
  • nature of governance or funding, etc. – private, public, charter, 
  • Nature of host – employer based, institutional, independent study
  • Educational functions – funding, instruction, learning products, regulation,

D-106

Education PEI


I commend the Government for gathering educational services together in a single Department.   It should improve communication, ease student transition and remove some duplication especially in numeracy and literacy.  It also allows the Department to broaden its mandates from simply delivering programs to that of encouraging and supporting education for all our citizen. Working within  single Department may also encourage schools to offer education services outside the normal mandate and operating hours. The latter could be a significant service. especially for small communities. 


Having said that, however, I am concerned for the quality of future workforce training, Although continuing to provide funds, the Government of Canada appears to have vacated its long-standing leadership role in the field. In years past, the Federal Government provided information, convened national meetings and conferences, insisted on standards, and provided a cadre of roving specialists available to Provinces and Institutions. It is my experience that, while all education needs strong leadership, workplace training especially needs to be protected from the bureaucracy of academia; if not, it will become theoretical, standards will narrow and probably lower and application or practice time be reduced or disappear.

 

Sincerely


Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
