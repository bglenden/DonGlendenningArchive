Silos in Education

On a number of occasions over the past year or so, people currently active in education, have spoken about educational silos, meaning that there is little interaction among providers of educational services.  That conjures up a vision of educators, labouring in splendid isolation from each other and from the outside world.  It may be an unfair image but I believe that it includes more than a kernel of truth.  The silos, and the silo mentality, have emerged for a number of reasons:

  • Professional educators, like other professionals, guard their freedom, and they have done it well.
  • Government has contributed to the silo mentality by allowing, perhaps even supporting, the building of silos.  This is most evident in the case of reading which is offered ty or through the Department of Education, the Department of Innovation and Advanced Learning, the College, the University, employers and a number of other agencies as well.  My search so far has not revealed any of the providers with a mandate to coordinate efforts of all.  It is interesting to note that BC, in its efforts to improve reading, has  has appointed a Superintendent of Reading

Each time I return  Im am struck by the changed silhoutteeWhen As I return to  my family As a follow-up to the discussion about PEI’s standing on PISA tests last year, readers may be interested to learn of a recent report from the OECD that shows a direct link between PISA test results and school autonomy; the article should also put to rest the debate over posting results. The information is especially timely given plans to change to a single board of Trustees.


Simply stated, there is a direct relationship between school performance and school autonomy.  In the words of the OECD Report:

  • “In countries where schools have greater autonomy over what is taught and how students 

are assessed, students tend to perform better. 

  • In countries where schools account for their results by posting achievement data publicly, schools that enjoy greater autonomy in resource allocation tend to show better student performance than those with less autonomy. 
  • In countries where there are no such accountability arrangements, schools with greater autonomy in resource allocation tend to perform worse.” 


Autonomy, or freedom to act, varies from country to country but may include such things as allocation of  resources, hiring and dismissing teachers, formulating budgets, decisions about curricula and assessment, course offerings and textbooks.  A quick scan of trustee roles reveals that, at least in Ontario and Alberta, trustees have much more decision making authority than Trustees on PEI.  


If you feel schools should have more autonomy, tell your MLA – and do it now while new regulations are being written. I’d also be interested in your views at standards2020@gmail.com


Sincerely


Don Glendenning
