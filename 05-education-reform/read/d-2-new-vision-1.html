Education Philosophy & Reform


January 28, 2016


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir: 


Education lacks a vision


Public education has lost its way.  It didn’t just happen this morning, or yesterday or even last year.  It has lost sight of its purposes, is inward looking, inflexible and remote.  The system needs a clearer focus, measurable goals, engagement with the people it serves, alignment of its various components, priorities and freedom to act with accountability for actions taken.  It must be frustrating to be a professional working within the system.   


It seems to me that the problems with our education system are primarily organizational; they are the result of a poorly defined vision, a failure to heed the   principle that, when organizing, “form follows function”, .  As a result,  we do not have a Department of Education but rather a Department of Public Instruction pushing a model that is expensive and also out of touch with the times.  


The importance of diplomas and degrees is on the decline.  My morning paper mentioned two more companies that are dropping college degrees as an entry requirement, a few days ago my paper carried an item on “changing college admissions”, another on December 26 about high school diplomas “coming up short”, an article on school staffing patterns, and the fear that pervades the public school system.


On Sunday, January 17, some seventy Islanders gathered at City Cinema to view a film, Most Likely to Succeed, the film described one school’s attempt to improve education through project based learning.  A project-based approach involves having students design and construct devices, investigate questions, undertake research, discuss ideas and generally challenge cittent thinking; while not a panacea, project-based learning appears to produce positive results.  


As a lateral thinker, it seems to me that project based learning may also have potential as an approach to educational reform.  My nomination for a project would be “basic skills” and, within the cluster of basic skills, let’s start with mathematics. 

Sincerely,

Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
