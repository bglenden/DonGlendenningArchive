Don argues that the public school system's grading practices have become confusing and lack integrity, allowing students to graduate with high school diplomas without achieving grade 12 level work. He proposes adopting "level" terminology instead of "grade" to describe achievement, as used by International Baccalaureate, PISA, P-CAP, and qualification frameworks worldwide. This would allow students to progress through subjects at different achievement levels according to their mastery rather than age-based groupings.


January 20, 2016


Editor,

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir: 

Integrity of the public school grading system


The fact that a Grade ten student is doing Grade six math is confusing; given this, it is seems possible that students could graduate from high school and be awaeded a high school diploma without really having successfully achieved a Grade 12 level on any subject. This is confusing to say the least but also calls into question the meaning of graduation and a graduation diploma. Social promotion and the new report cards, are beginning to assume the characteristics of a shell game. 


The simple resolution is to adopt a separate term for draling with achievement; in fact, some have already made the shift. The International Baccalaurte program  reportd subject levels, so does the Canadian Conservatory of music as does PISA uses six proficiency levels, the Province refers to key stages, P-CAP has 4 levels,  the Conference Board of Canada uses letter grades when referring to Provincial rankings on educaton and skills.  Many countries have developed qualification framework.  


If only to improve communication with studentds and psrentd, I urge the swithch , and do it soon


It’s time to select a different word to identify levels of achievement; but, “level” appears to be the preferred word. Changing the term would allow a student to study any subject at any level.  As a result, a grade 11 student could be taking level 7 math, level 4 physics and level 6 literature. The International Baccalaureate program does it, the Canadian Red Cross does it for swimming, some qualification authorities do it, and so should the public school system.
