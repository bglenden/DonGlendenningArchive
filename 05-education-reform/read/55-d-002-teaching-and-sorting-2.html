Extended exploration of incompatible roles in schools: education vs. training, general vs. vocational, credentials vs. qualifications. Core argument: schools have dual conflicting mandates—teaching/learning and sorting/ranking students. Distinguishes: learning (behavioral change), education (sum of formative experiences), schooling (government-funded formal instruction), credentials (what was taught), and qualifications (what one can do). Proposes independent qualification body separate from schools. Concludes schooling/credentialing and teaching are fundamentally incompatible roles; recommends schools focus on learning, external bodies handle certification. Extensively documented draft with multiple revisions showing refinement of ideas. - **Key People**: Teachers, learners, employers - **Key Organizations**: Schools, educational institutions - **Tags**: PROFESSIONAL, EDUCATION, MANUSCRIPT, ANALYSIS - **Eulogy Relevance**: HIGH - Central to his lifelong education reform philosophy; shows systematic thinking about role clarity and institutional design.

As long as I can remember there has been a debate about the role of schools with a fairly sharp distinction being made between education and training or vocational and general education. To the old issues we can add education versus schooling, and even more recently about credentials and qualifications. Here’s my version.

  • First we have learning; that’s when we engage in activities that change our behaviour; changes occur in in one or more of our attitudes, skills or knowledge;

  • Our education is the result of the result of experiences that have made an impact on us, however, because not two people have exactly the same genes or the same previous learning experiences, no two people have the same education.

  • The process of learning can benefit from the help of others in the form information, instruction, counsel, encouragement, feedback, resource material etc. Help comes from many sources especially the home, the community, one’s personal endeavours and of course schools.

  • Schools provide important support for learning, for ones education, but it is only a part. Schools help learners learn what governments believe to be important and what governments are prepared to pay for.

  • Educators have a habit of classifying the content to be learned as being general or vocational; many also believe that the general, as they have defined it, is more valuable than the vocational. But they are wrong; the content is neutral; whether it becomes general or vocational depends upon the objective of the learner and the standards to be met.

  • The study of any subject can take one to higher and higher levels

  • The role of educators is to design learning activities that will help learners learn in new fields or reach a level higher levels than they are at at present.

Now comes the tricky part:

  • Schools group students by age levels, set aside i92 days of instruction each year and help students learn as much as they can within those parameters

  • Society has asked and schools have complied with the request, that teachers not only teach but that they also sort, a kind of triage.

  • A study would likely reveal that there is insufficient change in students’ standing from year to year to warrant the cost of sotrtin, however the practice continues

  • This approach to sorting is inconsistent with the practice in other professions and places teachers in a conflict of role. Sports activities have coaches and referees, legal matters are dealt with by lawyers and judges, finance has accountants and auditors.

  • But even more important, feedback to students, parents and the public at large concentrates on the pecking order rather than learning.

  • The process is further complicated by the fact that the publicity around credentials, what the teachers have taught, overshadows the interest of the public in what has been learned.

In order to teach, one also needs a credential, a university degree, and a qualification, a teaching licence, the latter attesting to one’s ability to teach.

Are schools, colleges and universities institutions of learning and learning or are they also, some might say primarily institutions for sorting. Resolving this conflict will, I believe, go a long way towards helping us provide a quality education for every Canadian.

Degrees and diplomas are considered credentials in that they may provide entrance into an arena but are not an indicator of how well one will perform once there.
