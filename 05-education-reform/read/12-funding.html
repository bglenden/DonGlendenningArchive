Proposing that school funding should mirror post-secondary models—combining base institutional grants with enrollment-driven funds reflecting student choice—Glendenning argues this would improve accountability without increasing total spending. He advocates dividing school budgets into a guaranteed portion and an enrollment-contingent portion, such that schools gain or lose revenue based on attracting students. This market-based approach would require schools to respond to community preferences while preserving universal public education. It reflects his consistent theme of introducing accountability through choice.

Dear Editor:

Funding


It is a given that education is important to the well-being of our country and its citizens; one

recent report points out that, in spite of substantial increases during the past few years, Canada

still lags in investment in this area. Without doubt, even though enrolment is declining,

increased funding is still required to both keep abreast and to-make improvements - but just

increasing grants is not sufficient.


I have often observed how little choice there is in our education system especially in the early

years; it seems that we can have a choice for practically everyone who serves us except for

those who teach our children. I was interested, therefore, to read a number of articles earlier

this year pointing to a growing body of evidence that supports school choice as an instrument

for educationa I improvement.


ln my view, the issues of funding and choice converge at the postsecondary level where an

institution's income results from two separate decisions. One is the decision by the government

to make a grant to a school; the other is the decisions of a student to choose one institution

over another. Both trigger revenue to the institution and establish the operating budget. lt is

time to apply the same principle to funding our public schools.


ln a nutshell, I am suggesting that Government continue to pay the full cost of public school

education, but that the grant to a school be in two parts. The major grant would flow directly to

a school as it does today. The remainder of a school's funding would be enrolment driven and

would result from a student's choice of one school rather than another. Gaining or losing a

student would impact on a schools overall budget.


Having to respond to market changes is normal in our society and would, in my view, have a

positive impact on the public school system.


Sincerely
