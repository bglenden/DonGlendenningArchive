Occasional paper calling for comprehensive overhaul of PEI's education services, describing them as narrowly focused, cognitively based, bureaucratic, and lacking accountability. Glendenning argues that education policy should serve all 150,000 PEI citizens across six life roles (individual, citizen, family member, learner, producer, consumer), not just youth in schools. Identifies 11 key issues including purpose/mission clarity, structure, political embedment, standards, accountability, professional autonomy, and transparency. Emphasizes that public school system conflates attendance with education, echoing World Bank concerns.

No. 17 June 4, 2020

Quality:

Overhaul needed

Education services on PEI need an overhaul; they are narrowly focused, cognitively based, bureaucratic, lack accountability, and focus on program delivery rather than on learner development – they also lack a self-correcting mechanism.


I do not, we cannot, blame the current government, the previous government or any single government that went before for the current status; Government has a responsibility however, for instituting corrective measures.


PEI has some 150,000 citizens; all are potential users of educational services. Each Islander fills six life roles, that of an individual, citizen, family member, learner, producer and consumer. Islanders want our students to be literate and numerate, to become independent learners, to take care of themselves and learn how to work and play together. Education’s business is to help Islanders gain new and better skills, knowledge, attitudes and values. Our education need’s, interests and capabilities are many and varied, but policy documents are focused on providing services to young people through schools


Our educational explorations included a survey if citizen math needs, study of continuing education needs in the Souris area, public presentations, , internet document scans and countless discussions, ad hoc math committee, World Bank and OECD reports.

The current conversation, however, is politically filtered and often bogged down on matters of administrivia (school upgrading, credits, grades, noon meals, technology, etc.) at the expense of changes that help Islanders gain knowledge, skills, attitudes and values needed to help them to thrive in later life.


It is the writer’s view that much of the needed change is blocked (perhaps not encouraged is more accurate) by an outdated system


embedded in a body that can give it neither

The direction, freedom nor support needed to accomplish the task at hand. Government not only needs to change a number of existing policies and practices but also needs to allow, nay encourage, change to happen. The World Bank recently chided Ministries of Education for not upgrading their own operation and for with holding autonomy from schools.



Education needs a plan, something simple, some definitions, some guiding principles, description of services to be offered, services covered by government, and an organization that reflects the task at hand. Such a statement should be short, sharp and developed with public input – but not by outside experts.


Education is a recognized profession, it is time to allow them to enter the fray as professionals in their own right and not to be subject to a political filter..
