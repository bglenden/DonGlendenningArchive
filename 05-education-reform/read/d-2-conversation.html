A February 12, 2020 letter to the editor calling for public conversation about education. Don argues that service-centered education became product-focused, with educators withdrawing from public discourse into professional silos. He quotes an Ontario education activist's five critical questions and advocates for an all-party panel to lead structured public discussion about education fundamentals. This piece reveals Don's concern about public disconnection from education policy.

February 12. 2020

Dear Editor:

CONVERSATION NEEDED

Life is complex; people fill many roles; there is much to be learned - to communicate, to get along with others, to look after ourselves and .to look after the environment.  It’s more than many parents can handle; parents need help; the rest of us need help too. It’s in the interests of the community to provide help. But providing help is complex. There are so many people, such a range of differences, disparate needs, etc. etc.  Government wants to help, so they set up a system of schools; decide what everyone needs and that all young people must attend  – but service got lost, education become a product described by hours, lessons, outcomes, grades, requirements, diplomas, degrees, years of schooling, etc.; responding to citizen needs is no longer part of the equation; educators seem to have withdrawn from public conversation and, stay inside their respective silos.  “Service” and education got lost in the process.

It’s time It’s time for a public conversation about education; to quote a long- time education activist from Ontario:

“The public, all the people in an organized, preferably democratic, society/state should/must take ownership of how their young are educated. Everyone and especially parents should be partners in setting the responses to the following issues:  

  • why should the state educate it’s citizens?  
  • How should the state educate it’s citizens?  
  • Who should decide when a citizen is educated? 
  • What part of the state’s limited resources should be put towards educating every citizen?  Who decides how much?
  • Should schools be operated? As  a for profit business?  Like a community library? Like a not for profit hospital with a chief medical officer and a chief admin officer?

Currently there are so many players with different and conflicting agendas involved with public education it is no wonder “schools” are either dysfunctional or operational at a high social, psychological, emotional, organizational stress level...

.  Islanders deserve to be better informed but also to be consulted about education – what it is, who it’s for, principles on which it will operate, what government will pay for, etc.  Because of the fear factor, any conversation likely needs o be sanctioned, and probably led, by the Minister. In the spirit of political cooperation, perhaps an all- party panel could be struck to lead such a discussion.
