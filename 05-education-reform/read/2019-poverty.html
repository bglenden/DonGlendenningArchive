Nobel prize winners weigh in on public education


The key to improving education is better instruction; that’s the message in yesterday’s announcement of winners of the prestigious Nobel Economics Prize for study on Poverty. The award was for ground- breaking work in the study of health and education in Kenya and India.


The link between poverty and educational attainment is well documented, yet half the world’s children leave school without basic literacy and mathematical skills. And many Island children, even after 12 years of public education, require upgrading of basic skills in order to participate in further education.


The answer to inadequate performance, according to the study, is not more teachers, technology, textbooks or school meals, but rather better instruction, tutoring, and mentoring. This study, along with the PISA research a decade or so ago also calls for greater accountability.


A recent study by the Montreal Economic Institute credits “accountability” for the strong standing by Quebec on PISA test results and, as the Institute points out, at virtually no additional cost.
