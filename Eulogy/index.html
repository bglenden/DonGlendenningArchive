Eulogy for Donald Ernest Malcolm Glendenning

Brian Glendenning · February 19, 2026
Home › Eulogy

Glendennings are not much given to hyperbole. We tend to speak with restraint and moderation, with strong feelings and sentiment kept in check. My wife Marie tells me that when it's time for my own inscription on a gravestone it should just say “everything was fine.” As Dad's list of indignities grew over the past year, occasionally he would allow and I would agree that something or other was becoming “quite the nuisance.” With that as background, I will tell you that Donald Ernest Malcolm Glendenning was an exceptionally fine man.

Dad was born on September 8, 1929, in Black River Bridge, New Brunswick — the youngest in a family of nine children of Elmer and Jane Watling Glendenning. His father was a construction man who worked on major projects in the United States before bringing the family back to Little Branch in 1919. Savings he had left in an American bank were lost in the crash that same year Dad was born. By the time Dad came along, Elmer's health was failing, but he was not a man to sit idle. Dad's brother-in-law, Brydone Weeks, remembered Elmer having Walter wheel him down to the brook where the men were scything hay. He couldn't swing a scythe himself anymore, but he'd sit there with his whetstone, sharpening extra blades so the men always had a sharp one waiting when they came by.

Dad's mother, Jane, held the household together — and it was a big household. She baked bread nearly every day, and her specialties included Yankee toast and apple pie with cream. Her husband might tell her at noon that twelve or fifteen men would be in for supper, and she'd manage it. She loved to have visitors — Don could not once remember her saying she wished anyone hadn't come. And she believed in education, encouraging her children and everyone around her to continue their schooling.

Elmer died in May of 1938, when Dad was only eight. After that, the older brothers — Walter, John, Kenneth, and Norman — carried on the farm work, and Dad grew up learning alongside them. He remembered going to the woods with John and John singing hymns as they came home on snowy evenings. When Dad was sixteen and working the boats in Chatham, loading heavy pit props down in the hold, John could see him struggling. “Let me take that one, Donald,” he'd say — and he said it many times. It was a family where everyone did their share, but they looked out for one another.

Dad attended the Little Branch School through grade eight, then the Miramichi Rural High School. In 1947 he left home for Teachers' College in Fredericton, and by September he was seventeen and teaching in a one-room school at Kouchibouguac. He later won a Beaverbrook Overseas Scholarship to the University of London, crossed the Atlantic by ship, and made friends wherever he went.

At Bradley University in Peoria, Illinois, he met Carol Louise Chesney. They were married on January 31, 1958. Dad headed the technical teacher training program at the New Brunswick Institute of Technology. He then returned to Indiana University and earned his Ph.D., and later worked in Ottawa as a training specialist with the Department of Labour and the Department of Manpower and Immigration.

By 1968 Ottawa's Training Branch was being downsized, and Dad began to think about moving. He applied for two presidencies: Nanaimo and Prince Edward Island. Nanaimo offered first, but with a catch: the offer was conditional on him accepting a local candidate as his Vice President. Dad believed a leader needed the autonomy to build their own team. He declined the offer. By rejecting the west, he was opting for the east.

In 1969, Dad became the founding President of Holland College on Prince Edward Island, a position he held for eighteen years. Henry has described the early days of the College for you. Education was his lifelong passion. He believed schools spent too much time on grades and not enough on learning. Under his leadership, Holland College built its competency-based approach and launched major new programs.

His honours included the Order of Canada and an honorary degree from UPEI, and Holland College later named Glendenning Hall in his honour. After retirement, he ran a consulting firm and helped found the Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island.

But the public record tells only part of the story. Dad was a man who wrote poetry — quiet, careful poems he kept mostly to himself. After his mother died in 1964, he wrote of passing a store with greeting cards he would “purchase never more for Mother.” He wrote children's stories too.

He and Carol built a life on Prince Edward Island centred on the house on Queen Elizabeth Drive and the cottage at Glenway, overlooking Orwell Bay. The cottage became the family's gathering place — the spot where sunsets were watched, neighbours became lifelong friends, and children grew up. He wrote a poem about it: “In a world so filled with turmoil, how fortunate are we, for the experience of Glenway, our oasis by the sea.”

For thirteen years, from 1997 to 2010, Dad published The Family Chronicle — two hundred issues that connected relatives who didn't know they were related, from Scotland to Australia. When he reached Issue 100, he wrote: “Reaching 100 was never an objective but has become a milestone.” Then he carried on for another hundred.

Mom and Dad delighted in their granddaughters, Lisa and Laura, who came for long visits most summers, where they would fish, drive the tractor, and get bitten by mosquitoes. They travelled widely, but always came back to PEI and the cottage at Glenway.

Dad kept up his regular coffees with old friends and wrote many letters to the editor about education. In his eighties, he founded Education 20/20 because he wasn't finished. He kept writing occasional papers into his nineties.

His father had taught him a verse that stayed with him: “Try and try again, boys, you'll succeed at last.”

Among his papers, Dad had written down his thoughts about these occasions. He wanted his favourite hymns sung, which you will hear today: “The Lord Is My Shepherd,” “Haven of Rest,” and “Beyond the Sunset.” About that last one he wrote: “You know how often we watched the sunset at Glenway and how much I enjoyed it.”

An exceptionally fine man.
