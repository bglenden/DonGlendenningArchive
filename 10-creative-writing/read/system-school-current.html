A systems analysis of Canada's public school system written June 25, 2011. Don applies systems theory to education, defining a school system as a human-made entity with bounded components, relationships, designed outputs, and governing rules. He describes the current system's purpose as helping youth gain skills, knowledge, and attitudes for life roles, existing as either preparation for employment or personal growth. The document catalogs the system's basics (ages 6-16 compulsory attendance, September-June calendar), characteristics (state-run monopoly with limited participation), service gaps, and system weaknesses including focus on post-secondary preparation, diffused accountability, poorly defined standards, limited public input, outdated delivery model, and lack of coordination. This represents Don's sophisticated understanding of educational systems and their structural limitations in serving diverse learner needs.

School System?


School system:

  • is a human made system

  • With a boundary

  • comprised of a set of components and relationships of interconnected parts

  • Designed to work as a coherent entity for the delivery of outputs

  • The term system may also refer to a set of rules that governs structure and/or behavior

Most systems share common characteristics, including:

  • Systems have structure, defined by components/elements and their composition;

  • Systems have behavior, which involves inputs, processing and outputs of material, energy, information, or data;

  • Systems have interconnectivity: the various parts of a system have functional as well as structural relationships to each other.

  • Systems may have some functions or groups of functions



A school system refers to the institutions, programs and services provided by or with the approval of a provincial government. Comments in this paper deal only with what is commonly known as the public school system serving young people up to the age of roughly 18 years.

Components


The purpose of schools is to help young people gain skills, knowledge and attitudes thought to be required in normal life roles. There are two divergent schools of thought preparing for productive employment and growth and fulfillment.


The basics:

Children between the ages of six and sixteen must attend school in Canada. Schooling is provided without charge for all residents of Canada. Private schooling options exist, but charge with a yearly tuition fee. Most children begin school at age 4, with part time schooling called Kindergarten. Elementary school generally runs between grades 1 to 8, and Secondary, or “high school” between 9 and 12 (though some provincial and private institution variations exist).The school year runs from September to June, five days a week, for generally six hours a day.

Characteristics:

  • What is taught in school, the manner in which it is taught, and the recognition of what has been learned is also determined by the state.

  • State run

  • Monopolies

  • Limited public participation in policy setting

  • Very limited choice

Service Gaps

  • Infants and early childhood

  • Little or no access to specialists

  • Access to credit for self directed learning

  • Public information

System Weaknesses

  • Focus on preparation for postsecondary education

  • Diffused and obscured accountability

  • Poorly defined standards

  • Little opportunity for public input

  • Delivery model in conflict with current educational philosophy

  • State monopoly/closed system

  • Segmented/silos

  • Lacks diagnosis capability

  • Limited coordination among providers

  • Out dated delivery system
