TRANSITION


“A student’s ability to transition from secondary school to post-secondary education and the labour market is a key measure of the education system’s success for students” – CMEC, July 2016


The above statement by the Council of Ministers of Education Canada (CMEC) highlights a concern that is shared by many educators, employers, certification agencies and the community at large. The issue will not resolve itself and cannot be resolved by educators alone, but resolve it we must - and soon. Although not a simple issue, there appears to be some agreement that the performance level of a person in a field of study or employment is determined by:

  1. The nature and range of skills that a person can demonstrate,

  2. The level of performance at which each skill can be demonstrated,

  3. Clustering, and

  4. Relevance to the job at hand.


The following are given as typical examples of the transition dilemma; a young person who:

  • Graduated from high school but lacks specific course(s) required for entrance to program of choice.

  • Has high school credits for specific course(s) required entrance to postsecondary studies but lacks a high school graduation diploma.

  • Graduates from high school and later decides to pursue postsecondary studies but does not meet postsecondary entrance requirements.

  • Graduates from high school but lack essential skills to enter employment

  • Graduates from high school, secures employment but lacks essential skills to maintain or progress in employment

  • Does not graduate from high school and, therefore, is rejected for employment or further study even though he/she may have the required skills.


Conversations about the issue with fellow Islanders reveal a number of likely contributing factors:

  • Communication between and among the parties involved

  • Entrance requirements to postsecondary institutions

  • Assessment practices

  • Reporting practices

  • Benchmarks

  • Conflicting views on role of educational institutions

  • Credentials versus qualifications


The issue is being thrust to the fore for a number of reasons including:

  • Education costs

  • Skills needed for a 21st. century workforce

  • Changing nature of the economy

  • Coexistence of skill shortages and high unemployment

  • IT revolution


It is more than a school issue, consequently, we’ve invited a small working group of stakeholders to explore the issue to see what action, if any should be taken.


Possible participants:






According to the literature and our conversations, contributors to smooth transition include:

  1. Open lines of communication

  2. Clearly stated institutional entrance requirements

  3. Clear employer requirements

  4. Reliable assessments

  5. Relevant documentation about learner’s performance

  6. Responsibility for transition in someone’s position description

  7. Information and counselling


While transition applies to many, perhaps all subject areas, again from my conversations, I’m suggesting that we limit our discussion to the fields of math.


Public perceptions:

  • High school graduation cannot be counted on as a measure of academic achievement

  • Postsecondary institutions are accepting students who do not meet their requirements

  • Students with lower skill levels on entrance to postsecondary programs are likely to be less well prepared on completion of their program.

  • Millions of dollars are spent on remedial instruction in subjects already studied at the public school level

  • Postsecondary entrance requirements are too narrow

  • Expectations laid on the public school system are unrealistic

  • Standards not adequately defined

  • Institutions operate in isolation from each other
