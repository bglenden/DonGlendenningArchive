People are said t fill six life roles, that of an individual, citizen, family member, student, consumer and producer, an education system is to help people prepare for and thrive in those roles.


There are various suggestions as to the best way to prepare.

Typically, the structure of organizations is based on the dictum “form follows function”; as the basis for of the organizational structure. The Department seems to have ignored this rule.



To help people prepare education provides help in the form of information, instruction learning materials, coaching,


An education system those roles Preparing for employment, furthering ones academic studies, knowledge and skills of citizenship are frequent purposes. Departments of education need leadership in th evarious functions function a citizens or just to be self-sufficient in their future years.


The structure of the Australian Department of Education reflects a number of live roles – childhood development, citizenship studies, employment preparation the education task. Context

4. Autonomy – the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development


The literature speaks of the need for educational reform. The first step should be structural. Here are some suggestions:

  1. Purpose – the PEI education literature refers to helping people find a place in society; that place, I suggest, is a role and should be named or described – a citizen, a producer, a consumer or ???. Both content and assessment require a clearer purpose.

  2. Services - Education is a service. Typical education services include: information, counselling, instruction, products and technology, quality assurance, governance, funding and human resources. Learners may need one or all services at any point in their education journey and services should be front and center in our planning.

  3. Services need to be clearly identified and available to all Island citizens not only to citizens enrolled in schools and programs.

  4. Structure -” Form follows function” is still the standard when structuring any venture; one would expect, therefore, that the education structure would reflect services provided rather than clients served.

  5. Autonomy – as mentioned earlier, education is a service. Front line service providers (our teachers) need both the expertise and autonomy to respond to learner needs. The OECD tells us that PISA results are better for students in schools with autonomy for their day-to-day operation; the World Bank advises Ministries of Education to delegate. PEI should do the same.

  6. Accountability -. Mathew Bedard of the Montreal Economic Institute points out that “autonomy and accountability jointly applied” would cost practically nothing and allow education to better adapt to the diversity of student needs and aptitudes. Yet teachers and principals in PEI appear to have little freedom to tailor services to suit individual learner needs.



These issues are not new; no doubt readers have others to propose. All should be considered. They need to be dealt with soon, and should be dealt with in a very public manner.

Don Glendenning
