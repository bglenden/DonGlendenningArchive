Relevant Standards



A gap exists between what the public expects of schools and what schools deliver; the two do not speak the same language.  Two recent reports, a US report on Common Core State Standards and the Ontario Provincial Report Card address the gap but both, in my view, are still works in progress.  If working models exist, they have not come into my view - at least not yet.  In my view, exit standards is the pressing issue of the day.  By standards, I refer to the level of growth attributed to being in school.


Standard: A standard is a level of performance expected of a service or product


A standard has three components:

Criteria are the factors against which we measure.  Criteria for evaluating writing, for example, may include:

Vocabulary

Style

Use of reference

Sentence structure

Creativity

Grammar, 


2 A rating scale describes levels of performance; the scale for writing shows six levels:


6 Creatively written whiled observing acceptable standards of grammar, sentence structure and organization.

5 Very well organized showing precise and wide vocabulary, good sentence structure and appropriate grammar and some creativity.

4 Organized with few errors in grammar and sentence structure,

3 Organized with acceptable grammar and sentence structure.

2 Generally organized but shows limited vocabulary and significant errors in grammar and sentence structure.

1 Disorganized and unfocused with many errors in grammar and sentence structure accompanied by a limited vocabulary.


3 A benchmark is a level of performance which is widely recognized by practitioners and which serves as the reference point. A benchmark anchors a standard in reality.  A benchmark for reading could be as simple as the ability to read and discuss articles in the daily paper.   An average, competent or skilled plumber is “a person who comes to your house and can usually fix the plumbing problem without having to call or return to the shop. “ 


Although we may be able to remove judgment from the testing process, we cannot remove judgment from the standards process.  To use an example from the health field, a doctor subjects a patient to a variety of tests, then makes a judgment as to the next course of action.  The answer lies, not in the removal of judgment but in its consistency.
