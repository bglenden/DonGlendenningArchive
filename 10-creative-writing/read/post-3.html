This incomplete but insightful piece addresses Don's persistent frustration with conflating education and schooling. He defines education as "an experience that has a formative effect on the way we think feel or act," emphasizing it as a personal change process occurring across diverse contexts and relationships. The distinction matters crucially: schools organize directed learning experiences, but education extends far beyond institutional boundaries. He notes that much early education occurs in homes and families, with schools playing a supplementary role. The piece critiques misleading appropriation of "education" terminology by schools.

My brother-in-law used to delight in telling stories about the impeccable grammar of teachers of English, especially about the use of “I” and “me”.  My frustration, however, comes from the failure of people, especially educators, to distinguish between education and schooling.  The most recent being a question from a new friend about where I got my education; it turned out, what he really wanted to know was where I went to university.


On the one hand, when I think of a person pursuing or getting an education, I think of someone gaining new skills, acquiring new information or changing an attitude.  Education in this sense is a very personal process of bringing about change.  In fact, my personal glossary  defines educaton as an experience that has a formative effect on the way we think feel or act.  That would include adding new skills, knowledge or attitudes as well as improving the ones we already have.  Educational experiences can occur at any time or place with or without a the involvement of another person.
