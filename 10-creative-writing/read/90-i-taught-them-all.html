The adapted poem appears to reflect Don's broader educational philosophy that teaching technical skills alone is insufficient without addressing underlying human needs.

No. 89 April 27, 2020



I Taught Them All

A reflective poem about teaching.

Adapted from Naomi John White, Progressive Education (1943).



I have taught high school for ten years. During that time, I have given assignments, among others, to a murderer, a street-smart fighter, and a thief.


The murderer was a quiet little boy who sat on the front seat and regarded me with pale blue eyes.


The fighter lounged by the window and let loose at intervals in a raucous laugh that startled even the geraniums.


And the thief was a gay-hearted Lothario with a song on his lips.


The murderer awaits death in the state penitentiary; the pugilist lost an eye in a brawl in Hong Kong; and the thief, by standing on tip-toe, can see the window of my room from the county jail.


All these pupils once sat in my room, sat and looked at me gravely across worn brown desks. I must have been a great help to those pupils – I taught them the rhyming scheme of the Elizabethan sonnet and how to diagram a complex sentence.




Rediscovered recently

This poem has haunted me ever since I started teaching


Don Glendenning, Editor
