A substantial letter arguing that bureaucratic constraints represent the primary barrier to education improvement. Don advocates for increased autonomy at school and teacher levels, greater flexibility in scheduling and staffing, and local decision-making authority. He references his Holland College experience as evidence that organizational freedom enables quality improvement. He calls for an Education Council with broad community representation and revised Schools' Act to delegate greater autonomy. This piece synthesizes his core conviction that systemic improvement requires structural freedom combined with accountability.
Holland College & Institutional Legacy

Editor

Guardian

Charlottetown, PEI


Dear Sir:


The freedom to act, or alternatively an oppressive bureaucracy, is perhaps the distinguishing feature of most education systems in Canada today, and is a major barrier to educational improvement. The emphasis on time, content, conformity and batch processes are out of sync with the twenty first century. Most people I talk with, both inside and outside the education system, know this and are frustrated by it, but cannot seem to find the key to unlock it. Increasingly, people are in the mood for change, but where should one start?


Let’s deal quickly with responsibility; who is responsible for education? The simple answer is “We are”. We can blame the government, the PEITF, school Board, individual teachers or whoever, but when we are finished we still have the problem. So let’s start with ourselves. We all need become better informed, ask more questions, enter the debate, and reward desired changes. The kind of change people want is most likely when dozens of people speak to the Premier, the Minister, their MJA, their school board member, their school principal. Share your views with everyone who will listen. One friend mentioned convening a family gathering for the purpose of discussing education, and that family included a number of people who work in the profession. Engagement


The report of the Education Governance Commission contains useful information about the state of education on PEI, however, based on their findings I would propose a different set of recommendations; here are three:

  • That the Minister establish an Education Council reflecting a cross section of Island composed of users of the system, students, parents, employers, and others.

  • That the Schools’ Act be revised to delegate greater autonomy and accountability to schools. Principals need to be given more freedom in choosing the tools they need including the ability to adjust the budget, funds for professional development, staffing patterns, etc.




During the past year or more, I’ve had conversations on the subject of education with parents, employers, educators, retirees and more in kitchens and coffee shops across the Island. Some people sought me out, some were people I had just met who wanted to talk about education before we got down to business, and others were initiated by me. Some complaints arose from the experience of a child or grandchild; some are concerned about an unchanging system in a changing world,
