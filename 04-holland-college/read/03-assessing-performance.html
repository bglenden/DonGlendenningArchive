Using his high school shop class experience (40% theory, 40% practical, 20% attitude), he argues attitudes are undervalued—they're actually the most important employment factor. Assessing Performance


Much has been said about competency or outcome-based education.  It is one of the major educational issues of our day; it also has its critics.  Many of the criticisms, I find, apply to a version of CBE that is quite different from mine.  Such versions usually suggest a rigid, narrow and mechanical approach.  They are limited to a restricted list of outcomes, or they fail to address the whole area of standards and assessment.

Criteria and approaches for determining competencies have been described and approaches to their identification are many and varied.  In fact, books have been written on the subject.  A major gap exists in the literature, however, related to the assessment of trainee performance.  Performance assessment remains largely uncharted and some would say “un-chartable” waters.

When I took “shop courses” as part of my high school program, I recall being graded on both theory and practical work.  The theory was a result of my tests and examinations throughout the year.  My practical mark was for the projects which I had completed.  My final grade was based upon theory, practical and attitude.  The breakdown, as I recall, was 40% for theory, 40% for practical and 20% for attitude.  I’m told that, at least in some institutions, similar criteria are still in use.

It is difficult to defend the practice of averaging of school grades at any time, but there is no defense for averaging ratings for theory and practice I order to arrive at levels of performance.  Neither  can one, in good conscience, average ratings of a number of performance tasks.  Averaging theory and practice may be likened to determining  a person’s comfort level by averaging the temperature of a person with his head in a refrigerator and feet in an oven.  On average, he is comfortable.

My recent reading suggests a major realignment of this weighting.  Attitudes are much more important to finding and holding employment than 20 per cent suggests.  In fact, it is the most important factor, bar none.

Existing student rating systems are school based; they are not fund in life.  That’s unfortunate.  We should be using the same system that students will encounter in real life.  School based systems measure the acquisition of knowledge; real-life assessment measures the application of knowledge.

A rating scale should provide both criteria and levels.  Some possible performance criteria could include:

Quality ― the accuracy with which a person can consistently perform a task.

Quantity ―the speed with which a person is able to perform a task to an acceptable level of quality or the amount of work that a person can accomplish within a given time.

Problem Solving ― the ability to overcome unexpected circumstances in order to perform a task.

Leadership ―the ability to lead others in the performance of a task.

Supervision ― the ability to consistently perform a task at an acceptable level of speed and accuracy without (or with a minimum of) supervision.

And there are others.

These are criteria; how do we establish levels?  There are at least 3 easily-identified standards to be considered for occupational training.

A level of performance for which an employer, customer or client would not pay.

A level of performance which employers or colleagues accept as competent.  Performance at this level may be considered as a “benchmark” expected of any qualified and experienced person working in an occupation or field.

A level of performance which is reflected in the ability to lead, coach or supervise others.

The critical task is to be construct a rating scale that takes into account both criteria and levels in the context of an occupation.

How, you ask, does one take into account the information or knowledge intrinsic to the performance of a task?  The acquisition of knowledge in an occupation should increase one’s level of performance (or else why is it important?).  Knowledge, itself, is neither a criteria or a level, although it may be a factor in reaching an acceptable level.  Knowledge must be factored in ― it should not be ignored but it is not a performance criteria.

Rating scales and benchmarks are seldom, if ever, absolute; they are contextual and that context is income-generating employment.  Performance at a task cannot be scientifically measured but it can be rated.  An Instructor/Learning Manager is a mediator in the rating process ― interpreting the achievements of a learner in the context of the occupation.  This is an added reason for requiring instructors to have relevant and recent work experience.  At the same time, an instructor interprets the circumstances of the occupation to the learner.

The number of levels of ratings will depend to a large extent on individual preferences.  Traditionally, levels range from 0 to 100 or from A to F.  The ones which I encounter in the literature range from 4 to 8.  Six would be my preference.

These comments barely introduce the subject of performance assessment.  You can rest assured that you will be hearing  much more about it as time goes on. 

Dr. Donald Glendenning is a Past President of the Canadian vocational Association and President of Glendenning Associates Inc. based in Charlottetown.
