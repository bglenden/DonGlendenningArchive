Drawing on his experience with Holland College's year-round Secretarial Arts program, Don advocates for offering additional instruction days to students who earned B grades or lower, enabling them to achieve A-level performance. Reaching for an “A” 


Schools operate for a set number of days each year and students are encouraged to learn as much as they can in the time available.  In the world they are about to enter, the reverse holds true; standards are set with time to reach the standard being the variable.  


Can you imagine a patient being turfed out of a hospital because the standard recovery time was exceeded?  Or having your car returned with only three of the four brake pads replaced because the mechanic had to move on to another vehicle?  Or the cladding being left off your new home because the builder miscalculated the time required to complete the job?  Of course not.  So why do we accept it from our school system?


In the early days of Holland College, the Secretarial Arts operated on a year-round basis taking in 20 or so new students every month or two.  Students completed the program and entered the work force on their own schedule; Time in the program varied from 16 to 20 months to gain a work entry profile.  An “A” was within reach of most students if they put in the time and effort.


I believe that most students could raise their grades by at least one level if given the opportunity.  Why not, therefore, offer high school graduates who received less than an A, additional days of instruction so that they can raise their grade?  The cost would be minimal but the benefits would be considerable.  


CFPEI proposes to support a pilot a pilot project this summer in one subject this summer.

Letters of interest will be solicited for a project under which students, who received a B or less will be given an opportunity to try for an A.  The pilot is not to simply re-offer an  existing course.  Instead students would apply to raise their grade and, if selected, would be provided with instructional support to close the gap.  As soon as a student meets the requirement for an A, he or she should be given the credit and would drop out of the program.  


The Foundation sees itself as a catalyst and is seeking funds to provide an incentive for the idea to be tested.  


This is an invitation to support this initiative through a contribution to the Better Education Fund at the Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island.
