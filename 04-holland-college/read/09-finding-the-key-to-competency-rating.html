Article published in CVA Journal on competency-based education, proposing performance-based criteria for rating competency levels rather than traditional theory/practical breakdowns. Don draws on his high school shop course experience and argues for criteria including quality, quantity, problem-solving, leadership, and dependability. Reflects his influential thinking on competency-based education that shaped Holland College's instructional model.

Finding the key to competency rating


A formula based on an employer’s criteria for doing the job works


Much has been said about competency-based education.  Criteria for determining competencies have been described and models for undertaking competency analysis are in existence.  Little attention has been given, however, to the rating of competency levels.

When I took “shop courses” as part of my high school program, I recall being graded on both theory and practical work.  The theory was the result of my tests and examinations throughout the year.  My practical mark was based upon three factors: namely, quality, quantity and attitude.  The breakdown, as I recall, was 40 percent for quality, 40 percent for quantity and 20 percent for attitude.  The practical and theory grades were averaged to five a final course mark.  I’m told that, at least in some institutions, similar criteria are still in use.  Are they still valid for rating performance?  Let us look at some performance-based criteria.

Rating of performance must occur in some context.  Performance-based ratings measure the application of knowledge, not just the knowledge itself.  It is preferable that the same criteria be used both in school and at work.  The system must allow individuals to progress and should, therefore, allow for increased levels of performance based on experience.

Quality: the accuracy with which a person can consistently perform a task.

Quantity: the speed with which a person is able to perform a  task to an acceptable level of quality.

Problem solving: the ability to overcome unexpected circumstances in order to perform a task.

Leadership: the ability to lead others in the performance of a skill or task.

Dependability: the ability to consistently perform a task at an acceptable level of speed and accuracy without (or a minimum of) supervision.

These are not the only ones.

How do you ask, does one take into account the information or knowledge instrinsic to the performance of a task?  The acquisition of knowledge in an occupation should increase one’s level of performance (or else why is it important?).  level of knowledge about a task is neither a performance criteria or level itself, although it will be a factor in reaching higher levels of achievement.  It must be factored in ─ it cannot be ignored but it is not normally performance criteria.
