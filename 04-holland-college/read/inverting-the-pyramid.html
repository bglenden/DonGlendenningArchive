INVERTING THE PYRAMID 402



Years ago, while studying in England, I recall discussions about the education system in the UK versus that in the North America.  The consensus then, and that was many years ago, was that schooling in the USA provided a broad base that narrowed as  the student progressed through  first, second and third degrees.  UK students, on the other hand, started with a narrower base that broadened with later studies.  Consensus at the time was that both were roughly equivalent by the time a PhD was granted.  I have no idea if this still holds or even if the debate continues but I found the discussions interesting.


By the 1960’s, I was caught up in the rapid expansion of technical and vocational programs throughout Canada – an expansion led by substantial federal financial contributions to both new facilities and new programs.  They were exciting times during which I was frequently an observer and sometimes a participant in discussions about the appropriate mix and sequencing of general and specialized studies.  


Then came Holland College and the issue was no longer simply an academic exercise.  Actual decisions had to be taken, and soon.  Would we follow the traditional pattern of the first year being foundation courses of math, English and science or was there an other option.  It was not a question of whether general and specialized education should be taught but simply one of sequence.  My analysis suggested that there were at least two variables in play; one was the organization of knowledge and the other was the organizing of learning experience – and they do not result in identical pyramids.


The traditional approach to organizing studies, in a four year program for example, provides general education courses during year one - mathematics, science and communication courses.  A first year course in the subject that brought you to the institution in the first place, is relatively rare.  During the second year, you may have one course or, if you are lucky, two in your field of interest.  Your last two years will likely see a preponderance of courses in your specialty.  The theory behind this approach, apparently, is that having learned the “basics”, the student is in a better position to understand the specifics- learning proceeds from the general to the specific.


I am intrigued with the opposite view; that is, that learning proceeds from the specific to the general.  It seemed to me, at least in terms of career programs, that one should build on the experience, interest and incentives that brought the person to the college in the first place,  then broaden the field of study as the learner progresses.  Should a learner discontinue formal studies, he or she is more likely to have some employment skills.



The learning perspective also results in a pyramid but a somewhat different one.  Using the library, it may be likened to how a learner finds the book of interest.  When I visit a library, I usually have a key word in mind – the name of an author, a field i.e. Travel or biography or even a subject ier composting bins.  My objective is to find the appropriate reference as quickly as possible.  Yes, I admit, sometimes I go to brows a section but that is not my custom.




Traditional thinking is that students entering post secondary institutions should concentrate on general education courses first.  Typically, a student is lucky if the first year includes any study in the field he or she came to study.  More likely, the student takes required courses in English, Mathematics, Science, History; with luck, there will be one elective and with better luck, that course could be in a field of choice.  The traditional learner’s pyramid would look something like this:


My view is that learners can benefit from starting with specific tasks and as one and aggregating and generalizing on them. 


Is one better than the other?  That depends upon the learner.  Based on my experience, however, either option should be available.  For many adult or mature students, the inverted pyramid reflects their career path.    They entered the workforce with no or a limited amount of post secondary education, the picked up courses that were available or of interest as time allowed.  Frequently such courses were work related.  Later in life, they took additional courses to broaden their education, to fill in some gaps or to obtain a diploma or degree.


Some advantages of the inverted pyramid include:

Capitalizing on the student’s interest

Allowing students to enroll in some of their interest courses may reduce the first year drop out rate.

Students who drop out of a program will have likely gained some employment skills

I’ve seen no evidence that the traditional pyramid leads to a better prepared graduate.
