QUESTION 11A -PURPOSES AND ACTIVITIES 

For application to CRA for charitable status


APPROVED PURPOSES

The purposes of Education 20/20 Inc. as included in our incorporation documents are as follows:

1 To promote education and educational opportunity for every Canadian

2 To exercise leadership in the improvement in all facets of education

3 To foster cooperation between and among all stakeholders

4 Undertake and support research and innovation

5 improve public understanding of the education system


As part of the process of preparing this application, we reviewed and revised the objectives as found in our incorporation documents to better reflect our interests and plans. The review and rewriting have benefitted the organization; based on our experience since our incorporation and future plans, we propose the following objectives:


A)  ADVANCE EDUCATION BY ORGANIZING LEADERSHIP DEVELOPMENT ACTIVITIES FOR PEOPLE WHO PLAN, ADMINISTER AND DELIVER EDUCATION PROGRAMS.   


Recent Workshops

In the past year, Education 20/20 offered a number of workshops; all have been of 2 hours duration, led by volunteers, held in the late afternoon or  evening and included a presentation and round table discussions.


1)  Community Engagement and Early Childhood Learning with Alice Taylor as speaker and Anne Miller, Kim Watts and Brenda Goodine  as panelists, was  held at the Haviland Club on December 4, 2012 (Announcement and press clipping included with supporting documents)


2)  A Framework for Learning was held at the Quality Inn, Charlottetown, on October 21, 2013 and included presentations by Alice Taylor, PEI partner of Handle with Care Program, Eileen Conway-Martin, and Communications Director, Native council of PEI, and Brenda Goodine, Learning Manager, Early Childhood Education, Holland College.  Don Glendenning, President Emeritus of Holland College was chair the session. (Announcement and press clipping included with supporting documents)


3)  From a Learner’s Perspective, a workshop about learning was held on October 22, 2014 in the Stratford Town Hall.  The evening started with a youth panel (Callen Cudmore, Ryan Rioux, Iain Cudmore and Stephanie Mayne)  identifying the kinds of help they considered most useful when learning new things. The room was provided free of charge by the Town of Stratford; the panel was chaired by Tanya Craig, a Director of Education 20/20 Inc. The local McDonalds 


provided a free meal coupon to each panelist.  Mike Chapman, a volunteer, recorded student comments and, later facilitated a discussion to refine the list.  The results were brought together in the form of a “Learning Path”; since then the “learning Path” has been further refined at a workshop with students in the Early childhood Teacher Education Program at Holland College.  Other workshops are planned and, eventually, a document will be posted on our website. (Announcement and press clipping included with supporting documents)   


4)  Dialogues with Educators in which small groups meet in an informal setting to discuss issues of interest; the first was held on Wednesday, January 21, 2017 at the Haviland Club in Charlottetown.  Guest educator of the evening was Dr. Tammy Hubley-Little, leader of Curriculum Development for the English Language School Board.  Dr. Hubley-Little made a presentation on Student Achievement followed by questions and discussion.  Fifteen people participated including a university Professor of Education, the Head of Early Childhood Teacher Education at Holland College, several graduate students, a teacher of on-line courses, a retired Superintendent and a Reading Specialist.

Webinars

There is a large unserved and even growing body of people engaged in education outside public institutions; I refer to a dozen agencies on PEI part of whose educational programs have been accepted by the Department of Education for external credits, some private schools, home schooling parents, employer based training and a number of organizations receiving funding to mount programs for underserved youth.  Education 20/20 Inc. plans to offer a series of webinar-style 3-5 minute video clips for use by educators either alone or in groups.  Our first series, “A Learning Framework” is under development and consists of four sessions (1) Learners which provides background as to why learners differ, (2) Learning: sheds light on what happens when a person learns, (3) Levels of Learning: presents options on the meaning of levels of achievement, and (4) The Learning Path which follows a learner through a learning cycle.  Involved in the first series are: Presenter Donald Glendenning, CM, PhD; Henry Purdy, CM, CRA, and Sara Underwood, Office of Alumni Relations at Holland College.  





Work with employers:

About a year ago, the Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island conducted a survey of over 200 young people to better understand their issues.  (www.cfpei.ca). On reviewing their report, Education 20/20 was struck by the “preparedness gap”, that is the preparedness of young people for further education and for work.   Early in 2014, Education 20/20 Inc. organized workshops in six communities across the Island to publicize the findings, determine the extent of the concern, and identify actions, if any, that could or should be taken. Perhaps the most significant finding was the lack of communication about educational matters between providers and users of educational services.   


Employers and post-secondary institutions use high school graduation as a requirement for entrance to postsecondary institutions and to work, but tell us that having such a document is no longer adequate evidence of an acceptable level of basic or essential skills.  This is not surprising since teachers often report that in most class, student grade levels range from 2-3 grade levels above  to 2-3 grade levels below the nominal level of the class.  In other words, it is not unusual for have students whose math level is Grade 7 or lower and others with math levels of Grade 11 or higher.  Transcripts and diplomas do not tell the difference.  Education 20/20 has a small committee exploring the issue but has not been able to find well defined and broadly accepted achievement levels on school subjects.  Work is continuing


Other providers of educational services


We are all familiar with schools, colleges and universities as providers of educational services; we are less familiar with, and often overlook, the many other providers of educational services including youth organizations, cadet programs, sports development programs, the private sector and a range of NGO’s.  More to the point, staff in these groups is seldom included in conferences, workshops or other educational activities.  We have made contact with a number of community based training agencies such as Skills PEI, Workplace Learning PEI, Bridges and some private trainers; some already participate in current  activities of Education 20/20.  With the initiative by the Department of Education to increase credit for external courses, we see an increased opportunity and an obligation to help.  Although the dialogue is open, we have not undertaken yet any programs specifically designed to meet their needs.  


Schools not only certify they also sort; by sorting we mean issue credentials (Diplomas and Degrees)  Some see the issue as a conflict of interest others simply believe that  schools have lost their ability to sort.  We are working with the local Chamber to resolve the issue.  It appears that the assessment services through the Holland College Assessment Centre and Workplace Learning PEI will be able to provide the required services.    The committee is also exploring the idea of a separate certification arrangement similar to that provided by the Royal Canadian Conservatory of Music under which teachers teach but the conservatory certifies.



B) ADVANCE EDUCATION THROUGH INCREASING THE AMOUNT AND FLOW OF INFORMATION AND IDEAS TO, BETWEEN AND AMONG EDUCATION PARTNERS


It takes an informed and engaged community to educate a child; evidence shows that students perform better in schools with a high degree of autonomy but also in schools where parents and the community work together; in PEI, during the last few months, the Department of Education has dramatically expanded the number of external credits.  Education 20/20   believes that more needs to be done and that our organization can play an important role in (1) increasing the flow of information and (2) providing opportunities for “deliverers of educational services and “users of educational services” to dialogue.  


A newsletter, Open Education, is distributed electronically on a monthly basis to some 200 people; arrangements have been recently confirmed to produce it in both official languages.  (Copies included with supporting documents)


A Website, www.education2020.ca, is used to announce events, post occasional papers, host newsletters and survey results; in other words will become a centre for managing our information activities including the social media. 


Conversation/dialogue

One of the conclusions resulting from our preparedness workshops mentioned elsewhere in this paper is the need for direct dialogue between education and the community it serves.  To bridge the gap, we are organizing a series of discussions; each session will have a one page background paper, a presentation if appropriate, and followed by open discussion.  The objective is to increase communications rather than to reach agreement.  The pilot run of discussions will begin in early 2015 between 4:30 and 6:00 pm with a group not to exceed 20 people; additional groups will be organized if numbers warrant. (Copy of invitation included with supporting documents)


Formal presentations

It is important help policy makers become aware and up- to- date on issues about which they make policies.  In saying this, we are aware of, and have reviewed Policy CPS-022 (Effective date: September 2, 2003), and understand the limits placed on charitable organizations especially as they relate to lobbying and political activities


On September 25, 2014 Education 20/20 Inc. made a presentation to the English Language School Board on Prior Learning Assessment and Recognition during which we advocated for PLAR, Challenge for Credit and exemptions as a way of broadening studies, putting students in charge of at least some elements of their program, and increasing engagement.  (Copy of presentation included with enclosed documents)


On September 30, 2014, Education 20/20 Inc. made a presentation to the Prince Edward Island Standing Committee on Education and Innovation on A Framework for Decision-making. (Copy included with enclosed documents)   


We are seeking opportunities to address other bodies such as the Provincial Cabinet, the French Language School Board, Chambers of Commerce and Council of Maritime Premiers, MPHEC and others.


Working with the community at large

Parents play a pivotal role in the education of their children; children perform better when their parents and the community are engaged in the education process and yet, broadly speaking, the school system does not encourage deep engagement.  To encourage engagement, Education 20/20 Inc. has instituted an award of Excellence to a school or learning centre that best demonstrates a high level of engagement in its day to day activities, especially those activities over which the school has control.  (Announcements, descriptions and other supporting information provided with supporting documents).  


 We will continue to engage employers and educators in projects and panels to increase communication; service clubs will be encouraged to invite educators, especially principals, as speakers, and will make a special attempt to produce occasional papers and media releases about items of interest to them. 


(C) ADVANCE EDUCATION BY PROVIDING SUPPLEMENTAL EDUCATIONAL SUPPORT SERVICES


Education is a lifelong process; most of our educational funding, however, goes to institutions and for full-time instruction of the young.  Even at that, schools cannot, should not be expected to, supply all educational services for youth; neither can governments be expected to pay all educational costs.  There is a need for part time instruction and short courses but, even where they are available, offerings tends be limited to courses being offered to others on a full time basis.  Although technology has put knowledge building within easy reach of many adults, maintaining and progressing in the workplace may also require new skills and attitudes that can best be dealt with in group settings. 


Partners in the education process also include the family, community agencies, sports groups, service clubs, NGO’s, private sector, and individuals, to name but a few; we are giving priority to the following  gaps:


  • Study Centre(s)


Many students need a place to study, to meet with a tutor, to access occasional help and to interact with other learners; Education 20/20 plans to provide such places.  As demand for services grow, we see the need for a full service study centre where youth can get customized help such as counselling, diagnosis, assessment and even referral. Our longer term plan is to expand the service to a number communities as demand requires and resources allow.  At least at the beginning, tutors will be volunteers; space has been offered by the PEI Association of Professional Engineers; costs will be minimal and will be met through donations from individuals and businesses.


  • Emerging fields – 


Our world is dominated by technology.  Most computer users benefit from less than 10% of a program’s functionality.  There are contemporary computer languages that are learnable by virtually anyone.  Knowledge of a computer language not only helps us better use existing programs, but also enables us to start building some of our own tools, changing us from passive to active technology consumers.  We want open this door to more people, especially young people.  We are actively investigating introductory computer language courses on a part time basis available through fees or sponsorships at a cost of $100 per participant.  Robotics and 3-D printing are also on our list. 


Success in school depends to a significant degree to the level of social and emotional development of people entering school; there is a need, we believe, to provide information and assistance to parents of pre-school students as to ways to ensure young people are provided with appropriate experiences.  At the moment, we a re in active discussion with a view to offering the Handle-with-Care program, mentioned elsewhere in this document.


  • Imagine experiences

In the hurly burly of every say life, and especially in the context of school classrooms, there is seldom time for creativity.  We propose to provide such opportunities to Islanders of any age.

D)  ADVANCE EDUCATION BY SUPPORTING RESEARCH AND INNOVATION, AND BY PROMOTING BEST PRACTICES IN THE FIELD OF EDUCATION AND TRAINING

Education 20/20 Inc. realizes the importance research and innovation and will encourage research and researchers through publicizing research findings, identifying and promoting underserved areas, and featuring researchers in our public sessions. 

Existing research, it seems to us, focuses almost exclusively on institution based education, early childhood, the young and literacy. We believe that other issues also could benefit from additional research such as the role of credentials and qualifications, determining levels of achievement in key subjects, choice in education, delivery options, alternatives to age level grouping and continuing education




Survey of Interests and needs

We recognize the importance of surveys of educational stakeholders and, in fact, think that surveys results contribute to improving the quality of a product or service, and are especially important for delivery organizations, such as education, that lack choice.  Education 20/20 plans to undertake surveys; the first planned for 2015 is a survey of stakeholder views on the goals of public education as published on the Department of Education website.; we start with goals because they are a basic direction- setting activity, and are 


We plan a series of occasional papers, short papers on issues related to any aspect of education.  Papers may report research findings, best practices, innovations, and points of view.  Papers will be aimed at the general public and be widely available electronically.  Occasional papers provide information and views on a variety of subjects but also provide an opportunity, and even encouragement, to people to clarify their views and “grow’ in their chosen field. The first is a brief report on a series of workshops held throughout the Province earlier this year. Another, on Charter Schools, arises from a request following a public meeting, a third responds at least in part, to providing a framework making decisions about education.  (Copies of some papers, not yet released, are included with our supporting documents).
