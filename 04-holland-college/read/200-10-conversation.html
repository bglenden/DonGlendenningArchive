Newsletter No. 96 (February 20, 2020) advocating for broader community dialogue on education beyond schools. Don argues current discussions focus narrowly on schools, storms, technology, and classroom matters rather than larger agenda: preparing all Islanders (all ages, interests) for current and future challenges. He emphasizes education as service helping people with learning, though many view it as product delivery (credits, diplomas, degrees). Don proposes mechanisms for dialogue: PEI Board of Education (12-person general oversight, like Washington State or Holland College Board), curriculum advisory committees including students/parents/employers/post-secondary/certification bodies, and community education commons. He emphasizes education involves six life roles, and notes current narrow school focus misses broader service mandate.

Open Education

No. 96 February 20, 2020

When schools and ministries are managed well, learning happens-World Bank


INFORMATION AND DIALOGUE

We need to increase dialogue about education, not just about schools, storm days, technology, exams and classroom matters. And not just with parents, students and teachers. There’s a larger agenda, it’s about Islanders – people of all ages and interests, it’s about their futures, and how education can, should and will help.


Education is not just wise words doled out to bright young people; it is a service to all Islanders to help prepare for and deal with challenges they face today and those that lie ahead.


Most definitions that I encounter consider education to be a service to people needing help with their learning; on the other hand, many argue that the education system has changed and is now focused on delivering products – credits, diplomas and degrees. – rather than services. The Depart-ment’s description of role as one of “develop(ing) and deliver(ing) high quality programs and curriculum in English and French” supports that view. Education could benefit from more dialogue.
