Enabling Change

In education

May 1, 2012     No. 1


Restructuring the School System


The recent report of the PEI Commission  on School Governance recommended  that much of the operational responsibility for schools be delegated to school Boards.  There are sound reasons for that recommendation.  Simply stated, there is a direct relationship between school performance and school autonomy.  In the words of the OECD Report (1911):

  • “In countries where schools have greater autonomy over what is taught and how students 

are assessed, students tend to perform better. 

  • In countries where schools account for their results by posting achievement data publicly, schools that enjoy greater autonomy in resource allocation tend to show better student performance than those with less autonomy. 
  • In countries where there are no such accountability arrangements, schools with greater autonomy in resource allocation tend to perform worse.” 


While the nature and extent of delegated roles varies by country, autonomy usually includes authority to hire and dismiss teachers, formulating and allocating budgets, allocating resources, making decisions about curricula and assessments, choosing textbooks, and deciding which courses to offer.

In an article on New Patterns of School Governance in The ERIC Digest 141 - October 2000, Ron Renchler uggests that a system redesign include

  • a focus on educational results rather than on the methods by which they were achieved, 
  • newly defined relationships with the general public and parents, and 
  • a commitment on the part of the board to speak with one voice rather than as a group of individuals with individual agendas. 


In general, the writer is of the view that each school Board should have the autonomy to operate a school or family of schools under its jurisdiction with the same freedom as a private school with the proviso that the school or system must accommodate all of learners in its district.
