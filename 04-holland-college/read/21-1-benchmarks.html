Working notes on educational benchmarks and purpose statements. Don surveys Atlantic provincial education benchmarks: New Brunswick (full potential development), Nova Scotia (healthy society and economy), Newfoundland (leadership), PEI (student success opportunities), Ontario (multiple purposes). Discusses education context importance, particularly in career/technical courses, noting Holland College was founded by Premier Campbell as "alternative to university," profoundly influencing staffing, programs, and standards. Advocates clarity of purpose and consistent decision-making derived from that purpose. Includes definitions distinguishing standards from benchmarks and notes relationship to performance measurement and organizational change.

BENCHMARKS


Generally, education authorities  have a foundational statement about what they  are trying to 

accomplish through their education system.  I view such a statement as a benchmark in that Such a statement is important because, everything we do in education  flows from the purpose - I raise the issue because I believe that educational services, organization and standards flow from a statement of purpose

A  quick review of our Atlantic neighbours ewveals:

New Brunswick is focused on helping students gain tools to develop their full potential,

 Nova Scotia is focused on enabling students to “ develop their potential and acquire the knowledge , skills and attitudes needed to contribute  to a healthy society and a prosperous 

and sustainable economy

PEI – create opportunities for student6 success

Ontario has a set of purposes

The literature includes other examples of education systems preparing people for employment and also for participation in a “democratic society”.


Increasingly, I find reference in educational literature to education context and certainly learning context is very obvious in career and technical courses.  Some readers may recall the discussion leading up to the founding of Holland, the Premier of the day, Premier Campbell referred to the College providing an “alternative to going to university; that statement significantly influenced College direction – staffing, program offerings, facilities,  curriculum and standards.


This is not an argument       for” vocational schools” but it is an argument for clarity of purpose and increasing consistency of decisions falling from it.
