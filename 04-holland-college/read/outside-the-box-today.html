Don addresses three major challenges to educational reform. First, he advocates for continuous intake programs where students enter/exit based on achievement rather than fixed calendar periods, contrasting rigid time-based systems with outcome-based approaches like those at Holland College. He argues that staggered enrollment supports diverse learning rates and real-world employment patterns. Second, Don discusses Prior Learning Assessment (PLA), emphasizing it should be integrated into ongoing teaching rather than treated as separate gatekeeping. He warns against portfolio-heavy approaches that isolate PLA from the curriculum. Third, he proposes a "Record of Achievement" as an alternative to traditional transcripts—a comprehensive document recording significant skills, knowledge, and attitudes demonstrated throughout schooling, addressing employer expectations beyond university entrance requirements. He also outlines AQUA (Atlantic Provinces Qualification Authority) concept and includes detailed information on New Zealand's national qualifications framework and the Canadian Restructured School Plan (CRSP) model.

Outside the Box Today


Continuous Intake



Elsewhere in these my musings, I referred to the fact that education is driven by an out-dated administrative system where time on task is the constant and performance the variable.  The system focuses on groups not individuals and forces learners to begin and end studies, not based on learning progress but at the sound of a bell.  Teachers are required to fashion their teaching to fit available time rather than the system accommodating the needs of students. Learning theory collides with administrative practice and administration usually wins. The rigidity of the system, in my view, is a major deterrent to change. 


The reverse should be the case.  Doctors keep you under care or in the hospital until you are better; teachers should keep you in their care until you perform at an acceptable level.


Elsewhere I identified a number of beliefs or principles followed in designing the Holland College approach to learning and teaching.  One was the students should be able to enter or exit a program on their own time – that is, make time, not achievement, the variable.  Exiting on completion was not a problem,  except that it made graduation exercises difficult to organize; staggered or continuous intake, however, was different.


Entry and exit times to programs, courses and units are prescribed by the “system’ as are the number of days or hours of instruction available to each learner for each subject studied, and grades are awarded based upon how much was learned in the time available.  Even colleges and universities, institutions with considerable control of their operation, operate on a time based system   In addition to being at odds with real life, it paces instruction at a rate well below the rate of learning of many students; neither allows nor gives incentives to move through the system more quickly and results in some students listlessly waiting for the teacher and other members of the class to catch up.  Even after spending a life in the field, the logic of this approach learning still escapes me. Compare this with hospital treatment where the administrative system is designed to support the treatment of individual patients – if more days are needed to regain health, a hospital stay may be extended.


This delivery model is at odds with practices in the work world where one finishes one job before moving to the next.  Many years ago during a visit to Newfoundland, I learned that on graduation day in the vocational system in that Province, roughly the third week of June, some 600- graduates of the secretarial program would enter the labour market and compete for jobs.  Not only should intake be staggered, I thought, but also a staggered exit also has advantages.


Theoretically, a new student should be able to enroll in a program as soon as a seat becomes available.  It is a well known fact that no two students are at the same place in their studies at the same time in their life and, furthermore, students learn at different rates.  Even if, theoretically, two students were at exactly the same stating point, within days there would be major differences in their achievement levels on subjects being taught.   Individual entry and exit can and does work but this approach may require schools to operate on a different administrative model and requires teaching skills different from those used mostly in the traditional model.  Some instructors, I know, have great difficulty in applying an individualized approach.  In my experience, teachers with supervisory experience before entering teaching had fewer difficulties in adapting to a continuous intake model than teachers without such supervisory experience. 


Although Holland College experimented with multiple and continuous intake, our record was spotty to say the least.  One of our major problems was the many one and two-instructor programs.  On reflection, however, I still believe in the concept and would give it more attention in the future.



PLA Swamped by portfolios


Could PLA be defined simply as a “process used by learners and teachers to establish the range and level of previous learning”?


The PLA initiative is alive and well in Canada.  The recent conference in Ottawa was a bounding success.  I was impressed by the number, energy and commitment of delegates to broader and more effective implementation.  But I also left the gathering with some nagging concerns.  My concerns are shared, I know, by some educators with whom I have spoken both before and since the conference took place.


First let me say that I wholeheartedly support PLA at all levels of education and I see great value in it as an integral part of the teaching/learning process.  Current literature emphasizes the use of PLA for the purpose of gaining (or granting) course or program credit; it can be used also in support of learning.  In other words, it can be a useful tool for those who teach as well as for those who sort.  Using it for gatekeeping, although useful, is too limited an application.


Part of the problem lies with the definition.  I’ve looked at several.  They focus on gate keeping with most of the gatekeepers being in the college system.  Perhaps PLA could be more simply defined as a “process used by learners and teachers to establish the range and level of previous learning”.  Let teachers, learners, schools, certification bodies and the private sector use the tool as they see fit.


PLA is a continuing process


PLA needs to be integrated into the on-going teaching/learning process.  It should not be a thing apart, an add-on.  Learners should encounter it as they enter and work their way through an institution, program or course.  If we do not assess prior learning, how do we know the appropriateness of learning activities, how long it will take a person to learn it, or even whether or not the learner needs to engage in this learning activity, course or program at all?


In other words, instead of shaping the learner to fit into predetermined boxes called courses, can we shape courses to take advantage of the learner’s prior learning?


A conversation about PLA quickly turns into a discussion of portfolios.  A portfolio is useful but it is not the only tool.  The operative word in PLA is assessment.  A wide range of assessment techniques may apply; a portfolio is only one even though a very important and useful one.  The tools that can be applied to assessment generally are likely to have applicability to PLA as well.


Is a portfolio course necessary?  I’ve heard some horror stories.  The current emphasis on portfolio preparation, and the resulting courses in the subject, tends to make PLA something apart from ongoing education whereas PLA should be integrated into the teaching learning process.  If we do not integrate it, there is a danger of it being lost.


Is PLA age specific?


If PLA is a valuable tool in the education of adults who come back to college, why is it not so for younger adults also?  Even for widespread use in the K-12 school system?  And for all technical and vocational students?


CBE and OBE provide a natural home for PLA.  Couple them and we have a real winner; leave PLA out of CBE and is it really CBE?


As a first step in understanding, make a flow-chart of a learner’s route from school entrance to school exit.  Make a separate one showing the teacher’s role fro the point of receiving a class list to the completion of class records for each learner.  Study these “educational roadmaps” and identify the internention points where PLA would be useful.  I’m sure you will find a number.


I’m optimistic.  Full steam ahead.  Integrate the concept of PLA into both teaching and sorting.  Couple PLA with CBE and the next decade will see unprecedented innovation and improvement in the education of our citizens – both young and old.

RECORD OF ACHIEVEMENT


There have been many calls for changes to our educational system and there have been a number of responses.  Included among these responses are the following:


Competency based education

Back to the basics

An increase in the number of courses required for high school graduation

Changes in requirements to enter College and University

Introduction of technology education, and

Greater access to computers


An obvious area for examination has been overlooked and that is the way we describe the achievement of students when they leave the education system.  The traditional Diploma, Certificate or Degree tells us nothing about the individual, what he is like, what he has accomplished, or his strengths and weaknesses.  A transcript helps a bit but only describes achievement in a very narrow set of life experiences.  Transcripts record one’s ability to study, recall, repeat, and explain events, phenomenon’s, and relationships in a discipline or one’s ability to perform selected skills.  This may have some value if one is entering further study but has limited value for other purposes.  School leaving documents have become instruments for screening people but I question their value in describing meaningful achievement.


The current system of describing educational achievement puts an emphasis on subject matter or content, and even that tells little about the nature and scope of study within the field.  It fails to report or describe many “ non-academic” achievements that may be important to institutions of higher education or potential employers.  It is likely that the lack of an  adequate description of achievement deters or prevents individuals  from entering further education or employment where they could otherwise have succeeded.


To replace a Degree, Diploma, Certificate or Transcript, I propose that each person on leaving school, college, or university, receive a Record of Achievement.


A Record of Achievement is not “old wine in a new bottle” nor is it a rose by another name.  it is a new drink or a new flower.  A Record of Achievement  is a concise and complete record of significant skills, knowledge, and attitudes exhibited or demonstrated or performance by an individual while in the education system.  Such a document would record achievement during his/her entire school life and would show growth (or change) during the education process.


A Record of Achievement should include accomplishments in subjects or disciplines, describe ability to communicate (speak, write, listen), indicate the extent to which a person assumes responsibility for actions, skills of working with others, technological awareness, work habits, etc.  Records of Achievement should not limit themselves to simply identifying activities which a student engaged but rather should describe the strengths or weaknesses demonstrated through participation in those activities.


The development of a Record of Achievement of the kind describe above will take leadership and co-operation on the part of Educational policy makers.  The process of developing a Record of Achievement, should bring together all levels of education to establish both format and content.  This process, in itself, would be useful.


Ideally, a Record of Achievement would be more than that described in the above paragraphs.  It should include a continuing and life long record of educational achievement including that which relates to employment and educational growth through involvement in professional or community organizations.


The stumbling block to introducing Records of Achievement will likely lie with Colleges and Universities.  While their views must be considered, their views should not predominate.  After all, they only serve only 30% to 35% of young people who enter the public education system.  Employers, on the other hand, may welcome such a document.  My information is that immediate supervisors of new employees readily understand and adapt to Records of Achievement.  A different problem exists with the personal office of large corporations.  A record of achievement cannot be readily compared with customary documentation provided by applicants.  Personnel officers may call it information overload and the applicant may suffer.

The acquiring of an education and the certification of qualifications are two separate functions although the two are closely linked and frequently one is thought to be the other.  The fact that the delivery of both functions is, to a large degree, provided or driven by educational institutions often works to the individual’s disadvantage in terms of both education and certification.  While separate certification bodies exist, they appear to place undue emphasis on results of study and too little emphasis on what has been learned outside the formal education system.


AQUA


Concept


The idea behind this proposal is to establish an independent, private sector driven body with authority to assess and certify a person’s level of performance on a task or set of tasks such as found in an occupation or field.


Characteristics


AQUA will:


Be an independent organization with its own governing structure

Operate on a fee for service basis

Accept clients on direct application or by referral

Issue certificates based on demonstrated performance

On request, provide applicants with information on gaps to be filled to meet certification requirements. 

Make extensive use of advisory/working panels.


Benefits/Advantages


It is anticipated that the availability of AQUA will:


Remove barriers to certification encountered in the current approach

Focus certification more clearly on performance

Encourage more self study

Allow educational providers to focus on the teaching function

Allow a certification body to focus on certification without regard to teaching.

Removes length of learning time as a component of certification

CRSP

The CRSP model is:


· Personalized in that it responds to and supports individual learners as they become increasingly self-motivated and independent learners. The model is built around non-gradedness, continuous progress and individualized instruction and tracks each student from the point at which they enter the program to the point at which they exit with a credit.

· competency based in that it draws content from real life rather that cut across traditional subject lines of academic disciplines. Competencies written as applications of knowledge, skills and attitudes judged necessary to function in adult life.

· computer based in that it makes use of technology to deliver and manage instruction. The use of technology also allows the learner and advisors to manipulate large numbers and quantities of resource materials with a focus on that which is most valuable to an individual learner.

· a flexible structure is needed to support large numbers of individual learners.

Characteristics of the model include a flexible school day and flexible school year, the multiple entry and exit of students, variable instructional and learning time to accommodate the view that more students can succeed if additional time is allowed.

· Based on site-based management allows planning and decision-making to occur at the school level so that day to day administration can be collegial and collaborative.


While the writer recognizes his biases, CRSP is thought to be the only model which includes all components of a competency based system.

Although I retired from the college system in 1987, I was active as a consultant for a number of years thereafter. One of the most interesting assignments was a contract to design a competency based model for implementation at the secondary school level. It was both a stimulating and challenging assignment. The result was The Canadian Restructured School Plan, CRSP, which was designed to provide "an alternative to the existing lock-step, teacher centered, classroom based delivery system."


The methodology included the establishment of National Project Steering Committee, an extensive literature review, convening of three focus groups, visits to innovative schools throughout Canada, a survey of 40 recognized Canadian educational leaders and considerable discussion within the committee and between committee members and professional colleagues.


The report identified a number of major shifts occurring in education including a shift:


· From a system whereby students are admitted to school on an annuls or semiannual basis, to a more open system in which there is flexible entry and exit;

· From a rigid time-bound credit structure to flexible competency-based curriculum in which time is not held constant;

82- DACUM



DACUM has come a long way since the first analysis was undertaken in Ottawa in January of 1968.  For people not familiar with the term, DACUM is an acronym for Design A Curriculum and is a widely accepted approach to identifying competencies being practiced or to be learned – and it is no longer limited to an analysis of traditional occupations. And it is a Canadian idea at that.

 

DACUM is most widely used  as a rigorous, relevant, inexpensive and internally consistent approach  to identifying competencies in a field or occupation.  A major strength is the reliance on practitioners as the major source of information and a presentation of the competencies of a field on a single sheet..  As a result, DACUM escapes the semantics of the academic.  It is life based rather than research based and, therefore, is readily understood by people in the field since it reflects what they do and is stated in a language that the field understands.


The performance-based rating scale proposed by Adams is unique.  It includes all the required elements of a good rating scale ─ competencies, performance criteria, a scale, and benchmarks.  While it does not have rubrics or exemplars, they can easily be prepared.  On the other hand, it has benchmarks (industry-based ones) which are missing in most other assessment systems I’ve seen.  In my reading and discussion, I have not found an assessment system as complete.


A DACUM analysis is a tremendous tool for identifying and planning learning resources.  For each competency, one can identify the kind of human resource expertise required; facilities, equipment and supplies; appropriate learning activities; assessment techniques and strategies; and the best location for learning whether it be a classroom, laboratory, shop or actually in a real work situation.  


The model provides recognition for prior learning, self assessment, instructor confirmatiob of achievements,  a tool for career planning, a basis for learning plans, a place for recording learner progress and achievement, a foundation for certification and a planning tool for lifelong learning and an exit document.


Another component is that of program delivery and management.  The DACUM model also includes a simple yet comprehensive learning model and an equally straight forward model for the management of learning by teachers.    And it ensures that learners and teachers are “singing from the same hymnbook.”


Even wider adoption of the DACUM model suffers from:


the inclusion of “curriculum” as part of the acronym.  DACUM is not a curriculum but is useful tool for planning a curriculum.

DACUM developed out of an adult educational upgrading program and was first widely applied to trade and technical programs.  This is not a source where traditional educators look for innovation.

The DACUM-based learning model redistributes much of the control of the process from the teacher to the learner.

The rating scale and process is much more demanding than that presently used in the school system.  

Would replace the current emphasis in schools on what we know to one of what we can do with what we know.


It is 40 years since the first DACUM analysis was undertaken.  Much has changed since then especially in the field of information technology; many of these changes make a DACUM-like model even more appropriate.  Its introduction into the school system would give schools the capability of increasing flexibility, relevance, learner managed learning, …  It is a shame not to tap into DACUM’s rich potential.
