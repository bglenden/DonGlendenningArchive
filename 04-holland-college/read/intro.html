Introduction by Don Glendenning to "The Glendenning Letters" (April 15, 2014) describing his educational background as a "sandwich education" spanning 20 years with alternating layers of study and teaching, supplemented by summer schools. He details his career progression through elementary, secondary, and principal roles, teacher training, and federal government positions before becoming founding president of Holland College. Don explains his choice to write letters to editors rather than academic journals, believing systemic education change comes from citizen and parent pressure rather than internal system reform. He collected 62 published letters and notes many unpublished drafts. The introduction emphasizes public discourse over traditional academic publishing as his contribution to education debate.
Holland College & Institutional Legacy

Dear Reader:


The Glendenning Letters


Mine was a sandwich education spread over 20 years after high school graduation. A layer of

Study, perhaps a year or two, followed by a layer of teaching, then another layer of study and so

on, all seasoned with endless summer schools. As a result, I ended up with experience as a

teacher in both the elementary and secondary levels, a principal, six years a teacher

trainer and the same as a training specialist with the Federal government. During the same

period I earned several degrees, and held licenses to teach both academic and vocational

subjects. That sandwich nourished me in my role as Founding President of Holland College, a

community college established in Prince Edward lsland to broaden the range of educational

services available to lslanders. I write this only to support the view that my sandwich was

custom made, was somewhat unique and may result in a different perspective.


For a number of years I wrote a column for the Canadian Vocational Journal. ln 1995 I wrote my first letter to the Editor of the Charlottetown Guardian; it was about education in Atlantic

Canada and is the first letter in the pages that follow. That letter was a simple response to an

item of interest; 18 years later with our education system under scrutiny and pressure to

change, a friend suggested that these letters be brought together and made available to the

public as a stimulus for discussion. This volume is the result.


I found or reconstructed some 62 letters, all of which were actually published; there were

perhaps a half dozen not included because they were short and congratulatory notes, were not

about education, were sent to a different newspaper, or may not have been published. I also

found an equal number of letters in various stages of readiness; some of the latter look more

interesting than the published ones. Most of the letters would have benefitted from editing,

rewriting or even not having been written at all but I decided to include them anyway. I

suppose I shouldn't have been surprised by number that were repeats.


Readers may wonder why I write letters to the editor instead of writing for educational journals.

The answer is twofold, I do not consider myself a writer neither do I have the interest or

discipline to write a book. But secondly, I am of the view that the changes required in

education are not likely to come from within the system but rather will result from demands for

change arising from interested parents, citizens, students, employers t and the public at large.

Putting these letters in circulation is, I believe a contribution to that debate.


My thanks to all who, over the years, contributed to the ideas expressed herein; I hope they

contribute as much to your education as they did to mine.


Sincerely


Don Glendenning \

Charlottetown, PEI

April 15, 2014
