BACKGROUND


Mission/purpose of organization:  Education 20/20 Inc. is a federally incorporated independent, broad based, national, not-for-profit education organization, located in Charlottetown, PEI and is governed by a volunteer Board of Directors.  Our mission is to encourage and support a quality education for every Canadian; Education 20/20 Inc. is unique among educational organizations in Canada in that it is unaligned with any educational institutions, grade level, subject field or cause.   The organization operates with ten Directors, a network of some sixty people, and a mailing list of almost 300.  Enclosed is a list of activities by year since our incorporation.  Although most Directors have participated in externally funded projects in their other roles, this is the first such project for Education 20/20 Inc. and certainly our first request for funding from the Windsor Foundation.


Proposal – to develop outcomes based model for establishing education levels in the public school system.


Issue being addressed

The current approach to grading students raises three issues:

Should there be recognized levels of achievement in school subjects?

If so, who should set such levels?

Who should determine the level that ah individual has attained?

 

Relationship to Windsor Foundation Priorities


This project is at the root of improving the quality of education and actually addresses both major interests of the Windsor Foundation. The amount and quality of education will continue to be major determinants of our individual and collective futures.  It is recognized that raising levels of education by even one grade will have a significant impact on both an individual and a country’s future; the EU says by 6 percent on the first year and three percent in the longer term.  Most efforts to improve education, it seems, are focused on schools, new technology, increased engagement, better qualified teachers and project based learning.  We would argue that greater attention should also be given to what gets measured, how, when, by whom and how well the results are communicated.  Clearly identified levels, especially in basic skills (mathematics, communications, science, personal management and IT) should be part of the improvement process.


To deal with the issue of standards, many countries have developed qualification authorities with a focus on better coordination of inputs, (course content, level, prerequisites, time, etc.).  Our proposal calls for a different approach, that of measuring and reporting what has been learned – levels of achievement reached., 




PROJECT NARRATIVE


Following the publication of a survey of Island youth in late 2013 (CFPEI, Vital Signs, 2013), Education 20/20 organized public discussions for the purpose of informing the community of the report and its findings, learning more about youth issues, exploring issues of education and learning, and to identify actions to be taken.  Discussions were lively and wide ranging, and carried on well after the formal session ended.   Since then Education 20/20 has continued its exploration, both formally and informally, with postsecondary institutions, employers, teachers and education officials and concluded that:

The current school grading system does not adequately describe a person’s level of achievement

The use of “Grade” to designate both age and achievement level is confusing and misleading.

Levels of achievement should be determined by an independent body and not by teachers.

Traditional assessment practices are not adequate for measuring achievement levels

Outcomes based education calls for externally referenced criteria for assessment and grading.  

The large number of teacher empowered to “grade” students contributes to the inconsistency of school grades

Those who teach should not sort.

The growing use of social promotion creates uncertainty about the value of school based grades as measures of achievement.

Increasingly, it appears, report cards fail to differentiate levels of achievement.


Proposal – using mathematics as a pilot, Education 20/20 Inc. proposes to develop a framework for determining achievement levels based on outcomes. 


The challenge

Outcome Based Education has been around in Canada from 50 to sixty years.  During this time, the public school system has developed its own brand, Curriculum Based Outcomes. The challenge will be to find an assessment model that is compatible with an outcome base.


Work plan:

The project breaks new ground and is heavy on information gathering, consultation and idea generation:


Establish project Committee – the following people are committed to serving on a steering committee: The President of The Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island, Associate Dean of the new School of Mathematics at UPEI. A successful Island entrepreneur, and myself, the Founding President of Holland College.


Gather background information – Gather information about outcomes, assessment, benchmarks and achievement levels from local institutions and contacts, Western and Northern Canada Protocol, Ontario Qualification Framework, Qualification Authorities (Australia and New Zealand), PISA, P-CAP, and others

Adopt an outcome base - from the above literature search, we expect to find competencies of sufficient range and in a sufficient number to form the basis of the project but will modify as needed 

Develop a competency based rating scale – as far as we know, this step is absolutely groundbreaking in the education system. 

Identify achievement levels – the usual procedure is to cluster outcomes around age levels of students; we propose, instead, to seek out natural groupings within subject areas and to recognize the level of achievement regardless of age.  While their approaches differ from our proposal, we plan to take advantage of the work of the Ontario Qualification Authority and the Western and Northern Canada Protocol 

Testing – the testing protocol is yet to be determined.

Prepare final report
