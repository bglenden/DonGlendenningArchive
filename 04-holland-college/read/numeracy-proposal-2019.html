A major 2019 proposal document outlining Don's philosophy on numeracy versus mathematics. Don draws on the Scottish Department of Education and PEI's Early Numeracy Intervention Program to argue that practical application matters more than abstract mathematical theory. He proposes a "map of numeracy skills" and provides extensive budget details (total $13,790). This reflects Don's ongoing commitment to learner-centered, practice-based education—a philosophy consistent with his Holland College vision.

Issue being addressed

This proposal deals with the relationship of math instruction to life outside school. The current approach begins with an analysis of math; the literature suggests that it should start with an identification of math practiced by citizens. While there is overlap, learners end up with quite different skill profiles.


The Scottish Department of Education says: “Numeracy is the application of math and being numerate has been described as “having the confidence and skill to use numbers and mathematical approaches in all aspects of life - at work, in practical everyday activities at home and beyond, as consumers, in managing our finances, as parents helping our children learn, as patients making sense of health information, as citizens understanding the world about us.”


A report by the PEI Department of Education says on The Early Numeracy Intervention Program (ENIP) reports that numeracy “gives students the tools and the skills to solve problems logically, and to developmental flexibility, independence of thought, self-efficacy, and the ability to persevere”


While there is considerable support for numeracy instruction there is little practical help and instruction readily available for those who wish to try. The writer was not able, for example, to find a map of numeracy skills needed either for citizenship or entry level employment


It is our view that:

  • Math skills and numeracy skills are arrived at differently; numeracy skills are based on a survey of skills used regularly by citizens, employers or other identified group; math skills come from an analysis of the field of math. Both are needed but Islanders generally may be better served by higher levels of numeracy. A parallel, I suggest, is driver training. The ability to drive an automobile is essential in today’s society. While one must know something about the vehicle, one cannot learn to drive simply by studying the automobile.

  • Education needs a master list or map of everyday numeracy skills.

  • Improving levels of math require the cooperation of providers at all levels

  • The current approach to identifying math outcomes and developing curriculum is unlikely to produce the kind and level of instructional support needed by learners.

  • It is both feasible and useful to develop a math map that would serve as alternative basis of math instruction for citizens at large.

  • Competencies are the preferred basis for developing instructional materials


Proposal:

– to develop a map of numeracy skills needed by citizens, in everyday living including skills needed for entry level employment.
