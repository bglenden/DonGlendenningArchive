Draft version of Don's note to Quentin on educational assessment and preparedness gaps, documenting concerns about PEI youth's basic mathematics and literacy skills based on PISA, Vital Signs survey, Chamber of Commerce, and employer feedback. Don advocates for Chamber engagement with the Department of Education and exploration of third-party assessment services through Holland College and Workplace Learning to help employers identify qualified candidates.


Dec. 2, 2014

Assessment

EDUCATION 20/20 INC.

Skill level of Island youth

Background

Concerns about literacy levels of Islanders have been widely reported: PISA results, CFPEI survey, Charlottetown Area chamber, Home and School, Education 20/20 workshops, Employers concerns, PEI Literacy Alliance


Don

My concerns stem fro the findings of the survey. Vital Signs, by the Community Foundation of Pfrince Edward Island and, in particular, the “preparedness gap” in terms of preparation for post secondary education and for employment. Exploration of the subject led to conversations with UPEI faculty, Holland College teachers, and employers. In thew course of gathering information, discovered that both Holland College and Workplace Learning have an assessment capabilities that will likely be able to provide information emloyers to make decisions about job applicants – and they would be interested in working with employers.


Chamber

The Charlottetown and Summerside Chambers of Commerce identified the impact on the economy resulting from the lack of skilled labour, with particular to emphasis on basic education (Essential Skills, Employability skills and literacy)Availability of skilled peopleThe practice of social promotion puts make it difficult to compare levels across institutions


Education:

The Department of Education is focused on improving the education system generally and is instituting a new literacy test for senior high school students.




Employer issues:

  1. General level of basic skills

  2. Current document doesn’t differentiate between those who have required skills and those who don’t


Employer options:

  1. Wait for schools to produce better trained students

  2. Ask schools to provide better documentation

  3. Use the services of an external testing service to determine of job applicants have the skills needed.

  6. There is a need for a third-party independent assessment service on PEI

  7. Documentation of their skillsSchool report cards lack adequate description of levels of achievement

  8. Recognized assessment service would facilitate labour mobility

  9. While it could be useful in many subjects, discussion to date indicates that math has considerable support as a pilot initiative.


Don’s Recommendations:

  1. Chamber keep in regular contact with Department of Education about the improvement of education, including offering to work with appropriate curriculum committees

  2. Chamber explore arrangements with Holland College and Workplace Learning and, if successful, inform members

  3. Chamber ask that results of new Literacy test be made available to each students for their use when applying for work.
