This formal paper by Don Glendenning examines the conceptual and practical problems of assessment and grading in education. Drawing on Robert Paul Wolff's 1969 framework, Don distinguishes three separate functions: criticism (feedback for learning), evaluation (establishing performance levels), and ranking (comparative assessment). He argues these functions have become dangerously conflated in educational institutions. Don proposes that grading students is fundamentally not a school function—schools should focus on teaching, while separate qualification authorities (like Holland College's partnership with the Apprenticeship Branch) should handle certification. He advocates for Canada to adopt a model separating teaching institutions from credentialing bodies, eliminating confusion between credentials and qualifications.

About Education Assessment and grading

Occasional paper No. 10 July 14, 2014



Definition saaessment, grading, purposes, kinds

Assessment is part of the teaching process; teachers use the results of assessment to design and implement new teaching strategies to help learners learn

According to Robert Paul Wolff (Wolff: A discourse on Grading, 1969) there are three elements or kinds of grading:

  • Criticism which includes analysis and feedback about current learning; criticism is at the heart of learning and involves the give and take between teacher and student about what happened or should happen.

  • Evaluation to establish a person’s level in a subject and is, or should be, criterion referenced, similar to levels in music or swimming.

  • ranking to see how one stacks up against others of the same age, same number of years of study, in other countries, etc. Acquiring a credential is a sorting or ranking process; although the two functions are different, they appear closely linked and frequently one is thought to be the other. h functions is, to a large degree, provided or driven by educational institutions often works to the individual’s disadvantage in terms of both education and certification. Even where separate bodies exist for their issuance, confusion remains. While separate certification bodies exist, they appear to place undue emphasis on results of study and too little emphasis on what has been learned outside the formal education system. Unfortunately, over the years, the differences between credentials and qualifications has become blurred. Used interchangeably in media


A qualification authority would take its cues from the destination of the learner, focus on what a client can do and how well, examine each skills carried and assess their suitability also identify gaps for the individual would not simply administer . A qualification authority could also play a useful role in helping people identify gaps in their preparation for a given field of work.

The problem/conflict

  1. Lack of defined levels Schools are teaching institutions; their role is to help learners learn. Each learner decides what he or she will learn, although others, including teachers, influence that decision. ; as for ranking, schools could take a lesson from sports with both coaches and referees. Ranking results from competitions; students should be encouraged to participate and teachers and students can use the results but teachers should play no part in the ranking process.

  2. Conflict between teaching and sorting

It is anticipated that the availability of AQUA will:

  1. Remove barriers to certification encountered in the current approach

  2. Focus certification more clearly on performance

  3. Encourage more self study

  4. Allow educational providers to focus on the teaching function

  5. Allow a certification body to focus on certification without regard to teaching.

  6. Removes length of learning time as a component of certification

  7. Provide an avenue to certification for self directed learners

Grading students is not a school function


The role of a teacher is to teach, that is, to help another learn, it may be to help a person learn something new or to help a person reach higher levels in things they already know and can do. In either case, teaching often starts with a student taking a test. Tests of this kind are thought of as diagnosis, analysis or formative tests and are intended to identify the kind and level of help needed. Such tests may be specific or general depending on the kind of information that the teacher is seeking. The results form the basis of discussion between teacher and student and are not usually considered part of the grading process


Evaluating a student’s level of achievement is a different matter; minimum levels of achievement may be required order to gain acceptance into postsecondary institutions, to qualify for employment, to access a qualification or even to establish pay levels. Exams may be part of the process but establishing a level of performance may take into account work experience and volunteer activities. The current approach of attaching achievement to grades is confusing given that learners are at different levels wen they enter a course, that course time is constant and that learners learn at different rates. Apprentices receive their training through Holland College but are awarded certification through the Apprenticeship Branch, the examinations of International Baccalaureate students are marked off shore, and some fields like music and swimming are graded by their national organizations.


Aside from the quality issue, it is my view that establishing levels is neither a teaching or school function. Most developed countries, except for Canada and the US, have established separate bodies to perform this function. I think that Canada should consider the same but perhaps based od a better model.
