2008 proposal for an Educational Incentive Fund to drive systemic education change. Don identifies structural weaknesses including accountability dispersal, rising costs without productivity gains, discipline-based content delivery, vague standards, and lost flexibility. Proposes a fund encouraging schools to innovate with outcomes-based approaches, staffing flexibility, and cost accountability. Management involves the Community Foundation of PEI, private sector advisors, and education professionals. Includes Don's biographical summary highlighting his background as Founding President of Holland College, educational consultant, and international development expert with projects across Africa and Asia.

Feb 12, 2008

IMPROVEMENT INCENTIVE FUND


A Draft Proposal


BACKGROUND: 


Education is under considerable scrutiny and pressure to reform. Some of the criticism is valid; some is not. Provincial governments have responded in a number of ways including  restructuring school boards, lengthening the school day, encouraging charter schools, increasing teacher qualifications, specifying learning outcomes, introducing external examinations and providing teacher aides.  


These changes should help but some structural changes are also needed.  It is unlikely that the required changes will come from within the system; an external change agent is needed.  


FOCUS OF THIS INITIATIVE 


This proposal focuses on the following: 


Accountability that is widely dispersed

Costs that rise without reference to productivity 

Content drawn from disciplines instead of life 

Standards without definition   

Flexibility lost to administrative  convenience


The instruments of change include:


Choice on the part of learners

Teaching/learning models

Staffing patterns

Cost centered accounting

Standards of performance

Responsiveness to individual interests and needs 


CONCEPT: 


The idea involves establishing an Educational Incentive Fund for the purpose of encouraging and supporting changes as identified in the previous section of this paper.   Following the announcement of the Fund:


Schools will be invited to submit a preliminary proposal endorsed by the School Board

External assessor(s) will undertake a detailed review with the school of the operational context of the intervention

The school revises their proposal as required

The school and the Fund enter into a contractual agreement identifying timelines and dates and conditions for the release of funds

Quarterly progress  reports will be provided and each project wil under go a major annual review before authorizing further payments.

 

PROJECT MANAGEMENT


The management structure of the project is yet to be determined, however, it is anticipated that the following stakeholders will be key to minimizing bureaucracy and administrative costs:


The Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island is viewed as an appropriate host providing administrative support and taking advantage of their grant-making experience. 


A Private Sector  group will help keep the focus on administrative practices   


Professional Educators will be needed, both volunteer and paid, to assist in the design and assessment of proposals and the evaluation of performance


ESTIMATED COST


Not yet determined


NEXT STEPS


This is a concept not a proposal and the writer is interested in working with a partner to help shape a proposal as well as provide funding.  The next logical steps would be to:


Enter  into discussions with interested potential funder(s), and

Develop a more detailed proposal or perhaps a feasibility study


















ABOUT THE PROPOSER


Donald E. M. Glendenning



Donald Glendenning was born in New Brunswick, graduated from Miramichi Rural High School, Teachers College, New Brunswick Technical Institute, completed a year of study at London University (England), holds a Bachelor’s and Master’s Degree from Bradley University  and a PhD. from Indiana University.


His experience includes teaching at the elementary, secondary and post secondary levels, a high school principalship, a technical training specialist with the Government of Canada, 


Dr glendenning was the Founding President of Holland College and, under his direction for 18 years, Holland College was a recognized leader in outcomes based education, performance based assessment, flexibility and responsiveness to both learner and community needs.  In addition to the usual college level programs, Dr. Glendenning brought into being the Atlantic Police Academy, Culinary Institute of Canada and a School of visual Arts. 


Upon leaving Holland College, he established his own consulting firm and carried on projects both at home and overseas.  Canadian projects were many and varied the largest being the development of a competency based model for secondary education.  His overseas work included projects in Kenya, Swaziland, India, Pakistan and the ASEAN Region.


Dr. Glendenning was an active player on the national and local scene, has written  and spoken extensively.  Most recently, he played leadership role in the establishment of the Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island and still serves as a Director.  In the latter case, he led a partnership with the Federal and Provincial Governments and the private sector to implement a stabilization initiative in the arts.
