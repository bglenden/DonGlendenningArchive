CANADA COLLEGE


Schools no longer have a monopoly on the delivery of education and training.  Employers, labor unions, trade and professional associations, consulting agencies, and many others now deliver courses, workshops, and other training sessions to employees, members and the public at large.  Some agencies deliver a series of courses which result in a credential; other organizations simply deliver stand-alone short courses.  Some large US corporations offer their own degree programs.


The trend towards a multiplicity of delivery agencies is now well established in Canada and, given the current constitutional discussions, the proliferation will likely continue.  Canadians who are life long learners will have even more choices from which to choose training, including creating their own.  To this we can add self study and the rich experiences of participation and leadership in community activities.  Lifelong learners, therefore, can end up with a large number of certificates recognizing participation or achievement in individual courses but none which recognizes the continuous and collective achievement of the learner.


While educational institutions may consider previous educational and other experience when accepting students, they are not very anxious to and less than generous in giving credit for education dissimilar to that which they offer in their own institution.  Some schools are even reluctant to give credit for courses taken through their own extension departments.  A sheepskin curtain separates credit and non credit courses.


Why shouldn’t learners be able to accumulate credits earned in school, university, college, at work, through self study, seminars, workshops, etc. and have these credits aggregated and counted towards an appropriate educational qualification?  Is it possible to do?  Difficult to administer?  Or do we lack the incentive or will?


The idea of educational credit banks is not new.  The Open Learning Agency in BC operates such a scheme, however, it is limited to credits towards a university degree.  New Zealand, on the other hand, has established a Qualification Authority that works with industry, education and training providers, and the community.  It operates in the area of post-compulsory education.  Its objective is “to simplify the current confusing system of qualifications and make it easier for people to continue learning”.  England had its City and Guilds which are now being replaced with a competency based certification system.


Life experience is a more complex and difficult component to assess.  The notion of accepting life experience for educational credit has been around for several decades.  Attention to this facet of education in Canada is increasing and a number of Provinces have put or are putting a system in place.  My experience with Canadian efforts is limited.  The focus seems to be on converting life experience to a time based education credit (something like the Continuing Education Unit).  Time based credit is yesterday’s solution.  New alternatives should be examined.


Canada could use an agency, which would not deliver training or education but would simply operate a credit bank.  People of any age and in any Province could provide the bank with a record of their learning activities regardless of where and how they were obtained.  On requires, and after assessment, the “bank” could issue the appropriate educational certificate for which a person is qualified.


Canada College could provide such a service.


Canada College could be constituted to issue a high school certificate, college diploma or a university degree or other educational credential.  They should be serious credentials; not of the mail order variety.  Canada College would receive, verify, assess, equate, aggregate, record, store, dispense and certify; it would not offer courses.


A unique characteristic of Canada College would be its ability to assess non credit courses and life experiences and to convert them to educational credit.  A system of measuring performance would be required.  There is considerable experience available to draw from; however, considerable new ground must still be tilled.  Certification bodies provide one major source of expertise; CBE programs provide another.


During the past three years I have built 2 bridges, swamped a road, installed a drainage system, supervised some road repair and road drainage, prepared bills of materials, and carried out the related business transactions.  Perhaps I’ll apply to Canada College to have this experience assessed for credit towards a civil engineering certificate or diploma.  I must prepare my portfolio.
