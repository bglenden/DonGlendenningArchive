Examining the skilled trades shortage, Don distinguishes between licensed and unlicensed skilled workers and questions whether licensing requirements are realistic. He observes that education systems may bias toward prestigious occupations while practical trades lack public advocates. He notes that Holland College at its founding addressed this question—whether provincial resources should support all training regardless of local employment. He proposes recognizing skilled workers' organizations for self-certification, long-term public information initiatives, equal achievement recognition for trades, clear responsibility assignment, and dedicated funding.

Editor

Charlottetown Guardian

Charlottetown, PE


Dear Sir:


So we have a looming if not actual shortage of skilled workers.  Or is it a shortage of licensed skilled workers?  There’s a difference.  Perhaps we need both.


What’s the reason?  Is it opportunity to learn the necessary skills?  Is wage or salary levels?  Is it working conditions?  Is it the image of the occupations where the supply is short?  Is it the life style associated with the field?  Is it because so many businesses are small businesses?  Are the licensing requirements realistic and transparent?  Is it a bias in the education system towatds more prestigious occupations? Is it beause those who are skilled are lured away to greener pastures?  Is it because trtade training programs do not have public advocates?  It is probably all of the above


How many skilled people does our economy need?  At one time it was thought that four technicians were required for each engineer – and number of skilled workers was even higher?  The workplace has changed and so, I believe have the numbers.  In addition the field also includes technologists as well as technicians and many sillled occupations hae sub specialties.  It appears also that many people who are professionally trained work in roles below their capacity .  Is it because their specialty is saturated or do they take such jobs in order to continue to live on PEI?  

This space is too small to address all of the issues but let me make a few general comments.  Employers tell me that there is a shortage  generally and a great shortage in some occupations.  And yes, colleagues tell me that opportunities for training in skilled occupations are disappearing on college campuses across the country.


Courses are closed because they are expensive, numbers are low or employment opportunities are limited on PEI.  If this is ttrue, should the same logic not apply to other occupations such as lawyers, historians, and teachers?  That was an issue 35 years ago when Holland College was established.  The Board of the day decided that Holland college was for Islanders wherever they decided to go for employnent.



Some options to considert:

Encourage skilled workers to set up their own organizations and do their own certification and licensing.

Undertake a consistent and long term initiative to provide information about both needs and opportunities.

Recognize achievement in skilled areas in the same manner that we do with the professions.

Clearly identify who is responsible for developing and implementing a plan of action 

Earmark additional funds to the initiative.
