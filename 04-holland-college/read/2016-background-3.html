Policy analysis document addressing the erosion of high school diplomas as educational/employment screening devices. Documents that employers (Greater Charlottetown Chamber of Commerce, Summerside Chamber of Commerce) and institutions publicly express concerns about graduates' basic skills levels. Reviews admission requirements across Holland College (Grade 12 with general-level credits), UPEI (Grade 12 exam completion with 70%+ average), and employers (varying from diploma requirements to skills descriptions). Explores underlying issue: high school graduation no longer reliably signals adequate basic education. Proposes Education 20/20 research questions: school roles, employer perspectives on gaps, prior documentation, individual cases, student options, and alternative assessment strategies. Document articulates the central policy problem Don sought to address through his research and advocacy work.


Prog/Prep

Proficiency levels


Having a high school graduation diploma has been a filter to assure the public at large, employers and postsecondary institutions that the holder had a level of basic education needed to function in their next life role; users of this filter are reporting that this is no longer the case.


As part of a survey Island youth in late 2013 by The Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island (CFPEI), young people identified “preparedness gaps with regard to readiness for postsecondary study and the workplace”. Both the Greater Charlottetown Area Chamber of Commerce and the Summerside Area Chamber of Commerce are on the public record with their concerns about the level of basis skills of high school graduates, as are many parents and members of the public. While they have not said so publicly, institutional members have expressed similar concerns.


A quick scan of admission requirements show:

  • Holland College – “Grade 12 or equivalent with credits at or above the general level”; actual subjects vary depending on the student’s field of study.

  • UPEI - “successful completion of Grade 12 examinations In a University Preparatory program with an overall average of at least 70 percent in the following subjects …”; actual subjects vary depending on the student’s field of study.

  • Employers – some specify high school, some specify a high school diploma, college diploma, a college or university degree, and others simply describe the skills needed or work to be done


Education 20/20 Inc. believes the issue to be important and plans to explore the matter further starting with the needs of local Island employers. Among the questions to be explored:

  1. What do school see as their role in preparing young people for work?

  2. How do employers describe the gap?

  3. Has this need been documented already? If so, can we obtain a copy?

  4. Are there any documented cases of people who experienced the gap?

  5. What options are available to young people who experience the gap?

  6. What alternative assessment strategies are available to employers?


Proposed action:













Coping mechanisms:

  • Institutions and employers develop own sorting process

  • Convert school credentials to qualifications

  • Change graduation requirements

  • Develop widely recognized levels

  • More clearly defined benchmarks

  • Develop a common analysis

  • Better communication and consultation







  • Neither content and nor levels of basic skills are consistently defined

  • Low basic skill levels drag down the economy because reduced ability to perform.

  • There is evidence of duplication of effort in re-teaching of skills thought to have been taught in the public school system

  • Responsibility for closing or eliminating the gap is not clear.

  • Teaching of basic skills in should have higher priority than is now the case.

  • There is a need for further exploration of the basic education gap.
