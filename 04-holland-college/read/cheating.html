Published as Education 20/20 Issue article, this piece analyzes rising academic cheating and proposes competency-based education as a solution. Glendenning documents various cheating trends—plagiarism software arms races, file-sharing mentality, test anxiety, grade inflation—and identifies systemic causes: poor teaching, lack of rapport, and emphasis on grades over learning. He argues that traditional subject-based testing is vulnerable to cheating, while competency-based programs, which assess observable real-world performance over multiple occasions, make cheating nearly impossible. 20/20  YOU MAY CHEAT ON SUBJECTS

BUT NOT ON EDUCATION       _______________________________________________________________________A scan of items on cheating in The New York Times over the past few weeks, reveals that:


cheating continues to be a problem; it may be on the upswing or it may be that we catch more cheaters

Some schools require students to have their papers checked by a “plagiarism program”

Some cheating may be attributed to the widespread practice of file sharing, lack of understanding of intellectual property rights, 

Poor teachers who practice sloppy teaching, fail to establish rapport with students, 

Teachers as well as students are worried about high stakes testing

A number of law schools in the USA raised the grades of  recent graduates to gie them a better chance of finding employment with leading law firms.

Grade five students at a New York school were given back their test books so that they could reake some of the questions.

Students in the digital age see information as something to be retrieved and passed along, without really ever being internalized or mastered

One survey of 7-12th graders revealed that  52 percent admitted to some form of web-based cheating.

Cheating is more common in classrooms where students do not have a real  relationship with teachers, students feel that tasks and assignments are pointless or overwhelming, how well students are doing means more than  what they are doing, and achievement is viewed as a competition. 

Some universities have special technology equipped test rooms where students are under surveillance when writing exams


A system of determining student achievement on the basis of school subjects and written tests is vulnerable to cheating; elaborate systems to detect cheating and punish cheaters will likely do little to reduce it.


So what’s the answer? One contributor, who had served on Cornell University’s Academic Integrity Hearing Board, recommended that students be required to take an ethics course.   That certainly would do no harm but a better way, I believe, would be to design or adopt a system where performance is based on observable behaviour. 


Outcomes based education is one such system; unfortunately, its seems to have given way to curriculum based objectives which does not have the same potential; the other is competency based education (CBE).


 Competency based programs are modeled  on relevant life roles.  The process begins with a detailed analysis of the competencies, or actions, performed in real life.  From the analysis, skills are selected to be developed in the school setting.  Tests are used to diagnose and measure, however, actual grades are based on the persons observed performance using a rating scale consistent with that applied in real life. 


It is virtually impossible to cheat in a competency based education model; normally a rating would only be confirmed basd on a number of observations of a presentation being made, a report being given, the problem being solved.
