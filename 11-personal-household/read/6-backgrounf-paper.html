Comprehensive background research paper establishing the theoretical foundation for Don's achievement levels initiative. References public concern about educational standards, citing No Child Left Behind, labour mobility, and technology's impact on instruction. Don analyzes successful alternative models including apprenticeship programs, the Canadian Language authority, International Baccalaureate, and Canadian Conservatory of Music. He critiques current PEI practice for lacking externally referenced criteria and maintaining conflicts between teaching and grading functions. The document examines assessment versus evaluation (using Robert Paul Wolf's 1969 framework), identifies best practices internationally, and articulates characteristics of needed qualification authorities—emphasizing independence, laddering, and reduced bias.

A Background:


Standards for most products and services are usually established by or in consultation with users or the government acting on their behalf; not so in the public school system where those who teach, not only set the standards but measure and determine the level reached by students served. In the views of the writer this not only constitutes a conflict of interest but casts doubt on the quality of the product. The issue is further clouded because:

  • Outcomes, which are the foundation of standards, are based on outcomes of uncertain origin and from an unclear process

  • Absence of “externally referenced criteria” to the assessment process

  • Erosion of public confidence in public school standards

  • Results of large scale assessment (PISA, PCAP, ??)

  • On the contribution of education to economic development

  • Trend by employers to explore alternative selection criteria

  • Remedial activities by postsecondary institutions

  • School centered and, therefore, local in application

  • Confusing practice of assigning two meanings to “grade levels” to two dissimilar functions, age based grouping and achievement

  • Rise of technology-aided instruction and assessment

Assessment and Evaluation


Robert Paul Wolf (Wolf: A discourse on Grading, 1969) identified three purposes for school assessment:

  • Analysis or criticism (formative) designing additional strategies in support of learning. analysis prepares a teacher to provide meaningful feedback and is part of the ongoing give and take of the learning process

  • Evaluation- to establish a person’s level in a subject or field, criterion referenced

  • Ranking – how one stacks up against others


Duke University website

  • Assessment change in learning teaching and outcomes, interactive between teacher; intended to improve learning “information is learner-centered, course based frequently anonymous and not graded” Duke Education website

  • Evaluation focuses on grades and may reflect classroom components other than course content and mastery level – Duke University
