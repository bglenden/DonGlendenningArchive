Transitioning in mathematics-6


“A student’s ability to transition from secondary school to post-secondary education and the labour market is a key measure of the education system’s success for students” – CMEC, July 2016


The above statement by the Council of Ministers of Education Canada (CMEC) highlights a concern that is shared by many educators, employers, certification agencies and the community at large. Issues in transitioning arise for a number of reasons including:

  • Inconsistency in the way content is defined - skills, competencies or outcomes

  • Absence of a master list of math skills throughout the education system

  • Proficiency levels need further definition

  • Lacks a common criteria and rating scale

  • Math assessments and reporting not perceived as being reliable

  • Potential for conflict when teachers also grade

  • Limited, if any, consultation between and among those who teach.

  • Employers generally seem to have little input into deciding what is to be taught

  • Mixed messages about entry requirements from postsecondary institutions


Underlying beliefs about transition:

Although not a simple issue, there is considerable agreement that:

  • the performance level of a person in a field of study is determined by: the nature and range of skills that a person can demonstrate, level of performance at which each skill can be demonstrated, extent or degree of clustering.

  • Outcomes and competencies are not age related although the same cannot be said for proficiency

  • Bloom’s Taxonomy provides an appropriate basis for establishing levels of proficiency

  • For greatest impact, outcomes, skills or competencies should be stated in terms that apply directly to learners


From the literature and informed opinion:

A review of the literature and informed opinion reveals that the curriculum generated by Alberta is currently under review, that the MPEHC is now asking that program proposals be competency based, colleges identify competencies for the programs they offer, competencies are widely used in the health field, Queens University recently announced that it is converting programs to a competency based model and that a number of States in the North Eastern USA have switched to competency based format, as has the Association of Private Schools and Colleges in the US. All this to say that I think that consideration of alternatives is timely.


Recommendations:

It is recommended that, as a pilot project, Prince Edward Island:

  1. Adopt competencies as a basis for curriculum development

  2. Develop or adopt a “master list of math competencies” for use in the Island education system

  3. Develop or adopt a common set of criteria and rating scale

  4. Report student achievement in terms of competencies demonstrated and levels achieved
