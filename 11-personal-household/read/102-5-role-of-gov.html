The most developed version of the government role discussion, framing education as a service system requiring explicit resource allocation decisions. Don references Education 20/20's consultation with youth about needed educational support (information, analysis, feedback, instruction, counseling, coaching). Advocates for healthcare-style service definitions stating what government will fund. Proposes functional rather than school-based organization and questions current delivery assumptions. References OECD (autonomy), Montreal Economic Institute (accountability), UNESCO (access), and UNICEF (relevance) as reform guides.

Editor

Dear Sir

Re-setting direction

An education system is an array of programs and services put in place by a community to encourage and support the education of its citizens; support is usually focused on (1) helping individual citizens gain skills, knowledge, attitudes and values that help them thrive in the years ahead and (2) providing human resources needed to maintain the community as a place people want to live. From my perspective, the current Department has 150,000 Islanders potentially looking or help.

If we accept the above, we are then faced with the question as to what help is needed. Some time ago, Education 20/20 convened a group of young people and asked them that question; they responded with. a long list of ideas, chief among them being assistance with gathering information, analyzing experiences, obtaining feedback, obtaining instruction, evaluating selves, coaching others, practicing what is being learned, observing others, attending lectures, counselling and observing demonstrations. When asked about the order in which help was needed, not surprisingly, students followed quite different paths. Restated, the student list above is similar to typical education services such as governance, leadership. funding, regulation, information, counselling, instruction, quality assurance, learning products and human resources. Communities, employers, postsecondary institutions would likely be equally happy to respond to questions about their needs as well.

What kind and how much “educational support” should taxpayers pay for? The Canada Health Plan, I am told, has an agreed upon list of health services it will pay for; perhaps education should consider the same approach. It would be an interesting exercise and one that would help keep us focused on our prime purpose. IAs an example, Government may decide that the arts are important and offer “free” instruction, materials and supplies On the other hand, our government may decide to cover the cost of on-line instruction in math.

The Department seldom mentions improving quality but, should that be of interest, the Department may consider some of the suggestions of the OECD (autonomy for schools), the Montreal Economic Institute (accountability), UNESCO (access) and UNICEF (relevant curricula) may be worth considering. Wht portion of required courses should be students choice?

Then, there’s the question of how to organize learning resources; most organizations I’ve been part of, organize around functions. Health services in PEI re delivered in a variety of ways including private professionals, pharmacies, hospitals, clinics and a great variety of specialists. PEI seems to prefer delivery of educational services by its own employees. Some have suggested a commission rather than government, grants to families, delivery of some services through libraries, build schools. Of course, different approaches may apply to each service. Our Department ppears to delivering all education services trough schools which I liken it to delivering all health services through the local hospital. In all my reading, I cannot recall any articles arguing for government running schools. Readers may recall earlier items about service Gaps in Souris, lack of nigh classes nd short courses, desire formore relevnt courses etc. Focus on functions People, insstad of school boards education boards

The situation calls for action; step one, I suggest, is review and restatement of our intentions – a public process resulting in a public document setting out basic beliefs and underlying principles on which our system should be built. The initiation of such a process falls normally to government; it’s preparation, however, should include substantial public engagement.

As I write, I am reminded of a quotation in a book, Setting Instructional Objectives by Robert Mager, popular educator of some sixty years ago. In making the case for clear objectives, Mager asks “If you don’t know where you’re going, how will you know when you get there?” It’s time, I suggest, it’s time for Education in PEI to review and restate where “it’s going”

Sincerely


Don Glendenning

A long-time student of education
