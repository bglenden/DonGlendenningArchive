English-language newsletter no. 49 (February 20, 2017) edited by Don Glendenning on personal beliefs about education. Opens with John Dewey quote and shares Glendenning's core educational beliefs: all people can learn but may need varying time; learning is individual; early childhood education (first six years) is most critical; education's purpose is lifelong skill development; individuals bear responsibility for their own education; community future depends on citizen education; and government's role includes encouraging education, ensuring equity, and maintaining community skill pools. Congratulates Erin Johnston, Principal of Prince Street Elementary School (2015 Excellence Award winner), as one of Canada's outstanding principals. Highly valuable for understanding Glendenning's educational philosophy post-Holland College.

BELIEFS


John Dewey once said that if he could only know one thing about a person, he’d want to know what the person believes; interestingly, however, the matter of beliefs seldom arises in my conversations with educators.


As a place to start, let me share some of my personal beliefs:

  • All people can learn but some may need more time than others.

  • Learning is an individual matter but may occur in a group setting.

  • The most important part of one’s education is the part that occurs in the first six years of life.

  • The purpose of education is to help a person gain attitudes, skills and use of knowledge for the remainder of their journey through life.

  • The responsibility for one’s education rests with one’s self.

  • Our future as a community depends, in a large measure, on the nature and level of education of our citizens.

  • The role of a Provincial Government is (1) to encourage education among its citizens, (2) to ensure equity in its support and (3) to maintain a pool of skills needed to ensure the future of the community in which we live.

  • An education system should be designed to deliver educational services to the extent possible, as and when needed.


“Are beliefs cast in stone?” The answer is “no”; are they likely to change tomorrow? The answer is also “no”. Beliefs change as we learn new things; changes usually result in a changed word or two. Eventually, but not often, changes may become an entirely new belief. Having a set of beliefs, I find it to be very useful when it comes to ease and consistency of decision making.

POT POURRI
