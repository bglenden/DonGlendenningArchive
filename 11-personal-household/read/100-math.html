MATH FOR EVERYDAY LIVING

   D 100  A Discussion Paper                           May 2020

____________________________________________________________________________


In late 2013, The Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island (CFPEI) undertook a survey of over 200 Island youth to identify issues that directly affected them; the report, Taking the Pulse of Island Youth, available on the Foundation’s website at www.cfpei.ca, provides a snapshot of the findings.  Bright spots include a positive sense of community, emerging entrepreneurial spirit, volunteerism and new postsecondary offerings. The main concerns include a lack of jobs, surviving on low-paying jobs, educational deficiencies and drug and alcohol addictions.  On the subject of education and learning, the study reports a high rate of completion of high school and postsecondary education, a high level of inclusivity and relatively affordable tuition costs; young people also expressed concerns about their readiness for the next stage of life including: 


“preparedness gaps with regard to readiness for

postsecondary study and the workplace” – Vital Signs, CFPEI, 2013


Following the publication of the CFPEI Report, Education 20/20 organized six public discussions for the purpose of informing the community about the report and its findings, learning more about youth issues in our communities, exploring issues of education and learning, and to identify actions to be taken to close gaps.  Discussions were lively and wide ranging and carried on well after formal sessions ended.   Education 20/20 retained an interest in the subject and continued its exploration through informal discussion with educators, employers, students and the community at large. 


EDUCATION

Consistent decision making requires a set of basic beliefs about learners and learning; this paper is based on the view that:

  • Learning is an individual matter
  • Although groups do not learn, individuals may learn in a group setting.  
  • Learning results in a change of behaviour.
  • The role of a teacher is to help students change behaviour;
  • People are educated whether or not they attend school
  • Education should emphasize achievement not time spent or served 
  • Grading is not a teaching function and probably not an educational function.
  • High school graduation implies sufficient math to cope as a citizen in every day life





MATH

Mathematics is the study of the science of number, quantity and space; it is in demand to some degree by all citizens,  employers, institutions and the community at large. The Oregon Department of Education contends that math provides “a foundation for the study of science and technology as well as the interpretation of quantitative information in other subjects. Mathematics may be structured in a number of ways but structure does not appear to have a particular influence on the quality of instruction.  


Math is an essential skill needed to some level by all citizens and can be learned to the citizen level by all or practically all Islanders.  Furthermore, helping citizens acquire math skills for citizenship is one of the key roles of our public-school system but there appears to be no agreement, however, as to the nature and level of citizen math and how it should be determined.


Numeracy is also mathematics,  the application tof mathematics to solve problems in every day life.  Being numerate has been described as “having the confidence and skill to use numbers and mathematical approaches in all aspects of life – at work, in practical every day activities  at home and beyond, as consumers in managing our finances, as parents helping our children learn, as patients making sense of health information, and as citizens understanding the world about us.”

FINDINGSS

 Our search revealed: 

  • A 2002 multi-country study by the European Commission found that, if the national average educational attainment level is increased by a single year, aggregate productivity increases by 6.2 per cent right away and another 3.1 percent in the long run. 
  • The Conference Board of Canada (undated) reports (1) a “C” grade on numeracy skills, (2) that forty five percent of Canadian adults have inadequate numeracy skills – a significant in crease from a decade ago and, (3) no Province earns above a “C” grade for numeracy skills
  • A report by the PEI Department of Education on The Early Numeracy Intervention Pro-gram (ENIP) which found that numeracy “gives students the tools and the skills to solve problems logically, and to developmental flexibility, independence of thought, self-efficacy, and the ability to persevere.”  Typical numeracy lists are somewhat situation specific – personal, workplace, community and further learning.
  • The UK introduced a new measure of numeracy proficiency …  for all young people at the age of 14  
  • University of Canberra (Australia) reports success in “Delivering Numeracy in remote indigenous contexts”.
  • Extensive references are found to “Closing the Numeracy Gap in Ontario”
  • Math instruction could benefit from increased practice, increased writing and conversation about the subject.
  • Globe  and Mail, Canada’s Math Woes are Adding Up, March 4, 2014
  • Fraser Institute,”Math Performance in Cannada” , 2021

CONCLUSIONS

Based on the literature, the above noted discussions and the writer’s experience, the writer concludes that:

  • Math instruction in PEI is fragmented
  • Cooperation among providers is hampered by the lack of agreed- upon definitions of a number of key concepts. 
  • Broadly speaking, education lacks an overarching statement of philosophy and direction; 
  • There is no formal mechanism for coordination among math providers
  • Evaluation criteria for math should be the same for all levels of education.
  • Improving transition requires cooperation of all math instruction providers.
  • There is an interest among math providers in cooperating on a pilot project


RECOMMENDATIONS:

Based on our exploration, Education 2020 recommends:

  • An over-arching statement of beliefs and principles
  • Numeracy be offered as a separate subject on PEI
  • Numeracy curriculum be based on skills used by citizens in their everyday lives
  • Levels of numeracy be established separate from student grade levels
  • Numeracy programs be competency based. 
  • The Province strike a numeracy advisory committee that is broadly representative of the community served.
  • PEI adopt a dynamic community-based rating scale for numeracy
  • Student progress be recorded on a competency-by-competency basis.
  • Instruction be based on skills practised in sector or community served
  • Each student be given a master list of skills as a basis for planning and tracking progress.
  • A learning guide be developed for each competency 
  • Have students’ self- rate as part of the learning process
  • Adopt a common platform for curriculum development in mathematics to include field analysis, evaluation criteria and rating scale
  • Prepare a lexicon of relevant education terms – outcomes, competencies, rating scale, etc.
  • Identify key math profiles i.e., citizenship, employment entry and entry to post secondary institutions
  • Develop a learning guide for each competency. 

BENEFITS:

The benefits of adopting the above recommendations include:

  • An additional tool for employers when planning, hiring and promoting 
  • Provides  math transcript for job seekers
  • Diagnostic and counselling tool for employers and institutions
  • Improved communication among providers and users
  • Increased relevance of what is taught
  • Format relates to every day living 
  • A tool for the planning customized training
  • Reduces student confusion when changing schools
  • Reference tool for institutions when planning math courses
  • Guidance for workers/learners needing/wanting to upgrade smith skills
  • A reference for trade and professional organizations when organizing upgrading courses
