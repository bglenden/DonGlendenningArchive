Extensive analysis of literacy challenges in Prince Edward Island and Canada, presenting statistics showing 30-40% of Islanders lack adequate literacy. Examines definitions of reading, competencies, and curriculum outcomes in Atlantic provinces. Emphasizes that reading development begins in early childhood and home environments, arguing that schools alone cannot address literacy deficiencies without community and family support.

Some PEI statistics:

  • The Guardian, January 17, 2013 reported that “40 percent of Islanders haven’t squirted a level of literacy or numeracy to allow them to complete their high school studies.”

  • The Guardian, Sept 28, 2006: International tests place 15 year-olds in PEI lower than other Provinces in reading and writing.

  • The Guardian, Sept 28, 2006: Forty percent or 40,000 Islanders have skills so limited that they cannot deal with most printed materials such as newspapers or telephone books. Most can only read and understand simple language.

  • The Guardian, Sept 28, 2006: reading skills of many seasonal workers is lower than other workers. This restricts their ability to gain permanent employment.

  • The Guardian, Sept 28, 2006: About 80% of PEI seniors cannot read well enough to deal with everyday tasks, such as reading prescriptions

  • The reading skills of 30% of PEI adults are so limited that they cannot deal with most printed material – PEI Literacy Alliance

  • While literacy numbers are improving, literacy rates are not

  • PEI has a poor ranking on international tests






Definition:

So what do we mean when we say a person can or cannot read? A quick scan of the literature reveals a number of definitions:

  • The act of turning writing into meaning.

  • The ability to use printed information as meaningful language (Source unknown).

  • Specific abilities which enable a reader to read the written form as meaningful language, read anything written with independence, comprehension and fluency and to mentally interact with the message. (Source unknown)

  • A set of skills to enable readers to turn writing into meaning and achieve the goals of independence, comprehension and fluency (Internet)

Early years

It’s the home that determines how well and how soon we read; that means directing more help and resources to help parents, grandparents and siblings. What’s needed is development not schooling; it is different from schooling in that is focused to a large degree on formation, mental and emotional health, TLC, confidence and encouragement and not skills


Sending children to pre-school programs is not the same as providing supporting development. Occasionally I read articles that suggest that traditional early childhood programs may even interfere with development and contribute little to reading improvement.


That is not to down play the importance of teachers and teaching, teachers certainly help. They can and do help with skill building; unfortunately their schedules do not allow them to deal with deficiencies from the early years. They can help remove the deficiencies of the early years and they can help with skillbuilding.
