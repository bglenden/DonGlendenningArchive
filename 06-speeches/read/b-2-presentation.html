Math Presentation

Education


In Canada education is a provincial matter. Provinces agree, it seems, that they have a responsibility to help their citizens develop attitudes, skills and knowledge at least to the level of good citizenship; although not as clearly stated, Provinces also have a responsibility to ensure that their community includes the capacity to care for itself. In PEI, that means encouraging more and better education for all 145,000 Islanders and providing financial support for some – not an easy task surrounded by a world of rapidly increasing complexity and while experiences equally changing change in what we know, what we do and how we act.


It is widely recognized that education is a key factor in determining one’s quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of our citizens will determine to a significant degree the future of our community and country. One of the most frequently mentioned concerns by Islanders is the low level of basic skills in our workforce, among recent graduates and young Islanders generally. Evidence for concern arises from PISA results, recent CFPEI survey of Island youth, presentation to the Standing Committee on by, How Canada Performs; Provincial and Territorial Ranking, Education and Training; Education and Skills, and a series of workshops on Preparedness held throughout PEI by Education 20/20 Inc. While the concern applies to the state of education generally, this paper limits itself to skills we need to be functioning members of our respective communities and, in particular to the field of mathematics.


Education providers in PEI:

PEI, like most Provinces, has a mix of educational providers

  • The Department of Education and Culture delivers math instruction through the school system based on a curriculum developed by the Northern and Western Proto-col and used in six other Provinces.

  • The Department of Workplace Learning which, it appears, lets contractors determine instructional models

  • Postsecondary institutions who each follow their own curriculum development model but do within general framework used in sister institutions

  • Private schools generally follow public school curriculum guides,

  • Tutors, educational consultants,

  • Large employers frequently have the capacity to do own curriculum development and training whereas small employers adopt existing models and materials

  • Families who help as best they can

  • NGO






Underlying beliefs about math education:

We believe that a common set of underlying beliefs for math education would be helpful and propose the following:

  • the performance level of a person in a field of math is determined by the nature and range of skills that a person can demonstrate, level of performance at which each skill can be demonstrated, extent or degree of clustering.

  • Outcomes and competencies are not age related.

  • Mandating those who teach to also “sort” constitutes a conflict of interest

  • Bloom’s Taxonomy provides an appropriate basis for establishing levels of proficiency

  • To the extent possible, outcomes, skills or competencies should be stated in terms that apply directly to learners and be part of a learner’s support material

  • Alignment between potential competencies offered, those studied, levels learned, levels demonstrated, levels achieved, , credentials awarded and qualifications acquired.



Conclusions

Based on our collective knowledge of education generally of Education 2/20 Inc. and referring to education in mathematics in particular, and arising at least in part from the issue of transitioning raised by the Council of Ministers of Education Canada, we’ve reached the following conclusions:

  • An inconsistency in the way we describe math content through our education system - skills, competencies or outcomes

  • Confusion about levels of achievement and who defines them

  • Lacks common assessment criteria and rating scale used to assess student achievement

  • Reliability of math assessments and reporting

  • Potential for conflict when teachers also grade

  • Limited communication between and among those who teach and between those who hire the product of our teaching.

  • Need to involve all providers in the education fraamework


Recommendations:

Education 20/20 Inc. recommends that Prince Edward Island:

  1. Engage stakeholders in “Math Improvement Initiative

  2. Adopt competencies as a basis for curriculum development

  3. Develop or adopt an independently developed list math competencies for use in the Island education system

  4. Adopt a standard list of assessment criteria

  5. Adopt a common rating scale for competencies

  6. Report student achievement in terms of competencies demonstrated and levels achieved

  7. Prof dev day

  8. Ease transition

  • UPEI - “successful completion of Grade 12 examinations In a University Preparatory program with an overall average of at least 70 percent in the following subjects …”; actual subjects vary depending on the student’s field of study.

Organizationally, most provinces operate a public-school system devoted to serving the needs of young people, a relatively autonomous postsecondary sector, and a conglomeration of services operated or administered through provincial ministries of training, and develop-ment. Add to this a federal responsibility and funding in support of education for economic development, for which education is a major contributor, and we’ve increased the issue of communication and lessened the likelihood of a focused response.



MATH EDUCATION

The PEI Curriculum Guide for Grade 9 mathematics includes a goal for students to “become mathematically literate adults”. It is an achievable goal, at least for most students but, if math test scores and public comment are used as the measure, the goal remains a challenge rather than an accomplishment. On the economic side, a 2003 multi-country study from the European Commission found that if the national average educational attainment level is increased by a single year, aggregate productivity increases by 6.2 per cent right away and by a further 3.1 per cent in the long run


Provider who deliver to youth follow a curriculum approved by the Department of Education based on a Curriculum Based Outcomes model. Other providers base math instruction on a from a variety of sources. On the other side of the coin, “receivers” of students have a variety of math interests and needs not always well documented or understood. A common menu of available math “ingredients”; master list of math skills or competencies do exist with the best known likely being Common Core State Standards in the USA.


An overview of math curriculum in Canada reveals:

• Institutions of all kinds and at all levels basing curriculum on a form or model of outcomes; the weakness, using my culinary analogy, they’ve released them only as recipes. Our preference would be for a list of ingredients from which we select items to give education our own flavor.

• Front line providers are not allowed to develop their own recipes

• “Receiving” organizations are not willing, able or, in some cases, allowed to articulate their needs using the standard list of ingredients.

• In some cases, there appears to be little interest in knowing users’ needs

• One of the common characteristics of outcomes and competencies is the failure to separate the analysis step from that of which competencies to be taught at which level. Such a separation would greatly increase flexibility and increase the uses to which analyses could be used.


TRANSITION:

Although not limited to mathematics, the CMEC, in a media release following its meeting in July 2016, drew attention to the issue of educational “transition” which, in the writer’s view, is a major contributor to our math woes. The above statement by the Council of Ministers of Education Canada (CMEC) highlights a concern that is shared by many educators, employers, certification agencies and the community at large. Issues in transitioning arise for a number of reasons including:

• Inconsistency in the way content is defined - skills, competencies or outcomes

• Absence of a master list of math skills available throughout the education system

• Proficiency levels need further definition

• Lacks a common criteria and rating scale

• Math assessments and reporting not perceived as being reliable

• Potential for conflict when teachers also grade

• Limited, if any, consultation between and among those who teach.

• Employers generally seem to have little input into deciding what is to be taught

• Mixed messages about entry requirements from postsecondary institutions


PEI

• One of the common characteristics of outcomes and competencies is the failure to separate the analysis step from that of curriculum development. Such a separation would greatly increase flexibility.

• Without a carefully constructed analysis it becomes easy to confuse levels of achievement from other outcomes.

• Needed at some level for day-to-day living

• Often a prerequisite for accessing many opportunities in later life

• Lacks a widely available and accepted analysis of outcomes or competencies

• Lacks widely accepted agreement as to what constitutes levels of performance \


• The lack of a common curriculum development in PEI contributes to the issue of tran-sition


NOTES:


• the literature and informed opinion reveals that the curriculum generated by Alberta is currently under review, that the MPEHC is now asking that program proposals be competency based, colleges identify competencies for the programs they offer, com-petencies are widely used in the health field, Queens University recently announced that it is converting programs to a competency based model and that a number of States in the North Eastern USA have switched to competency based format, as has the Association of Private Schools and Colleges in the US. Ontario is seriously review-ing curriculum development in Mathematics and the Education report in Newfound-land includes a section on Mathematics. All this to say that I think that consideration of alternatives is timely.



RECOMMENDATIONS:

Solving the math problem “problem” requires collaboration by all levels of education – public schools, post secondary institutions, continuing education, employers, SkillsPEI and certification bodies.; it cannot be solved by the public-school system alone.

  1. Increase autonomy of schools

  2. Increase accountability of teachers

  3. Adopt competencies as a basis for curriculum development

  4. Develop or adopt an analysis of math competencies for use throughout the education system

  5. Develop or adopt a common set of criteria

  6. Develop or adopt a common and rating scale

  7. Student achievement be reported on the basis of competencies demonstrated

  8. Convene a working group of math instruction “providers and receivers” to examine the math transition issue with a view to recommending ways of improving the transi-tion

  9. Establish an ongoing advisory committee of providers and users

  10. Annual professional die for math teachers at all level

  11. Share instructional materials
