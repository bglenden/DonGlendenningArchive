(166 words):** Don challenges Canada's credential obsession, noting that displayed degrees tell little about actual competence. He critiques how institutions award credentials based on pre-set programs that may not reflect real occupational requirements. Don distinguishes between degrees, diplomas, and certificates, finding transcripts more useful than general credentials. He argues employers rarely request formal credentials during hiring and suggests current credentialing systems fail to address employer concerns about communication skills, math/science knowledge, Canadian history, and work habits. The piece questions three fundamental issues: what credentials society needs, who should award them, and on what basis. Don advocates for reform of credentialing practices to better serve both students and employers, moving beyond institutional convenience toward meaningful assessment of actual competencies needed in the workplace.

Canadians like credentials. Places of work, whether they be trade, technical or professional, often display credentials of people who work there. I am not sure if such displays are intended to impress or intimidate. Most credetiaals I enclounter are issued by universities although a few are issued by professional and technical associations.  Such displays tell about some life choices of the bearers have made such as where they went to school, the level of schooling they attained, and maybe some specialized courses they have taken.  . Presumably they are placed in a conspicuous location to support the notion that the bearer, and the person providing yhou service, is competent to provide the service you are waiting for.


As the user of services provided by an establishment, I put this information in the category of interesting but not very useful. That is not to downplay the sweat equity students put into the  acquisition of diplomas and degrees but seldom do the items on display tell me about the competence of the person about to serve me.

While everybody has their pet peeves about the education system, the complaints I hear usually are about:


· Communication skills · Math/science skills · Canadian history or geography · Work habits and attitudes


Our current approach to credentialing seems not to address these concerns.


It is unlikely that we can or should prevent educational institutions from awarding credentials. Based on my own experience and in talking with employers, I question if credentials now offered really serve the public and student needs.


On reflection, one is left with three questions. What credentials do we need and why?

Who should award the credentials we need? On what basis should they be awarded?
