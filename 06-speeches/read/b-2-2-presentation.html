Transition in Math

Education


Education is a provincial matter. Provinces encourage education for all; they accomplish this objective by funding programs at least to the level of good citizenship for all citizens. Provinces also have a responsibility to provide skilled people to care for the community in which we live.


It is widely recognized that education is a key factor in determining one’s quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of our citizens will determine to a significant degree the future of our community and country in which we live and work. While our system of education ranks well in the world, it falls short of the expectations of many citizens. Evidence of and concern about that gap can be found in relation to PISA results, recent CFPEI survey of Island youth, presentation to the Standing Legislative Committee, provincial and Pan-Canadian reports. While the concern applies to the state of education generally, this paper limits itself to the field of mathematics.


Educational services are delivered in a variety of ways including:

  • Families in the home

  • Direct delivery by the Department of Education, Early Learning and Culture through the public-school system

  • The Department of Workforce and Advanced Learning through grants and contracts

  • Postsecondary institutions

  • Private schools and colleges

  • Tutors, educational consultants, NGO’s and associations

  • Employers and employees and related associations

  • Individuals enrolling and paying a fee


Given the above, it is not surprising that coordination and communication is an issue. In fact, the Council of Ministers of Education, (CMEC) drew attention to the issue of educational “transition” in a press statement on July 7, 2016 in which they highlight their concern, a concern that is shared by many educators, employers, certification agencies and the community at large. Our review of the matter suggests that transition issues likely arise from:

  • Number and variety of agencies involved in a Province

  • Differing foundations on which curriculum is developed - skills, competencies or outcomes

  • Absence of a common curriculum framework

  • The approach to defining levels of achievement.

  • Differing assessment criteria

  • Absence of a common rating scale

  • Large number of people engaged in the assessment process

  • Potential for conflict when teachers also grade

  • Limited, if any, consultation between and among those who teach at different levels

  • Limited representation on advisory committees

  • Mixed messages about entry requirements from postsecondary institutions


MATH INSTRUCTION

Math instruction PEI is based on the conventional structure: arithmetic, algebra, geometry, trigonometry, calculus, probability and statistics and is provided at all grade levels; its goal, as stated in the PEI Curriculum Guide for Grade 9 mathematics is for students to “become mathematically literate adults”. It is an achievable goal, at least for most students.


Not all math providers start from the same base; the public-school system use a Curriculum Based Outcomes model developed under The Canada North West Protocol the same curriculum is used in several other Provinces. Post secondary institutions tend to use a more traditional curriculum model although more and more they are turning to competencies. The US literature is witnessing a major swing to competencies with the best known likely being Common Core State Standards. On the other side of the coin, “receivers” of students have a variety of math interests and needs not all of which are adequately documented. The writer’s search for list of skills common to all levels was not successful.


Curriculum development involves laying out the education “course” for students, or more accurately, education courses for students. It is a key component of successful transition and involves four steps:

  • An analysis/description of the subject or field under consideration

  • Identification of skills required by learner to meet his or her education objective

  • Funding and education resources to support the student’s quest

  • Documentation/attestation of what has been learned


We believe that math instruction would benefit from a common set of underlying beliefs such as:

  • A person’s performance level in mathematics is determined by the nature and extent of skills that a person can demonstrate, level of performance at which each skill can be demonstrated, and the extent or degree of clustering.

  • Outcomes and competencies are not age related.

  • Mandating those who teach to also “sort” constitutes a conflict of interest

  • Bloom’s Taxonomy provides an appropriate basis for establishing levels of proficiency

  • To the extent possible, outcomes, skills or competencies should be stated in terms that apply directly to learners and be part of a learner’s support material

  • Alignment between competencies offered, competencies studied, levels learned, levels demonstrated, levels achieved, credentials awarded and qualifications acquired.


Conclusions

It is our view that issues in math education arise from:

  • An inconsistency in the way content is developed - skills, competencies or outcomes

  • Confusion about levels of achievement

  • Lack of common assessment criteria

  • Reliability of math assessments and reporting

  • Potential for conflict when teachers also grade

  • Limited communication between and among those who teach and between those who hire the product of our teaching.

  • Level of alignment throughout the system


Recommendations:

Education 20/20 Inc. recommends that Prince Edward Island:

  1. Engage stakeholders in a pilot project in one math sector – arithmetic.

  2. Adopt competencies as a basis for curriculum development

  3. Issue an independently developed analysis of math competencies for use in the Island education system

  4. Adopt a standard list of assessment criteria

  5. Adopt a common rating scale

  6. Report student achievement in terms of competencies demonstrated

  7. Establish an ongoing advisory committee of providers and users

  8. Organize ab annual professional event for math teachers at all levels

  9. Make instructional resource material available throughout the system


Benefits:

  • Broaden the base of resource materials

  • Reduces cost

  • More consistent interpretation

  • Increased accountability

  • Eases transition

  • Improves communication
