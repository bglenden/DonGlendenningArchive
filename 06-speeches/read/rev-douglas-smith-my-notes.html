Don's personal speaking notes (January 16, 2013) for Charlottetown Women's Probus Club presentation on Rev. Harvey Douglas Smith's life and legacy. Chronicles Smith's birth in Saint John (January 10, 1912), education at King's College, ordinations (1936, 1938), teaching posts, and transformative work founding MRHS. Includes Don's reflections on lessons learned about individual impact, community involvement, and broad educational vision.

Charlottetown Womens’ Probus Club

January 16, 2013

Rev. Harvey Douglas Smith

1912 - 


My first reaction was to talk about education



  • Most important issue of our time, more important than energy or global warming
  • Kind of education we provide today will determine, to a large extent, our future both as individuals and as a country


Then I thought about the early years of Holland College and prepared notes accordingly


Decided against it

One should be careful about visiting ones passions on ones friends


Tell the story of Rev. Douglas Smith.


 

  • Born:  Saint John, NB, January 10, 1912 
  • Died: Probably in Washington State before 1980
  • Married Yes


  • Formal studies: 1934 granted B.A. from Kings College in Halifax. 


  • Ordained:


1936, May 31 Ordained a deacon at St. Paul’s Church in Saint John, NB. 

1938, June 6, - Ordained a priest at the Cathedral in Fredericton

1948, November 15, Licensed as Priest Assistant, Trinity Memorial

Church, Montreal





Served:

  • 1936 - On staff Rothesay Collegiate School  
  • 1937 –Appointed Deacon of St. John the Evangelist Church, Bay du Vin, Parish of Hardwicke, 
  • 1940 – moved to the Diocese of Nova  Scotia and later; active in th

Parish church in Windsor Nova Scotia;  “Station Chaplain

  • 1945 - On leave from Montreal Dioscese


  • 1946- March 8, Chaplain at RCAF, Greenwood, NS.
  • I946-47 Principal, Miramichi Rural High School
  • Served on the Faculty of St Paul’s School for boys, Concord, NH
  • 1955 Was Canon of St. John’s Cathedral, Spokane, Washington  and Headmaster of St. George College and Preparatory School, Spokane


Documents: Several pieces of correspondence from Rev. H. Douglas Smith to the Deputy Minister of Education, Fredericton, at the Provincial Archives during the time period 1937-

.  


Work in Bay du Vin:


  • 1937 - Took up his duties at St. John’s Anglican Church, Bay du Vin, on August 24, 1937 
  • 1937 -  Started classes with 8 students “in his own study at the Rectory”;
  • 1938 – petitioned Department of education for money to hire teachers; Department could not make grant to private agency so a temporary board of Trustees was set up and money flowed to them; hired a teacher to help; Joined by local community leaders in initiative.
  • Classes proved so popular that they had to relocate first to the church basement and later to the church hall across the road.  Smith also hired two additional teachers, Miss Elizabeth Stevenson and Mrs. Dorothy Claytonburg. 
  • July 31, 1939 - Request for school in Bay du Vin area approved by Department of Education; as a result, the six districts of Black River Bridge, Victoria. Little Branch, Bay Side, Bay du Vin River and Bay du Vin Beach decided to consolidate and join forces to establish the first Rural High School of the Province.


  • A School Board was appointed to carry on the construction. Rev. D. Smith was the chairman, A.M. Cameron,  vice–chairman, Harrison T. Smith, treasurer, Ian MacNaughton, secretary, Woodbury Williston, Bertrand Williston and Jack Godfrey as additional members”.


  • On the opening of classes in September, 1939, still in Bay du vin, numbers had grown to 67 student, a Principal, Mr. A. T. Patstone and teachers, Miss Jean Barnes in Home Economics, and Miss Beryl MacAloney in academic subjects. 

  

  • In addition to the usual subjects, offerings included home economics, shop and music; in fact, an orchestra had been formed by the fall of 1940. In the early years, students were expected to plant school gardens.  It seems to me that we were paired and each pair had a small plot.  Come fall, the vegetables were canned and kept in the closet under the stairs.
  • 1946-47 – In touch with Fredericton about establishing a residential school for boys across road from school; program would include forestry and serve “delinquent” boys


Lessons learned

  • Shows that a person can make a difference
  • MRHS – first of a class of rural high schools (49) over next decade
  • Ingenuity of getting grants
  • Community involvement
  • Broad definition of education



Thank you
