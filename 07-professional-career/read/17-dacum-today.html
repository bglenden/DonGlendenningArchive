DACUM Today


Recently a friend, with no association with Holland college, asked about the status of Holland College’s delivery model; that evening I scanned the internet and was amazed at the number times and situations in which the name turned up. But how does one capture the story?


Initial/Early Adopters


Dissemination of information about DACUM in the 1970's occurred from five sources:


1. The British Columbia Department of Education developed DACUM charts. They were used primarily as a curriculum development tool and mostly by curriculum developers within the Department.


2. Nova Scotia NewStart Corporation used the DACUM model for its occupational courses. Following the completion of the project in 1972, Adams introduced the DACUM approach to a number of organizations in Canada. He also conducted workshops in the USA.


3. Holland College introduced the complete DACUM model as developed by Adams in 1970. The College's commitment to modular and individualized education caught the attention of the community college sector and resulted in many visitors and requests for college staff to act as resource persons to other institutions. While detailed records of visitors and off-Island presentations exist, presentations were made in every province and usually more than once.



Second Tier

were obtained from developers, including themselves, and distributed for a fee to other

Humber College recast the charts to a format which lent itself to easy copying and

transmission.

The Word DACUM


First we have the word itself. DACUM was coined in Canada in 1968. One would not expect to find it generally in the newspaper nor in normal conversation. One could expect it to occur occasionally in education literature; one would expect to find it in literature of technical and vocational technical and vocational education, curriculum development and training in and for industry.


Indices?




DACUM or DACUM Chart


A DACUM or DACUM chart may describe a single sheet presentation of a curriculum, as was the original DACUM chart, or a single page presentation of a task or competency analysis as developed at the Nova Scotia NewStart Corporation in Yarmouth, Nova Scotia. Difference may be inferred by knowing how they were created or are used but can only be determined through a visual inspection




DACUM Model


An approach to organizing, delivering and managing experiences for learners based on the model developed by the Nova Scotia NewStart Corporation, Yarmouth, Nova Scotia. Characteristics of the model include a DACUM based competency analysis chart including a competency based rating scale the hands of each instructor and learner, learner self assessment with confirmations by instructors, learning plans, learner paced, immediate feedback,

4 Early Adopters




A search of the internet soon reveals the extent to which DACUM has spread and it is not possible to trace the spread in great detail. The following appear to be the earliest transmitters.









Zambia


In April, 1970 Howard Clement took a temporary posting to Zambia where he lead curriculum development activities for a CIDA funded major training project. As one would expect, Clement introduced the DACUM approach. Clement's papers include actual charts produced in Zambia and photographs of DACUM analyses before the cards were removed from the wall.


The photographs show, and Clement confirmed, that analyses were developed using a blackboard on which lines or bands had been drawn. Statements were written on cards or strips of paper. When the outline was finished, a photograph was taken. Figure 9 shows part of an analysis developed in Zambia, dated August, 1971. DACUM based curriculum development in Zambia reflects the work being done at that time in British Columbia.


CVA




Humber College


Humber College established a DACUM Exchange in 1974. The Exchange obtained copies of DACUM charts and, without comment, converted them to a format which was easier to copy and distribute.


Humber College also developed DACUM charts. As Rick Embree recalls, the first charts were developed in 1972 following a workshop by Robert Adams. Again, Embree recalls that early charts were in trade areas with the most likely candidate for the first chart being either heavy equipment repair or welding.



National Centre for Research in Vocational Education (Ohio State)


According to information provided by Robert Norton, a long time member of the above noted Centre, he and other staff of the Centre first visited Holland College to observe the DACUM process in May 1973.


In January 1976, Lawrence Coffin of Holland College travelled to Ohio to conduct a DACUM Workshop for Centre staff. In December, 1976, centre staff began to offer DACUM training using their own staff.


The expansion of the Centre's use of DACUM beyond that noted above is beyond the scope of this paper.





By the mid-1980's, DACUM, or at least the analysis component, had wide application throughout Canada and the United States of America. Since then, its use has become international. In 1986, Adrienne Burleigh visited the USA, Canada and a number of other countries and concluded1


"DACUM has widespread and growing use throughout both Canada and the United States of America. While the application of DACUM demonstrates small local variations, the concepts outlined by Adams have been adhered to and, in all cases, the tenets that underpin the DACUM process are instantly recognizable in all situations."


Macklin2, in his Masters' thesis at Simon Fraser University, concluded that "... DACUM still stands, unchallenged, as the pioneer curriculum development system for competency-based vocational training".




DACUM BASED CURRICULUM DEVELOPMENT IN BC


From information provided by Franklin (August 25, 1994), a copy of notes used by Norman Mair in a speech at Langara Campus (believed to be in late 1972 or 1973) and notes from Bateman himself, it appears that John Bateman used the chart developed in Ottawa in January 1968 with his cooking class at the B.C. Vocational School in Burnaby.


Following his work with Clement in preparing and documenting the DACUM process, Franklin continued to develop learning activity batteries and later DACUM charts in BC. The earliest charts in the writer's possession are dated August 1972 a portion of which is shown in Figure 5. The writer also has in his possession a photograph titled "Gas Fitter Curriculum Chart". It is not dated, however the layout of the chart reflects earlier rather than later chart versions.


From the photograph, it appears that early charts were constructed on a chalkboard using strips of paper to show the starting point of modules and the relative length of time required to learn them. The photo also shows a "Prerequisite (of) 66 hours of Basic Mathematics, Basic Science, Basic Fluidics, Basic Electricity (and) Basic Thermodynamics". A column in the photograph also shows presumably, the anticipated learning hours for both theory and practice for each band. Some later charts provide these figures on the back of each chart. In a letter to the writer, Franklin referred to "waxed cards" which were stuck on the wall or a chalkboard "and were removable enabling us to change things around as we went along." Clement also used this approach in Zambia.


A review of the BC charts in the writer's possession show that:


• The early charts reflected the curriculum development ideas practised by Clement.


• Later charts dropped the relevance of time to the length of the block.


• Charts included a mixture of "knowing and doing".


• Blocks became more or less a standard size.


• Charts did not include milestones.


The approach to curriculum development still left the identification of what was to be taught or learned primarily in the hands of the instructor or curriculum planner although the actual committee included "knowledgeable trades people".




DACUM continues to be used in British Columbia and an examination of a number of charts developed during the early years reveals changing chart design during the period 1973-1975. Although different charts have different colours, individual bands are not identified by colour. The length of blocks no longer reflect the importance of the skill; blocks are essentially the same length. DACUM's application is primarily as a tool for curriculum planning and development. At time of writing, however, it still does not have widespread support throughout the college system.
