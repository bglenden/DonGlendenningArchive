Re-engineering Education


Our education system is quite outdated; it emphasizes schooling rather than education, is inflexible,

accountability is scattered, there is too much emphasis on time spent in a class or on a subject,

allows little choice by learners and lacks clear and consistent standards.


Tinkering with the system won't do. We need to do more than strike a new committee, undertake a

new public survey, add or change courses, increase teacher pay, revise the curriculum, add more

professional development days or purchase a few new computers. The system could profit from

some re-engineering. But where do we start? The following structural changes would be high on

my list.


Provide each student with an "education advisor", a person with a role similar to that of a family

doctor, with whom the learner or parent could consult at any time on matters pertaining to the

education of their child. Most of us would not be very happy if we had a new family doctor each

year, especially if the new doctor was not of our choosing. An education advisor would, for the

most part, provide advice and direction during at least K through 12, however, it could be longer if

needed. Students could elect to change advisors but advisors would have no choice in selecting

students; advisors could refuse to take a student only if they were not accepting additional students.

The educational advisor would provide an access point to the education system, provide continuity

for each learner; the advisor would work closely with each learner providing advice, instruction and

referral to specialist teachers as needed. Student transcripts would be signed off by the educational

advisor.


Our current system of awarding degrees, diplomas and certificates interferes with getting an

education. Teaching and certifying are very different functions; they serve different purposes.

Pursuing an education is more than, perhaps even entirely different from, pursuing a diploma or

degree; in fact, awarding degrees and diplomas, in my view, is not even an educational function.

Education should be seamless and lifelong; awarding diplomas and degrees Balkanizes the field

and the function. In the same way, good health is not measured by the number of trips to the doctor

or hospital, the list of medicines prescribed or to an exercise regime, a good education is not

described by courses taken, years studied or certificates awarded. These activities may contribute

to one's education but are not a measure of one’s education.


Degrees, diplomas and certificates are or represent qualifications; they provide recognition to the

recipient for activities in which they engaged; they are also a useful screening device for

employers. If we need certificates, let us set up one or more qualification authorities to award them

- not unlike the City and Guilds in England or the New Zealand Qualification Authority. Teaching

and sorting are two entirely separate functions and having teachers and institutions both teach and

sort constitutes a conflict of interest.


A qualification authority should do what its name implies, assess an individual's skills and issue an

appropriate qualification. Qualifications should begin with an assessment process and not be linked

in any way to years of schooling or courses taken. A person interested in obtaining a qualification,

would make application and undergo an assessment. If the applicant meets the standard, the

qualification is granted; if the applicant does not meet the standard, no qualification is awarded but

the student is given a detailed set of requirements to close the gap. That description could be taken

to an educational institution and form the basis of additional studies. Or the individual could study

on his own and return at a late date for a new assessment. The cost of examinations and awarding

degrees and diplomas would be met through a separate fee.


Learners should have more choice including more choice as to when, where and what they will

study. But the system also should be designed so that learners can choose their schools and even

their teachers. Taking advantage of choices may result in additional cost and inconvenience to a

learner or parent including the cost of transportation - but that is so in accessing many other

services. Obviously, choice will be greatest in larger centres. Excess demand for an individual

teacher would be dealt with in the same way as excess demand on a lawyer or dentist. This change

will demand considerable creativity among educational planners; however, consumers have choices

in other sectors of their life so why not in education. If we believe in choice, we will find a way to

allow it to happen


Paying people for what they know or how long they've known it is overdue for a change. We need

to replace the current system of teacher pay from one that emphasizes what the teacher knows to

one based on what a teacher does. Currently teacher pay is based on a grid that takes into account

preparation and years of experience. That model needs to be replaced with a system that takes into

account the work done, number of people served and complexity of task. Some teachers, but not

necessarily all, should be able to dramatically increase their earnings; the current system, I feel,

holds back deserving teachers. The top of the salary range needs to be moved up dramatically;

teachers should be able to earn as much as a lawyer, accountant, dentist or a medical doctor. The

current grid does not and will not allow this to happen.


No other professional is under the same kinds of strains and pressures as teachers; they tend to be

lone operators for long hours each day. It need not be that way. I recall visiting a school a few

years ago in which the school's ranking rose from second lowest in the system to second highest

during a two year period; officials attributed the change in ranking, not to new curriculum, not to

longer days, not to more in-service training for staff, but to a dramatic increase in student/adult

contact. What the school did was to replace 7 teachers with 14 teaching assistants. A review of

school staffing is certainly in order. But the system could also benefit from more highly qualified

specialists, in the area of diagnosis for example, to be available to teachers who deal with difficult

situations on a daily basis. The same specialists would also be available to students by referral.

This service could well be of greater benefit to learners and teachers than some of the services now

provided by Departments of Education.


When I make suggestions like these in casual conversations, I am usually met with "But wouldn't

that be difficult?" Probably. But the question should be "Would a learner benefit from increased

choice, published standards and more contact with adults in his/her learning? Having an education

advisor? If the answer is no, the ideas should be abandoned. If the answer is yes, we should seek

ways to make it happen.
