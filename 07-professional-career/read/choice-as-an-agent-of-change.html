Major essay (May 11, 2014) advocating choice as educational reform mechanism. Don argues individuals differ in experiences, interests, learning styles; choice-making is an important educational skill; individuals should take responsibility for choices; consumer choices drive service improvement. Contrasts limited educational choice with choice available in other sectors (church, professional services). References Bay County (Florida) School Authority 2005 open enrollment example. Proposes four choice dimensions: School choice (location), teacher choice (chemistry between teacher and student), scheduling choice (flexibility in time spent in school), and graduation date choice (based on competency not age). Argues choice removes excuses, increases accountability, and creates window of opportunity for system transformation. Notes Alberta's variety of school types (public, private, charter, religious, specialty arts/hockey) all open to residents. Emphasizes that most other services are similarly limited in small communities, so rural constraints shouldn't prevent choice. Well-reasoned argument with practical implementation suggestions.


CHOICE AS AN AGENT OF CHANGE



I’m writing this from Panama City Beach, Florida.  The topic arises from an item in this morning’s paper - a half-page advertisement by the Bay County School Authority, which read in part as follows:


Where do you want your child to attend school?

School Choice

Open enrolment will be held February 1-28,2005


The advertisement goes on to say that the following must complete a “School Choice Selection Card”:

Parents of children entering kindergarten, 6th and 9th grade

Parents of children wishing to change schools

Parents of children new to the area


I thought back to when our children were young and we were purchasing a home; our choices were limited in part because we had to send our children to the nearest school.  Where we lived and where our children went to school should be separate decisions.  I understand that learners today have some choice as to where they attend school and are not as restricted to the schools nearest where they live.  Canadian and US newspapers have been carrying articles on the results in the recent OECD international testing program, and the need to do better.   In the context of that debate, I suggest that we consider increased choice as an agent of change in the educational system.


Freedom to choose is an important right; at the highest level, we favour a system of government that provides a large degree of choice ay the governed as to who will govern – not a perfect system but a good system. 


Significant consumer choice, I believe, has a positive effect on suppliers of products and services – and education is a service.  In the retail trade, for example, choice increases the range of products and services available, introduces more competitive pricing, lowers costs and improves service.  Stop and think for a moment, we can choose our church, dentist, lawyer, mechanic, mayor, retail outlet and doctor but we have limited choice of schools our children attend and no choice in their teachers.  Some benefits would accrue, I suggest, if greater choice were available in the education sector.


In addition to the above, I believe that choice provides a measure of checks and balances on to any system and those checks and balances are the result of individual decisions by hundreds or thousands of consumers as compared to a system where improvement is legislated or imposed by those who govern.   


The advertisement raises in my mind the whole question of choice in education; if choice improves our society generally, why not use choice as a way of improving the educational system?  Why not give consumers of educational services an increased voice in the services they use.


Friends say it is impossible for a significant choice option; end of discussion as far as they are concerned.  I counter, “But is it desirable?  The discussion usually ends there.  They dismiss the idea out of hand.  

 



While there are many potential intervention points, let me suggest four.   


In view of my opening paragraph, choice of schools is a major candidate.  There already exists surplus physical capacity in the system so choice would not likely require the building of additional schools.

Choice of teachers is a natural for consideration.  Difficult if not impossible in small systems but certainly possible where there are a number of teachers teaching the same specialty.  For choice of teachers to have an impact, it seems to me that we would have to a few more teachers than the system requires and that the impact, if any, has the potential to be felt in the teacher’s earnings.  

Could we provide more choice to learners as to how much time they spend in school?  Again, my morning paper of a few days ago indicated that the option of more home study is being considered in the local school system.

Learners learn at their own rate; in effect, some students in a grade perform above grade level while others perform at or below grade level.  This means that many students will have completed grade, and graduation requirements, much earlier than the present system allows.  Why shouldn’t those students be allowed to graduate when they meet the requirements?  Would it be desirable to allow students to take exams when they are ready rather than when the teacher wants to give them?  Why not let a student challenge any exam whether or not they took the classes?  Can we develop a system that removes the emphasis on time and allows any learner to challenge for credit in any subject and at any level?  Of course we can, if we decide it is desirable.  

While schools offer a wide range of subjects and levels, choice is restricted by the entrance requirements of colleges and universities.  My reading reveals little if any, relationship between what is studied in high school and success in college or university.  If this is the case, here is another opportunity for choice?


I am a believer in giving learners more opportunities to make choices in their education.  Not only should choice be available, it should be promoted.  Having said this, I realize that in small communities will have more limited choices than larger communities.  But most other services are limited as well.  That is the nature of small communities.  In a community with several schools, however, choice should be taken for granted.  


Increasing choice in education, I suggest, would remove some excuses by learners for not attending or not progressing,  (“She doesn’t get along with the teacher.”), would it increase accountability of the one who chooses – the learner.  In some small way, making one choice also increases one’s ability to make other choices; it would also increase the responsibility for outcomes arising from a choice.   


The education system was never in a better position to introduce choice; let us not ignore the window of opportunity.
