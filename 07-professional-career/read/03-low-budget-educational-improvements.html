Low budget educational improvements


Short list of low-budget educational improvements


  1. Develop a philosophy of education


Although much has been written about education, Prince Edward Island does not have a current and comprehensive statement of philosophy, goals and direction. The current Philosophy of Education, found on the DEECD website was approved in 1990(we do not know when it was written) and is limited to the education of children as provided in schools – yet we all know that education is much more than schooling and that adults also need education and access to education services. There is little in the way of part time, night class and continuing education classes, information and educational counselling for students and parents, education providers operate in silos, on As a result various segments exist in operational silos with no evidence of a comprehensive plan to serve all Islanders.


  1. Reinstate exemptions


Organized education lacks incentives to improve educational products and services. Where awards exist, they focus on individual student achievement rather than improving the education process, and it is the latter issue that is most pressing at this time. At a minimum we need a better balance, a focus on improving the system for all versus rewarding the few. Exemptions, one low-cost incentive available at Colonel Grey High School, was cancelled in 2012 with a vague reference, at least in the press, to some new scheme under development; Islanders are still waiting. As a minimum, exemptions should be reinstated then expanded at least to the extent available in other Provinces.



  1. Encourage self-study and tutoring

Most public education is focused on instruction; virtually absent is support for self-study, practice, self- evaluation and tutoring, clinics, testing centres and study areas. If we want life long learners, we need to encourage it



  1. Re-shape the role of school principals


instructional leader, administrator, community liaison



  1. Make basic skills a priority

We all have statistics that we pull out in support of our concerns for the level of literacy among Islanders of all ages. There is also evidence that strong basic skills are the key element of a good education. If it were a health problem, our Chief Medical Officer would be front and centre in solving the problem. Someone, presumably the Minister, should declare it a basic skills a priority and let our educators set about solving it.
