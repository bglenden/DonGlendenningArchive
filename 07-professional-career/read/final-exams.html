Published as "MY 200 WORDS" commentary, Glendenning questions the term "final exams," suggesting it contradicts lifelong learning ideology. He distinguishes between exams for learning (diagnostic, improving) and exams for qualification (certifying competence), arguing only the latter belong outside schools. Schools should assess ongoing learning; external qualification authorities should determine competence for employment or further study. He advocates making validation available to lifelong learners, so people acquiring skills outside school can have learning recognized. Final Exams


While browsing the morning paper (Guardian, January 25, 2010), my eye caught a reference to final exams; it seemed so ominous.  What, I wonder, makes exams in January, or any other time of the year, one’s final exams? Are they for students with a terminal illness?  Are these final in terms of individual students or final as far as the institution is concerned?  Then I wondered, if my doctor called my next exam my final exam, what would that mean?   Would that be even more ominous than a final exam for learning?


A final exam for students implies, at least for me, that students are not expected to learn anything further.  The term is at odds with the concept of life-long learning.  The day or week after exams are finished, a student may have a significant learning experience; maybe even while taking the exam.  The exams mentioned in my morning paper are more likely to be semester or year-end finals.  But they could also reflect the fact that the school will no longer offer that subject ad, therefore, the school will no longer assess student performance in it.


One’s record of learning in school plays a significant role, I believe, when seeking employment or acceptance to further study.  So where, in the scheme of things, can a student who has had significant learning experiences outside school have that experience validated, or measured and reported?  If we believe in lifelong learning and want to encourage it, and I think that most people do, we should make it easy for people who learn outside the school have that learning validated.  


Examinations are, and should be, an important tool in helping people learn.  The assessing of what has been learned for the purpose of  obtaining a qualification, or obtaining employment, is an entirely different matter, and has no place in schools.
