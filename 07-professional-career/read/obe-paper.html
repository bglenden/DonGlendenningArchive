A detailed analysis of Outcomes Based Education philosophy and implementation, contrasting traditional content-focused instruction with learner-centered, outcomes-driven approaches. The paper examines William Spady's OBE paradigm, including three premises (all students can learn, successful learning promotes more learning, schools control success conditions) and four power principles (clarity of focus, expanded support, high expectations, design-down approach). Don critically assesses both strengths (consistency, accountability, accessibility) and weaknesses (lack of standard models, grade-level confusion, teacher autonomy concerns) of OBE implementation. He reviews exit outcomes from Saranac Lake schools and Atlantic regional outcomes, ultimately recommending renewal of outcome identification processes and development of implementation handbooks—demonstrating his evidence-based, reform-minded approach.

Outcomes Based Education



Background



Traditionally schools focused on content to be learned, whether it be on the arts, science or business. Fields are divided into subjects, courses and units and lectures or lessons for the purpose of teaching and learning. Assignments and assessment followed suit. One’s education, at least the part experienced in school, became the study of a series of discrete experiences too seldom integrated into a coherent life related whole. Vocational educators, on the other hand, based their planning and teaching on an analysis of skills of an occupation , and more recently, skill programs focus on competrencies. Perhaps the best known approach, DACUM, was developed in Nova Scotia and has since spread world wide


OBE changes the focus of education from content to learners and organizes teaching and learning around outcomes and measures change based on performance regardless of when the learning took place. According to William Spady, a sociologist xxxat the University and one yime professor at the Ontario Institute for Studies in Education in Toronto, outcomes could be written with “traditional, transitional, or transformational goals in mind”. It appears that the leadership in OBE dealt with broad principles only; as a result there are no recognized standards for identifying out comes or implementing OBE. As a result, there are many versions of OBE, most focusing on outcomes only.


Spadyʼs “OBE Paradigm” (1994) is based on three premises and four principles.

The premises are:

1. All students can learn and succeed but not on the same day and not in

the same way;

2. Successful learning promotes even more successful learning; and

3. Schools control the conditions that affect directly affect successful

learning.

The four “power principles” are:

1. Clarity of focus on culminating outcomes of significance;

2. Expanded opportunity and support for learning success;

3. High expectations for all to succeed; and

4. Design down from your ultimate, culminating outcomes.


Definitions:

  • Outcome-based education is a student-centred, results- oriented, standards-referenced system premised on the belief that all individuals can learn.

  • Outcome - , an outcome is a culminating demonstration of learning.-Spady


Characteristics of OBE System:

  • A clear and unambiguous statement of outcomes

  • A list of outcomes in the hands of both teacher and learner

  • Program design starting with outcomes

  • High expectations for all students.

  • Help to be focused on each learner

  • Expectations for each learner is clearly identified;

  • Learner progress is based on demonstrated achievement

  • A variety of instructional strategies and assessment tools

  • Time and assistance adjusted to support individual learning style

  • Focus on output not input

  • Progress reported on each outcome

  • Outcomes developed in consultation with the community




Elements of OBE

  • Outcomes – brief statement about each

  • Assessment criteria

  • Levels

  • Benchmark

  • Reporting



Strengths:

  • consistency - in course offerings across the educational system

  • accountability - expectations for learning are clearly stated, and frequent assessment processes help both teacher and student identify progress toward meeting the outcomes

  • accessibility - clearly defined outcomes enable learners to demonstrate achievement of those outcomes through prior learning assessment processes regardless of where and when learned


Weaknesses:


Based on a review of the literature, discussion with people working in OBE systems and my own experience, the most common problems/frustrations are:

  • Lacks standard development and implementation models

  • There is a tendency to attempt to identify outcomes for each grade level of a subject. To write That is not the case; Instead, an outcome is likely to be valid for several years with are likely to be Confusion between outcomes and levels

  • Confidence in assigning levels of performance.

  • Some systems have morphed into curriculum based outcomes.

  • Teachers feel they no longer have control of what’s happening.

  • Difficult to implement OBE when school is operated in a subject centered time-based mode.

  • Except for the DACUM model, I was not able to locate a handbook for outcome developers.

  • Benchmarks

  • Confusion between outcomes and levels

  • Failed to clearly differentiate/illustrate levels

  • Identification of the outcome is one thing; identification of performance on that outcome; how you organize for learning and teaching is a third

Atlantic education Outcomes


For the past 20 years or more, the four Atlantic Provinces have cooperated in educational matters including the development of a common set of outcomes; while this makes sense, it also may make it more difficult for one province to make changes. Curriculum material for all PEI courses and grades are available on the website of the Department of Education and Early childhood Development. Log on to www.gov.pe.ca/eecd then follow the links through Curriculum/Administrators and Educators/ curriculum and support documents. In fact, I recommend that students make a copy of the outcomes for each of their subjects.


Comment:

The Province is to be commended for its commitment to and perseverance with Outcomes Based Education. Although outcomes are reviewed on a regular basis, it is time to update the model and implement other elements. Before doing so, however, the process of outcome identification should be renewed and a handbook prepared.
