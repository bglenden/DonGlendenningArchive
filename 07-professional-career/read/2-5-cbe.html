Some notes on Outcome Based Education

By

Don Glendenning


NOTE: Part of my learning style involves putting ideas and information on paper; these notes reflect my stage of knowledge and thinking about Outcomes Based Education.


Background

Outcomes based education (OBE), like competency based education (CBE) seemed to be a major focus of educational conferences during the latter part of the twentieth century. In my own mind, the two are soul mates with OBE being the broad descriptor when speaking of education and competencies being a description of the outcome at the interface with a learner. I must hasten to add, however, that this is a personal view rather than one shared broadly in the profession. It is not surprising, therefore, that even a quick scan of websites of Provincial Departments of Education, quickly reveals the extent to which educational planning has been influenced by the Outcomes Based Education movement, especially in the area of curriculum development.


Academic educators then, and mostly still today, focused on content, that is dividing subjects such as mathematics, literature, science and so forth into courses and lectures for the purpose of teaching and learning. At the time was that small cadre of educators who laboured in the field of vocational education; their instructional efforts always started with an occupational analysis and the adoption of CBE was a natural fit with their practice.


Promoters of OBE proposed to organize education, or perhaps more accurately that part of education that occurs in schools, around educational outcomes and to measure change based on performance regardless of when the learning took place. The proponent, and self-proclaimed expert, was William Spady, a sociologist xxxat the University and for a while, a professor at the Ontario Institute for Studies in Education in Toronto. According to Spady, outcomes could be written with “traditional, transitional, or transformational goals in mind”. For a variety of reasons, some of which I will deal with later, the proposed and actual introduction resulted in considerable turmoil led usually led by parents or teachers, or some community pressure group.

Guidelines for Writing Course Learning Outcomes

Learning Outcomes written at the course level should:

  • state clear expectations - learners know what they have to do to demonstrate that they have achieved the learning outcomes;

  • represent culminating performances of learning and achievement; ( meaning the highest stage of development, or exit, end performance)

  • describe performances that are significant, essential, and verifiable;
    (meaning that performances can be verified or observed in some way and that they represent more than one small aspect of behavior; this also means that the performance is considered to be essential for success in the course)

  • preferably state only ONE performance per outcome;

  • refer to learning that is transferable;
    (meaning that the learning can readily be transferred from a class to a work place environment, or from one workplace environment to another, etc.)

  • not dictate curriculum content;
    (meaning that there could be a number of different ways to achieve the outcome.)

  • reflect the overriding principles of equity and fairness and accommodate the needs of diverse learners.

  • represent the minimal acceptable level of performance that a student needs to demonstrate in order to be considered successful.

(Source: Guidelines to the Development of Standards of Achievement through Learning Outcomes, 1994. College Standards and Accreditation Committee)



Anatomy of Learning Outcomes

Learning Outcome statements may be broken down into three main components:

  • an action word that identifies the performance to be demonstrated;

  • a learning statement that specifies what learning will be demonstrated in the performance;

  • a broad statement of the criterion or standard for acceptable performance.
