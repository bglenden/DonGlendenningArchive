Draft newsletter (2008) presenting Glendenning's philosophy on personal giving plans. Advocates for intentional, strategic charitable giving rather than reactive responses to every solicitation. Shares personal anecdotes about different giving approaches and argues that donors should identify causes reflecting their values and capacity. Emphasizes targeting support toward projects creating systemic change rather than perpetual maintenance. This draft reveals his practical wisdom about charitable responsibility and individualized approach to community benefit.

Community Foundation News

No. 80 , 2008




Personal Giving Plans


There are many worthy causes but we cannot give to all


A Giving Plan is your personal statement of gifts you plan to make to others and reflects your interests, the needs of others including your community and your capacity to give. Such a plan should also include a capacity to respond to unanticipated needs, in case of an emergency. A giving plan should also factor in our donations in kind including the donation of time and talent


Each of us must decide on our own capacity but we probably should give until we think we have given a bit t much.


I know people who automatically give to everyone who comes to the door. Another keeps all requests until December 1 and then makes his giving decisions. Some do not give to telephone canvassers. A couple gives directly to individuals, usually young people, who have specific needs. Instead of giving give to someone on the street I tke them to a nearby restaurant and buy a sandwich or coffee. A summeriislander supports specific projects to0 help the needy, arrange for Pat and the elephant to transport someone in need.



I am increasingly of the view that we should target some of our giving to specific projects that supports change. Some examples follow: help your church bring a special speaker or special music, sponsor a newsletter, take your volunteers to lunch, plant a tree as a memorial, support a struggling artist,
