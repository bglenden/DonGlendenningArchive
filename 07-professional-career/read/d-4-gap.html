Open Education No. 68 (December 17, 2016), authored by Don Glendenning discussing responsibility allocation in education between learners, parents, teachers, and government. He argues learners are responsible for their own learning (with parental support), but government's prescriptive education model shifts responsibility from family to bureaucracy. Don critiques that education leadership has imposed their model on citizens while assuming responsibility for defining what should be learned and when. He notes the gap: unlike health or justice, limited independent guidance exists for parents and learners about next steps, resulting in young people entering work or post-secondary education unprepared. Don questions what education services Islanders need and what delivery methods would serve them best, challenging fundamental assumptions about education's organization and purpose.

Nothing is so unequal as the equal treatment of unequals – John Terry


RESPONSIBILITY


I had a couple of interesting conversations recently about who is responsible for what in education; let’s start with learners.


Since only learners can learn, it seems reasonable that learners are responsible for their own learning; presumably that translates to parents and guardians. Learners need help with their learning; parents need help with helping; help may be about how to learn or what to learn – or both.


Helping others learn is a complex, expensive and important matter; government encourages people to learn new skills. In fact, government not only provides encouragement but has developed a model for all to follow; the model involves what will be studied, where, and when, it provides he teacher o provide instruction and reuns a qualification system

boht a curriculum and buildings in which instruction is provided.furthermore Government also demands that all citizens, must participate in the model they offer -or pay their own way,



Teachers and other educators have been helping others learn for years; they can map out what should be learned, when and for how long well in advance of the learner’s arrival. Seems to me that the responsibility now shifts from parent to bureaucrat.


Some people pay for the help they need and sometimes taxpayers pay for that help; sometime taxpayers pay for help whether or not it is needed. Education leaders think they know the best kind of help for young people and convinced taxpayer not only to pay for it but to make taking instruction for a dozen or more years a requirement, again whether the learner needs it or not.


While it starts out that teachers helping parents, it soon changes, or so it appears, to parents helping teachers. Unlike health or justice, there’s very little independent help available to parents and learners as to what they should learn next. As a result, often young people end up in work or postsecondary studies without having the relevant basic skills.


Times are changing; what education services do Islanders need and what’s the best way o deliver them?
