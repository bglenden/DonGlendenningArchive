Open Education No. 80 (September 15, 2019), authored by Don Glendenning discussing curriculum reform movements in Canada and internationally. He notes Ontario's introduction of math teacher testing, Alberta's curriculum overhaul emphasizing essential skills, UK and Australia adding numeracy courses. Don identifies key questions: content expansion vs. fluency improvement; competency versus content base; community input on practical math needs; student practice application; support material format; teacher math application training; citizen numeracy requirements. He proposes overarching curriculum principles: separate teaching and grading; replace credentials with qualifications; base programs on needed/practiced skills; implement dynamic assessment; modernize report cards; improve accountability; introduce differentiated staffing; ensure professional autonomy.

ABOUT MATH

Educational philosophy and practice meet during curriculum development. Nevertheless, according to Wikipedia, “There is no generally agreed upon definition of curriculum.” - and therein lies a potential problem.


An item in this morning’s Globe and Mail (August 28, 2019) about Math Education in Ontario caught my attention; the Ontario Government intends to introduce a math test for new teachers. Ontario has also announced a new careers curriculum designed to help students transition into the workforce; a base of essential skills is a must.


Alberta is in the midst of a major overhaul of curriculum in the public education system. One of the planned outcomes is in improvement in essential skills; a high level committee has ben established to examine past practices and reset direction,


There is an interest in basic skills in PEI as well although no plan for improvement as been released.




The UK and Australia are adding numeracy courses to the high school level, I\m sure there are many others


Here are some of the key questions?

  1. Do we need more or different content or is a matter of greater fluency with what we now know?

  2. Should we switch to competencies as our base?

  3. Why don’t we ask the community what math skills they use /need for every day life?

  4. Do students get enough practise applying their math skills?

  5. Should we rethink the format of our learning support material?

  6. Should teachers have a course on “math applications”?

  7. How much math do citizens rally use in their every day lives?

Don Glendenning, Editor
