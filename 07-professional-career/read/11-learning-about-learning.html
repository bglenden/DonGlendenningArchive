Learning About Learning


In preparation for a teaching career, at age 17, I attended Teacher’s College in Fredericton, N.B.  Given the need for teachers, the Province then enrolled students twice a year for a six months training program.  While still only seventeen, I commenced teaching in a one-room rural school.  It was an experience I will never forget.  The students and I survived; whether they learned is another matter.

The years which followed were filled with teaching and summer school as I went on to complete the requirements for a teacher’s license.  During that period, also, my teaching career included experience at both the secondary and post-secondary levels.

I still recall, at least some of the information and instruction received in Teacher’s College about developing lesson plans, using audio visual aids, maintaining the school register, school law, psychology, teaching methods, etc.  But I do not recall much about the teaching-learning process.  This process has since become an interest of mine and is the subject of this column.

The heart of schooling is, or should be, the teaching-learning process.  We should spend much more time learning and thinking about it.  It provides a framework for the teaching we do; it provides a framework for our decisions as teachers.

In our study of the teaching learning process we need to consider the teacher, the learner and the process.  Our starting point should be the learner.  If we get the learner’s role and activities right, the rest will follow. 

Organizational theory suggests that every organization needs a mission statement and it should be published.  The process of developing a mission statement is important for an organization and is usually highly consultative.  Many organizations have such a statement prominently displayed throughout their premises.  Schools, colleges and universities should have them too.  So should training departments.

Even more important for training and education organizations is to have a published learning model.  This should focus on what the learner does.  What the teacher/trainer does is a separate matter.  Having a learning model in place does not guarantee anything except that someone in the organization has consciously thought about an appropriate learning model for your organization.

Does your school have published learning model?  If so, you are in the minority.  If not, why not?  I’ve visited many schools in my day but seldom have I been shown one.  Yet, in my view, it is one of the first things that should be established.  It should be part of both the program planning and teacher education process.

Your learning model may apply to a course or to an entire program and it should allow for both full-time and part-time learners.

If your school does not have a published learning model, reach for a piece of paper and let’s sketch one. DO IT NOW.  It only takes a few minutes to draft the first one; it will take a lifetime to perfect it.  I suggest that you draw the “ideal” model – the one towards which you strive rather than your existing one.

On the left hand side make a small box and insert the words "Student registers for class”.  On the right side of the paper, draw another box and print the words “Student exits class”.  Now plot the steps in between.  Include all the steps and show ones which are required as well as those which are optional.

Your list of steps will probably include such steps as student reviews course objectives, takes tests, prepares course papers, participates in class discussions, performs laboratory experiments, completes work placement, takes final examination, exits course without completion, receives course credit, etc.

Now put the steps in sequence.  Which steps must be taken first?  Which ones can be skipped?  Draw arrows showing the alternative paths between entering and leaving a course.

As you list the steps, ask yourself the following questions:

What steps does/should a student take?

In what sequence should they be?

How do I know?

Answer as thoughtfully and honestly as you can.  Answering the last question may take you back to your textbooks and to recent educational and training journals.  Your model will likely be revised as your knowledge and experience grows.  I change mine regularly.

Even though I no longer teach, I am still interested in the activities and the sequences of a learning model.  Perhaps my students would have fared better had I thought more about learning during my preparation for teaching.  For those who are still active in education and training, it is still not too late.
