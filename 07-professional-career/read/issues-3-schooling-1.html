This lengthy document explores systemic issues in schooling, treating issues as complex problems requiring deep analysis. Don distinguishes between issues, symptoms, and incidents, and provides methodology for issue analysis (who, what, when, where, why). He addresses five major issues: Focus on Knowledge (learner-centered versus subject-centered instruction), Professional Freedom (autonomy and accountability), Delivery Model (administrative versus educational functions), Assessment and Standards (independence from teaching), and Credentials and Qualifications. The paper advocates for learner-centered approaches, teacher autonomy in instructional decisions, separation of coaching from referee functions (grading/ranking), outcome-based education, and independent assessment bodies. Implementation strategies include public campaigns, school autonomy, community advisory boards, and learner-focused policies.

BACKGROUND

              

Issues are topics, problems or concerns of importance to groups of people; they are the big questions.    Issues are not simple problems for which definitive answers can easily be found.  On the contrary, they are complex and of significance. Once understood, the simpler questions can be dealt with and progress resumed.   It’s not always easy to describe an issue in precise terms; sometimes we confuse issues with symptoms and incidents.  An issue may be current, that is a problem of today; emerging and potential issues, on the other hand, focus on those threats and opportunities that are like to impact on life tomorrow.  But the field is messy.  Issues arise in a variety of ways but often become of concern when they impact on personal interests, activities or events, or are related in some way to concerns of friends or family.  When trying to sort through issues, it is often useful to begin one’s analysis with the basic questions of who, what, when, where and why.  It may also be useful to browse or skim broadly in the field, such as browsing the internet, to get a feel for the issues.  In the case of education, while the organization is mine

ISSUE NO. 1: FOCUS ON Knowledge 


Each learner comes to a learning situation, whether at home or in school, with a unique set of health conditions and also a unique pattern of skills, knowledge and attitudes; but they also have a unique set of interests, plans and ambitions; both what they’ve experienced and what they plan to do will have an impact on their learning.  Some may simply need information or advice, some may want instruction, feedback or resource material; all need encouragement.  Most if not all of the help needed is available within the community including the local school.  We simply have to find ways to identify and harness it for the benefit of learners.

All learners arrive at a new learning activity with previous experience; that’s one of the ways that learners differ.  They grew up in different homes, with different values, different culture, different relationships with other family members, different communities, their culture and even their access to learning opportunities; and the list goes on.  That’s the reality for all people.

But they also have different levels of wellness, physical, mental, emotional and social.  They have different interests, objectives, plans for what they want to do or be.

And they all need support of a kind and at a level that differs from fellow students. It differs at least in part because of their previous experience but also because of the lack or mix of support found elsewhere in their lives


  • Schools organize instruction around subjects, grades and time; they do so in spite of the fact Someone even went so far as to describe the role at the secondary level as that of an instructor rather than a teacher.  Teachers report that they have little time to focus on learners and their needs. Schools should focus, instead on learners and marshalling resources to help them learn.  One way, of course, is to organize instruction It is not always easy but schools must find ways to deliver support to students as, if and when they need it.  Schools can be organized to reflect learning with students being grouped to reflect the help they need or want rather than by age.  Testing should be reorganized to identify strengths and weaknesses and made available to that students may test out of sa module or course when ready, and move to the next level.  The use of technology such as on line courses may help.   Individualizing student assistance should be an objective but will not always be possible. On the other hand, grouping is likely within reach.
  • And then, of course, there’s the matter of finding additional time for students needing extra help.  Here’s where extended days, weeks or even the extended year comes.  I’ve often thought that it would be interesting to see the impact on learning of an extra 30, 40 50 days would make to a student’s performance.
  • Providing individual learner support should increase student engagement, something that also improves learning.

  • Make decisions as to how best serve each individual
  • Assign grades at their sole discretion

ISSUE 7: LIFE BASED OUTCOMES


Dr. William Spady, a sociologist and founder of the Network for Outcomes Based Schools, is widely credited with founding Outcomes Based Education (OBE).  


OBE is a student-centred, results-oriented, standards-referenced system of education premised on the belief that all individuals can learn.     According to Spady, outcomes could be written with “traditional, transitional, or transformational goals in mind”.  A scan of the literature suggests that most outcomes are used to meet traditional goals.  


An OBE system is learner centered in that:

  • what is to be learned is clearly identified; 
  • each learner’s progress is based on demonstrated achievement;
  • each learner's needs are accommodated through a variety of instructional strategies and assessment; and 
  • each learner is provided time and assistance to realize her or his potential. 


Outcome statements always include an action word that identifies the performance to be demonstrated; some OBE users consider assessment criteria part of the outcome while others treat assessment as a separate issue.


Outcomes for PEI school courses can be found on the PEI Department of Education website.  Parents may find it useful to review curriculum guides and outcomes for the courses being taken by their children; students may find a copy of the outcomes helpful in orienting them to their program and courses.

ISSUE  : FAMILY EDUCATOR


The process of getting an education is a complex one; it involves developing the knowledge, skills and attitudes that enable us to live a full and productive life; choices abound and each choice has its own consequences.    Families have a family doctor, perhaps a financial advisor, often an accountant, sometimes a lawyer and perhaps other advisers, but when it comes to education, a much more complex field, learners and families are left on their own.  


Jan Hodges (“Careers education needs to be an ongoing dialogue, in which information is discussed at every stage”, Manchester Guardian, Wednesday, May 29) identified the need to expand existing school-based counselling with “independent advice and guidance” about career education for 16-18 year-old youth; information would be directed not only to young people but also to their parents and teachers.   


The need for advice on educational matters, however, exists well beyond careers and school walls; people of all ages could benefit from information, counseling, direction, advice and sometimes just an attentive ear.  Guidance counselors work with students in school; educational advisors would be available to anyone but would mostly serve people not in school.
