ISSUES

Issues are topics, problems or concerns of importance to groups of people; they are the big questions. Issues are not simple problems for which definitive answers can easily be found. On the contrary, they are complex and of significance. Once understood, the simpler questions can be dealt with and progress resumed. It’s not always easy to describe an issue in precise terms; sometimes we confuse issues with symptoms and incidents. An issue may be current, that is a problem of today; emerging and potential issues, on the other hand, focus on those threats and opportunities that are like to impact on life tomorrow. But the field is messy.



ISSUE 1: FOCUS

Schools organize instruction around subjects, grades and time; they do so in spite of the fact that students progress at their own rate. Someone even went so far as to describe the rolE at the secondary level as that of an instructor rather than a teacher. Teachers report that they have little time to focus on learners and their needs. Schools should focus, instead on learners and marshalling resources to help them learn. One way, of course, is to organize instruction It is not always easy but schools must find ways to deliver support to students as, if and when they need it. Schools can be organized to reflect learning with students being grouped to reflect the help they need or want rather than by age. Testing should be reorganized to identify strengths and weaknesses and made available to that students may test out of sa module or course when ready, and move to the next level. The use of technology such as on line courses may help. Individualizing student assistance should be an objective but will not always be possible. On the other hand, grouping is likely within reach.

Colleagues And then, of course, there’s the matter of finding additional time for students needing extra help. Here’s where extended days, weeks or even the extended year comes. I’ve often thought that it would be interesting to see the impact on learning of an extra 30, 40 50 days would make to a student’s performance.

Providing individual learner support should increase student engagement, something that also improves learning.

ISSUE 2; PROFFESSIONAL ADVICE/REPORTING

Schools are teaching institutions; we place our children in their care for which we pay schools about $10,000 per student per year. Students, and parents, deserve factual information about their progress. Direct feedback to the learner and parents is crucial. For each subject, a student should be told how well they are doing. In reading, for example, a student deserves to know the vital signs of a good reader, comprehension, vocabulary, flow, and the steps that the school is taking or recommending to improve each subject. Based on the results, teachers should have a better understanding of what the student has learned and what is required if he/she is going to meet their learning objectives. Results may also show that the teacher’s strategy needs changing or more time may be needed.

It would appear, however, that much of the assessment process results in the ranking of students, to determine who is ahead and who is behind on the curve. This approach is useful for picking winners but does little to help learners improve. It is likely that the relative ranking of a student on a subject changes little from semester to semester so why continue to emphasize it. Furthermore, at least in my view, establish a ranking is neither an educational function or school responsibility. While it may tell a student’s standing in relation to others, it tells nothing about corrective action. Furthermore, asking teachers teach and sort places a teacher in a conflict of interest.

It is not by accident that sports include both coaches and referees, lawyers present their cases but judges decide, we have accountants and auditors, and we read consumers reports before making decisions on major purchases. There are many good reasons for sorting students but, when that happens, students deserve independent assessors.
