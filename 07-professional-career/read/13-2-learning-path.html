Learning path

No. B-2 About 13-2 December 23, 2014


To learn is to change behaviour to engage in an experience that has a formative effect on the way we think, feel and act. A person who has learned has new knowledge a new skill or a different attitude. To help us better understand the learning process, Education 20/20 invited a panel of young people to identify how they went about learning and what kinds of support were most helpful. Key words were recorded on a flip chart; the list was long, however, on further discussion the panel members settled on the following steps they took the learning path:



Help for learning

  • Gather Information Seek counselling

  • Attend lecture Use resource material

  • Reflect on experience Use resource material

  • Reflect on experience Observe demonstration

  • Take diagnosis Attempt task

  • Obtain feedback Undergo assessment

  • Take instruction Motivate self

  • Self evaluate Observe others

  • Coach others Practice







Students were also asked the order in which those steps occurred; not surprisingly, individual paths differed. As an example one student said his first step would be to Google the topic; another said if it involved a new skill, he would probably attempt it first, while others liked the traditional lecture as a place to start. Since panel members were in school or recent graduates, much of the discussion revolved around gaining new knowledge rather than skills or attitudes.


It also became evident that different purposes for learning did not substantially change the nature of the path followed although they may well make the path longer.


A skeleton learning path appears on the other side of this sheet reflects an attempt to chart the support that students found useful and to do so in a manner that would respect all learning styles. It’s our first attempt. We think that it is a good start, but it is only a start. We would welcome your input into making it better.
