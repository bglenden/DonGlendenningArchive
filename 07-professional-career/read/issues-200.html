TYPICAL ISSUES IN PUBLIC EDUCATI0N

___________________________________________________________________________

It is widely recognized that education is a key factor in determining a citizen’s quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of citizens determines, to a significant degree, the qualities of the communities in which we live. Education is complex, made so by the fact that each person is unique in so many ways - unique in basic physical, mental and emotional makeup, unique in the range and nature of previous experience, unique in perceived futures, and in terms of learning pace, style and needs.


Given these complexities, some system-wide issues are not surprising:

  1. Terminology – a common terminology improves communication throughout the organization. Nowhere is the error more prominent than the failure to differentiate between education and schooling.

  2. Purpose – purpose provides the fundamental reason for establishing an organization, a reason for which something is done or created. Preparation for citizenship, personal development, self- management and preparation for work are common purposes.

  3. Services –alhough education ervices are seldom identified, they are usually thought to include: information, counselling, instruction, learning products and technology, quality assurance. governance, funding and human resources. Learners may need any or all services at any point in their education journey.

  4. Structure –The structure of an organization is based on the belief that “form follows function”. Typically government departments structure reflects branches such as life roles or services rendered; PEI “education” seem to focus instead, on clients served.

  5. Autonomy - The OECD tells us that PISA results are better for students in schools with autonomy for their day-to-day operation.

  6. Accountabilities -Mathew Bedard of the Montreal Economic Institute points out that “autonomy and accountability jointly applied” would cost practically nothing and allow education to better adapt to the diversity of student needs and aptitudes. .

  7. Collaboration – education thrives on collaboration and communication; Paul Bennet speaks of the “muffled voices of teachers” who work alone in classrooms, in fact the profession itself is often said to operate as a silo in the community.

  8. Content is the cornerstone of the education process and must align with purpose and beliefs; outcomes provide a degree of specificity; competencies are even better.

  9. Transparency - calls for open communication about education and the education process

  10. A role of Department -to encourage, support and regulate education services
