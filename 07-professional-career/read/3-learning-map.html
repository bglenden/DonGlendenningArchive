A learning map

I cannot remember when I saw my first roadmap but it seems to me that it came out of the glove compartment every time we drove beyond Chatham, Nw runswick. Our map  revealed the road from home to Chatham, across the Miramichi on the ferry (Frances Ullock) and north on Route 8 to Bathurst – my father’s place of birth.

My father seldom looked at  the map; he had travelled this road many times before, but it was a point of conversation for other family members.  I still remember conversations about the Little Eskadellic, Big Eskadellic and Tabisintac Rivers and hills as we travelled northward. I sometimes wish my school had provided equivalent maps of the path through British History and the intricacies of algebra. My book case today contains a variety of maps, traditional and learning. They provide a record of where each student had been and some places yet unexplored.

In i970 Holland College introduced leaning maps (called DACUM Charts) to incoming students. They provide an overview of learning possibilities in the emerging learmning learning journey

learning maps provide learners with  the many individual components of their emerging education journey. They also were a place to review and record progress.
