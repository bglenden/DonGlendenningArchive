DACUM AT HOLLAND COLLEGE



Holland College, a College of Applied Arts and Technology, was established in Prince Edward Island in 1969 with a mandate to serve all Islanders except those seeking university credit.  Donald Glendenning was contracted as president Designate and his position confirmed when the College Act was proclaimed.  Glendenning was committed to implementing a flexible, relevant and student-centred approach to education.  The College opened its doors to students on September 2, 1969 and operated during the first year in a traditional mode.  


During the first year, Glendenning and  staff engaged in regular and disciplined discussions about an instructional model for the new institution.  By early 1970 the following set of principles not only emerged but had significant if not full support: (Figure 8).

































Figure 8:  Principles Adopted by Holland College


1. Skills required by an occupational field should be identified by persons in the field.


2. Students are responsible for their own progress.


3. Learning should be stressed instead of teaching.


4. The role of the instructor shall be to assess, diagnose, prescribe, tutor, but not to be the sole conveyor of information.


5. Programs shall be individualized to the extent that resources allow.


6. The College should operate on a year-round  basis.


7. We should schedule resources rooms, materials and instructors instead of students.


8. Evaluation should be as realistic and meaningful as possible in keeping with evaluation in the work environment.


9. We should maximize student-staff  interaction.


10. Students should be able to enter and exit from a program at any time.


11. Advanced credit should be given for proven performance.


12. Students shall be able to continue their learning in a systematic way even after leaving Holland College.


13. Ratings are based on performance, not attendance.


14. Instructors are accountable for student progress.




At the same time, the College was searching for a model of curriculum development, program delivery and student management that would support the above beliefs.  The search included a regular reading and monitoring of the literature as well as intelligence  gathered through the education system.  Search results included:








A visit by Lawrence Coffin and Malcolm Lodge, two instructors at the College, to the Canadian Armed Forces school in Kingston, Ontario.  Project POET (Performance Oriented Electronics Training) was designed to allow students to progress at their own pace in each unit of the program; the weakness was that students who finished a unit had to wait until others finished before they could proceed to the next unit.

Lawrence Coffin visited Montmorency College in Quebec where a cadre of curriculum developers, over a three year period, were developing instructional modules for a new college to open three years later.  The cost was prohibitive for Holland College.

Glendenning visited the Technical Education Research Centre in Cambridge, Massachusetts to view first hand their competency based work in electromechanical technology.  A team of curriculum developers followed electromechanical technicians with a video camera to document their every action.  The findings were incorporated into three separate documents one for skills required for the entry level, intermediate level and advanced levels.  The cost made this approach prohibitive to Holland College and the format of three separate documents interfered with the natural flow of learning – the spiral curriculum.

During the summer or  fall of 1969, Howard Clement, Technical Support Centre, Canada NewStart Corporations, while on a visit to the Prince Edward Island NewStart Corporation in Montague, PEI, provided Glendenning with some DACUM materials including a copy of a cooking curriculum analysis.  

In late fall of 1969 or early in 1970 Glendenning, Lawrence Coffin, Henry Purdy? and Archie MacFadyen also of Holland College, visited Nova Scotia NewStart in Yarmouth to see firsthand the DACUM model in operation.  



Based upon the set of principles identified at the beginning of this chapter, the College soon concluded that the model developed by Nova Scotia NewStart Corporation came closest to the model that the College was seeking.  Adams was invited tto Holland College in March, 1970 to lead the first analysis, that of electronics.  Analyses quickly followed for Secretarial Arts and Commercial Design.  On September 2, 1970, all new and returning students enrolled under DACUM model.  


1971 Article appeared in CV Journal about Holland College including its commitment to DACUM model.


1971 Holland College staff acted as resource people and presenters at meetings, workshops and conferences in other provinces. 


1973 Visit by staff from the Ohio Centre for Research in Vocational Education.


1974 DACUM introduced to staff of the Ohio Centre for Research in Vocational Education by Lawrence Coffin from Holland College.


1976 Holland College assumed responsibility for all trade, occupational, apprenticeship and adult education on PEI and began converting trade and occupational programs to DACUM model.


1976 Holland College instituted a DACUM based training program for its instructional staff.


During the years which followed, Holland College staff were frequent speakers and DACUM facilitators for educational institutions and industry, including presentation at local, National and International conferences.  In addition to their activities in Canada the U.S.A., their travels took them to Swaziland, Nigeria, Pakistan, China, New Zealand, Malaysia, the Caribbean, the Philippines and Belize. 



The first DACUM chart developed at Holland College was for the Secretarial Arts Program in March 1970.  This was followed quickly by analyses of Electronics Technology, Commercial Design and Resources Planning.  From then on, all programs were developed and managed in a DACUM mode.  This event moved DACUM from the domain of curriculum developers and researchers to the reality of an operating educational institution.


Holland College, the first college to implement the DACUM model, still follows the conceptual model developed by Robert Adams. 


While Holland College adopted the DACUM model developed at Nova Scotia NewStart, the College, like so many other DACUM users, made some adoptions.  Briefly stated, they were: 


(1) For each skill identified on the analysis, Holland College prepared a learning guide.  The guide was intended as a road map for a student; learning guides did not include the program content.


(2) The rating scale was changed to a four level scale.


(3) General Areas of Competence (GAS) were moved from the left to the right side of the chart. 


Holland College, In Step with Holland College Charlottetown:  Holland College, 1972
