The January 20, 2014 eleventh issue, edited by Don Glendenning, examines PISA (Program for International Student Assessment) results and their implications. Don cautions that while PISA provides useful feedback and international comparison, test results should not drive all educational decisions. He highlights an OECD survey of 11 participating countries showing that schools with greater autonomy tend to perform better on PISA. Critically, Don notes that when schools account publicly for results through achievement data, greater autonomy correlates with better performance, but without accountability arrangements, autonomous schools can perform worse. This balanced perspective reflects Don's sophisticated understanding of educational governance. The newsletter reminds readers of the January 20 workshop on youth preparedness and directs readers to OECD resources. Don recommends the Vancouver Sun article on British Columbia school report card reforms.

PISA Again


PISA results are a bit like the Olympics; they are announced, generate a flurry of activity and publicity and then fade from view. But they are important, they provide useful feedback, they point to areas requiring attention, they allow us to compare ourselves with others, but they should not drive everything we do.


A couple of years ago the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development, OECD, the sponsor of PISA tests undertook a survey of 11 countries that participate in the PISA testing program. I am not sure of exactly all the survey included but I was particularly interested in the following findings, and I quote:


  • “In countries where schools have greater autonomy over what is taught and how students are assessed, students tend to perform better.

  • In countries where schools account for their results by posting achievement data publicly, schools that enjoy greater autonomy in resource allocation tend to show better student performance than those with less autonomy. In countries where there are no such accountability arrangements, schools with greater autonomy in resource allocation tend to perform worse.”


To read the full report, readers are encouraged to log on to the OECD website, http://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/education/pisa-in-focus_22260919 where you will find the above report on School Accountability and Autonomy (Report No. 9), but a number of other interesting reports as well
