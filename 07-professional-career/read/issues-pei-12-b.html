EDUCATION ISSUES

Working List

The literature speaks of the need for educational reform; my exploration reveals the following early issues:

  1. Purpose not clear - purpose sets the stage for what we do and how we organize to do it. The PEI education literature refers to helping people find “a place” in society; we need to better define that “place”. Life roles have been described as that of an individual, citizen, a producer, a consumer, family member and student. All roles can benefit from education but all may not need the same mix or time.

  2. Terminology not posted – The concept of “education and “school” are used interchangeably; the World Bank cautions countries about this practice.

  3. Functions/services not identified - Education is a service. Typical education services include: information, counselling, instruction, products and technology, quality assurance. governance, funding and human resources. Learners may need any or all services at any point in their education journey; they should b e clearly identified.

  4. Structure doesn’t match task -” Form follows function” is still a standard when structuring any venture; one would expect, therefore, that the basic education structure would reflect branches of education or services offered. Instead, the Department appears to be structured around levels of schooling and clients served.

  5. Schools need more autonomy - education is a service. Front line service providers (teachers) need both expertise and autonomy to respond to learner needs. The OECD tells us that PISA results are better for students in schools with autonomy for their day-to-day operation; the World Bank advises Ministries of Education to delegate downward.

  6. Accountabilities not clear - Mathew Bedard of the Montreal Economic Institute points out that “autonomy and accountability jointly applied” would cost practically nothing and allow education to better adapt to the diversity of student needs and aptitudes. Yet teachers and principals appear to have little freedom to do so.

  7. Collaboration - education thrives on communication and collaboration. Paul Bennet speaks of the “ muffled voices of teachers” who work alone in classrooms. In addition, the profession acts within a silo in the community.

  8. Department description does not reflect Department role. – “develop and deliver curriculum” fails to capture the significance of what educators do

  9. System focuses on schools rather than education

  10. Standards should be for posted for all to see.

These issues are not new; no doubt readers have others to propose. All should be considered. They need to be dealt with soon, and should be dealt with in a public manner.
