Don examines the credentialing system in Canada and who should award credentials. He questions whether displayed diplomas and degrees actually indicate competence to serve, noting his experience as an employer revealed transcripts more useful than degrees. Don critiques how institutions award credentials after completing pre-set programs based on criteria that may not reflect occupational standards, limiting student choice and resulting in graduates following similar instruction regardless of individual interests. He contrasts academic credentials with more specific certificates and calls for reconsidering what credentials are needed, who should award them, and on what basis. Don emphasizes that teachers teach while schools sort learners, and this sorting function profoundly impacts future life choices. He questions current approaches to credentialing in addressing employer-valued competencies like communication, mathematics, history/geography knowledge, and work habits.
