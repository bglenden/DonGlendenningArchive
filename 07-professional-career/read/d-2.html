Reading


Reading is often referred to as a key skill, a “golden skill”, a tool skill, in that, being able to read provides access to so much other learning. But what is reading? I’ve asked the question a number of times recently. There is often a pause before an answer; it is not a question that is asked very often. Some “elevator speech” answers appear below:

  • The act of turning writing into meaning.

  • The ability to use printed information as meaningful language (Source unknown).

  • A set of skills to enable readers to turn writing into meaning and achieve the goals of independence, comprehension and fluency (Internet)

From the perspective of the reader, differences in levels of performance in reading are of two kinds; the reasons for the differences will be dealt with in a later issue

  • Range of skills meaning that one reader can read can read a greater variety of material than the other; for example. One can read scientific articles and the other cannot.

  • Levels of performance meaning that of the skills both readers have, one can read at a higher level, greater vocabulary, more fluency, better comprehension and faster


Concern about reading is widespread. During the past few months I’ve talked about education with Islanders from Souris to Alberton and, invariably, they expressed concern about reading. And literacy is a frequent subject in our local media whether it be the Raise A Reader initiative, the work of the PEI literacy Alliance, initiatives by the Atlantic Ministers of Education, or the need for skilled workers. And informal reports suggest that reading levels are improving.


Reflecting on what I’ve hear and read in recent weeks, here are a few suggestions:

  • Early detection

  • Better definition of outcomes

  • Criterion referenced assessment

  • Include on report card

  • Professional support to early childhood

  • Increased engagement of learners


If our education system can only do one thing for our citizens, it should be to teach them to read
