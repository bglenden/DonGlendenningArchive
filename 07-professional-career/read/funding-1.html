Transitioning in mathematics

Fund-1

“A student’s ability to transition from secondary school to post-secondary education and the labour market is a key measure of the education system’s success for students” – CMEC, July 2016


Agree that:

  1. Differences in math levels between and among individuals may be attributed to:

  • Number of skills learned,

  • Nature of skills learned

  • Student performance reached on skills learned

  • Extent and manner in which skills are clustered.

  1. Instruction in the public school system is based on curriculum outcomes; universities and colleges increasingly are adopting educational outcomes and competencies as a basis for instructional programs; it would be advantageous for all levels of education on PEI to be using the same curriculum framework







The above statement by the Council of Ministers of Education Canada (CMEC) highlights a concern that is shared by many educators, employers, certification agencies and the community at large. The matter of transition in mathematics has been a topic of informal discussion with a number of Islanders over the past year with a view to identifying what, if anything, could/should be done to improve the transition process. The paragraphs that follow are an attempt to capture some of the views heard ‘round the table

Conclusions;

Based on the literature and the above noted discussions, the writer concludes that:

  1. Math instruction on PEI is fragmented in that it provided by a number of agencies

  2. Differences in math levels between and among individuals may be attributed to three factors: number of skills learned, level of skills learned and degree of clustering of skills learned.

  3. Instruction in the public school system is based on curriculum outcomes; universities and colleges increasingly are adopting educational outcomes and competencies as a basis for instructional programs; it would be advantageous for all levels of education on PEI to be using the same curriculum framework

  4. A potential for conflict arises when the body that instructs also “grades”

  5. Grading students is a regulatory function, not an instructional one

  6. The large number of teachers authorized to grade students signals the likelihood of inconsistencies across the system.

  7. Cooperation among providers is hampered by the lack of agreed- upon definitions of a number of key terms such as skills, competencies, education outcomes and curriculum outcome

  8. Broadly speaking, education lacks an overarching statement of philosophy and direction; the existing educational philosophy is nearly thirty years old and covers only public schools

  9. There is no formal mechanism for coordination across the spectrum of providers

  10. The lack of consistent assessment criteria across the system is confusing to learners.

  11. The way content is described differs from provider to provider.

  12. Public school mathematics curriculum is due for a “review and re-write” in 2019

  13. The group expressed an interest in working together on a pilot project


The literature provides mixed messages about the nature of competencies, outcomes and skills; it probably does not make any difference which term we use, however, my reading suggests that it would reduce the confusion if both public and postsecondary institutions defined them the same way and, at the student level, statements be as precise as possible in stating ”the knowledge, skills and abilities that a learner must acquire or improve during or by the end of a learning situation”.


Recommend:

  1. Standard descriptions/definitions for words such as skills, competencies and outcomes

  2. Identify a common list of math outcomes /competences as a basis for instruction/student learning

  3. Establishment of an independent body to determine official levels of achievement and a procedure for assessing levels achieved by individual students.
