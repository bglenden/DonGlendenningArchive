Don Glendenning discusses transition frameworks from secondary to post-secondary education and labour markets, outlining criteria for math competence and the need for common assessment standards. Reflects ongoing education reform work and stakeholder collaboration.

Souris Innovation Fund


“A student’s ability to transition from secondary school to post-secondary education and the labour market is a key measure of the education system’s success for students” – CMEC, July 2016


The above statement by the Council of Ministers of Education Canada (CMEC) highlights a concern that is shared by many educators, employers, certification agencies and the community at large. The matter of transition was the focus of a number of discussions by representatives of The Department of Education, Holland College, UPEI, employers and Education 20/20 Inc. While the purpose was an exchange of information and ideas, it appears that there is considerable agreement that transition may be improved through:

  • The following criteria differentiate math competence: (1) nature and range of skills that a person can demonstrate, (2) level of performance at which each skill can be demonstrated, (3) degree of clustering, and (4) relevance to the planned role

  • Outcomes and competencies are not age related

  • A person’s proficiency level may reflect age of learner.

  • Education outcomes should be stated in terms that apply directly to learners

  • A common set of criteria for measuring achievement would be useful

  • Consultation among math providers should be continued


Discussion continues about:

  • Basis for describing what has been learned - skills, competencies or outcomes

  • A common curriculum map or master list of math skills from which schools draw what they will teach

  • Common set of criteria for establishing math levels

  • A common statement as to what constitutes a level of proficiency

  • Role of independent assessment


Related information:

In recent weeks we’ve heard that the MPEHC is now asking for program competencies, colleges identify competencies for the programs they offer, competencies are widely used in the health field, Queens University recently announced that it is converting programs to a competency based model, most developed countries, (Canada and the USA excluded) have national qualification authorities as does the Province off Ontario, the Association of Private Schools and Colleges plans to issue reports based on competencies, Province of Alberta plans to revise its Math Curriculum. All this to say that I think that our discussions are timely.
