Hallmore Tractor

By Don Glendenning


  1. Harold Hall and Stanley Stavert, employees of Bruce Stewart manufacturing, founded Hall and Stavert in Charlottetown in the early 1930’s. Whereas Hall Manufacturing, established by Harold Hall’s father, was widely known for its threshers, fanners, and horsepowers, Hall and Stavert was known for its “Marine Hardware and Motor Boat Equipment. Their catalogue No. 21, undated, carries such items as hoists, stuffing boxes, steady bearings, pumps and fittings, and propellers. By 1940 they had branched into making farm tractors, Hallmore tractors – or to be more precise, conversion kits so that farmers could convert their old cars into farm tractors.


  1. Most farm tractors were purpose built, however, during the 1920-1930 period, a number of companies built conversion kits that allowed a farmer to convert his auto (whether a Model A, Chevrolet, Oakland or whatever) so that it could be used as a tractor. The conversion kit usually consisted of a sturdy rear axle with rear wheels having strong cleats/lugs and was bolted to the car front end just behind the transmission. It was sometime referred to as the poor man’s tractor. A farmer usually removed the back half of the body of his automobile, from the windshield back, detached the rear end and bolted on a tractor rear end, the conversion kit, and voila, he had a tractor. The car could be driven in high gear because the gearing found in the new rear end gave the tractor the required pulling power.


  1. Around 1940, Hall and Stavert produced a conversion tractor.



  1. Although aware of conversion tractors generally, I only became aware that such equipment was manufactured in PEI in 2004 or 2005 through a conversation with Jim Stavert, son of one of the founders of the company. Jim told me of a phone call from a Mr. Heim from Chicago enquiring about Hall and Stavert.


  1. During a conversation with Dr. Wayne Cameron in the fall of 2008 at coffee break at the Eco-Centre in Mount Stewart, I mentioned my involvement in searching for heritage farm equipment, especially equipment made in PEI. Wayne mentioned having been born in the Mount Stewart area, still owning some property there and, among other things, there were some old pieces of equipment that I should take a look at. We agreed to talk further.


  1. On a fine sunny day in April 2009, with Wayne as guide, we toured the Head of the Hillsborough and surrounding area. At Wayne’s farm in Cherry Hill, formerly owned by Bea and Billie Crawford’s family since the 1930’s, were two steel wheels which appear to match those from a Hallmore tractor; there were other pieces buried in the grass but we were not able to retrieve them for identification purposes at that time


  1. Bill Crawford was a locally but widely renowned aircraft mechanic from WW11 Lancaster days in Britain. After the war he rebuilt Warren’s sawmill and the associated dam before working with Maritime Central Airways in it’s earliest days when they were flying refugees from Europe and then developing the northern Dew-Line airstrips from scratch and then doing early Northern Ice-Patrols. He later worked with  Enamel Heating Products overhauling Sabre Jets in Charlottetown and then with several local aircraft service companies at Charlottetown Airport. When MCA was moved to form Eastern Provincial Airways in Nfld, he retired to his old family home farm and machine shop where he built another saw-mill, a wind-powered generator and numerous other “inventions”. The Hallmore wheels logically date from his pre or early wartime mechanical innovations and activities.


  1. The “find” on Wayne Cameron’s property consisted of two tractor wheels in excellent condition as described below:


Outside diameter including lugs 37”

Diameter excluding lugs 33"

Width 8"

Approx thickness of rim 5/16th
Spokes (8)- 11" x 2" x3/8th welded to  large flat disc of 1/4" steel
Inner disc -24&1/4" with a 53/4" center hole and 5 bolt holes of 1"
Lugs (16) - cast, 2" height tapered and incorporated stud bolted through the
rim in two rows of eight.


  1. I had with me General Catalogue No. 21 published by Hall and Stavert in Charlottetown the catalogue is undated but thought to have been published around 1940. Although mine is a copy of a copy, it clearly depicts a Hallmore “conversion tractor”.


  1. In May, while at his farm, Wayne searched further and found an axle (Ford) including front wheel bearings with a pattern of holes that matched perfectly the pattern and size of those in the wheels. Using an available assembly like this would have avoided the necessity of manufacturing the hubs, bearings and seals with their required accuracy, alignment and fit. The driving gear is missing; that is some sort of rear end with differential and ring or “bull gear” to supply power to the wheels. Wayne arranged to transport the three pieces to Orwell and donated them to the PEI Museum and Heritage Foundation.


  1. On Friday, June 5, Tom Hall and Jim Stavert, sons of the founders of Hall and Stavert, Tom LeClair, Linda Berko, Brent Partridge visited the Orwell Heritage site to view the pieces. To assist in the identification process, Jim Stavert had brought along a rendering of an early Hallmore conversion kit. While there were some slight differences between that depicted in the literature and the actual pieces we concluded that they were normal changes based on their experience with the product. It was our conclusion that the pieces found were genuine Hallmore tractor wheels but perhaps a later version.


  1. In February, 2009, I phoned the Agricultural Museum at the former   "experimental farm” in Ottawa asking if they had or knew of a Hallmore tractor.  On my return yesterday from warmer climes, there was a telephone message waiting from, and the name was not clear, Ron Klingender? (1-866-442-4416). In short, the museum does not have a Hallmore tractor nor had he heard of it.  Furthermore, he did a preliminary search among folks he knew and drew a blank.


  1. Further informal searches on PEI finds people who remember the Hallmore tractor but no actual artifacts.
