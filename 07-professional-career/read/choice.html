A brief but forceful argument for expanding learner choice in education. Glendenning observes we choose churches, dentists, lawyers, mechanics, and doctors, yet education provides minimal choice. He argues choice improves quality, introduces competitive pricing, lowers costs, and improves service across all sectors, so why not education? The document reflects his market-oriented perspective on educational improvement, suggesting that consumer power drives institutional accountability better than bureaucratic oversight.

COMPETITION



I write this while in the USA where the media reports and comments almost daily on school reform including the takeover by the State of  ”troubled”  schools,  majors and minors for high school students, settling the length of the school year, the distribution of new and experienced teachers among schools, high school graduation requirements, incorrect FCAT scores, and school vouchers.  Instead, I suggest that we think outside the box and try increasing learner choice.  


Significant consumer choice, I believe, has a positive effect.  Choice increases the range of products and services available, introduces more competitive pricing, lowers costs and improves service.  Stop and think for a moment, we can choose our church, dentist, lawyer, mechanic, mayor, retail outlet and doctor but what choices do we really have about the education of our children?  

  

If choice improves the quality of service in other sectors, why not put it in play in education?
