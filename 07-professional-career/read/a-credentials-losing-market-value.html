Analysis of shifting market dynamics for educational credentials. Don argues that degrees and diplomas—historically "blue chip stocks" of personal portfolios—are declining in value because they reflect readily available knowledge rather than demonstrated competence. Institutions have been protected monopolies with little incentive to innovate or compete. The emerging problem: credentials don't mirror community needs, are inflexible and front-loaded, expensive to produce, and haven't adapted to technology. Discusses alternative credentials ("knock-offs") and discerning consumers demanding better value. Written April 2014.
