For immediate release

Date: July 8, 2016


Separate testing service needed


Some employers are wondering if they should have job applicants take an achievement test before offering them a job; some employers already do. The reason is simple. Employers find that “high school graduation” is not an adequate measure of basic skills, especially such skills as measurements, writing work reports, making change, mental arithmetic, reading maps or giving directions. When asked what math skills should be taught, one employer replied “Teach them logic”. The skills people identified are also referred to as essential skills, employability skills or even the “skills of everyday living”.


Many employers ask for “high school graduation” in their advertisements, but that’s too large a “mesh” thinks Dr. Don Glendenning, President of Education 20/20 Inc. “there are too many variables in the way school grades are determined for that to work: confusion between school grades and achievement level, wording of outcome statements, social promotion, assessment process, rating scales and the nature of benchmarks, to name but a few.” Glendenning thinks that grading should be separated from schools, removed from teaching responsibilities and handled by an independent organization – “the way we do with apprenticeship.”


Glendenning came to the realization that testing and grading were separate functions about a year ago in conversation with a group of students about the kind of help they needed with their learning. “We plotted student comments in the form of a flow chart or path and the separation between teaching and grading became crystal clear.” A simple achievement test would likely give most employers all they need to know to make employment decisions. Most developed countries, other than Canada and the USA, have national qualification framework help solve these kind of issues.


In the meantime, Glendenning suggests that, if new employees are expected to write reports, employers could ask for a copy of their marks on writing and communications; that should tell more than high school graduation.
