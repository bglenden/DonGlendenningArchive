A CALL FOR CONCEPTUAL CLARITY -B

Doc 5

As a frequent participant in conversations about education, I am struck by the absence of definitions and boundaries; communication and understanding may suffer as a result.


Education is complex; information plays a key role. Learners have different interests, experiences, capabilities and needs; they also fill a variety of life roles. Those who teach, or other-wise serve learners, must be very clear in their communication. Conceptual clarity heightens our understanding of key words and concepts; lack of clarity is a significant issue for public education.

Conceptual clarity is a must to understand the people we serve, the services we provide, learner needs, product, processes and values especially when the subject carries layers of subjectivityClarity must be addressed directly so that the concept of education won’t become diluted and potentially ineffective. Lack of clarity may arise from an idea itself, from the person promoting the idea or from the person receiving the idea; Lack of clarity could also signal conflicting ideas and levels of discomfort or disagreement within the education family group itself and some say “clarity” is also related to self confidence. Conceptual clarify is also a must in planning and assessing performance. Education has a reputation for generating “educationese”; in fact, one can purchase an education “jargon generator”. The existence of a jargon generator is a signal of a potential Problem.


EXAMPLES

  • Department adopted “Education and Lifelong Learning” in title; are both terms needed?

  • Many references to “helping young in society”; doesn’t Department serve all -lslanders?

  • Philosophy paper refers to helping young people to “secure a place” in society; How do we design programs to prepare peole for “a place”?

  • Department refers to its goal as one of “developing and delivering programs” to young;” This doesn’t seem to capture the role as visualized by most Islanders

  • Most literature emanating from Department focuses on school matters; seldom deal with education issues

  • Recent report by World Bank cautioned developing countries for treating “education” and “going to school” as though they are one and the same. Aren’t we doing that also?


Purpose is key to planning education services and to measuring results; our neighbours say it thus:

  • Alberta is “preparing citizens for tomorrow”

  • New Brunswick is helping citizens gain “attributes needed to be a lifelong learner”

  • Anon “preparing for life, work and citizenship”.
