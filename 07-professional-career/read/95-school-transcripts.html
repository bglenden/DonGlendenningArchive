Don advocates for more comprehensive student transcripts that detail specific competencies achieved rather than just overall grades. Using Math 421A as an example, he proposes breaking down transcripts into component skills (sequences, polynomials, geometry, trigonometry, data management) and rating progress on individual outcomes. This reflects his broader commitment to outcome-based education and transparency in student achievement reporting.

Open Education

No. 95 August 20, 2020

“Kids who have less, need more”

SCHOOL TRANSCRIPTS

Seems to me that schools, colleges and universities owe their students a more complete record of their studies; perhaps parents and taxpayers should also be included. Such a report, if shared with a potential employer, could even go a long way toward landing a job.


I have before me as I write, a curriculum guide for Math 421A; content is listed under the following headings:

  • Sequence and Series

  • Polynomials

  • Relations and Functions

  • Coordinate Geometry

  • Trigonometry, and

  • Data Management


Why not show a grade for each? A report on numeracy might include a report on each of the following:

  • Numbers

  • Decimals

  • Money

  • Time

  • Measurement

  • Data and analysis, and

  • Chance


Better still, how about a rating on each outcome attempted? I am sure that there are other ways to break out subject areas to show just what has been learned.


Some schools are replacing grades

with personal profiles; seems to me that personal profiles will call for better tracking of individual progress. It is likely that a common set of outcomes or competencies could serve as a master list of math skills for all grades. With personal profiles, a student in Grade 9 or 10 could be given a math map on which he or she could plan learning activities and record progress for most. Why not, all math studies.


The writer is also of the view that students would benefit from a common rating scale (see Newsletter 90) used at all levels of a subject, preferably one that is also found in the community. A master list of skills and a common rating scale could dramatically improve communication throughout the education system.


As readers will recall, education in New Brunswick is undergoing overhaul. It will be interesting to see how they deal with reporting of student progress.


Don Glendenning, Editor
