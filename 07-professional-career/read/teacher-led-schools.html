Teacher-led Schools



Palmer Park Preparatory Academy is a school without a principal; this Detroit school is teacher led, and teachers are union members. And Palmer Park, serving students in k-8 grades, is not alone in the move to give teachers both increased freedom and accountability.


A variation of this idea has been around for a number of years but the practice seems to have found increased support with calls for improved accountability and standards. School authorities seems to


The above development is significant, I believe, for several reasons.

  • Teachers at Palmer Park are professionals; they are willing to make decisions about the education of students and be accountable for their actions. Palmer Park Preparatory Academy is a new school serving students up to the fourth grade

  • Their union is interested in education as well as improving the working conditions of their members

  • The school Board is confident in their roles and involved in education not just traditional governance. They realize that there is more than one delivery model and are willing to experiment





Other examples of teacher led schools include:

  • The Boston Teachers Union School opened in 2005 and, when fully operational will serve students in grades K through 8. The Boston Teachers Union operates the school under an agreement with the Boston School Committee.

  • In Minnesota, a teacher co-op called EdVisions has coordinated a network of charter schools since the 1990s

  • The Math and Science Leadership Academy (MSLA) in Denver, Colorado is “union designed and teacher led” that emphasizes team coverage, peer evaluation and individual attention. The school follows the Denver Curriculum, salary scale and budget.

  • Teacher cooperatives in Milwaukee contract with the the Milwaukee School Board district to run about a dozen schools.

Appear to be at least two management models

  • MSLA model with one or two managing teachers/partners

  • Law Firm model with a Manager reporting to a partner’s committee

Walt Gardner reports “That's because schools that are run by teachers report higher morale, less turnover and greater motivation to improve, according to Education Evolving, a St. Paul policy group specializing in the matter. The finding is not surprising, since teachers have a more direct professional stake in these schools. It's analogous to owning one's own business, rather than being merely an employee.”
