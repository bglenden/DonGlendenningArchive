Preparedness initiative


INTRODUCTION


Mission/purpose of organization: Education 20/20 Inc. is federally incorporated as an independent, broad based, national, not-for-profit education organization, located in Charlottetown, PEI and is governed by a volunteer Board of Directors. Our vision is a quality education for every Canadian and mission is to support and strengthen a culture of learning for all Canadians; Education 20/20 Inc. is unique among educational organizations in Canada in that it is unaligned with any educational institutions, grade level, subject field or cause. The organization operates with ten Directors, a network of some fifty people, and a mailing list of about 250; an application for charitable status has been filed with Canada Revenue Agency; in the meantime, The Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island accepts donations and pays invoices on our behalf. Attached please find a list of activities carried out to date, activities planned for the coming year. Although most Directors have participated in externally funded projects in their other roles, this is the first such project for Education 20/20 Inc. and certainly our first request for funding from the Windsor Foundation.


Proposal: to develop a map for learners showing basis skills required to enter further study or employment.


Issue being addressed

The proposal addresses one of the major issues of the day in education, namely the low level of basic education skills demonstrated by young people, including high school graduates. The attached one-pager describes how Education 20/20 arrived at this decision. See Appendix A for details


Characteristics of public education:

In putting forth this project, the proposer is aware of the following characteristics of the education enterprise, including:

  • Silo nature of schooling: The nature of the education enterprise reinforces silo practices, time based instructional, segmented instruction, age based grouping, Institutional boundaries, single teacher led classrooms, and laboratory based curriculum development, The most readily identifiable educational silos on PEI include the public school system, the university, private schools, colleges literacy training, continuing education, and the many “NGO” education and training projects. The scattering of educational responsibilities govern epartmntsleaves the Premier as the only person with authority or responsibility to deal with the issue in an integrated way.


  • Curriculum alignment should ensure that each element of education supports the program as a whole. Alignment starts with a clear mission statement, attainable and measurable goals, relevant curriculum, appropriate instructional methodology, unbiased assessment and accurate reporting.






  • Subject centered resources and instruction. Education is a very personal and individual developmental experience; school instruction, on the other hand, is a product delivery system with the product development by one professional and the delivery by another. There is little inclination or opportunity for customizing at the point where student and teacher meet or for needed attention to the development in the affective and psychomotor domINS.The literature , leaders and the community call for change but, at least to date, there is little evidence that the call is being heard.



  • Education standards exhibit the following shortfalls: criterion referenced standards; age based achievement levels of achievement and a conflict in roles between teaching and grading.

PROGRAM NARRATIVE

Relationship to Foundation Priorities

The Windsor Foundation has two major areas of interest

  • Disadvantaged or vulnerable youth: The participants are disadvantaged youth in that they lack certain basic skills , this proposal addresses the issue of basic skills, those skills needed to gain entry to the next phase of life and, without which many are branded as failures. We are aware that sometimes this may result from poor decisions by students themselves; on the other hand, we are also aware that the gap may from incorrect information, biased counselling, streaming, out dated course content, poor teaching, inflexible scheduling, lack of encouragement and mentoring, and the list goes on.

  • Higher education initiatives: Although not as well documented, we’ve also been made aware of the many young people now in college and university who struggle with their studies because of low level of basic skills; the writer actually visited make-up classes as part of the information gathering process. the duplication of services is a strain on taxpayer’s dollars.


Proposal:

To develop a one-page curriculum map of basic education skills which can serve as a master list of competencies. Teachers may use the list to identify what is to be learned, students to track what has been learned, curriculum developers as an analysis of the field, mployers to identify the expectations of a new employee, credentialing and qualifications bodies as a basis for their work. We anticipate a separate map nor each of the basic skill areas.


Timelines:


Benefits:

  • Brings clarification to the issue of basic skills

  • An aid to self-study

  • An aid to employers when hiring new employees

  • Improve communications between and among stakeholders

  • Demonstrate some components absent from the current educational process

Appendix A

Education 20/20 Inc.

Preparedness 2- Skills of Citizenship

Introduction:

In late 2013, The Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island (CFPEI) undertook a survey of over 200 Island youth to identify issues that directly affect them; the report, Taking the Pulse of Island Youth, available on the Foundation’s website at www.cfpei.ca, provides a snapshot of the findings. Bright spots include a positive sense of community, emerging entrepreneurial spirit, volunteerism and new postsecondary offerings.


The main concerns include a lack of jobs, surviving on low-paying jobs, educational deficiencies and drug and alcohol addictions. On the subject of education and learning, the study reports a high rate of completion of high school and postsecondary education, a high level of inclusivity and relatively affordable tuition costs; young people also expressed concerns about their readiness for the next stage of life including:

“preparedness gaps with regard to readiness for

post- secondary study and the workplace” – Vital Signs, CFPEI, 2013

Following the publication of the CFPEI Report, Education 20/20 organized five public discussions for the purpose of informing the community of the report and its findings, learning more about youth issues in our communities, exploring issues of education and learning, and to identify actions to be taken to close gaps. Discussions were lively and wide ranging, and carried on well after the formal session ended. The final report included a number of conclusions and recommendations of which the recommendation below triggered this proposal.

Further study be undertaken to clarify the gaps in preparation for further study and for work - (Preparedness, Education 20/20 Inc., Occasional Paper No. 1)

Education 20/20 has continued its exploration under the title, “citizenship skills” meaning those required of any citizens and considered to be a basic foundation for life changes that are likely to occur including skills needed for entrance into employment and expected for acceptance into postsecondary studies. As evidence I refer to PISA test results for Prince Edward Island; a 2013 survey of issues of Island youth conducted by the Community Foundation; a presentation to a Standing Committee of the PEI Legislative Assembly by the Charlottetown Area chamber of Commerce; How Canada Performs, a Provincial and Territorial Ranking, Education and Training; Education and Skills, and a series of workshops on Preparedness held throughout PEI by Education 20/20 Inc. The PEI Department of Education has not taken exception to the findings nor, to the best of our knowledge, assumed a leadership role that includes corrective action. Based on our consultations, a pilot in the area of math has the most likely chance of success.

Based on our explorations, it is the view of Education 20/20 that:

  • The math gap is real

  • Low basic skill levels on PEI are an issue

  • Low basic skill levels are dragging down the economy of PEI

  • Basic skill levels are not consistently defined

  • Skills thought to have been learned while in high school, are being taught again in Adult Basic Education program at Holland College.

  • PEI lacks a master list of recognized basic skills

  • PEI lacks a single body to give direction to its education efforts

  • Basic skills do not appear to be a priority


Proposal: That Education 20/20 Inc. take steps to the nature, extent and causes of the gap.
