DACUM: A CANADIAN ACHIEVEMENT


Draft

The decade of industrial expansion immediately after WW2 was made possible by the influx of skilled immigrants from Western Europe. By the late 1950’s, however, it became evident that Canada could no longer depend on this source of supply. Furthermore, large numbers of Canadians remained unemployed even though Canada suffered from a shortage of skilled workers. The Technical and Vocational Training Assistance Act (1960) provided massive funding to remedy the situation. During the 1960’s Canada and the Provinces mounted a technical and vocational training program to prepare Canadians to participate in the post war industrial expansion. Cost were shared by the federal and provincial governments and resulted in an initiative that was the envy of many countries. In the mid 1960’s, TVTA administrators joined with officials working on the issue of high unemployment and slow economic growth and pooled their efforts in support of a series of demonstration projects. These projects, called Canada NewStart Corporations, were funded by the Federal Government but given a large degree of independence as to the clientele and focus of their research.


The Nova Scotia NewStart Corporation was established in Yarmouth, Nova Scotia in 1967 with Tom Jones as Director and Robert Adams as Director of Occupational Training; the project focused on skill training for rural youth. Given Adams experience in occupational and functional analyses, it was not surprising that analysis was a cornerstone of his research. In Nova Scotia, Adams worked directly with local employers and unemployed youth to develop relevant training for people wanting to improve their lot in life.


BC Version


Each training program was preceded by an analysis of competencies practiced by high performers in the occupation. Adams convened a group of practitioners and, those who supervised them, with Adams, himself, facilitating the analysis process. The group engaged in a three-day brainstorming process during which individual competencies were printed on small cards and posted in rows on a large blank wall. When completed, competencies were transferred to one side of a large single sheet of paper. Called, DACUM (Developing a Curriculum) the chart itself was simply a map of competencies in an occupation or field sequenced from left to right roughly as they would be encountered in the occupation. Competencies were selected as appropriate for the purpose of curriculum development, student tracking, school transcripts, a basis for certification or a map of further training. The result was a single-sheet presentation of competencies which could be used by a variety of users. With DACUM chart in hand, Adams developed a competency based delivery model






Holland College, a College of Applied Arts and Technology in Prince Edward Island was established by an Act of the PEI Legislature on April 15, 1969; on May 1 the Board of Governors approved a paper outlining the nature of its offering and style of operation; the College opened its doors to students on September 2, 1969. Staff met weekly to and soon developed a set of operating principles for the new institution; next came a search for an operating model. Visits were made to Canadian Forces Base Kingston to view Performance Oriented Electronics Training (POET), the Technical Education Research Project in Cambridge, Mass (Biomedical Equipment Technician Program) and modular curriculum development activities at College Montmorencie, a new CEGEP located in Quebec City, all the while keeping an eye on innovations happening elsewhere. Exploration also included a visit to the Nova Scotia NewStart Corporation in Yarmouth, Nova Scotia to see DACUM in operation following which Adams was invited to Charlottetown on March 2-4, 1970 to lead the first analysis, that of Secretarial Arts.


The Holland College Diary carries the following item dated July 2, 1970:

“College adopts individualized learning program adapted from DACUM system as developed by Nova Scotia NewStart Inc. Program is to be based on performance objectives and will provide for: (1) recognition of previously acquired skills; (2) continuous intake; (3) individual rates of progress; (4) consultative evaluation; and (5) individual termination times. System is to be known as STEP (Self Training and Evaluation Process)”


In the mid 1970’s, Holland College became a test site for resource materials for a teacher education project under development by the Vocational Education Centre at Ohio State University. During discussions leading up to the contract, the Center Director, Robert E. Norton, visited Charlottetown. Norton was impressed with the DACUM mode, became an advocate and driving force in its spread, although a modified version, throughout the USA.


As to DACUM’s originator, Robert Edwin Adams was born in Swan Lake, Manitoba in 1932, where he also attended school. His father not being well, Bob took over the operation of the

the family farm at a very early age; he moved to Winnipeg where he studied and later taught at the Manitoba Institute of Technology. Following completion of Bachelor’s degree at Bradley University, Bob moved to Ottawa where he worked in the Training Branch of the Department of Labour as an occupational analyst. In 1967 he moved to Yarmouth where he was Director of Training at the Nova Scotia Newstart Corporation. With the close of the Newtart Corporation, Bob moved to Dartmouth Nova Scotia where he operated his own firm, Competency Systems, until his death in 2016.


Don Glendenning
