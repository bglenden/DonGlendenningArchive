A brief document on occupational standards focusing on the distinction between what someone can do versus how well they do it. Drawing on his federal labor department experience, Don notes that standards discussions historically focused on training inputs rather than performance outputs. He argues existing standards emphasize range of tasks but neglect performance levels, illustrated by the difference between apprentice and journeyperson performance on the same skill. Don advocates for identifying broad performance criteria (quality, speed, supervision required) and establishing employment-based levels that are recognized by practitioners, clearly stated, readily understood, and easily applied. He contends the challenge remains unmet of creating standards that balance comprehensiveness with practical applicability.
