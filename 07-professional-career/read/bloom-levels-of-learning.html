Don applies Bloom's Taxonomy to educational progression, advocating for mastery-based systems over time-based grading. The document explains six cognitive levels: Remember (lowest), Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, and Create (highest). Don argues this framework allows students to reach needed performance levels at individual paces, addressing his concern that "students learn at different rates" and that "how quickly one can perform is different from how long learning takes."

One of the areas on which I disagree is that of the school grading  system. Given thatstuents learn at different rates, it is highly unlikely that all will have reached the same lvel of performance in the same lwngth of time. I favoura  system that allows a student to attaing a needed level even if it takes a bit more time.  How quickly one can perform a task is one thing; how long it takes to learn it is quite another.


I favour a system that shows progression such as Bloom”s Taxanomy shown blow:


  • 5 Create - In the revised Bloom’s taxonomy, creating something 

original or substantially new is considered to be the highest level 

of thinking. Verbs such as ‘generate’, ‘plan’ or ‘produce’ tell learners

 that they are required to work at this level.  

  • 4 Evaluate - Learners working at this high level of thinking may 

be asked to ‘critique’ or ‘check’ materials.  

  • 3 Analyze  - At the analytical stage, learners are commonly 

asked to ‘differentiate’, ‘organize’ or ‘attribute’ facts, data or 

subject matter.  

  • 4 Apply - If learners are asked to ‘implement’ or ‘execute’ a task

 or action, they would likely be wo rking at this level of thinking.  

  • Remember - Action verbs such as ‘recognizing’, and ‘recalling’ tell 

     the learner that the learning is at the lowest level of thinking.  

  • Understand - Work at this level is likely to require actions such as ‘interpreting’, ‘exemplifying’, ‘classifying’, ‘summarizing’, ‘inferring’, ‘comparing’ and ‘explaining’.
