KEY ISSUES

___________________________________________________________________________

Key issues include:

  1. Terminology – a common terminology improves communication throughout the organization; the most obvious, according to the World Bank, in the practice of using the terms “education” and ‘going to school” interchangeably.

  2. Purpose – purpose sets the stage for what we do and how we organize to do it. The PEI education literature refers to helping people find “a place” in society’; we need to better define that “place”. People are said to fill six life roles, that of an individual, citizen, a producer, a consumer, family member and student. All roles can benefit from education but all may not need the same amount mix or time.

  3. services – although education is a service, services are seldom mentioned; sub-services include: information, counselling, instruction, products and technology, quality assurance. governance, funding and human resources. Learners may need any or all services at any point in their education journey.

  4. Structure -”Form follows function” is the standard when structuring any venture. Departments of Education, however, appear to structure around clients served.

  5. Autonomy - front line service providers (teachers) need both expertise and autonomy to respond to learner needs. The OECD tells us that PISA results are better for students in schools with autonomy for their day-to-day operation.

  6. Accountabilities - Mathew Bedard of the Montreal Economic Institute points out that “autonomy and accountability jointly applied” would cost practically nothing and allow education to better adapt to the diversity of student needs and aptitudes.

  7. Collaboration – the literature tells us that education thrives on collaboration and communication; Paul Bennet speaks of the “muffled voices of teachers” who work alone in classrooms, in fact the profession itself, sems to operate as a silo in the community.

  8. Education system – Although Provinces generally refer to their education systems, the writer’s review found mostly school systems.

  9. Fear factor – although the “fear factor” may be too strong a term, the writer has encountered a number of instances that support it’s existence.

  10. Information – system documation and research findings are important components of in-sevice education.

These issues are not new; no doubt readers have others to propose. All should be considered. They need to be dealt with soon, and should be dealt with in a public manner.

06/12/23
