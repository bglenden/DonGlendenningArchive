Correspondence from Don to Wally on constitutional bylaw reform for Glenway Estates, written from Florida. Discusses philosophy of consultative decision-making and references his work with Community Foundation of PEI, showing his principles of inclusive governance.

Date; March 19


To: Wally


From: Don


Subject: Glenway Bylaws


Greetings from Clearwater Beach; we’ve been here a couple of weeks and enjoying it.  Weather has been great.  Not doing much but enjoying it anyway.


We have been having much difficulty with our internet while in Florida; mostly a problem with the hotel internet service but, besides that, I am able to receive messages but not send them except via my web mail.  It is a nuisance to say the least.  We usually visit the library daily to read the papers and use the internet.  As a result, this note comes as an attachment rather than just an Email message.


I’ve finally gotten back to the subject of my earlier Email and I thank you for your comments.  In fact, I have immersed myself in reading the bylaws and recasting some key Articles consistent with your thoughts; I am satisfied that we can make it work as you propose but also think that expanding the number of Directors is also an alternative.  Let me recap.


The residents of Glenway brought into being an Association to protect its interests and carry on its business.  We did this through lengthy discussions; participation was both encouraged and allowed.  I recall at least some participants expressing an interest in retaining the model, a view that I certainly support.  


Since then, and on reflection, I am of the view that our current constitution and bylaws interfere with the consultative process rather than foster it; furthermore, I believe that a broader consultative process should be restored and reinforced.  I would like to see all/most business concluded directly by residents rather than delegated to some and ratified by all later.  The issue, therefore, really about the extent to which we want to open the decision-making process to all residents.  To do this, changes in, or interpretations of, the constitution and by laws will be required.  


As you know, I am active in the Community Foundation of Prince Edward Island (CFPEI.  Our constitution allows for members although we do not have any at the moment; the Association also has 12 Directors and the customary officers; the role of the Executive is to act if needed between meetings of the Directors.  For many years we operated only through meetings of all Directors.  In recent years, however, we have also been holding Executive meetings as well as committee meetings.  


Earlier this year, I sent a note to the President, who is also in Florida as I write, suggesting that we dispense with Executive meetings and revert to a model whereby all our business is conducted only by Directors.  I made this recommendation based on my perception that (1) too much Director time, much duplicative, is spent on administrative matters, (2) CFPEI has become less participative than formerly, (3) new Directors are not getting the orientation to and knowledge about CFPEI that they need, (4) Board has become fragmented.  How he President will react, I do not know but the recommendation reflects my views and, I believe, is consistent with what I believe about the operation of our Association.  CFPEI also has an annual and open annual meeting, which is usually very brief, where it deals with the kind of agenda as provided for in our bylaws.


Having said this, Wally, I certainly open to either alternative but believe that the simplest way to accommodate my concerns would be to encourage a larger Board.  


I trust that you and Val had a good winter; I realize that you may well be in Spain as I write; if so, enjoy yourselves and have a great family visit.


Regards


Don
