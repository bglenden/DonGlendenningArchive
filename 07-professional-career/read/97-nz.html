Don analyzes emerging approaches to post-secondary funding in Ontario and Australia, focusing on performance-based funding mechanisms and employment outcomes. He reports Ontario's shift toward accountability metrics including skills development, graduate employment earnings, experiential learning, graduation rates, and community/economic impact. He then details Australia's restructuring of university fees, reducing costs for teaching, nursing, clinical psychology, and languages while increasing fees for humanities and communications—a strategy explicitly designed to align incentives toward workforce needs. The piece illustrates Don's ongoing interest in international educational trends and alternative funding models.

PERFORMANCE BASED FUNDING

The Ontario Government is making significant changes in the way colleges and universities will be funded. A press release pointed out that “Our Government believes in making institutions accountable for student success … that’s why we are making sure Ontario’s publicly assisted postsecondary institutions have a clear mandate that is focused on the needs of students and equipping them to succeed in rewarding careers.” The criteria:

  • Skills and job outcomes

  • Graduate employment earnings

  • Experiential learning

  • Skills and competencies

  • Graduate employment rate in a related field

  • Institutional strength/focus

  • Graduation rate

  • Economic and community impact

  • Research funding and capacity for universities, and apprenticeship-related for colleges

  • Research funding from industry sources/funding from industry sources

  • Community/local impact of student population

  • Economic impact (institution-specific)



JOB READY GRADUATEs

The (Australia) Sydney Morning Herald of October 15, 2020 carried a major announcement about changes to university fees.

“Fees for humanities and communications subjects will rise by 113 percent. Law, commerce, economics, will increase by 28 percent. A full four-program in these disciplines will cost students about $58,000.

Degrees in teaching, nursing, clinical psychology, English and Languages will become up to 42 percent cheaper. For example, a three- year nursing degree will cost $11,850 down from $20, 412.


Agriculture and maths degrees will become around 59 percent cheaper.
Science, health, architecture, environmental science, IT and engineering degrees will be up to 18 percent cheaper. Fees for medicine, dental, and veterinary science degrees will remain largely stable.”



Don Glendenning, Editor
