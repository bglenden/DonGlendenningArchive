EXPECTATIONS OF HIGH SCHOOL GRADUATES


High school graduation is an important event in our lives.  We look forward with anticipation and backwards with considerable satisfaction and nostalgia.  Graduation marks a turning point in our lives.  For many, it means parting with long-time friends, entering college or university, moving from home or finding a job.  Graduation exercises and the related social activities also play a part in our nostalgia.  A high school diploma helps us make many of these transitions.


But what does, or should, a graduation diploma signify?  Or for that matter, a college or university diploma or degree?  A person with a diploma should be different that one without, or else why have diplomas.  The distinguishing characteristics between a person having a diploma ;and one who does not are not clear to me.  Should we be able, for example, to distinguish between a graduate and a non-graduate without seeing a transcript or diploma?


Employers like their new employees to be high school graduates.  Why?  What do they see in a graduate that they do not find in a person who has not graduated?  Or is being a graduate simply a handy screening tool?  If it is the latter, employers are missing out on a vast pool of potential employees.


A high school graduation diploma is granted on the basis of having completed a specified group of courses and some options for, I believe, a total of 18 credits.  The requirement also includes an acceptable grade.  Prizes and honors go to those who excel.


Current graduation requirements are quite rigidly adhered to and, I am told, although some choice is possible the pressure is on students to take as many of the “traditional” courses as possible I understand the reason for this since schools are judged by the number of students who go to college or university.  The rigid pattern of requirements deters students from broadening their education and exploring areas of interest.  The reason for not encouraging choice, I am told, is that it may affect a student’s chances of acceptance at a university.


I am not aware of any evidence to support one pattern of courses over another.  About ten years ago, I conducted a survey to try to locate evidence in support of the current pattern of courses required for graduation.  I wrote to every Deputy Minister of Education, college and university presidents in Atlantic Canada and a large number of school superintendents.  I asked them for the evidence to support the graduation requirements.  I received many responses but only one provided information which was relevant to the question.


I understand, on the other hand, that grades earned in high school appear to be a predictor of success in university.  This is not surprising since secondary schools put great effort into preparing students for university and the nature of universities is an extension of that in a secondary school.


Changes are in the offing which, I believe, will more adequately describe the achievement of high school graduates.  This is a result of school focusing more clearly on graduation outcomes and designing programs to help students achieve them.  The four Atlantic Provinces have agreed on the following Essential Graduation Learning’s:

GRADUATES WILL BE ABLE TO:


Respond with critical awareness to various forms of the arts and be able to express themselves through the arts.

Assess social, cultural, economic and environmental interdependence in a local and global context.

Use the listening, viewing, speaking, reading and writing modes of language(s) and mathematical and scientific concepts and symbols to think, learn and communicate effectively.

Continue to learn and to pursue an active, healthy lifestyle.

Use the strategies and processes needed to solve a wide variety of problems, including those requiring language, and mathematical and scientific concepts.

Use a variety of technologies, demonstrate an understanding of technological applications and apply appropriate technologies to solving problems.


Schools are redesigning their programs to help students to meet these outcomes.  It is an excellent start but more needs to be done.  First of all, the outcomes need to be expressed in the language of students and the public.  They now reflect the language of educators.


Next, a copy of these expectations should be placed in the hands of every teacher, parent and student so that all will know what is expected in order to graduate.  Then, we need to reconsider how we rate achievement on each outcome.  And, finally, these expectations should form the basis of school transcripts.
