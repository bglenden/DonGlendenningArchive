The literature speaks of the need for educational reform; the first steps, I suggest, should be structural. My exploration reveals the ollowing:

  1. Purpose not clear- purpose sets the stage for what we do and how we organize to do it. The PEI education literature refers to helping people find a place in society; we need to better define that “place”. Life roles have been described as that of an individual, citizen, a producer, a consumer, family member and student. All roles can benefit from education but all may not need the - same mix at the same time. That’s part of what makes service complicated.

  2. Terminology seldom defined – The concept of “education and “school” are used interchangeably; World Bank cautions countries about this practice.

  3. Education services not identified - Education is a service. Typical education services include: information, counselling, instruction, products and technology, quality assurance. governance, funding and human resources; Learners may need any or all services at any point in their education journey and services should be front and center in our offerings.

  4. Structure doesn’t match task -” Form follows function” is still the standard when structuring any venture; one would expect, therefore, that the basic education structure would reflect services offered. Instead, the Department appears to be structured around clients served.

  5. Schools lack autonomy - as mentioned earlier, education is a service. Front line service providers (teachers) need both expertise and autonomy to respond to learner needs. The OECD tells us that PISA results are better for students in schools with autonomy for their day-to-day operation; the World Bank advises Ministries of Education to delegate. PEI should also delegate.

  6. Accountabilities not clear - Mathew Bedard of the Montreal Economic Institute points out that “autonomy and accountability jointly applied” would cost practically nothing and allow education to better adapt to the diversity of student needs and aptitudes. Yet teachers and principals in PEI appear to have little freedom to adjust services to suit individual learner needs.

  7. Collaboration – the literature tells us that education thrives on collaboration yet. Paul Bennet speaks of the muffled voices of teachers. They work alone in classrooms, in fact the profession itself, operates as a silo.

  8. Curriculum outcomes unrelated to life roles.

  9. Coordination of services lacking at the community level - Souris experience; community interests and needs not factored in

  10. Department role understated – “develop and deliver curriculum” fails to capture the significance of what educators do

  11. Future of individuals and community are largely determined by level of education, yet educators seldom if ever urge citizens to continue their studies


These issues are not new; no doubt readers have others to propose. All should be considered. They need to be dealt with soon, and should be dealt with in a public manner.
