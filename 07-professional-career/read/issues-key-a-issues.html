The literature speaks of the need for educational reform; my exploration reveals the following issues:

  1. Purpose not clear- purpose sets the stage for what we do and how we organize to do it. The PEI education literature refers to helping people find “a place” in society; we need to better define that “place”. Life roles have been described as that of an individual, citizen, a producer, a consumer, family member and student. All roles can benefit from education but all may not need the - same mix at the same time.

  2. Terminology seldom defined – The concept of “education and “school” are used interchangeably; the World Bank cautions countries about this practice.

  3. Education functions/services not identified - Education is a service. Typical education services include: information, counselling, instruction, products and technology, quality assurance. governance, funding and human resources. Learners may need any or all services at any point in their education journey.

  4. Structure doesn’t match task -” Form follows function” is still the standard when structuring any venture; one would expect, therefore, that the basic education structure would reflect services offered. Instead, the Department appears to be structured around clients served.

  5. Autonomy - education is a service. Front line service providers (teachers) need both expertise and autonomy to respond to learner needs. The OECD tells us that PISA results are better for students in schools with autonomy for their day-to-day operation; the World Bank advises Ministries of Education to delegate.

  6. Accountabilities not spelled out - Mathew Bedard of the Montreal Economic Institute points out that “autonomy and accountability jointly applied” would cost practically nothing and allow education to better adapt to the diversity of student needs and aptitudes. Yet teachers and principals appear to have little freedom to do so.er needs.

  7. Collaboration – the literature tells us that education thrives on collaboration and communication; Paul Bennet speaks of the muffled voices of teachers who work alone in classrooms, in fact the profession itself, operates as a silo in the community.

  8. Department role understated – “develop and deliver curriculum” fails to capture the significance of what educators do

  9. School system not an education system

  10. Tracking student growth

  11. Climate: collective mood, morale,

  12. Culture: the way we d things around here

  13. Ranking

These issues are not new; no doubt readers have others to propose. All should be considered. They need to be dealt with soon, and should be dealt with in a public manner.
