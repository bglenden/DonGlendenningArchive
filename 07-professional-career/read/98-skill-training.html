Open Education

No. 98 August 20, 2020

What knowledge is of most worth? – Spencer 1860


SKILL DEVELOPMENT

The future of Islanders and the Island community will depend in large measure on the nature and extent of education gained by our citizens – young and old. Educators, therefore, should be front and centre working with community leaders, providing services and shaping education outcomes.


Islanders have had access to quality skill training for the past 100 years, first under the Agricultural Aid Act of 1912, and under replacement legislation regularly thereafter. Quality was assured through stipulations on standards, staffing, curriculum, inspections, national conferences, newsletters and visits.


Contacts tell me that the leadership role no longer applies; agreements have been concluded with each province under which Provinces are reimbursed for agreed upon expenses. As a result, many technical courses are technical rather than skill development.


For the past fifty years, PVI and PCVHS have supplied work entry training for the local market, people who often went on to become small business entrepreneurs. The gap is often mentioned by the business community.


Citizens ask why employment training is not available or so limited?; I cannot answer. So let me ask “why” on their behalf.


Spencer (see above) has this to say:

  • “Not only in times past, but almost as much in our own era, that knowledge which conduces to personal well-being has been postponed to that which brings applause. Men dress their children's minds as they do their bodies, in the prevailing fashion. Thoroughly to realize the truth that with the mind as with the body the ornamental precedes the useful, it is needful to glance at its rationale. This lies in the fact that, from the far past down even to the present, social needs have subordinated individual needs, and that the chief social need has been the control of individuals.”


But I would really like to hear the official PEI response


Don Glendenning, Editor
