ABOUT SCHOOLS


John Dewey, in School and Society, said “Relate the school to life, and all studies are of necessity correlated”.  In spite of this advice,  schools operate on a model far different from the world outside their doors.   Dewey’s “life” may be interpreted or described many ways.  I like the model put forward in the Oregon Education Act of 1991 which identified the following life roles: individual, learner, producer, consumer, family member and citizen.  


Although the significance of each role varies from individual to individual and changes as we mature, we all want our children to reach an acceptable level of performance in each role.  But helping our youth prepare for each role is more than most parents can handle so we’ve established schools.  The community thinks education is a such a good thing that taxpayers foot the bill.  


In our lifetime, everything seemed to work well.  People who went to school or stayed in school for longer periods found jobs and earned more money.  This worked so well that the government built more and bigger schools.  The offer to help became a requirement to attend.  Schools have taken on a life of their own. Both people and the school became convinced that going to school and getting an education meant the same thing.  

  

In the real world, became a place of change, and the pace also increases; schools didn’t seem to notice.  People in the real world work differently, in different places, with different tools and within changing organizational patterns.  But not schools, they  continue to focus on doing the same thing but doing it better and better. The output of schools for each learner is the growth that is attributable to interventions made by school employees – teachers, librarians, guidance counselors, etc.  The clearer we specify the desired exit performance, the more likely learners are to achieve it.  The better we describe the difference schools make, the more likely we are to meet our community’s expectation and of the return on their investment.  The ultimate objective is to prepare young people to function in adult roles.  


The above being the case, schools should look to the community when they plan school programs.  What mix of life roles should a school offer?  What attitudes, skills and knowledge will best serve learners both while in school and in later life? What are the community norms? How do schools organize for the task? 

Schools offer a safe and controlled environment to experiment, to try new things, even to fail.  Society has placed few restrictions on what schools can or should do but neither has it described well its expectations.
