EDUCATION TODAY: A PERSONAL VIEW

By

Don Glendenning


In recent months, the writer, a retired educator, has engaged in discussion, reading and reflection on public education. Although these notes are the author’s, and his alone, the process revealed considerable concern among the public at large, including retired educators, for the current state of education. Ideas for improvement, although less from the consultative process, are also likely to be shared by many. Although the author lives in Prince Edward Island, much of what he says can be applied equally well in other Provinces.

The Culture of schools

The Globe and Mail on Wednesday, February 9 carried an article by Andre Picard on “Why patients need a say in their health care”. The article describes a health care system “where the needs of the system almost always take precedence over those of the individual patient”. It is a culture where “patients don’t get choices, where dialogue is discouraged, where patient-provider relationships are fleeting, and where investing in customer service is not a priority.” Many parents would likely to describe the public school system in a similar way.

Schools help young people prepare to function in a variety of life roles. To the extent possible, therefore, schools should model the society into which students are being graduated. For example:

  • At work, one finishes a task and then moves to another; in school students move to another after a set time whether or not the task is finished,

  • Students begin work when the teacher enters the room and finish when the teacher leaves the room; society needs people who are self-directed

  • Schools lack a service mentality; they have a guaranteed clientele regardless of the quantity and quality of their work,

  • Schools operate as old-time industrial organizations even though the work structure has become collegial.

  • Students are not encouraged to take responsibility for, or to invest in, their own education. If they miss a day, so what?

  • The system obscures accountability of both students and teachers

  • Teacher pay is based on education and experience; it should be based on job size and performance.


These are cultural issues; they cannot be resolved by simply adding new courses. Instead, we need to change the way schools operate. Not only will these changes make life more satisfying for teachers and students, but it will better reflect the the community they will live in.
