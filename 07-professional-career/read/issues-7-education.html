(175 words):** Don Glendenning presents a detailed analysis of structural issues requiring public dialogue about education's future in PEI. He emphasizes that education is fundamentally complex due to learner diversity, and systems require clear purposes, functions, and structures. Key issues: (1) Terminology—educators use single terms for multiple concepts without definitions, conflating education with schooling; (2) Purpose—PEI's purpose statements lack detail needed for development and assessment; (3) Structure—failing to organize around identified functions; (4) Autonomy—OECD research shows schools with operational autonomy achieve better PISA results; (5) Accountability—requires defining missions, values, goals, and ensuring transparent communication; (6) Standards and grading—conflicts arise when teachers both teach and grade; competency reporting preferred; (7) Human and social capital—isolated teachers lack collaborative culture. Don advocates moving from product-focused (credentials) to service-focused education, and calls for public engagement in systemic change, extending an invitation to Islanders (June 5, 2023).

It is widely recognized that education is a key factor in determining each citizen’s quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of our citizens will determine to a significant degree the quality of the communities in which we live. Government support is justified for two reasons, one to support personal growth and development for every citizen; the other is to ensure the common good. Educating is a complex matter made so by the fact that each person is unique in many ways, unique in basic physical, mental, emotional make-up; unique in the range and nature of previous experience, unique in perceived futures, but also unique in terms of learning pace, style and needs.


An education system is a set of services put in place by a community to encourage and support the education of its citizen’s given the numbers served and the wide range of prior experience, capabilities and interests of learners, designing and operating an education system is no easy task. These complexities have given rise to the following structural issues:


  1. Terminology – Educators are creative people; they try to accommodate a number of concepts with a single term. Learning excellence as an example. On the other hand, the writer seldom finds definitions in educational writings and certainly is left with the impression that going to school, in all its configurations, naturally leads to a good education.
