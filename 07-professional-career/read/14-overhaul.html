December 2020 paper analyzing structural impediments to educational improvement, drawing on World Bank research and Don's experience chairing community education discussions. He identifies political context challenges (politicization within government departments affecting long-term planning), mission ambiguity, organizational structure limitations, insufficient autonomy, unclear accountability, fragmented culture treating education as product rather than service, and inadequate instructional support materials. Don argues that many functions should delegate to schools and intermediary bodies, citing OECD evidence that student performance improves with school autonomy. He advocates treating education as a service to learners rather than a product to deliver, emphasizing learner-centered rather than teacher-centered support materials. The paper calls for clear vision-setting as prerequisite for structural reform.


Restructuring Education


Occasional Paper No. 14 December 10, 2020

________________________________________________________________________________

... professional development needs to be embedded

in the curriculum development process—Mooney and Mausbach

…

A few years ago, I chaired a half dozen public sessions across PEI about the education concerns of youth. Sometimes, discussions continued long after sessions closed. After one session, a side conversation between two trustees turned to “structural” impediments to better education. A recent reading of a World Bank report on education, although not using the term “structural”, reminded me of trustee comments and prompted this letter.


By structural issues, I refer to policies, procedures, culture, staff, and physical arrangements; structural impediments include:

  1. Political setting: Provincial Governments have a leadership role in encouraging, supporting and regulating education. But embedding the delivery process in a government department presents its own challenges – politicization, long term planning, communication, and operating style. The “fear factor in education, I suspect, is due at least in part to the political framework in which education operates.


  • Mission: Mission statements vary; most encourage all citizens to improve their education, show support some citizens especially younger ones and regulate the field.


the World Bank worries that Ministries of Education view going to school and getting an education as one and the same. The goal of our Department as one of “developing and delivering programs” to Island youth reinforces the World Bank concern.


  • Structure

Organizations usually organize by function; education functions are usually thought of as governance, instruction, research, education products, quality assurance, information, and counselling, The World Bank believes that some of these functions should be delegated to schools and other intermediary bodies most school administrators I know support the World Bank view.


  • Autonomy: the OECD (who gives us PISA tests) tell us that students in schools where the school has autonomy for a school’s day to day operation, do better on PISA tests than other students. Yet my Informal evidence suggests that neither principals nor teachers in PEI have the autonomy the OECD believes is needed.


  • Accountability is a “duty to report (give account”) for events tasks and experiences.” The education task is to help Islanders gain skills, knowledge and attitudes of value to both citizens themselves and to our community. We seldom talk about expectations and accountabilities so I am not sure just how well they are understood.


  • Culture:

There is a strong connection between an organization’s culture and an organization’s performance. Culture is based on connections - strong and overlapping connections between and among members of the education community. It appears to outsiders, however, as though we have separate systems – public schools, apprenticeship, post secondary education, employer based training and private schools. An education plan for the Island would be useful.


  • Service or product - Dictionaries that I consult consider education to be a service to people who want/need to learn. My sense of what’s happening, however, is that education has become a product. As a result, much of focus is on years, days, hours of schooling, credits, courses, diplomas and degrees; the fact that employers use degrees as a screening device is further evidence of the switch.


  • Instructional support: refers to the manner in which learning support materials and services are organized and delivered. Although the literature tells me that curriculum development is in support of learners and learning, material that I encounter seems designed to support teachers. rather than students. Covid-19 has forced reconsideration of the teaching-learning process. We can expect increased pressure for more learner support materials in the future – outcome elaborations, self instructional materials, self assessment and individual profiles in the years ahead.


My exploration suggests that changes are called for, that change is a team effort; Government has a leadership role and setting direction is a government responsibility. Part of the leadership role is clearing roadblocks i.e. structural impediments.


As readers would expect, the first step in bringing about change is to create a clear vision; it should be written and widely circulated, and must be done before reconstruction commences.














Don Glendenning, Editor
