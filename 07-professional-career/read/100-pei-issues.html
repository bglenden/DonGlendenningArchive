KEY ISSUES ___________________________________________________________________________

It is widely recognized that education Is a key factor in determining a person’s quality of life. In the same way, the collective education of our citizens determines ,to a significant degree, the qualities of the communities in which we live. Education is complex, made so by the fact that each person is unique in so many ways – unique in basic physical, emotional and mental make up, unique in the range and nature of previous experience; unique also in perceived futures and unique in terms of learning style, interests, pace and needs.

Given these complexities, some system-wide issues are not surprising:

  1. Clarity – a common terminology improves communication throughout the organization; the most obvious example, according to the World Bank, in the practice of using the terms “education” and ‘going to school” interchangeably.

  2. Purpose – purpose sets the stage for what we do and how we organize to do it. The PEI PEI education literature refers to helping people find “a place” in society’; we need to better define that “place”. People are said to fill six life roles, that of an individual, citizen, a producer, a consumer, family member and student. All roles can benefit from education but all may not need the same amount, mix or time.

  3. Services – Learning is complex; support services include: information, counselling, instruction, products and technology, quality assurance. governance, funding and human resources. Services should be publicly available as needed. .

  4. Structure -”Form follows function” is a standard when structuring any venture. Departments of Education, however, appear to structure around school levels and clients served.

  5. Autonomy - front line service providers (teachers) need both expertise and autonomy to respond to learner needs. The OECD tells us that PISA results are better for students in schools with autonomy in their day-to-day operation.

  6. Accountability - Mathew Bedard of the Montreal Economic Institute points out that “autonomy and accountability jointly applied” would cost practically nothing and allow education to better adapt to the diversity of student needs and aptitudes.

  7. Collaboration – education thrives on collaboration and communication yet teachers and schools seem limited to silos.

  8. Communication – Paul Bennet speaks of the “muffled voices of teachers” who work alone in classrooms; others have noted that the teaching profession itself is often a silo in the community.


  1. Transparency – both the profession and the community would benefit from increased public information and discussion about the field,

  2. Documentation – Given the size and complexity oof he taskm system would benefit from documentation of education components.

These issues are not new; no doubt readers have others to propose. All should be considered. They need to be dealt with in a public manner soon, and should be dealt with soon.
