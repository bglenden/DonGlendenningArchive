RESTRUCTURING EDUCATION

IN PRINCE EDWAD ISLAND

Doc 5

It is widely recognized that education is a key factor in determining each citizen’s quality of life; in the same way, the collective education of our citizens will determine to a significant degree the quality of the communities in which we live. In recognition of its importance, governments provide support for public education. The investment is justified for two reasons, one to ensure a high level of personal growth and development for every citizen; the other is to ensure the common good.


Based on recent media reports, one can conclude that there are gaps between what Islanders have and what they want in the way of education. Literacy levels, PISA results, recent CFPEI survey of Island youth, presentation to the Standing Committee by the Charlottetown Area Chamber of Commerce, views expressed by the Summerside Chamber of Commerce, a survey by the PEI Home and School Federation and a series of workshops on “Preparedness” held throughout PEI by Education 20/20 Inc.


That changes are needed is not debatable; where change begins depends upon where on fits in the organization and ones priorities. My experience, review of the literature and discussion with Islanders results in the following recommendations..


  1. Standard terminology - Terminology – Educators are creative people; they try to accommodate a number of concepts with a single term. Learning excellence as an example. On the other hand, the writer seldom finds definitions in educational writings and certainly is left with the im-pression that going to school, in all its configurations, naturally leads to a good education.

To be meaningful, a purpose state-ment must be easy to understand, explain why the work is important, contribute to the alignment of activities, and be manageable.
