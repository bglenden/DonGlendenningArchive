SCHOOLS AS INCUBATORS 



In my earlier item, I identified exit standards as the urgent issue of the day.  I say this because of the frequency with which the subject arises in the media and in private discussions; the school system is usually blamed.  Schools, colleges, universities and professional and certification bodies “raise” their standards but complaints continue.  A gap exists between public expectations and what the system delivers; perhaps it always will.    


We fill many life roles in the course of a life time.  The Oregon Education Act of 1991 identified the following six: individual, learner, producer, consumer, family member and citizen.  The relative importance of each role varies from individual to individual and will change over time.    People will move into some roles without formal schooling but it is generally recognized that, at some point in our lives and for a varying length of time, all can benefit from help in the form of instruction, advice, coaching, information, counseling and practice.  The amount of help needed varies from individual to individual; that is one of the complications of teaching.  But the ability of each learner to function in society is the final test of the school system


Schools are incubators in that they prepare young people for life in the adult world; they help people in their most formative years to develop skills needed in their adult lives.  Schools offer a safe and controlled environment to experiment, to try new things, even to fail.  Society has placed few restrictions on what schools can or should do but neither has it described well its expectations.


Schools do not operate on the normal business model in which a person or a company invests in a product or service and a consumer or client purchases the product or service.  In the case of education, there are at least three sets of stakeholders and their interests overlap.  It is not always clear just who is the customer?  the learner?  the service provider? the investor?  Where does a prospective employer fit in?  Who is accountable to whom and for what?


The output of schools for each learner is the growth that occurs between entering and leaving the school system – that is, growth attributable to the efforts of teachers.  The clearer we specify the desired exit performance, the more likely learners are to achieve it.  The better we describe the difference schools make, the more likely we are to meet our community’s  expectation and of the return on their investment.  The ultimate objective is to prepare young people to function in adult roles.  For some learners, the transition from schools occurs without completion of high school, for others the transition takes after Grade 12, for the remainder the transition occurs after a stint of postsecondary study.


Educators measure quality by input criteria, subjects taught, years of schooling, institutions attended, entrance requirements, qualification of instructors/teachers, facilities, access to new technology and level of performance on tests and exams.  What the learner can do with what he knows, Performance is not measured but must be inferred.    


Taxpayers, consumers, potential employers, on the other hand, judge graduates on what they can do – on the nature and level of service provided or the product displayed.  A consumer of goods and services judges on the basis of the quality of work or service, time required to complete the task, communication skills related to the task, good work habits, product knowledge, work habits, abilities to do the assigned tasks.  The public   rates performance, that is, the application of knowledge to solve a problem or to make a product.  These are output measures.  


Little progress in raising standards is likely until these conflicting views are reconciled; it is time to align the content and measurement of training programs with standards of the community.  Dialogue needs to begin soon; external interventions may help to launch the dialogue.
