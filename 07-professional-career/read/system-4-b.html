Structuring Education-4-B

4-11

Our future as individuals and as a community will be determined, in large measure, by the nature and level of education demonstrated by our citizens. Education is an umbrella concept and includes all experiences that have had an impact on the way a person thinks, feels and acts wherever those experiences occur and however they are triggered. The key to a person’s education, the literature tells us, are early experiences triggered in and through the home and family followed by the influence of teachers. A good education is described as one that prepares a person to handle events occurring in their daily lives; task of educators is to help make that happen.


Frick (Indiana University) tells us that an education system requires (a) a student (b) a teacher, (c) content, (d) context, and (e) the community/culture. The writer, on the other hand, considers an education system to be best described by the services offered; the Business Roundtable identifies the following education services: Information, counselling, Instruction, quality assurance. products and technology, funding and human resources.

Key factors in creating an education system include:

  1. Purpose -is about direction, what one hopes to achieve. It provides the fundamental reason for establishing an organization and is the key element from which everything else follows. PEI literature describes our system as one of “developing and delivering”, “providing for the development of children” and” helping students secure a place.” None are sufficient, I suggest, on which to build an education system.

  2. Clarity - The World Bank, the world’s largest funder of public education, has cautioned its members about confusing education with going to school; PEI also needs to be careful.

  3. Mission/Vision

  4. Structure-Form follows function is the standard when structuring any organization; PEI structure, it seems, reflects clients rather than functions.

  5. Education is a service to students; a devolved delivery structure is recommended. Yet I find that, in spite of all arguments in support of delegation, teachers and principals have little freedom to make adjustments to better serve student needs.

  6. Diagnosis

  7. Accountabilities are not clearly articulated; Mathieu Bedard of the Montreal Economic Intitute tells us that “autnomy and accountability jointly applied “would cost practically nothing and allow education to better adapt to the diversity of student needs and aptitudes”. The OECD also tells us that student results on PISA tests ae better in schools that have autonomy for their day to day operation.

  8. Grades are an attempt to quantify the amount or level of learning. Testing typically iocuses on the acquisition of knowledge. Self assessment first is receiving growing support and is thought to help bidge the gap beweem schooling and education.


The above structural issues need attention. Our Department of Education, in its leadership role, bears a special responsibility.
