February 28. 2013

Some notes on reading

By

Don Glendenning


NOTE: These notes are put together to document and support discussions on the subject of reading and are intended to reflect the subject from a learner’s rather than a teacher’s perspective.


Our futures, as individuals and as a community, depend, to a large degree, on the nature and level of education available to, and actually attained by, our citizens. Education helps us to develop our individual potential to the fullest and prepares us for our many life roles* that of an individual, family member, learner, citizen, producer and consumer. This means engaging in a variety of activities with a view to assembling a variety of skills, knowledge and attitudes supportive of functioning in those life roles. Based on this thinking, the quality of one’s education would be measured by how well one is able to function in the various life roles.


One’s education is the result of everything that we learn in our lifetime that has had a formative effect on us. It starts at birth with some of our most important learning occurring in the first few year of life; skills learned at that time, to a large degree, determine what we become in later life. Learning is a partnership and nowhere is that partnership more important than in improving the reading level of every citizen.


Key to success in education generally, is reading, the golden skill, a tool for lifelong learning. Statistics indicate reveal the magnitude of the task. We are aware of the attention given by our Ministers of Education in Atlantic Canada to increasing literacy rates, including reading, and this increased attention appears to be paying dividend. We also know, however, improving reading is more than a school problem, it is a community problem, even a development problem, which can be traced in part to the early years and outside the influence of the school.


While we recognize that reading and learning to read or read better. occur in the context of other learning to communicate generally, including listening, writing, speaking, it is our view that it will be useful to examine each component separately and that we should commence with a common understanding of what it means to read.

Increasing reading levels

There are many possible causes of poor reading skills, some neurological, some environmental and some may be both but, without doubt, a rich environment in early life is a key to later reading success. Ideally, reading levels are improved when:

  • Education is important

  • Parents are readers

  • The community is engaged in the education process

  • Reading is stressed at school

  • Quality of schools

  • Reading skills are valued

  • Children have access to computers


Some Early signs of difficulty:

Parents or grandparents may be the first to notice signs of reading problem; early signs include trouble with:

  • Recognizing words that start with the same sound.

  • Letter and number symbols

  • Repeated ear infections

  • Associations between sounds and letters.

  • Pronouncing new words

  • Breaking words apart into sounds

  • Remembering the names and sounds of the letters

  • Skipping words in a sentence and doesn't stop to self-correct

  • Guessing rather than sounding out unknown words

  • Remembering names and sounds of letters

  • Rhyming

  • Reversing letters–such as writing "d" for "b" or "u" for "n"

  • Reversing words, such as "nap" for "pan"

  • Being reluctant to read, or read aloud

Key Performance Criteria:

There will always be differences among readers; how well one reads compared to another, is usually measured in terms of:

  • Range of material one can read

  • How well one comprehends what has been read

  • Fluency

  • Degree of assistance required

  • Speed

  • Vocabulary

  • Flow


How well do I read?

While knowing competencies and assessment criteria is useful in its own right, people interested in improving their reading may find it useful to have set of statements that reflect the public view of reading levels. If, as the writer believes, one’s education is intended to help one operate successfully in a variety of life roles, then we should look to “life roles” to describe the various levels. Hopefully, one could find or create a rating scale that describes reading levels for any and all ages but especially all adult readers say from Grade 9 through the remainder of secondary school, through postsecondary studies and into life as an adult - a scale that would be easily understood by the individual learner, parents, teachers, employers and the public at large.


While I found a number of scales against which to measure one’s reading level, none captured all the elements I thought necessary so, using rating scales from a variety of sources, I created the following:

  • 6- Read a wide variety of complex printed materials, include technical material, with fluency and ease, and without assistance

  • 4- Read a range of printed materials with independence, fluency and ease

  • 3- Integrate information from a number of sources into a single text

  • 2- Read a number of pieces of text to find information, narrow range of material

but requires occasional assistance when reading the daily newspaper.ut

Recommendations:

  • Replace outcomes with competencies

  • Provide each learner with a personal reading record – a list of competencies and indicating their levels on each

  • Establish a minimal level of performance in order to graduate

  • Clearly identified assessment criteria

  • Prepare a written description for each performance level – not to exceed seven.

  • Identifying a benchmark such as “Level three denotes a person who can read and discuss articles found in the daily newspaper”.

  • Making all of the above information available to learners, parents and the public.

  • Including “reading” as an outcome on report cards.

  • Is there a standard format for identifying outcomes

  • Where do people learn to identify outcomes

Appendix 1

Reading Competencies
Extracted from a report

Prepared by the Competencies Task Force of CEI

—The North Carolina Conference of English Instructors
August 11, 1995

  1. Comprehension Competencies
    The student will

      1. Comprehend the meaning of literary texts, business documents, and textbooks.

      2. Identify and differentiate between main ideas or themes and supporting details, noting transitions.

  1. Analysis Competencies
    The student will

      1. Differentiate between fact and opinion.

      2. Understand the writer's point of view or bias.

      3. Identify the ethics, assumptions, and cultural and historical context.

      4. Recognize logical arguments and logical fallacies.

  1. Interpretation and Evaluation Competencies
    The student will

      1. Interpret, evaluate, draw inferences, and support conclusions with evidence from a text.

      2. Recognize the presence of ambiguity and multiple interpretations.

      3. Relate texts to life situations.


  1. Language and Style Competencies
    The student will

    1. Identify the author's tone, voice and diction, developing an appreciation for the author's style, artistry, and perspective.

    2. Develop an awareness of inference and of denotative and connotative meanings of words.

    3. Comprehend the uses of figurative language.


  1. Research Competencies
    The student will

      1. Use indexes, dictionary, thesauruses, and other library resources.

      2. Read and understand basic information and text in electronic media.
