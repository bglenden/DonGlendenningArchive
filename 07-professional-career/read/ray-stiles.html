Personal letter from Don recounting his 1955-56 study year at the Institute of Education, University of London on a Lord Beaverbrook Scholarship and his friendship with Ray Stiles, whom he shared accommodation with. Reflects Don's international education background and personal relationships from his formative professional years.

Dear Dominic:


Again, thanks for your note about your mother’s health and father’s death. Carol and I send our prayers and our sympathy.  It must have been a difficult year for your family; these times are never easy but I am sure that there are many happy memories from earlier times. 


The “golden years” are not always golden, and the past year for your family was a roller coaster year indeed.  When Christmas passed without the usual note, it raised concern so your note was not a complete surprise.  


In 1955-56, I studied at the Institute of Education, University of London, on a Lord Beaverbrook Scholarship and lived nearby just off Russell Square at, I believe, College Rooms, Woburn Square(?).  It was a fairly small residence and housed students from the UK and other Commonwealth countries.  I shared a room on the second floor with Ray Kilfoil, another Canadian, and your father shared an adjacent room with, I believe, Les Ingham from New Zealand (although I am not absolutely sure of the latter).  


Although your father and I did not take any classes together, because our rooms were beside each other, we met frequently when entering and leaving our rooms and usually shared a table at breakfast which was provided in a basement kitchen.  I also remember many cups of afternoon or evening tea and conversation.


Your Dad and I seemed to “hit it off” and found ourselves lingering in conversation, exploring the city together, and occasionally going to the theatre and sometimes to church.  That was before Ray and Molly were married and I know that Ray went home some weekends, (Would that be to Watford?).


In the spring or early summer of 1956, Ray suggested an outing to Hythe in the Dover area.  We spent a couple of nights and lucked out weatherwise.  The seafront there was beautiful and I remember exploring the area and walking along the bluff overlooking the English Channel.


While I do not remember your Dad’s exact age, I believe that he was slightly older than I (I will be 80 in September). We talked about wartime Britain and he spoke of conditions on the home front; I remember the vivid picture he painted of the air full of aircraft leaving on air raids to the continent and also the even larger numbers of aircraft leading up to D-Day.  In 1955-56, as you would know, London still bore the marks of all the bombing and I recall, in particular, the acres and acres of bombed out blocks in the Barbican area.  While I do not believe that he served in the active army, I think that he may have had a stint of service just at the close of the war – but perhaps it was only as a civilian volunteer.


I returned to Canada in August, 1956 and, over the years, we continued to exchange Christmas notes.  On two occasions while visiting England, I visited your Dad and Mum – but I forget the exact years.  Once I visited alone and on another occasion Carol and I traveled together to Norwich, both times by train.  Ray and Molly were thinking of changing houses and they took Carol and I on tour to show a new place they were considering.  I also remember visiting them in their home which had a portion of a wall dating back several hundred years.  I do not recall just how many years but to a Canadian, it seemed very old.  And didn’t they live first after their marriage in the Schoolhouse?



Obviously he loved his church and spoke often of that connection.  After finally getting a driving permit, he also spoke about his trips to the seashore and walking the beach.  For a while both your parents were very active in an initiative to save small schools.  Didn’t your mother edit a newsletter on that subject?  Politics and government were frequent topics of conversation and, if my memory serves me well, your Dad was a firm supporter to the Liberal Party and hoped at one time that they might form a government.  


Their letters always included information about the family – what you were doing, where you were living, etc.  Lindsay, as I recall, at one time was thinking of making a visit to Canada.  Did she ever make that trip?


We keep well; yes, we have the usual aches and pains, I have angina and a few years ago had an ileostomy but we get about quite well.  We just returned from a motor trip to Florida in the southern US, a distance of 2100 miles each way – so we feel fortunate indeed.


I did not know about the Canada connection; if you could give me some precise information about names, dates and locations, I would be glad to share whatever I can.  The internet makes information gathering so much easier than once as the case.  I find the local genealogical societies a good place to start but also have had responses to letters in the local papers.


Again, thank you for your letter about your Dad and Mum; although not good news, I certainly appreciate your thoughtfulness in writing.


And please give our warmest regards to Molly.  To you, Lindsay and Martin, our warmest regards and do keep in touch.


Sincerely


Don and Carol Glendenning
