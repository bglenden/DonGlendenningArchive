NUMERACY- N-3

---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------


Background

School at all levels, it seems teach math; it is considered a key skill in all life roles. The Prince Edward Island Government public-school math curriculum guide describes math as the “study of the science of quantity, number and space” and lists goals of its math offerings as being to “prepare students to solve problems, communicate and reason mathematically, appreciate the value of mathematics, make connections between mathematics and its applications, commit themselves to lifelong learning and become mathematically literate adults using mathematics to contribute to society.” Typical math subjects include algebra, calculus, geometry, measurement, number, permutations, probability, relations and functions, statistics and trigonometry.


Numeracy, on the other hand, is also math, sometimes described as the application of math ; ‘instead of digging deeper into the formal and abstract world of mathematics, learning more mathematics, and become more fluent with mathematics, numeracy fosters the understanding and application of our mathematical knowledge and quantitative sense”. being numerate has been described as “having the confidence and skill to use numbers and mathematical approaches in all aspects of life - at work, in practical everyday activities at home and beyond, as consumers, in managing our finances, as parents helping our children learn, as patients making sense of health information, as citizens understanding the world about us.” . Numeracy is said to operate especially in life settings, personal, community, and career.


In fact, it seems to the writer that public concern about math is really a concern about the need for math in solving every day problems.; in other words, about numeracy.


Growing support

The literature shows growing support for instruction in numeracy; some teachers teach it directly while others support numeracy through on-line courses. Most seem to consider numeracy as an additional offering and not a replacement course. The National Numeracy Organization (UK)proposes:

1. Every teacher become a teacher of numeracy. That does not mean that every teacher becomes a maths teacher but that numeracy, just like literacy- is recognized as an intrinsic part of every subject across the school curriculum.

2. A new measure of numeracy proficiency should be introduced for all young people at the age of 14. This would establish a national benchmark for the level of numeracy needed for further study in all subjects between the ages of 14-19

3 An additional and universally respected qualification in numeracy (or core maths) is needed alongside GGCSE maths. Politicians and curriculum and qualifications regulators must give serious and urgent consideration and introduction of dual GCSSEs in maths and numeracy


A report by the PEI Department of Education on The Early Numeracy Intervention Program (ENIP) reports that numeracy

“gives students the tools and the skills to solve problems logically, and to developmental flexibility, independence of thought, self-efficacy, and the ability to persevere”


The Scottish Education Department suggests eight categories of numeracies: estimation and rounding, number and number processes, fractions, decimal fractions and percentages; money, time, measurement, data and analysis, and chance and uncertainty. Scotland Education tells us that “Being numerate helps us to function responsibly in everyday life and contribute effectively to society. It increases our opportunities within the world of work and establishes foundations which can be built upon through lifelong learning. Numeracy is not only a subset of mathematics; it is also a life skill which permeates and supports all areas of learning, allowing young people access to the wider curriculum”


Careers New Zealand suggests that basic numeracy, needed for the workforce, should include:

Counting quantities for a customers, The use of percentages and subtraction when giving a discount, using division when calculating costs per head, measuring the area of shapes, calculating fuel consumption and understanding tables in reports and interpreting graphs.



Community concern

In spite of the importance of numeracy, my search of the literature and call to friends did not result in lists of typical Numeracy skills; they are arrived at through a survey of skills practiced by citizens, employers or another identified group. Education 2030 survey generated a list of some 84 skills under the following heading: (1) Estimate and calculate with whole numbers,(2) Use fractions, decimals, percentages, rations and Rates ( 3) Use spatial reasoning, (4) Recognize and use patterns and relationships, (5) interpret statistical information, (6) Manage Personal Finance, (7) Use Measurement. Until they are publicly identified, responsibility assigned to a teacher, measured and asssignrf to a teacher, they are unlikely to be taught .
