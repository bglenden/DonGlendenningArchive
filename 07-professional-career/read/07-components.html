A technical but accessible exposition of the four components comprising a true educational standard: competencies (observable, measurable, community-drawn actions people can perform); criteria (quality measures like consistency, speed, safety); levels of performance (distinguishing how well someone performs, not just whether they can); and benchmarks (community-based reference points anchoring performance levels to real-world contexts). Glendenning critiques the vagueness in existing educational standards and provides his own "Glendenning version," arguing that effective standards must integrate all four components. He uses practical examples like editing or driving to illustrate how performance levels vary independently from basic competence.

 STANDARDS


Should note be two standards school and community or employment


Everyone seems to have a favourite story about school standards.  Most seem to involve reading, writing, geography, Canadian History and work habits. It has ever been thus; criticisms go back at least to the time of the Greeks but that is not to say that the issue should be ignored.  


Old timers seem to think that school standards have deteriorated.    Based on some of my old exam papers that I came across recently, I think that my children were more advanced in their school studies than I was at that stage of schooling,  Maybe; I really don’t know.  The reason I am not sure is that, to my way of thinking, educational standards have never been well defined.


For the purpose of this article::

The terms outcome and competency interchangeably

I distinguish between testing for the purpose awarding improving learning from testing for the purpose of granting recognition.

I have focused on the public school system although much of what I say applies to postsecondary education as well.

Since school systems profess to be outcome based, this article will focus on outcomes or competency based education.


Although I have browsed many a bulletin from Departments of Education, I have not found a satisfactory description of what constitutes a standard – at least not one satisfactory to me. So, hers is the Glendenning version.  By educational standard I refer to the level of performance required of a student in order to receive recognition.  


An educational standard includes four components:


Competencies 


We demonstrate what we have learned by what we can do.  Statements of what we are able to do are known as competencies.  Competencies:

.

Describe an observable action

Drawn from the community being served 

Are attainable

Are measurable

Be stated in clear 






2 Criteria


Criteria are the unadorned measures used to assess a person’s performance on a competency and should not be confused with components of a skill or sub-skills.  For example the ability to read and write are a sub-skills of communications and are not themselves performance criteria.  Common criteria for assessing performance include: 

Quality of the product or service

How quickly one performs, 

Ability to perform under unusual conditions

Coping with unanticipated problems

Degree of supervision requiewd

Consistency

Minimizing risk/safety of self and others




3 Levels of performance


Defining differences is not easy. Bloom’s taxonomy of learning shows six levels of learning in the cognitive domain, five in the affective domain and seven in the psychomotor domain.  Since people may be looking for a middle level or “C” thosoe forcing raters, either ones’ self or others, away from an easy C.  Presumably one who cannot perform at all would rate a zero.  On the other hand, at the top level of performance one would be expected to be able to perform at an acceptale level even under extenuating circumstances.  Onne might think that the ability to teach a skill would represent the highest level of performance; on the other hand, I would argue that teaching is a separate skill.  


4 Benchmark 


A benchmark is a point of reference which anchors a rating scale to the community being served.  Reaching the benchmark level is not the aim of the course, it simply gives meaning to the discussion about one’s level.  A “C” could indicate that a new driver is ready to drive under normal driving conditions in moderate traffic during daylight hours, a “C” might describe a laboratory technician who is capable of performing with occasional supervision, a “c” for a teacher could mean a teacher with a good knowledge of the subject(s0 being taught and a reasonable array of standard teaching techniques.  Such as may be used by a teacher with a couple of year’s experience. The identification of a benchmark must be sufficiently clear that it is readily identified in both the mind of the teacher, the student and the public at large..


Obviously, the subject requires a much more detailed examination;  the above constitutes only an entry point into the discussion.
