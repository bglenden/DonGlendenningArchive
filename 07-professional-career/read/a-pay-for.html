Don proposes a health-care-model approach to publicly-funded education, specifying which educational services government should cover (essential skills, entry-level occupational training, counselling, qualification exams, management training after five years employment) and which it should not (such as calculus). He argues this focus on learning outcomes rather than school operations would shift educational policy fundamentally. The document also discusses emerging trends: learning pods gaining permanence, interest in mini-courses for continuing education especially during pandemic-related layoffs, and context-based learning (citizen, personal, health, career) drawing parallels to magnet schools like PCVHS and PVI.

WHAT PART OF EDUCATION

SHOULD GOVERNMENT

PAY FOR?

Canadians boast about their health care system; it covers the cost of many, but not all, health care services. Government encourages people to adopt healthy practices and also regulates the health field. Service is triggered by a person with a health concern contacting his or her family doctor. Service is provided by or under the direction of a family doctor. I wonder how this would work in education?


Let me fabricate some clauses:

  • Essential skills – full cost for each resident regardless of age

  • Skills training to entry level for any occupation practised on PEI

  • Counselling sessions for student and guardian

  • Qualification examination on recommendation of instructor

  • Management training – available after five years of full-time employment

  • Calculus – not covered


This approach would certainly increase the focus on what is or has been ,earned rather than on the role of schools.

Mini courses

Learning context

One of the conclusions arising from the Education 20/20 “Citizen Math” study is the need to rethink the importance of context in the teaching/learning process. My reading on the subject suggests four separate contexts for numeracy - citizen, personal, health and career. My reading suggests that context count in all learning. Some schools, so called magnet schools, organize around the arts, sports, health services, aviation and technology. In some ways, Prince County Vocational High School and the Provincial Vocational school filled this role
