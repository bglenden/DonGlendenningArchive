HANDLE WITH CARE


Thanks to the leadership of Alice Taylor and Eileen Conway-Martin, a parent program called Handle with Care is making its way into communities across the Province. Ms. Taylor and Ms. Conway-Martin, partners with The Hincks Dellcrest Centre in Toronto, train facilitators who live and work in these communities to bring the program to parents and others who work with and care for children.


Handle with Care is a set of simple, interactive strategies to help parents and others who work with young children in various settings to promote their mental health. The term “mental health” refers to people’s social and emotional well-being.


Handle with Care is based on a number of important assumptions about mental health. Here are some examples:

  • How we feel, think and act;

  • How we cope with the ups and downs of everyday life;

  • How we feel about ourselves and our life;

  • How we see others and our future;

  • How stress affects us;

  • How we deal with the negative things that happen in our life; and

  • Our level of self-esteem and confidence.


Supporting children’s mental health is an everyday aspect of health care, just as we support healthy bodies by ensuring children eat well, exercise and get enough sleep.


  • Handle with Care uses a strength-based approach that assumes that every parent and caregiver has problem-solving skills, potential resources and wants what’s best for the children in their lives.

  • The program is for anyone who cares for or works with children, including communities with particular risks and challenges.

  • The spirit of the program is one of celebrating parenting and caregiving, recognizing the value of every parent, caregiver and child.


Programs planned after March break include the following communities:

  • Alberton

  • Wellington

  • Summerside

  • Charlottetown

  • Souris

POT POURRI


Math was the subject of a recent informal survey. A dozen people (parents, a retired university professor, a College teachers, university student, and entrepreneurs were given a list of math competencies and asked to select the ones that were important for every citizen. None chose vectors or simultaneous equations.
