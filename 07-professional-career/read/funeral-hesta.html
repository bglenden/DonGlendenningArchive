Don's funeral oration for Dr. Hesta MacDonald, honoring her four decades of community service, educational advocacy, and their personal friendship. Demonstrates Don's reflections on civic engagement and his relationship with a significant figure in Holland College history.

Hesta Arletta (Baker) MacDonald

Charlottetown, Prince Edward Island

October 24, 2009



Today we celebrate the life of Hesta Arletta (Baker) MacDonald, a woman who, in a quiet and unassuming manner, was an icon of motherhood and citizenship in our community.  


One could list the many organizations to which she belonged and the honours and awards received, and the list would be long and impressive.  But Hesta did not join a group just to belong; she joined with others to make the Island a better place to live, work and raise a family.


The causes for which she worked are a good reflection of who she was, of her interests and contributions.  They include:

Working for a new Rural High School in Eldon where she lived

Speaking out on issues of drug use and child abuse, 

building bridges to the Aboriginal Community on PEI, 

advocacy for improved and more accessible public education,

leadership setting directions for Holland College 

Preserving our heritage

Putting on the record,  memories of earlier times

Establishment of a fund to purchase books for school libraries

And to this list, we must add her church



I’ve known and worked with Hesta for more than 4 decades, initially as the first elected chair of the Board of Governors of Holland College, later as our Information Officer and for the last two decades, as a friend.  During the latter period, we had many cups of coffee together and sometimes an actual lunch (although she was a very light eater).  I’d pick her up at her home, (she was always ready on time) and we would go some where we hadn’t been before.  Sometimes we arranged our schedule so that other friends from her Holland College days could join us. 


On reflection, our discussion topics were predictable


First was family – she rejoiced in the accomplishments of children and grandchildren and great grandchildren, and sometimes anguished over their current rough spots.   Her love, care, concern and pride always shone through.  Obviously, she was a loving, caring and concerned mother; a love I know that was returned.  Her concern for her extended family reminded me of the same qualities I remember in my mother.   Not all families are so fortunate; these are memories to treasure.  She often recounted visits from family, visits to family, visits to Falmouth or Fredericton, and most recently a wedding in St. John, New Brunswick.

We usually talked briefly about an issue of the day, usually local or national but always from the view of how it should be rather than what was wrong. “There must be some way” was a frequent opening statement

We were of the same vintage so we often reminisced about our early years, for hers in Falmouth and mine in the Miramichi.  We also spoke of our first years of teaching in one-room schools and, of course, we reminisced about Holland College.  Recently we recalled an incident in Niagara Falls while on a tour of Ontario colleges.  Somewhere in our collective archives, we thought, there is a posed photo of some Board members, who shall remain nameless, attempting to push me into the Niagara Gorge while Hesta tries to save me.  Hesta often pulled those she worked with back to high ground.

And always, there was a discussion about education, a subject close to her heart.  She believed that education was a partnership between home and school and she provided great leadership in making that partnership a strong one.  Most recently, our discussions centered on the importance of reading.  She viewed it as the key skill for all later learning.  Our last conversation on this matter was less than a month ago.


Hesta MacDonald listened well; she was soft spoken, knowledgeable, articulate and passionate about many things but especially about family, heritage and young people.  Her story lives on through her family, in her writing and in our memories.  She was kind and generous, had a great sense of humour, acted with great dignity and was held in high regard by all who knew her.  She loved to read, enjoyed family gatherings, was a good visitor enjoyed Sunday drives through the countryside especially to the old homestead in Little Sands.  She usually worked the daily puzzles in the newspaper and did so the day before she died.  And over the years, many a hand was warmed by mittens knitted by Hesta.  As an active church member, she did not preach her values b simply lived them on a daily basis.  


Carol and I were out of Province before Thanksgiving.  On my return, I phoned Hesta to see is she was available for coffee.   My standard “How are you” brought an unusual reply, ”Not very well”   Obviously, something had changed.



Our community is richer for the life and works of Hesta Macdonald.  On behalf of the many friends gathered here today, I extend my sympathy to Rebecca, Graeme, Kenneth and Alice, and through them, to all members of their families.


Yesterday, a mutual friend passed along the following saying   “Some people come into our lives quickly and go.  Others stay for a while and leave footprints on our hearts.  And we are never, ever the same.”  Hesta MacDonald left imprints on many hearts.
