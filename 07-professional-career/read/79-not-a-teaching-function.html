NOT A TEACHING FUNCTION

Assessment is an integral part of teaching; it is a process of gathering information about a performance for the purpose of initiating improvement. Results are the subject of discussion between learner and teacher and play a role in formulating the next steps in the learning/teaching process.


Not so for grading; it is a process of applying standardized measurement to determine a person’s level of achievement. Grading is a regulatory function and, if administered by teachers may even damage the teacher student relationship.


Grading is a high stakes function; it deserves fairness and objectivity, that’s why many developed countries have turned to qualification authorities. Apprenticeship presents an appropriate separation between the teaching and grading function. Perhaps it’s time for an Atlantic Qualification Authority to serve the Region; it could even be hosted by PEI.







GRADES AS CAPITALISM

The recent article on “Grades are capitalism in Action” drew a tsunami of responses –at least, a tsunami in terms of “Open Education”. In terms of numbers, support and rejection were in balance; strength of views varied excellent to “horse manure. My thoughts on grading are presented in the column to the left.


STUDENT ABSENTEEISM

According to the Washington Post (July 30, 1019), 40% of students with high absence rate come from low income families; many students think about dropping out when they are still in the sixth and seventh grade. A sense of belonging, academic purpose, instruction that’s relevant, more career pathways, help with homework, socializing and strong relationships improve attendance.


Don Glendenning, Editor
