Don proposes replacing traditional grades with detailed competency-based transcripts that break down subject areas into specific skill categories. Using Math 421A as an example, he suggests reporting grades for each math category (sequences, polynomials, functions, geometry, trigonometry, data management) and proposing numeracy competencies (numbers, decimals, money, time, measurement, data analysis, chance). He advocates for "personal profiles" with common rating scales across all educational levels and subjects, arguing this would improve communication throughout the education system and give students clearer roadmaps for tracking individual progress.

Open Education

No. 96 August 20, 2020

When schools and ministries are managed well, learning happens-World Bank


FULLER TRANSCRIPTS

Seems to me that schools, colleges and universities owe their students a more complete record of their studies; perhaps parents and taxpayers should also be included. Such a report, if shared with a potential employer, could even go a long way toward landing a job.


I have before me as I write, a curriculum guide for Math 421A; it categorizes math as:

  • Sequence and Series

  • Polynomials

  • Relations and Functions

  • Coordinate Geometry

  • Trigonometry, and

  • Data Management


My proposal is to give a mark for each category. if reporting on numeracy, give the rating for each category:

  • Numbers

  • Decimals

  • Money

  • Time

  • Measurement

  • Data and analysis, and

  • Chance


I am sure that there are other ways to break out subject areas that would be even better in showing student progress.


In proposing this change, I am also thinking of the replacement of grades with personal profiles and the need for better tracking of individual progress. It is likely that a common set of outcomes or competencies could serve as a master list of math skills for all grades. With personal profiles, a student in Grade 9 or 10 could be given a math map on which he or she could plan learning activities and record progress for most. If not, all of their further studies.


The writer is also of the view that students would benefit from a common rating scale (see Newsletter 90) used at all levels of a subject, preferably one that is also found in the community. A master list of mat skills and a common rating scale could dramatically improve communication throughout the education system.


Don Glendenning, Editor
