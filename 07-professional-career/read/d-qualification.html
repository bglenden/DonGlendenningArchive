Needed: An Atlantic Qualification Authority


Sometimes we need a piece of paper to prove what we’ve learned; this represents a sea change from the days when credentials, a high school diploma or college degree, would open most any door.  But diplomas and degrees are not enough, anymore, employers want evidence as to what a person can do with what he/she knows.  Welcome to the world of qualifications.


There’s some confusion as to the difference between qualifications and credentials.  My definitions are simple.  Degrees and diplomas are credentials; they tell what and where a person has studied.  A qualification, on the other hand, certifies that a person has the attitudes, skills and knowledge required to perform a role or a task.


Most countries have qualification authorities, except Canada and the US, that is; it’s probably because we don’t have a National Education Department.  I’m most familiar with the one in New Zealand which regulates the nature and quality of courses offered at both the secondary and postsecondary level.

PROJECT NARRATIVE


Following the publication of a survey of Island youth in late 2013 (CFPEI, Vital Signs, 2013), Education 20/20 organized six public discussions for the purpose of informing the community of the report and its findings, learning more about youth issues in our communities, exploring issues of education and learning, and to identify actions to be taken to close gaps.  Discussions were lively and wide ranging, and carried on well after the formal session ended.   The final report included a recommendation that.   


Further study be undertaken to clarify the gaps in preparation for further study and for work

-(Preparedness, Education 20/20 Inc., Occasional Paper No. 1

Since then Education 20/20 has continued its exploration, both formally and informally, with postsecondary institutions, employers, teachers and education officials and concluded that:

  • The use of “Grade” to designate both age level and achievement level is confusing; we recommend that “Grade” continue to be used for this purpose.
  • A separate classification (we recommend “Level”) be used to denote achievement level in a subject. 
  • The adoption of Curriculum Based Outcomes demands a review of achievement levels.
  • Mathematics be chosen for the pilot project 
  • An independent body is needed to conduct assessment for the purpose of assigning levels of achievement.
  • Traditional assessment practices are no longer suitable


Weaknesses of the current system include:

  • The sheer number of institutions with authority to grant grades.
  • Inherent conflict when those who teach also grade
  • Different approaches to curriculum development
  • Confusion arising from the use of “Grade” to both classify the age of the learner and as a level of achievement
  • Growing use of social promotion
  • Changing style of student reporting
  • Lack of benchmarks 


Proposal – as a pilot project and using mathematics as a vehicle, develop/test a model for establishing outcome-based achievement


Steps:

  • Establish Steering Committee –to be chaired by President of Education 20/20 Inc. and include but not limited to educators,  employers, public at large and the Community Foundation.
  • Gather background information - local institutions, Western and Northern Canada Protocol that provides math curriculum to most Provinces, Ontario Qualification Framework, Qualification Authorities (Australia and New Zealand), PISA, P-CAP, 
  • Secure  working list of outcomes or competencies  
  • Develop or adopt a rating scale;
  • Criterion referenced
  • Reflective of Bloom’s taxonomy
  • Prepare final report
  • Implement – work with institutions and employers to implement


Innovative and beneficial features:

  • A single analysis of a subject available to all educational institutions 
  • Provides the same view of the subject for all users, teachers, students, parents, examiners, employers and parents.
  • Improves capability for tracking student learning
  • Allows for identification of a variety of profiles drawn from the same analysis 
  • Supports independent study
  • Bridges institutional and employer needs
  • Encourages communication and teamwork 
  • Allows learners to situate their learning in the context of the larger field 



Not surprising, people often confuse credentials and qualifications.  Diplomas and degrees are credentials and are awarded by institutions to people who have successfully completed a program or course of studies.  

Credentialling criteria are mostly knowledge based and are heavy on input measures, time, content studied, qualification of teachers; they also emphasize what has been studied rather than what has been learned.  The reliability of credentials is limited, however, due to the large number of issuing organizations , reliance on teacher judgement and conflicting school policies such as social promotion.  A qualification, on the other hand, is an actual measure of what the holder can do with what he or she knows.  As an example, apprentices may take their formal training  in high schools, colleges, industrial training programs or even the armed forces.  Training is based on a common occupational analysis, agreed stages, on-job training and common exams, common analyses, both provincial and national examinations and an endorsement known as the Red Seal.  Other Canadian examples of the separation of credentialing and qualification include the work of the Royal Canadian Conservatory of Music, Canadian Red Cross, Canadian Swimming. 


Education 20/20 Inc. supports  the establishment of a Canadian qualification framework and an independent body to run it.  Such an authority would assess and certify a person’s level of performance on a task or set of tasks such as found in an occupation or field of study. For example, a qualifications authority would assume the grading function now handled by teachers but not the formative or dfiagnostic function. The qualifications authority would focus certification clearly on performance that matters support lifelong learning, and self-directed study and thereby allow schools to focus on formative assessment practices that serve to help students learn. A qualification authority would, in effect, serve two clients, organizations (i.e., employers) desiring an assessment of prospective employees and individuals wanting to establish the level and suitability of their qualifications. While qualification authorities we are familiar with, focus on approving courses, accrediting institutions, and analyzing current offerings; we believe in a qualification authority that assesses individuals directly, not one that massages the minutiae of hours, outcomes, teacher qualification of education providers. A qualification authority could also play a useful role in helping people identify gaps in their preparation for a given field of work.  


Education 20/20 Inc. supports a qualification authority that is independent with its own governing structure, accepts clients on direct application or by referral and issues certificates as appropriate based on demonstrated performance. Additionally, the authority could (on request) provide applicants with information on learning gaps in certification requirements. A qualification authority would not, however, organize competitions, rank students, or grant credentials.  


A review of the literature supports the value of a qualification framework. Benefits include easing the assessment burden on teachers and the school system, improving the consistency in reporting, serve as a n agency to evaluate foreign credentials, easing the mobility process, improving communications between and among education stakeholders, encouraging self- study and reducing the incidence of grade inflation.
