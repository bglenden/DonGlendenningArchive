Don explores the historical J-Course system (Job Instruction Training, Job Relations Training, Job Methods Training) from WWII Canada, designed to rapidly upskill workers entering manufacturing. These focused, brief courses emphasized practical job skills with reference cards for worker review. Don advocates reviving this model for modern workforce development, seeing parallels with contemporary mini-credentials and apprenticeship programs. He draws on personal experience teaching instructors at community colleges and trade institutes, emphasizing the value of focused, skills-based education for employment and lifelong learning.

J-COURSES SHOULD BE

BROUGHT BACK

WW2 saw Canada transition from an agrarian to a manufacturing nation; training of all kinds and in great measure was required for people entering or already in the workforce and uniform. One of the innovations was the introduction of J-Courses – short courses focused on doing a Job.

  • Job Instruction Training (JIT) taught front line supervisors how to instruct new workers in their jobs.


  • Job Relations Training (JRT) dealt with relationship issues in the workplace


  • Job Methods Training (JMT) dealt with helping supervisors deal with managing the work process.


Courses were sharply focused on skills needed by people thrust into a whole new work situation. Courses were a week or two in length and included essential skills of the job. workers who completed a J-Course were given a card, about the size of a business card, which carried the main points for their review. if needed. Somewhere among my souvenirs I have an original J-card.


J-Courses may also help reduce costs to people entering the workforce for the first time.


While many support J-Courses because of industry, I see their value in helping people, especially young people, along the road of both employment and lifelong learning. I am reminded of J-Courses when I read about mini-credits offered by Colleges in the USA, needs and opportunities in the construction trades in PEI, the perceived switch from qualifications to credential, the use of credentials as a screening device, and the general lack of employment readiness in graduates.


Remnants of the J-Course movement lingered well into the 1960’s and were promoted in both Ontario and Quebec through their Industrial Training Societies. My personal experience with J-courses came into play when teaching new instructors in our trade and technical institutes - fore-runners of our community colleges.


Don Glendenning, Editor
