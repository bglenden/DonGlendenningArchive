Synthesizes curriculum development research and Don's personal analysis of PEI education needs. Reports conversations with Island employers, parents, students, and educators revealing gaps in understanding of curriculum terms (outcomes, competencies, skills). Addresses mathematics education as key competency for new economy while remaining underperforming goal. Provides detailed observations and recommendations for PEI system improvements, including adoption of competency basis, dynamic rating scales, and independent assessment body.

EDUCATION

Our futures and the future of our community will depend in large measure on the nature and level of education gained by our citizens. Provinces encourage their have a responsibility to develop attitudes, skills and knowledge needed to function in the 21st century; they also have a responsibility to ensure that their communities have the skilled people needed to care for the community in which they live. A 2003 multi-country study by the European Commission found that, if the national average educational attainment level is increased by a single year, aggregate productivity increases by 6.2 per cent right away and by a further 3.1 per cent in the long run.


In most Provinces education is offered through a public-school system devoted to serving the needs of young people, a relatively autonomous postsecondary sector, and a conglomeration of services operated or administered through provincial ministries of training and development as ell as skills gained through informal and self study and often on a self-supporting basis.

  10. A number of education systems are dropping the practice of grade levels.

  11. While there is a need for both the study of arithmetic and numeracy, citizenship and entry level employment seems to be best served by an emphasis on numeracy,



With regard to education, conversations with Island employers, parents, students and educators about math education reveals that:

  • Island educators lack a common understanding of a number of common curriculum terms including outcomes, competencies and skills

  • Institutions and employers desire better information about what learners know and can do based on earlier instruction.

  • Student achievement is the result of (1) number of skills that can be demonstrated, (2) range of applications and (3) levels reached

  • The current curriculum platform should be amended to cater directly to student learning.

  • Competences are becoming the format of choice as a basis for curriculum development.

  • The use of overlapping over lapping terms to describe basic, essential and key skills is confusing.


MATHEMATIC EDUCATION

Mathematics is considered one of the key competencies needed to prepare people for he new economy and, for the most part, is organized around the conventional structure of mathematics: arithmetic, algebra, geometry, trigonometry, calculus, probability and statistics. The goal for students of PEI Grade 9 mathematics is to “become mathematically literate adults”. It is an achievable goal, at least for most students but, if math test scores and public comment are used as the measure, the goal remains a challenge rather than an accomplishment.


Provider who deliver to youth follow a curriculum approved by the Department of Education based on a Curriculum Based Outcomes model. Other providers base math instruction on a from a variety of sources. On the other side of the coin, “receivers” of students have a variety of math interests and needs not always well documented or understood. A common menu of available math “ingredients”; master list of math skills or competencies exist elsewhere with the best known likely being Common Core State Standards in the USA.


OBSERVATIONS AND CONCLUSIONS:

Based on the literature, discussion and my own experience, there is considerable agreement that:

  • The education system has a responsibility to provide mat instruction in support of citizenship as well as for personal development.

  • Part of the issue of transition results from differing approaches and models applied at different grades

  • One of the common failures of outcomes and competency based instruction is the failure to separate the analysis step from that of curriculum development; separation greatly increases flexibility, adds to its value as a counselling and tracking tool and increases the uses to which analyses can be put.

  • Education generally and curriculum development in particular needs a glossary of education terms

  • The practice of linking age and proficiency levels is confusing,

  • The performance level of a person in a field of study is determined to a large extent by the nature and range of skills that the person can demonstrate, level of performance at which each skill and the degree of clustering.

  • Bloom’s Taxonomy provides an appropriate basis for establishing levels of proficiency

  • For greatest impact, outcomes, skills or competencies should be stated in terms that apply directly to learners

  • .

  • There appears to be little coordination or consultation on curriculum matters among math providers in PEI

  • The outcomes model used in PEI public schools should be replaced by a platform based on competencies.

  • The writer was unsuccessful in his search for Canadian curriculum development standards.

  • Math instruction could benefit from a master list of math skills available throughout the education system

  • Student transition through the system would benefit from common assessment criteria and rating scale

  • The current approach to math assessments and reporting is not perceived as being reliable

  • Vesting student grading with teachers increases the probability of grade inflation and bias in grades.

  • Employment math needs get scant consideration when planning math courses.

  • Postsecondary institutions need to look beyond course levels when identifying entrance requirements.

  • There appears to be some confusion between what constitutes a different competency and a level of achievement on an existing one.

  • Education lacks a widely available and accepted analysis of outcomes or competencies

  • The writer as unable to find any surveys of entrance requirements either for post secondary institutions or employment.

  • Establishing and awarding grade levels is not a school function

  • Improving education levels requires collaboration by all levels of education – public schools, post secondary institutions, continuing education, employers, SkillsPEI and certification bodies.; it cannot be solved by the public-school system alone.

  • Current organization results in a number of service gaps including the function of quality assurance.

RECOMMENDATIONS:

It is recommended that PEI:

  1. Adopt competencies as a basis for program and curriculum development for education programs.

  2. Disconnect Grades 10, 11 and 12 from the grading structure and recrd progress on a competcy by competency bsis

  3. Adopt a dynamic rating scale that Develop or adopt a holistic rating scale applicable from Grade nine up.

  4. Adopt a qualification stance on standards and establish an independent assessment body to establish levels and administer tests to those who want/need a qualification

  5. Convene a user-based math advisory committee
