THE COMMUNITY OF BLACK RIVER IN 1939



Although these notes focus on the community in 1939, a bit of background may be in order.


The Black River


The Black River is about 7 miles south of Chatham, New Brunswick and, like most rivers in Eastern New Brunswick, flows from west to east eventually emptying from the south into Bay du Vin which is part of the Miramichi River estuary.  Black River is thought to have received its name xxx.  The Eastern boundary of the Black River was the Victoria Bridge near its mouth although technically the river extends beyond the bridge before melding with the waters of the Bay du Vin.  The Black River has many tributaries the largest being the Little Black River, better known as Little Branch. Which joins the Black River near its mouth.  


The Black River area was, and to a large extent still is, heavily forested with black, red and white spruce and balsam fir, some pine, white and yellow birch, maple, beech with a scattering of ash, xx.  While the forests were and continue to be significant, Black River began to switch from forestry to farming earlier than some of its neighbouring communities.



A Brief History


Although land had been granted along the shores from Hexham east, settlement of the Black river only occurred after the turn of the century and by 1850 settlers could be found as far west as the Meadows near the headwaters of the river.  


The first settlers were United Empire Loyalists who, after the American Revolution of 1776, sailed north along the New England coast and, for the most part, entered the Bay of Fundy landing at Saint John, New Brunswick.  Many moved up the Saint John River with some crossing to the east coast via the upper reaches of the Miramichi; others came directly to the Miramichi.    Loyalists were soon followed by settlers from the old country - most were Scottish and Presbyterian.  After six to eight weeks on the ocean, they set foot in the New World.  Some after an initial settlement elsewhere in the Maritimes, found their way to the Miramichi work in the shipbuilding.  Once on the Miramichi, exploration and settlement developed on its tributaries including the Black river.


From the Miramichi, settlers soon pushed into the Black River and its tributaries; once there, they climbed its banks and, finding a level spot, unpacked their belongings and had a bite to eat.  Then they pitched their tents and began among the virgin timber.  The land was heavily forested and the first task would be to make a clearing and prepare lodging for the coming winter, Initial homes were made of logs cut on the property by hand; crevices between the logs were stuffed with moss to keep out the cold and snow.  Logs not needed were used for firewood; the remainder was burned.  With the arrival of warm weather, the first seeds many brought with them – including wheat, oats, corn, beans, etc - potatoes and other garden vegetables were planted and harvested among the stumps – and all by hand.  By winter, a root cellar had been built to keep vegetables from freezing during the long winters.


A cow and a pig were purchased from neighbours in the nearest community and a shed provided for their shelter – a horse would be added later.  In time houses became homes, roads were constructed, then churches and schools, and the community of Black River became a reality.   


Transportation between settlements was mainly by boat hence the fact that initial homes were located near the riverbank.  Settlers would make the occasional trip by boat to Loggieville for supplies.  Later, commercial boats carried passengers and supplies dropping them off at various locations along the coast often taking fish on the return trip.


The dates on which grants were made also reflect the fact that, in the case of Black River, settlement proceeded up the river from its mouth. Adjacent lands along at Hexham and in Miramichi (Bayside) were granted in the 1780's and 1790's; settlers began to spread up the Black River, at least along the south side, after the turn of the century.   Initially blazed paths, and eventually roads were made between communities.

The people


By 1939, there were 120 households along the Black River and its tributaries, two churches, four schools and a community hall.  It was a stable, hard working and God-fearing community where people made their living from the land, sea and forests.  But it was a community about to change.  


The old family names predominated although a few names disappeared; I can only think of one new family to the community.   Presbyterian and United – only one Roman Catholic family


Whether it was the United Empire Loyalist or Scottish roots, or both, the community from the earliest of times valued education.  It was not surprising, therefore, that Black River was among the leaders when school consolidation was first discussed in the late 1930’s.  In fact, the Miramichi rural High School, built in the neighbouring distinct of Bayside, was the first of a series of rural high schools constructed in New Brunswick.  That honour was soon shared by a similar rural High School on Deer Island; some 50 others had to wait until the close of WW2.


Conflict in Europe soon called its sons and daughters both to the armed forces but also to the plants producing goods and services for a country at war.  Although most returned from the war, many returning veterans as well as those engaged in the industries of war no longer made Black river their home.  Most returned, some did not.  Some returnees continued to make Black River there home but many became part of the great movement from country to city and from fields, farms and woods to factories in central Canada.




The Economy


My reading suggests that early settlers spent much of their time eking out a living.  Lumber and fish appear to have been the first cash crops.  Shipbuilding and the supply of lumber for the British market was a major industry.  The advent of steel and steam brought an end to that industry.  A report of a visit to black river in 1833 notes that settlers in the area were heavily engaged in agriculture while another report about Bay du Vin in the following year notes their continuing heavy dependence on lumbering.


It was primarily a farming community.  Farms were small and mixed; most folk grew their own food.  Barter was the order of the day.  Little cash traded hands.  A half-dozen milk cows and some young cattle, a couple of horses and the horse-drawn implements, 25 hens, maybe a few geese and ducks, meat and vegetables, and usually had a pig or cow for winter meat.  Eggs and butter were traded at the local store, and later in Loggieville or Chatham, for sugar, tea and other staples.  Employment was practically nil; a few people worked at the local sawmill.  Many went off to work in the woods during the winter and drifted for salmon in the summer.  Not much cash traded hands.



Most people farmed – or at least, they lived on a small farm

Mills


The History of Bay du Vin records a sawmill in Bay du Vin operated by Ortho Robichaud in 1789.  The same history also reports that in 1833, Mr. George Fowlie erected a water-powered gristmill.  The mill, together with wheat, oats and oatmeal was totally destroyed by fire (actual date of fire not known) but believed to be shortly after the mill was constructed; arson was thought to be the cause.  An undated newspaper clipping quotes Eldon Fowlie as saying the gristmill was very busy during WW! Supplying flour to many communities along the Miramichi.  In 1932, after being idle for many years, the gristmill was remodeled.


The gristmill was the site of a training program, offering carpentry and perhaps blacksmithing, for young unemployed med on the community just prior to WW2.  I do not believe the gristmill operated after that.


Fowlie’s also operated a sawmill at the same site but I do not know when it was established, however, the sawmill was operating in 1939 and, I believe, was the largest employer in the community. employing about a dozen men.


John W. MacNaughton operated a sawmill across from his home in Upper Black river during the summer months.  I know that it was operating in the late 1940’s and believe that it was operating also in 1939.  It was driven by a gasoline or diesel engine.





Road system


According to the History of Bay du Vin, 25 pounds was appropriated in 1819 to develop a road from Black River to Bay du Vin and, in 1824, John Bailey Williston was given a sum of money to build the road from Bay du Vin to Black River to a width of 20 feet.




By 1939, it was a ribbon development of unpaved roads following the course of the river on both the north and south sides; a side road led to the Wells brook settlement and another road looped around the Branch


Roadways were often below the level of the surrounding fields.  In addition, the sides of the road were covered with blueberry bushes, shrubs and small trees.  Some roads also were bordered with wooden rail fences.  As a result, winter travel was often by ice on the river or temporary roads through farmer’s fields or adjoining woods. They were roads designed and to hold snow in winter when times were demanding year round transportation.

 

Road maintenance was minimal.  A horse drawn grader was available on schedule to scrape ditches and shape roads.  Each landowner was required to provide a certain number of hours of Statute Labour, better known as “stated labour”.  Work done with a team of horses counted for more than the efforts of a man with pick and shovel.  I cannot remember any powered road equipment in my time although I am sure it existed elsewhere


The Bark Road was used in winter to haul bark from the Black River area to be used in both Chatham and Newcastle during the shipbuilding era.  It is likely that started up the Mackenzie Settlement, crossed woodland properties in the Branch and Black River, crossed the Black River, travelled along the short cut towards Point Aux Carr then turned west, crossed the Napan road and eventually ended up at Chatham or Newcastle.  Even when I was a youngster, I remember someone pointing out the a bark road which by then was only an overgrown road where it crossed our property but could be more clearly seen and perhaps even used where it crossed the McKnight Road.


While I am not sure of the year when the Richibucto road was paved, I do recall traveling once with Uncle Jim Watling to Chatham by horse and wagon – and we traveled the unpaved Richibucto road (Route 11) It was newly built and covered with fine grey gravel.



Bridges


There were three covered bridges in Black River.  The longest, the Victoria Bridge had a pier in the center of the span and was located near the mouth of the River.  The Cameron Bridge at Middle Black river was the shortest consisting of a single span without any central piers.  The Little Branch Bridge had near the mouth of the Little Branch River was composed of two spans - one long and covered and the other short and open.   The spans met at a common pier which was a wooden crib filled with field stone and clad with plank.  


All three covered bridges were of the same design consisting of large trusses designed to take the weight of the bridge and the wagons crossing them.  Other than nails and spikes, were the huge steel vertical steel rods helping to support the weight.  The bridge had a shingled pitch roof and the sides were boarded vertically. The main floor was planked crosswise with a final flooring of 2-inch plank running lengthwise.  


Smaller streams were crossed by open bridges with the bridge resting on a crib filled with stone at each end of the span.  Cribbing was usually made of cedar poles filled with granite and graveled to form part of the roadway.  Most of the open bridges had a railing for safety.  


Each residence was reached from a small bridge or culvert spanning the ditch running along the side of the road.


Schools


In his book, Black River and its First Settlers, A. W. MacDougall points out that most of the early settlers in Black River spoke Gaelic.  Scots gave high priority to education and it was not surprising that their attention turned to providing instruction first in a barn belonging to Archibald Cameron, Sr. and later teachers provided instruction in their own homes.  In 1825, residents petitioned for the erection of a school which was built across the road from the Richard Godfrey residence.  This school, roughly half way between the Little Branch and Cameron Bridges, seems to have served all of Black River and as far east as Bay du Vin.  About 1845, the district was divided with one school being build at Middle Black River and another at Bayside (now Miramichi).  A few years later a school was erected at Little Branch; the Little Branch School burned a few years later but was soon replaced.   The date of construction of the Victoria school is not known.


Schools were constructed of wood and covered with eastern cedar shingles, which soon turned gray from weathering.  Typically, schools had an entry where one shed ones heavy outer garments.  On the other end was a woodshed capable of holding 8-10 cords of wood required to keep the school warm in winter.  Some had a pump to provide fresh water; some had a pump that did not work; at still other schools students trudged to a nearby farm house summer and winter carrying water back to the school in an 8 quart pail.


Schools were also served by an outdoor toilet, usually a single building with separate entrances for boys and girls.



Schools in the area offered instruction to the end of grade eight. After that students frequently stayed with relatives or friends in Loggieville or Chatham while they completed the Grade nine or higher.  Many young people, including my sister Venetia, wrote Normal School entrance Exams after Grade Nine and enrolled directly in the Provincial Normal School in Fredericton.  A year later she was a full-fledged teacher.    


About 1937, the Rev. Douglas Smith, Rector at St. John’s Anglican Church in Bay du Vin provided tutoring in the Rectory to high school students in the area.  The response was positive and the program expanded.  Soon additional teachers were required and classes were held in the church basement and the local hall. Students from Black River walked, bicycled or traveled by horse to attend school.  In 1939 the Miramichi Rural High School, the first of its kind in New Brunswick, was erected at Bayside (later renamed Miramichi) and classes were moved from the church in January 1940 with 67 students enrolled.


The New High School district included the three Black river Schools – Little Branch, Victoria and the Cameron School.  The district also included Bayside, Auburnville, Bay du Vin and Bay du Vin River.  With the arrival of the Miramichi rural High School, one-room schools provided instruction only to Grade six; grades 7-11 were available at MRHS with bus transportation to the high school being provided by the Godfrey brothers.



Churches


According to A. W. McDougall, most of the first settlers were Presbyterian and would have been served by an itinerant ministers and missionaries.  The first church, thought to be Presbyterian but used by all Protestants in the area, was built in Bay du Vin in 1812.  In 1818 the people of Bay du Vin, Burnt Church and Tabusintac petitioned for a minister to serve the three charges.  The people of Black River decided to build their own church and, on November 20, 1836, St. Stephens Presbyterian Church was officially opened.  The first building, I believe, was erected between the present church and the cemetery.  The church became dilapidated and, rather than undertake repairs, a new building, the present St. Stephens Church (at that time Presbyterian) was opened on December 30, 1906.


As a result of the creation of the United Church of Canada in 1925, members who wished to continue in the Presbyterian faith found themselves without a building.  For a few years they met in member’s homes or the school.  In 1930, St. Paul’s Presbyterian Church was officially opened and St. Stephens continues to serve the community as St. Stephens United Church.  


Burial Ground


The Black River Cemetery is situated on a point of land known as Gunn’s Point, and later Church Point; it was part of Lot No. 4 originally granted to Enoch Gunn on June 3, 1812.  The property was purchased by Enoch Godfrey in 1820 and the first burial, I believe, was that of Enoch Godfrey’s father-in-law, Robert MacBeath in 1923.  A more detailed history of the church and the cemetery can be found in an article by Ernie MacLean in the Spring 2001 Issue of Generations.   Although the nearest church was in Bay du Vin, other burials of Black River folk took place at Gunn’s Point even though the land was privately owned.  In 1836, a new church, St. Stephen’s Presbyterian, was opened in an area now part of the cemetery.


Following Church Union in 1925, St. Stephens United Church gave full burial rights to those former members who decided to continue in the Presbyterians tradition.  Today, this sacred plot is considered a community burial ground.

Homes 


Homes were modest; full two story homes were rare.  Cladding, including roofs, was eastern cedar, usually weather-beaten.  Wood provided energy for both cooking and heating.  Homes with furnaces were fortunate, indeed.  Electricity did not arrive for nearly a decade.  Telephone service was available but not in every home.  Horses still were the most common form of transportation both summer and winter.



Recreation


Recreation in the early days of the community revolved around the home, the school and the church. 


People, adults and children alike, visited homes of relatives, neighbours and friends. often staying overnight; Sunday visits were also frequent.  Even the briefest of visits involved lunch and more often a full meal.  It was not unusual for kin, including children to visit for a week or so.  Summer brought church picnics at the Bayshore


Churches were active social centers.  Families went to church for sure on Sunday morning, and often went as well for an evening service.  Mid week services were common as well as special services such as Christmas  and Easter.  Churches also organized church supers either at the church itself, at a local community hall or at a member’s home.  


Schools organized both a Christmas and June funtion sometimes in the afternoon and sometimes at night.  Schools were often used for dances both to provide recreation for young people and to raise money for a worthy cause.

In the winter, this was augmented by winter sports especially skating and coasting. dances.



Summer was also a time for family picnics, swimming, berry picking or even picnicking with the men during planting or harvesting.  The Black river and its tributaries were known for its trout fishing.  As son as the ice was out of the river, men and boys dug a few worms, cut a straight alder for a pole, bought a fishing line and a few hooks and headed for their nearest fishing holes.


Winter sports were more limited and usually consisted to coasting or skating after which a group might gather at one or more homes for a lunch and a mini-party.


Darts


We sometimes played darts.  Not having a proper cork dartboard, we played against a barn door or wood wall. Neither did we have darts so we made our own.


To make a dart, one followed these easy steps:


Find a bottle cork – I cannot recall which household items came in bottles with cork but in our house, it was not wine.

Sharpen a horseshoe nail.  A horseshoe nail was square, slender and pointed.  A bit of filing helped.

Push the nail through the cork from the larger diameter to the smaller diameter.  Be careful not to split the cork.

Find two matching wing feathers from hens - preferably feathers that the hen had discarded although I know of feathers having been forcibly removed from a reluctant hen.

Push the quill, end of a feather, into the large end of the cork on either side of the head of the horseshoe nail.  Again, this step often resulted in splitting the cork.

Try the dart turning the feathers as needed to ensure a smooth flight.


Exercise extreme caution that no one is nearby when throwing darts.

(A 2x4 was not strong enough.)  In order to balance the plank, the lighter person would move a bit closer to the end of the plank.  A great demonstration of one of the laws of physics

Stores


At one time, the community had many small stores usually located in part of the home but sometimes, as with the one at John W. MacNaughton, in a separate small building beside the road.  


By 1939, as far as I can determine there were only two grocery stores.  Sandy MacNaughton ran a small store in his home in Upper Black river beside the school.  It appeared to offer minimum groceries, candy and pop.


The other one, operated by George Adams, was at the North end of the Little Branch Bridge.  While part of an extended house, it had a separate entrance and shelves loaded with groceries in the manner of a real store.


Adams also sold gasoline for motorcars.  The gasoline was stored in an underground tank but was pumped by had to a glass chamber at the top of the pump.  Measurement was by the gallon and measurement was by the eye. From the top of the gas pump, gasoline flowed by gravity to the gas tank in the car, located in early models in front of the windshield.  By 1939, however, automobiles were using fuel pumps.

Electric Power


Electric power came, as I recall, to Black River in the fall of 1948.  The hydro poles had been put in and wires strung the year before.  Mum had the house wired, I think, by Harry Simmonds, months before power was turned on.  I was teaching at the Miramichi at the time and remember coming home from school, probably in September or October, and looking up the hill to see the house lighted for the first time.

Dentist


At one time, Black River had a resident dentist in the person of Doug Dower.  His office was set up David Murdoch’s house on the north side of the river just below Uncle Jack Godfrey’s.  I cannot recall whether this was a full-time or part time office.  I do remember, though, that Cameron Russell, my brother-in-law, went to him one Sunday afternoon to have a tooth extracted – extracted, as I recall, without the aid of laughing gas or freezing; Norman remembers going to Doug Dower also on Sunday.  I think that he soon closed his office and moved to Chatham.  Years later, he had a fishing/ hunting camp on the river just above the Horton’s Creek bridge.





Blacksmith shops


Sinclair Williston lived near the Hexham corner and operated a blacksmith shop.  His home was in a long lane and one came to the blacksmith shop on the left side of the lane before reaching the house.  It was a relatively small shop with a brick forge, bellows, anvil, hammers and tongs, etc.  And of course, like all blacksmith shops, had a very definite smell of coal smoke.  Sinclair did all kinds of blacksmith work, made and sharpened horseshoes, made or tightened wagon rims, made fittings and shoes for sleds, and of course, shod d horses.  It seems to me that horses were shod outside in nice weather but could step inside when the weather was cold or it was storming.  It seems to me furthermore, that he had a special contraption to hold horses that were hard to shoe.


The other blacksmith shop was operated by Johnny Dick in Upper Black river.  The smithy was very close to the road.  Although I cannot ever remember visiting the shop, I am sure that he did all the usual blacksmithing.  While farm horses used shoes both summer and winter, winter shoes were much sharper and had to be sharpened more often.  



Agricultural Society


With the recent passing of Harry Watling, I was reminded of the Agricultural Society #103.  I do not know the history of agricultural societies generally or of the one in Black River but hopefully someone will prepare their history some day.  I’ve often wondered what happened to all the old records.  I was told once that the books had been turned over to the Agricultural office in Chatham.  I think that there were other agricultural societies in Napan and beyond but none east of Black River.


An Agricultural Society was like a Co-op.  Farmers banded together and bought seed, lime and fertilizer in bulk and sold it to themselves.  Seems to me that they also handled some agricultural pesticides or herbicides.  Farmers were canvassed each winter; their needs aggregated and then orders were placed. Lime and fertilizer was ordered by the ton (by the boxcar) either loose or bagged.  It came to the station in Loggieville.  When it arrived, John, Walter, Ken and Norm would go to Loggieville and shovel the lime or fertilizer from the boxcar to the wagons for farmers who had bought it.


When I was young, my Dad kept the Agricultural shed; this meant that he kept the books.  The only auditor I recall was Johnnie McKnight.  And I remember him coming to the house and going over the books.  When he finished, he inscribed the following: “Audited and found correct, John C. McKnight”.  The shed was well-built with a pitch roof, probably 10 to 12 ft wide and 20 to 24 feet long but that is only a guess.  It sat by the west side of the lane across from our back door.  Dad was the Secretary Treasurer and operated the shed.  I remember seeing the bags of timothy, alsike clover, red clover, alfalfa, etc.  Farmers would order the amount of each they wanted and then mix their own.


Walter became Secretary Treasurer after Dad died and moved the shed to his place.  I remember the men jacking up the shed and putting extra long bunks on the bobsled to carry the weight of the shed.  Then one morning, after a light snowstorm, a team of horses was hitched to the bobsleds and the shed was taken to Walter’s.  When Walter left Black River, I believe that Harry Watling operated the “shed”.

First some comments about the year 1930:

Black River, like the rest of Canada, was in recession.  There was little or no cash; it was a barter economy.  

Families spent much effort just putting food on the table, planting, cultivating, harvesting, preserving and storing for the winter.

Telephone service had not yet arrived

Most travel was by horse and wagon or horse and sled;

There were a few cars but many were stored in barns for lack of funds to buy gas

Social gatherings were held at either the church or the school

The community hall was still in the discussion stage

$2.00 was an average wage, Walter received $1.50 a day for shingling, neighbours traded days for cutting wood and threshing, some equipment was owned jointly.

Dad sold 2x4 to the new hall for 25 cents each, a load of gravel cost $1.50 delivered.  Hired men received $.50 to $1.00 per day plus board.

Roads were built and maintained by hand or with horses; landowners were required to perform so many days of roadwork each year.  This was known as statute or stated labour.

Sadler’s sold long pants for a dollar a pair, McKendys sold 6 water glasses for 29 cents and fancy water pitchers for 45 cents; Rubenstein’s sold fresh country butter for 22 cents a pound

A subscription to the Gazette cost $2.00 per year
