Don's personal memoir of his father Elmer Glendenning, recounting scattered memories including conversations on a porch couch, waving to Richard Godfrey, a harrowing experience with horses during a storm inside Victoria bridge, his father's death in 1938, and the large funeral that followed. These intimate recollections reveal Don's close family connections and early life experiences in rural New Brunswick.

Dad

June 26 ?1875-May 18 or 19, 1938 – about midnight



I’ve told quite a bit about my father in my family documents and family chronicles so will not repeat it here.


Actually remember very little direct contact with my father


Dad had made a wooden couch, it was in the front porch, porch was across the whole front of the house and closed in; Dad was stretched out on the couch and I was stretched out with him; it may have been the tome either Grace or Venetia was getting or had just been mentioned because I said something about getting married Dad said, No, I won’t be here/there but your mother likely will be


One summer day, we were driving from the Branch towards the church, in the buggy, may I was driving in one of the light was driving wagons/  Richard Godfrey was in his back field, quite a ways from the road.  Dad raised his hand about shoulder height and waved.  I asked Dad why he waved and he said something about recognizing people.


John and Dad and I

Team of horses

Alex Major's probably Alex's father heavy winds and threatening rain – near Uncle Jack Godfrey’s

Alex suggested that we step the team into the barn floor until the storm was over, Dad said no

We started home Were inside the Victoria bridge

Horses got scared

John got off and led the horses through the covered bridge. Bridge actually was blown a bit sideways


Remember when he died; Uncle Chip had come down from Bathurst and stayed a few days.  He died about midnight; think I remember the commotion of in the house but did not get up   Next morning I came down stairs don’t recall who told me Dad had died but I seemed to know.  His body was back from embalming.  Mum took my hand and walked  me in to the parlour.  I remember someone asking Mum if she had been in yet; she said no and then held my hand and took me in to the parlour.  Later in the morning I remember Ken and Norman piling up some of the furnace wood, presumably tidying up for the funeral


As I recall, it was a large funeral, Bedford Peters, Dad’s cousin was in the RCMP, directed the traffic.  He opened the gat by the hen house, the orchard field, to provide more parking spaces.  I also remember to long string of cars and presumably wagons although I do not remember.
