Extensive first-person memoir written by Don describing his childhood schooling at Little Branch School in Black River. Details about four one-room schools in the area, daily challenges including lack of running water, physical punishment with strapping, and ingenuity in student life. Notable anecdotes: water carried from Don's house, eating lunch from Dominion shortening pails cooled in the brook, and being contracted to start the school fire at 7:30 am. Describes transition to Miramichi Rural High School in January 1940 with its modern amenities and influence of Macdonald Schools. Sent to Kaye Lister for retired teachers magazine in March 2007.

As I Recall:


Some memories of the Little Branch School


There were four one-room schools in Black River; I lived near, and attended, the Little Branch School as did my brothers and sisters before me.  Although I lived close to the school, many students lived a mile or more away.  For most students, the Little Branch School provided their entire formal schooling; a fortunate few stayed with relatives and attended high school in Loggieville or Chatham.


The Little Branch School had a pump that didn’t work and a sink that did.  Listening to my older siblings, however, the sink seemed to be used mostly as a place to ‘strap” students who acted out – strapping meant using a 15 inch ruler across a hand held on top of the sink stand.  Drinking water, a pail in the morning and one in the afternoon, was carried from our house by a couple of boys designated for the task each day.  It was more like a half a pail by the time it reached the school, especially if transported down the hill between two boys on a sled.  Obviously there was no water for washing your hands. 


The school was down the hill and on the other side of the cove from our house and in winter, our hill became an extension of the playground.  It was also a favourite place in the community for evening coasting parties.  The rising tide filled the cove twice a day and at least one school mate found himself up to his neck in salt water one noon hour.


Arbour day was special; we trudged to school with rakes, hoes, pails and scrub brushes to help with a spring clean up; we usually finished by one o’clock and were allowed to go home.  


Arbour day was also special because it was the one day of the year I was allowed to carry lunch. My Mother packed a lunch in a 5-pound Dominion shortening pail – red with yellow lettering as I recall. (Some students carried blue-and-white Crisco cans but ours were always a Dominion.). I don’t remember the sandwiches but I do remember the milk. Mum filled an empty vanilla bottle – the large size and I tied a piece of string to the neck of the bottle and hung it in the brook until lunchtime. The milk was “oh so cold” and tasted so good.  The milk from the brook was in contrast to our usual milk kept on the basement floor because we had no electricity in those days.


The school was heated by a wood stove.  One year, I was contracted to start the fire.  I went to the school about 7:30 am, started the fire and then went home.  By the time others arrived for classes, the school was warm and comfortable.   One needed water to clean one’s slate; I usually used a small vanilla bottle and a piece of cloth.  If you forgot to empty the bottle or take it home at night, the water would freeze by morning.  Occasionally a frozen bottle would be thrown into the fire and would make a big bang when it exploded.


About 1938, Rev. Smith, the Minister at the Anglican Church in Bay du Vin, started high school classes in the Manse.  My two brothers and a sister attended classes there; in summer they walked five miles each way, or rode bicycles; in winter, one of my brothers took other students by horse and sled.  Later Goymer Williston transported students by truck. 


In January 1940, the Miramichi Rural High School opened, the first rural high school in the Province, and students from a number of one-room schools transferred there on completion of Grade six.  MRHS had battery powered electric lights and inside toilets – what a change from the “four holer” at the Little Branch school.  MRHS, with an emphasis on school garden, manual training and home economics reflected the influence of the Macdonald Schools scattered across Canada.


With the new high school came a school bus operated by the Godfrey Brothers.  A chassis was purchased and the body was constructed by local craftsmen; it does not look much like today’s buses but it saved a long daily walk.  The bus was purchased later by Gordon Dick to carry students from Napan and Point Aux Carr to the high school in Chatham.



March 2007:  Sent to Kaye Lister for use in the retired teachers magazine
