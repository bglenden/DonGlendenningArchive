Background notes on the Hallmore Tractor



Hall and Saver was a partnership of Harold Hall and Stafford Stanley established in Charlottetown the 1930s.  They operated a foundry and produced equipment primarily for the agricultural and fishing industry.  Around 1940, they produced a conversion tractor.


Most farm tractors were purpose built, however, during the period, a number of companies built conversion kits that allowed a farmer to convert his auto (whether a Model A, Chevrolet, Oakland or whatever) so that it could be used as a tractor.  The conversion kit usually consisted of a sturdy rear axle with rear wheels having strong cleats/lugs.   


I became aware that such equipment was manufactured in PEI, probably in 2004 but most likely in 2005, through a conversation with Jim Stavert, son of one of the founders of the company.  Jim told me of a phone call from a Mr. Heim from Chicago enquiring about the Hallmore tractor.  


I was curious about the pieces in Mr. Heim’s possession and encouraged Jim to follow up.  In fact, I searched the internet and found an address for Mr. Heim, 15065 Spruce Road, Lockport, Illinois, 60491 and drafted a letter for Jim’s consideration.  Jim revised and sent the letter but there was no response.


The scene shifts to a summer evening in 2006 at my cottage and a conversation with a neighbour, Dr. Arleon Kelley now living in Elgin, Iowa.  Knowing that Dr. Kelley traveled frequently on Interstate 80 between Iowa and Indiana, and that Mr. Heim lived close to that highway, I wondered if Arleon would visit Mr. Heim if the opportunity arose.  On Arleon’s return to Iowa at the end of the summer, he and his wife took time to look up Mr. Heim.  Through a telephone call and later correspondence I learned more about the visit.


Arleon pulled into the Lockport home and fortunately Mrs. Heim was home early that day.  An hour’s drive further away, He met Mr. Heim at the family business – a manufacturing plant now being closed.  He had a large collection of tractors including a black and red Hallmore tractor conversion on an Oakland chassis and in nearly mint condition.  Tractors were being prepared for storage but eventually are going to a museum under construction in Michigan City, Indiana planned for a 2007 opening.  Unfortunately, Dr. Kelley had left his camera in PEI and, therefore, was not able to take a photo.  As Dr. Kelley was departing the younger Mr. Heim said in effect “now Dad, we know where that tractor will go”  


On September 12, 2006 I sent an email to Mr. Heim but did not receive a reply; sometime later I attempted a call to Mr. Heim but he was not available and I did not leave a number.



Don Glendenning
