Don recounts observing a handyman's consistent, self-directed, quality work and wonders why schools don't develop similar practical rating scales for employment-bound students and vocational learners. He advocates for rating scales with five or six competency levels that focus on practical attributes (speed, accuracy, initiative, safety, quality) rather than abstract grades. This approach, he argues, enables self-assessment discussion and strengthens soft skills and personal attributes. He also notes Ontario's 2020 math curriculum redesign incorporating financial literacy and coding, suggesting this reflects growing recognition that math education must connect to real-world application.

RATING STUDENT PROGRESS

Had someone doing some work around my cottage recently, just handyman stuff – fixing stairs, staining deck, repairing railing, replacing an outdoor light fixture, etc.  He does good work consistently, always know what has to be done next, takes care of his tools and practices safety. And doesn’t waste much time.


I’m wondering why we don’t have develop a similar rating scale for school studies – specially for students who are employment bound  They’d work also for adults in  “vocational” programs. 


Most rating scales have five or six levels with a brief description of each; as an example:


“Can perform some elements of this task but not at a sufficient level for the work force.”


This style of rating scale provokes and allows great discussions when used as a basis for self assessment and certainly is an ideal  way to strengthen soft skills and personal attributes.
