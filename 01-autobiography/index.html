2020 99 -2 Rating Scale Don recounts observing a handyman's consistent, self-directed, quality work and wonders why schools don't develop… ▸ more
Don recounts observing a handyman's consistent, self-directed, quality work and wonders why schools don't develop similar practical rating scales for employment-bound students and vocational learners. He advocates for rating scales with five or six competency levels that focus on practical attributes (speed, accuracy, initiative, safety, quality) rather than abstract grades. This approach, he argues, enables self-assessment discussion and strengthens soft skills and personal attributes. He also notes Ontario's 2020 math curriculum redesign incorporating financial literacy and coding, suggesting this reflects growing recognition that math education must connect to real-world application.

A thought-piece dated July 24, 2004, in which Don challenges the common conflation of education with schooling. Drawing on Good's Dictionary and quotations from George Bernard Shaw and Bud MacDougall, Don argues that education encompasses learning from home, church, peers, travel, and community—not just formal schooling. He critiques schools for narrowly focusing on academic disciplines and advocates for including teamwork, problem-solving, and life skills in educational transcripts and student evaluation.

This extensive document (spanning ~5800 words) provides comprehensive history of Black River as Don recalls it. Coverage includes the physical geography of the river, early settlement by United Empire Loyalists and Scottish settlers post-1776, the progression from forestry to farming, and economic activities including shipbuilding, lumber, and fishing. Don documents place names (Upper Black River, Middle Black River, Little Branch, Fowlie's Mill, McKenzie Settlement), major institutions (four schools, two churches, community hall), the mill economy (Fowlie's gristmill and sawmill, John W. MacNaughton's operation), and the impact of WWII on community stability. He includes personal economic data from his brother Walter's 1947-48 record book showing butter prices (45 cents/lb), egg prices (30 cents/dozen), and woodcutting/threshing crew practices. The document captures Don's detailed knowledge of community infrastructure and traditional rural life before modernization.

Places Elmer within context of Maritimer migration wave to "Boston States" for economic opportunity.

1833 Fowlies Mill Don documents Fowlie's Mill on the Little Branch River, citing an 1833 Gleaner quote praising George Fowlie's new… ▸ more
Don documents Fowlie's Mill on the Little Branch River, citing an 1833 Gleaner quote praising George Fowlie's new gristmill producing "superior quality" flour. The sawmill was constructed adjacent to the gristmill; in 1939, the gristmill was defunct but the sawmill still operated, both water-powered. Don recalls the old gristmill served as a training center for carpentry and blacksmithing around 1939-40 as part of a national program for unemployed youth. He mentions boarding participants including Hes Ross and Hugh Gibson. Photographs from Everard and Bertie MacLean (dated 1940) show John Fowlie, his horse and cart with mill ends, and George R. Fowlie operating the sawmill. This demonstrates Don's access to community archival materials and personal anecdotes about pre-war employment programs.
