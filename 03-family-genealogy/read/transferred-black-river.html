From: "Ron Robichaud" <ronnyr@nb.sympatico.ca>

Subject: Early sawmills In Bay Du Vin and Black River

Date: December 1, 2005 5:37 PM


Don,


I have collected the following information.  Would you know if the Buckleys

from Rogersville conducted  a sawmill near the mouth of two mile brook




The Williston Sawmill At Black Brook In Loggieville


John Thomas Williston of Bay Du Vin formed a partnership with his brother

Phineas in 1833 and erected a water powered sawmill at the mouth of Black

Brook in Loggieville.  In 1849 Alexander Fraser purchased the mill from the

Williston brothers.  Alexander Fraser was commonly known as Galloping Fraser

due to his constant running back and forth between the mill and the wharf on

horseback to oversee the operations.  His mill was destroyed by fire in

1855.

In 1856 Galloping Fraser made another attempt at the lumber business.  He

erected a new steam powered sawmill this time at St. Andrews Point on the

river front.  It failed to prosper for him and he sold out to Peter Mitchell

of Newscastle.  In 1872 Robert Alexander and John Stewart purchased the

mill.  Next Guy Bevan controlled the mill until the New Brunswick Trading

Company purchased it about 1888.  The Trading Company built a large store in

front of the mill which was later enlarged and remolded to become the

Terminal Hotel.  In 1889 the mill came into the hands of the Bank of

Montreal.  In 1896 George J.Vaughn bought the mill for $15,000. which

included a lease on 50 sq miles of crown land.  In 1900 he sold the company

to A.F. Bentley.  In 1904 the mill was acquired by the A&R Loggie Co who

erected a box mill beside it.  In Aug of 1944 a fire destroyed both the

lumber mill and the box mill.  A picture of the A&R Loggie sawmill and box

mill can be seen on page 33 of “A History of Loggieville”

It should be noted that in 1895 the Loggie family were influential in

changing the village name from Black Brook to Loggieville

Ashley Colter of Fredericton purchased the A& R Loggie Company in 1945.  A

small portable sawmill was constructed on the old mill site and it ran for

about 8 years.

[Ref “A History of Loggieville” by J. Fraser]



The Fowlie Sawmill At Little Branch River In Fowlies Mills


George Fowlie received a grant of land at Little Branch River.  In 1833 he

constructed a dam, grist mill and a water powered sawmill on the Little

Branch river.  The sawmill was twice destroyed by fire, but Fowlie rebuilt

each time and managed to conduct a sawmill at Little Branch River for more

than 100 years.  George Fowlie’s second son George Jr. conducted the Fowlie

mill until his death in 1899 after which a nephew took charge of it.

During the late 1930’s, the Gill Lumber Co of Barnaby River had all of the

timber harvested off their Black River limits sawn at the water powered

Fowlie mill located on the Little Branch river



The Stymiest Sawmill At The Mouth of The Bay du Vin River


Benjamine C.Stymiest Sr. came as a loyalist from New York to the Miramichi

in 1783.  He quickly petitioned and received land grants in 1774 from the

newly formed government in NB.  He received the following grants Lot #2 on

the east side of the Bay Du Vin  390 acres and Lot #3 in Escuminac Meadows

96 acres.  In 1790 Benjamine Stymiest Sr. built his first water powered

sawmill at the mouth of the Bay Du Vin River.   He died Bay Du Vin on July

23 rd, 1823.



The Stymiest And Williston Sawmill On The Upper Bay Du Vin River


Benjamine G.Stymiest Jr. was also a loyalist who came with his father from

New York to the Miramichi in 1874.  In 1802 Benjamine Stymiest Jr. and his

brother in law john Bailey Williston petitioned and received a 360 acre land

grant lot #13 on the  Bay Du Vin river approximately 6 mile upriver from the

mouth.  On this site they set up a grist mill and sawmill.  John Bailey

Williston was also a loyalist who emigrated first to the Annapolis Valley

region and then later to the Miramichi where in 1788 he married Phoebe

Stymiest, daughter of Benjamine C. Stymies Sr.

In 1825 Benjamine G. Stymiest Jr. sold his interest in the sawmill at Bay Du

Vin  to his partner and brother in law John Bailey Williston and moved to

Tabusintac where previously in 1824 he petitioned and received the following

land grants in that area Lot #1 of 300 acres and Lot #3 of 100 acres both on

the Stymiest Mill Brook.  He used the money from the sale of his interest in

his Bay Du Vin sawmill to construct another sawmill on his Tabusintac land

grant. [Ref “Down By The Old Millstream”]

John Bailey Williston died in Bay Du Vin in 1834 at the age of 77.



The Graham Sawmill At Bay Du Vin Mills On the Bay De Vin River


This early sawmill was owned by James Graham and was located up the Bay Du

Vin River on the west bank situated just below the present day Buckley

property.  The river was dammed and the mill had a gang saw which was water

powered.  The mill was eventually taken over by the Snowballs and was up for

sale in 1864.  It was again offered for sale in 1881.  According to the

Gleaner it was described as water powered saw mill on the Bay Du Vin River

capable of sawing 30,000. ft daily.  It is uncertain as to whether the sale

was completed.  Eventually it was burned down.  An 1876 map of this area

shows a small sawmill community at this location with a school.  The Buckley

’s from Rogersville may have had a sawmill near this same location as there

is labeled “The Buckley Mill Est” near the mouth of Two Mile Brook on the

1876 Roe and Colby map of Northumberland County.

[Ref “Looking Back, A History of Bay De Vin”







The Buckley Sawmill At Chambers Point On the Bay Du Vin River


Donald Buckley was a lumberman who operated a summer sawmill at Chambers

point on the Bay Du Vin River.  Doinald Buckley moved his family to

Rogersville around 1880 and continued in business until succeeded by his son

John and Dvid who were successful lumber operators there for many years

under the name J.D. and D.J. Buckley.  In 1910 the brothers erected a large

rotary sawmill at French Fort Cove. [ Ref Dictionary of Miramichi

Biography ]

It is thought that Chambers Point is located near the mouth of Robichaud

Brook.

Hello Don:  Firstly, let me apologize for the delay in replying.


Now, in respect to the barn.  My father told me that a barn located in the pasture on the high ground, just south of where the Old Pine Tree stood, blew down in a cyclone.   I think he used some of the lumber from that barn to build the shed on the back of the main barn, near the house.   He also mentioned that there had once been a house near that barn, and that it had been sold to Mr. Jack Gibson and became the summer kitchen on the back of Mr. Gibson's homestead.    I understood that the house and barn had been build by my father's uncle Enoch Godfrey, who later married and settled on the south side of the river in Centre Napan.   


            I remember that someone spoke of the cyclone that damaged the Victoria Bridge, and it may have been the same storm.   I cannot comment on the year, either on the barn or the bridge, but 1932 would be a probable year.


            I have searched the photos from home but there is nothing of the Cameron School or the Cameron Bridge.   I also mentioned it to Barbara, and the only suggestion is Helen Mac Lean.   


            Otherwise, it was great to hear from you.   I hope to be in Black River for the Mac Knight reunion at the end of August - all things being equal.



The very best regards, Ed Godfrey.

Hi Don,

 

John W. MacNaughton, my maternal grandfather, was quite an entrepreneur.He  

had a store, situated just a few feet from the road, and the store had a small  

office, a counter, and lots of storage space. I don't know what he sold, and  

when I was a boy the shop was used mainly for storage of items such as sheets 

of  salt cod.  I also remember the shop containing a lot of uniforms for the  

militia group rhat John W. captained.  In addition to the shop, he was a  

surveyor, he once had a fox farm (you could smell fox in the empty pens years  

after that venture closed), and he sold gravel..  He also had an unusual  

dredge, and he was involved in some plan to harvest mud from the Black  River, 

although heavens only knows what was to be done with it.As part of that  mud 

business, he had a boat powered by steam that was kept in a boat house a  hundred 

yards or so west of the Black River Bridge.His most successful  undertaking was 

the sawmill, and he sold all kinds of lumber, shingles,  firewood, and sawdust 

(used for storing ice over the summer and for banking  houses to protect 

against frost).  The saw mill burned twice, and after the  second time that was 

the end of it..

 

John E. Dick (pronounced Johnny Dick) always used his last name so he  

wouldn't be confused with John E. [MacNaughton].  John E. Dick had a very  well 

tended apple orchard, and once a year he made root beer, which troubled my  

grandmother, who was a very strong member of the Women's Christian Temperance  

Union. His house was on the west side of a hill that we thought very, very  steep, 

and it probably was, for horses pulling a full load of hay or lumber or  what 

have you.  At the foot of the hill was a slight bridge over John E.  Dick's 

Brook, where I would go fishing.  I once caught a hook in my hand,  and I went 

straight to John E. Dick's farrier shop to get tended to. The  Blacksmith barn 

was right on the road, and it was very dark, and smelled of  burnt horn.  

There were always a few folks sitting around in the gloom,  waiting for horses to 

be shod and metal objects to be mended.  Next to a  pig slaughtering, the 

pyrotechnics in John E. Dick's blacksmith shop was the  best show in town.

 

Sandy MacNaughton's store was in his house, next to the Upper Black River  

school.  We always went there after school -- he sold penny candy, gum,  slates 

and slate pencils and regular pencils and writing paper.  I'm sure  he sold 

other things, but those are the ones I remember.  Sandy was a very  slight, 

small man, who was always pleased to show us a German officer's helmet  from World 

War I.  I never thought to ask him how he came by it. His wife  was a very 

large woman.  She was called "Little Annie." and she was the  telephone 

operator, and it was generally believed that she listened in on every  telephone 

conversation in Black River.It was usually Little Annie who tended the  store.

 

I hope this is what you are looking for.
