The Family Chronicle

No.35   February 28, 2003

                                                                                                                                 ________________Follow-up


Donna (Russell) Goodfellow confirms that their flinch Game came from my sister, Grace.  I must now conclude that Flinch was a game unique to our family.  Parker Brothers still sells the game; I bought two games yesterday.


Dorothy (MacKay) Lind wondered about Ken’s Hill.  As one left the Little Branch School heading for Loggieville, one passed the Jim Finno property.  It was a large two-story home in a long lane and near the river.  From the turn to the brook was what we called the “Finno property.”  Dad had bought it from Jim Finno many years ago.  Still traveling towards Loggieville, one climbed “Ken’s Hill”.  On the right, towards the shore, stood the Ken MacDonald house.  Further along, still on the right hand side of the road, was a small bungalow built originally, I believe, by Weldon MacDonald.  Ken and Cameron MacDonald were Weldon’s sons.  As a matter of interest, Cameron retired back to Black River and built a home on the hill where the Ken MacDonald property once stood.


I’ve also heard the breakfast story.  During a health class, the teacher was asking students what they had for breakfast.  Ken is reported to have said “Some mornings we have porridge and some mornings we have "slipgut”,  Slipgut was a term used for porridge made from home grown wheat ground into flour.  (At one time there was a gristmill up the Branch but in my time, grain was taken to the “Monks” in Rogersville to be ground)


The Ford truck


On Saturday, we finished work early and went in town; stores were open until nine.  John usually picked up a number of people from around Black River and took them to town; he also brought them back home usually sometime after 10:00 or eleven o’clock.  The truck had a covered box and often there would be 10 or more people crowded into the back of the truck.  The last thing before leaving town was to drive up main street and past Ben’s to see if someone needed a drive home.  Most trips were uneventful, however, I recall the truck hitting a skunk one night at the foot of the hill by Doug Campbell’ s.  Another time on a Sunday morning, we found a pair of false teeth in the back of the truck.


On another occasion, John drove Roy Watling and his date to Hardwicke after a dance and, on the way home, a calf jumped out in front of the truck and was killed.  I woke to a commotion.  Seems to me that Uncle Jim came over and skinned and dressed the calf in the early morning hours.  There was some damage to the grill of the truck.


My first Dance


One late afternoon about the second week of June 1946, Elinor and I were in the front yard, perhaps even raking the yard, when Harry MacLean walked by on the way home (from work?).  We spoke as he walked by and he asked me if I was going to the dance in the Black River Hall that night.  I went for the first time and had my first dance, a “Lancer”, with Norma Williston.  Norma and I were in the same Grade at the Branch School and later at the Miramichi Rural High School.


That summer we often went to dances two or three times a week at the Black River Hall, at Mel Adams’ hall, Napan School and probably elsewhere.  One night a fight erupted at the Black River Hall and spilled out into the yard emptying the hall.  Francis MacNaughton and I even went to dances in Sunny Corner.


Jim Finno


Earlier I mentioned the Finno home by the river.  Jim Finno was blind.  I believe that he had a dog and stories were told about him having a shotgun.  He had a small apple orchard, which included a “transparent” apple tree.  Transparent were an early apple, a brilliant yellow (but not to be confused with the “Dutch Apples”) and sweet to the taste.  The Finno apples were reputed to be the best.


One evening about dusk, a number of us decided to try some of his transparent apples.  We went to the wharf and then started up the hill towards the house walking near the riverbank.  When we were still about 150 feet from the house, Mr. Finno came out on the porch.  We did not wait but scrambled over the steep bank.  I forget about the others but recall that I ended up with a sprained ankle.



Plane Crashes


The Miramichi was a hive of activity during the war.  The airport housed both No 10 Air Observer School using Anson’s and No. 31 Elementary Flying Training School using a bi-wing plane – a Fleet Canuck it was called.  All were painted bright yellow presumably so they could be spotted easily if they crashed.  The Miramichi area was designated a low-flying area so low flying planes were common.  Stories were told about a plane flying under the Newcastle Bridge.  It seems to me that a plane caught the radio aerial at Add Fowlie’s.  A plane came down once in Harper’s field because of engine trouble; it seems to me that one may have landed one time on the ice near Victoria Bridge.


One small plane crashed in Hexham in Herb MacDonald’s lane.  It was a snowy day, probably November of 1941 although it could have been in 1940.  No one was hurt.  On the way home that evening, the school bus stopped at the end of the lane and we walked in to see the plane which was standing on its nose.


Later, another plane crashed this time in the summer.  I would guess in September because the grain had not been cut.  Mum and I were on the porch cleaning eggs when we heard a plane go over and suddenly went quiet.  Apparently the plane was following the Little Branch River from Fowlie’s Mill toward the mouth when the left wing struck a tree.  The plane crashed at Alf MacLean’s shore.  Harry MacLean was first on the scene; I got on my bike and was there soon after.  Again, the plane was on its nose at the bank.  One many was killed – a man by the name of Neely, as I recall.


Often when a plane would be down and its whereabouts not known, we would sit in the dark at a window for hours at night in case we could see a flare.  We never did.


Model Planes


Building model planes was a hobby of wartime.  I built three.  The first, and my most successful one was a Spitfire.  It probably was 12 – 15 inches in length, was made of balsa wood glued together with “banana oil” and covered with a thin paper.  Mine was powered by a rubber band, (didn’t know that small engines existed or couldn’t afford one if I did) which was attached to the propeller and threaded through the fuselage and fastened at the back of the plane.  The rubber band was twisted by turning the propeller.  When the propeller was released, the Spitfire would lift off the floor unaided in the length of the kitchen.


I cannot remember my second plane but the last was much larger, either a Norseman or a Lysander – the wings were at the top of the fuselage and it had both floats and wheels


The School Stove


Each year someone was contracted to start put on a fire at the Little Branch School so that the school would be warm when the rest of the students and the teacher arrived.  I did it one year, I think the winter of 1940-41 but I am not sure.  Nor do I remember how much I was paid.  Each morning I would go down to the school by or shortly after 7:30 and start the fire.  During the day, anyone could be asked to fill the stove.


A Cautionary Lantern


Another time, probably when I was in High School, the Branch Bridge was under repair.  There were several piles of bridging material piled on the side of the road on the north side of the bridge.  Mr. Fred A. Fowlie was the man in charge and I received 35 cents per night to hang a lantern, or perhaps two, to alert people to the construction.  Each evening just before dark I would fill the lantern with oil, clean the globe and carry it to the site.  In the morning, before going to school, I brought the lantern home.  Seems to me that I got a cheque at the end of the job for $25 - $30 dollars.


Of course, oil lamps and lanterns were our only source of light.  We had three oil lamps and at lasts two lanterns.  Each morning, Mum cleaned the lamp globes and filled them with oil then placed them on a high shelf in the kitchen near the foot of the back stairs.  Lanterns also needed oil and cleaning and were hung in the back entry.  If they weren’t properly trimmed and cleaned, sometimes you had to light a second lantern to find the first one.


Later, I recall we got an Aladdin lamp – one with a mantle that gave off a very white light.  It used gas, naphtha perhaps, rather than kerosene.
