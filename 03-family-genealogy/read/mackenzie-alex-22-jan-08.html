Sudden Death at Tabusintac

TABUSINTAC, NOV. 16 - On Monday, Nov. 6th, death with a startling suddenesness removed from our 

midst one of our oldest and best known citizens - Isabella McKenzie. Although seemingly in her usual 

state of health in the morning, about noon she complained of violent pain and asked that the doctor be 

sent for, but before medical help could be procured she passed away. Miss McKenzie was the eldest 

daughter of Alexander and Effie McKenzie, who were among the earliest Scotch settlers in Tabusintac. 

She was a woman of strong religious conviction, a lover of God's house, a diligent attendent on the means 

of grace, and by her candour, uprightness, and honesty of purpose won the respect of the whole 

community. She leaves behind her to mourn her departure a brother, Murdock McKenzie, and three sisters, 

Mary at home, Mrs. David Edmunds in Chatham, and Mrs. Jones in Boston, all of whom have our 


sympathy in their bereavement. Rev. J.R. McKay conducted the service and paid a fitting tribute to the 

memory of the deceased.

The World - 18 November 1911

WORTHY CITIZEN PASSES AWAY

Angus McKenzie Died at His Home Here Monday Morning

FUNERAL WEDNESDAY

Mr Angus McKenzie, a highly respected business man passed away at his home here Monday morning 

after an illness of about six months of cancer, aged 59 years.

The deceased gentleman was born in Tabusintac, Northumberland county, where he was educated. He 

moved to Campbellton about fifteen years ago and entered into business, which he has sussessfully 

conducted up to the time of his death. A widow and a niece, Miss Godfrey, survive.

Mr McKenzie was an active member of St Andrew's church, and took a deep interest in church matters. 

Especially was he interested in missionary work, contributing liberally to this movement. In the church his 

presence will be missed.

He was for two years the Poor Commissioner for the town and as such was ever a kind protector of the 

needy. Of a kind and generous nature none who were truly in need ever sought aid in vain.

About six months ago, he suffered from a growth on his throat which on examination proved to be cancer. 

He went to Montreal but nothing could be done for him and he awaited the end with Christian fortitude and 

patience. Ever cheerful and bright, he was during his illness an example of a truly Christian man.

The funeral will take place from his late home, Water Street, tomorrow (Wednesday) afternoon at 2 o'clock. 

Internment will be in the rural cemetery. The bereaved have the sympathy of all.

7. Rodrick MacKenzie  (Alexander) was born in Assynt, Sutherland, Scotland. He was christened on 23 Jul 1808 in Assynt, Sutherland, Scotland. He died on 9 May 1893 in The Hardwoods, Little Branch, NB. He was buried in Black River Bridge, NB. 



Mr Roderick McKenzie, a notice of whose death appeared recently in the ADVANCE was a native of Sutherlandshire Scotland. 

He came to America over 50 years ago, residing for some years at Pictou and then at Tabusintac. He came to Black River about 

40 years ago. He died in his 85th year, although his death was incorrectly stated at 95 in both the ADVANCE and Telegraph. 

The subject of this brief sketch was a good specimen of a man, in his phsyique, tall, straight, and wellformed, and of an 

agreeable presence. He had excellent mental and moral qualities; honesty and integrity characterized his actions. He possessed 

the tenderness of heart, the manly determination and the energetic perseverance of a true highlander. Many acres of the "

Hardwoods" of Black River were cleared by his axe, and there is now a fine farm - the result of his industry - in the midst of the 

forest. The deceased was an elder of the Presbyterian church of Black River, and took a deep interest in the cause of Christian 

truth, and the advancement of vital religion. He lived respected, and beloved and died regretted by all who knew him, and his 

memory will be held in regard for many a day. He was a humble follower of his Saviour and his end was peace. It is to such men 

as Roderick MacKenzie that New Brunswick owes much of its prosperity.

Miramichi Advance - 1 June 1893

19. Isabella MacKenzie  (Hector, Alexander) was born on 6 Jan 1836 in Assynt, Sutherland, Scotland. She was christened on 14 Feb 1836 in Assynt, Sutherland, Scotland. She died on 11 Aug 1917 in Napan, NB. She was buried on 13 Aug 1917 in Black River Bridge, NB. 



THE LATE MRS. GODFREY _Mrs. Isabella Godfrey was born in the Parish of Assynt, Sutherlandshire, Scotland, th 6th of 

January 1834. When four years old her parents, Hector MacKenzie and Margaret MacIntosh, emigrated. 

Landing at Pictou, N.S. the family remained there for almost six years; when Mr. MacKenzie, who was 'a great seaman' was 

engaged by Wm.McLeod and settled in Tabusintac. He was accompanied by his brother Roderic, and a sister Effie. Effie married 

and remained at Tabusantic, but after ten years the brothers gave up the sea and entered 'the Hardwoods' of Little Branch to 

farm. Conditions there were crude and primitive. The dwellings consisted of mere huts into which the homemakers were glad 

enough to poke themselves. The old fashioned wooden plow, her father carried on his back for nearly two miles. Split shingles 

provided a source of scanty revenue. These were carried the same distance, loaded in canoes, poled to Chatham and sold for 

perhaps one dollar a thousand. _One day in August, when about 26 years of age, Isabella MacKenzie was married to John 

Alexander Godfrey and took up her residence on the homestead at Black River, now occupied by her eldest son James. Rev. 

Chas. S. Ogg officiated. He was a Scotch minister, whom Mrs. Godfrey described as a little, short man and stern, who remained 

in the congregation about three years. _The surviving children of the marriage are: James, of Black River Bridge; Margaret, now 

Mrs. Chas. Whittman of Paradise West, N.S.; Elizabeth, who lives with an aunt in Campbellton; Enoch, Mary, now Mrs. 

MacDiarmid, Alexander and Albert, all four of Centre Napan, N.B. _Many years ago Mrs. Godfrey's parents, her sister, Mary and 

brothers Angus and George moved to Ontario. The father returned and died here in 1876, the mother died in Tavistock about 24 

years ago and Angus died in Campbellton, N.B. where his widow remains. Only George survives, living at Hayesville, Ontario. 

The MacKenzies were big, able men, zealous Christians who never neglected the reading of the Bible and prayers. Roderic 

served a stalwart elder of Black River congregation for many years. _Mrs. Godfrey was received into full communion with the 

Presbyterian church under the Rev. John Robertson. Her husband died 7th March, 1890 and for about 20 years Mrs. Godfrey 

has lived with her children, making her home principally at Enoch Godfrey's Centre Napan. It was there on Saturday 11th August, 

that she died, from the effects of a fall, and from there that she was buried. The funeral was conducted by R. A. Russell, 

Undertaker. The pallbearers were Messrs. Chas. Bremner, John Gibson, Donald Watling, and A. G., J. W., and Mathew Dickson. 

Service was held at the house in the presence of a large gathering friends and interment made at Black River Cemetery on 

Monday afternoon by Rev. J. M. Faser. _Mrs. Godfrey will be remembered as a woman of kindly heart and wide sympathies, 

eminent for her ability to perform and her readiness to do a service; the touch of a born nurse was hers and she was doubly 

endeared to all who were under her deft hand during illness. Charitable in her judgements of others, she often turned a 

conversation saying, "We should not be talking so, poor thing she meant no harm." In her youth she thought nothing of walking 

from "The Hardwods" to Chatham and up to three years ago was still active on her feet - while so late as last winter she was 

regular in attendance at Missionary and Patrotic meetings and knit several pairs of socks for the soldiers. Beloved of all, a good 

mother, a true friend, an estimable neighbor and a faithful Christian, she has gone to her reward. Her course is run but her works, 

her memory and her influence endure._The World  - 22 Aug 1917
