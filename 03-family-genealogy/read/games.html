Charming recollection of childhood games and pastimes in Black River, including Anti-I-Over (a team ball game played over buildings), buckety wade (teeter-totter), homemade darts made from cork and horseshoe nails, and the card game Flinch which was unique to the Glendenning family. Don credits his sister Grace with introducing Flinch via a Parker Brothers game sent from Salem, Massachusetts. Also describes snow-crust skating and coasting with a humorous incident of his front wheel dropping through.

Anti-I-Over?


Donald Edge was trying to recall the name of a game played by youngsters in Black River.  It was, I believe, Anti-I-Over although I am not sure of the correct spelling.


It was played between two teams separated by a building – usually the house, the barn or a large shed.  One team would throw the ball over the building – anywhere along the roof.  The other team would wait for the ball to appear and would try to catch it.  If not successful, they threw the ball back over.  If successful in catching the ball, team members would run around the building and attempt to catch (hit the other team with the ball.  If they did, they won.


This is my recollection of the rules of the game but I would welcome comments.


What’s a “buckety wade?


I am not sure of the word or it’s spelling but didn’t we call a teeter-totter a buckety wade?  Seems that we made ours with a 2x6 plank over a fence or gate, or perhaps a sawhorse. (A 2x4 was not strong enough.)  In order to balance the plank, the lighter person would move a bit closer to the end of the plank.  A great demonstration of one of the laws of physics



Flinch

Follow-up


Donna (Russell) Goodfellow confirms that their flinch Game came from my sister, Grace.  I must now conclude that Flinch was a game unique to our family.  Parker Brothers still sells the game; I bought two games yesterday.


Flinch was a very popular game at our home.  Although I played it, I especially remember a crowd of young people, only the boys I think, gathered around our dining room table of an evening.  I remember the Harpers, Adams and our own crew and I think Roy and Win and others.  


Flinch is played with one or more packs of cards; cards are numbered from one to 15.  One has a hand, a flinch pile where you lay down a card at the end of your turn and a stack of cards from which players take new hands.  The objective is to get rid of your flinch pile first.  If you had a card in your flinch pile and did not play it, you were flinched – that is, the player who spotted it gave you a card from his pile.


I know that we played it at home, Venetia played it with her family, Ken and his family played it and probably others.  Flinch was first produced by the Flinch Card Company in Kalamazoo, Mich.  in 1902.  Parker Brothers produced it under license from 1904, and later bought the rights.  It was produced in Canada under license by Copp Clark Co. Ltd.  The Museum of Games at Waterloo University bought a copy of the game in 1979. 


I’ve asked a number of people and Flinch seems to have been unique to our family.  My game was produced by Parker Brothers in Salem, Mass.  Doreen (Glendenning) Nicol suggested that the game came to us from my sister, Grace, who lived at one time in Salem and who probably sent it as a Christmas gift.  Makes sense.


Carol and I spent Christmas in Ottawa; I took my Flinch game along.  Carol, Doreen, Janet and I played Flinch – and enjoyed it.  I wonder if any readers remember the game.



Darts


We sometimes played darts.  Not having a proper cork dartboard, we played against a barn door or wood wall. Neither did we have darts so we made our own.


To make a dart, one followed these easy steps:


Find a bottle cork – I cannot recall which household items came in bottles with cork but in our house, it was not wine.

Sharpen a horseshoe nail.  A horseshoe nail was square, slender and pointed.  A bit of filing helped.

Push the nail through the cork from the larger diameter to the smaller diameter.  Be careful not to split the cork.

Find two matching wing feathers from hens - preferably feathers that the hen had discarded although I know of feathers having been forcibly removed from a reluctant hen.

Push the quill, end of a feather, into the large end of the cork on either side of the head of the horseshoe nail.  Again, this step often resulted in splitting the cork.

Try the dart turning the feathers as needed to ensure a smooth flight.


Exercise extreme caution that no one is nearby when throwing darts.



The Crust


I was out to the woods recently and was able to walk on the crust – at least where there was still some shade.  The crust refers to the frozen top inch or two of the snow and was common in the springtime but could occur at other times after a mild spell.  Temperatures at night would be below freezing but the days would warm up and the snow, and ground showing through it, would soften up.


The crust was great for coasting, and for taking a short cut when visiting neighbours.  One could even bicycle on the crust although it was often tricky.  I recall one incident when riding towards home from the Branch corner and my front wheel dropped through the crust and threw me off.  The crust was also great for coasting; unfortunately it usually marked the end of the coasting season.
