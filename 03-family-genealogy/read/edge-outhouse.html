Written by Donald Edge (NOT Don Glendenning; received as email correspondence Feb 2003), this witty and detailed essay describes life in Black River using outdoor privies and chamber pots, drawing from childhood memories and observations. References Don's Watling cousins and their remarkably modern indoor privy within their barn complex. A valuable resource for understanding the material conditions of early 20th-century Black River life and provides context for Don's own childhood experiences in that community. Edge's piece reflects on outhouses as "hallmarks of democracy" and symbols of self-reliance.

The Outhouse




"And it shall come to pass, that instead of sweet smell there shall be stink…"

[Isaiah 3:24]




My grandparents' house saw many firsts.  It was the first house in Black River 

to have a telephone, the first to have electricity, the first to have running 

hot water, and the first to have an indoor toilet.  Of course the toilet and the 

light bulbs and the hot water depended on the electricity, and that came from a 

generator that rarely worked.  Thus we basked in firsts but like everyone else 

in Black River we used kerosene lamps and bathed in a bathtub filled by lugging 

up stairs water heated in  the kitchen wood stove. Youngsters weren't worth the 

lugging, and were thought to have not much dignity, and so late on Saturdays 

they were bathed in a zinc washtub set in the middle of the kitchen floor.  In 

the most private of moments everyone resorted to  an outdoor privy and chamber 

pots. 


Chamber pots could be quite fancy.  Every home in Black River had one or more 

wash stands, each containing a small bowl for washing, a water pitcher, a 

wastewater bowl, and a chamber pot.  Today these are just for show, but when I 

was a boy these were the hallmarks of a well kept home and a public display that 

cleanliness is next to Godliness. Given the circumstances and the equipment, we 

were about as clean as could be expected.  For that matter, we were probably 

about as Godly as could be expected, all things considered.


I was always too young to be trusted with carrying a brimming chamber pot 

downstairs and outside to wherever filled pots went to, and so I know nothing 

about that end of the business. Nor to this day do I care to.  


Outhouses, privies, were small clapboard or shingled buildings; ours was 

whitewashed inside and out, rather elegant touches. Outhouses had a door, and 

running along the back there was a low bench that usually had two holes--a 

larger one for adults and a smaller one for children. It took only one mistake 

for a child to know which hole to use. There were exceptions to the outhouse law 

of simplicity. I knew of one outhouse where the holes were covered by a hinged 

lid, and I once saw an outhouse with real toilet seats.


Outhouse amenities were few.  There were nails for hanging clothes, an Eaton's 

catalog,  and a  bucket containing quicklime and a small scoop. The catalog 

provided reading for those sitting, and the catalog's underwear advertisements 

provided inspiration for those  standing. Sitters used the softer pages of the 

catalog, but a new catalog was never available until sitters had to resort to 

the hard glossy pages.  When there were guests, there might be toilet paper, 

although I never saw any in anyone else's outhouse.  Scoops of lime were 

sprinkled on the "night soil" to hasten decomposition and keep down the odor.  

Some outhouses also had a stick that was rattled around in the latrine hole to 

scare away any real or imagined creature that might do you harm, or, if the soil 

pit was not completely enclosed, to shoo the occasional chicken out of harm's 

way. 


The front door to the outhouse had a small opening to admit light; usually the 

opening was a carved half moon, an ancient symbol for female.  Early estates had 

two outhouses--one for females and one for males, and the male door  carving was 

a sunburst.  Our one-room schoolhouse in Upper Black River had an outhouse with 

separate doors separate two-holers for boys and girls; I don't remember what 

symbols or words were on the doors, probably "Boys" and "Girls."  At night it 

didn't matter what your symbol was, you had to have a lantern, and for that 

purpose one was kept  by the door nearest to the outhouse. On winter nights, of 

course, there was always the chamber pot. There was probably some etiquette 

covering guests' use of the chamber pot, and I would like to know it.


The bottom part of the rear outside of the outhouse was hinged to provide access 

when it was time to shovel out and cart away the night soil. Some folks believed 

that this soil was the best fertilizer for the kitchen garden, while others were 

so leery of that notion that they declined to eat vegetables at homes where this 

was the practice. As far as I know, it was not the practice at our home.  I hope 

not. But then I've been living in cities for quite a while.


Most outhouses were situated below the level of well water, an aromatic distance 

from the house,  and as privately placed  as space,  getting there time, and 

terrain allowed. My Watling cousins had what I thought to be the best of all 

outhouses in that it was indoors.  You went from their kitchen into a series of 

attached barns, and at the end of one barn floor was the privy--one could 

scarcely call it an outhouse.  Thus users were at all times sheltered from the 

elements.  Moreover, if memory serves me right, there were four holes! 

Intriguing.


Today outhouses are objects of fun, hillbilly symbols, marks of  backward people 

living in primitive conditions.  But to my way of thinking, outhouses and 

chamber pots are hallmarks of democracy, disposals for false modesty, examples 

of simplicity and practicality, essential parts of the system that forged  the  

unassuming, self-reliant  folks I knew as a child in Black River, New Brunswick.





Donald Edge

February 2003
