Comprehensive genealogical chart documenting descendants of Archibald Cameron (b. 1769, Scotland; d. 1861, Black River NB) and Mary McKeller Cameron. Covers nine generations of family lines in Black River, Northumberland County NB area, with detailed birth/death dates, marriage records, occupations, and burial locations. Archibald and Mary emigrated August 1802 to Miramichi with eight sons and three daughters. Includes extensive notes on children (Hugh Ewen, John, Donald, Alexander, Marjorie, James, Murdoch, Christina, Dougald) and their descendants. This genealogy provides family context for understanding Don's heritage and deep roots in the Black River/Miramichi region spanning 200+ years. Third-party genealogical research document.

Notes for JOHN ARCHIBALD (ARCHIE) MACNAUGHTON:

Archie was raised on a farm on the north side of the Black River and east of the road that led to the covered bridge.   The school was on the hill over looking the river.  Access to the farm was off the Victoria Bridge road and most of the property was on the east side of the McKnight Road. 

He was overseas during the First World War with the 26th Infantry (?]  He stayed on in the Reserves and would attend summer camp at Sussex, N. B. every summer.  During the Second World War,  he went overseas with the North Shore(N.B.) Regiment and was killed on D-Day, June 6, 1944.

He was a farmer/fisherman, as were most of the men  at that time.  When the ice was strong enough on the Miramichi Bay, he would sled the shanty, his living quarters, and the hovel, for the horse, onto the ice on the bay, cut holes in the ice and drop the nets.  They were fishing for smelts for Loggies. During the winter, he would leave his home early on Monday morning  and return home on Friday night.

They had to be aware of the weather changes and conditions so they would not be caught on the breaking ice and be drowned or swept out into the Northumberland Strait on the ice.

Also, during the winter, the wood supply for the following year would have to be cut and hauled home before the snow left the ground.  Many a child went to the woods on the bob-sled and returned home sitting on a pile of hardwood trees which were going to keep them warm the next winter.  While in the woods, a small fire would be built and a small Domestic Shortening pail would be hung over the fire for tea.  It was said that if the pail had a dent in the side, as most did, they would not boil over but the water would swirl around and around.


The following is from Willis D. Hamilton's Dictionary of Miramichi Biography;

MacNaughton, John Archibald, farmer and military officer; b. Black River, 7 Oct 1896; s/o of John A. MacNaughton and Maria Cameron; m. 1929, Grace Helen MacNaughton, d/o John A. W. MacNaughton and Elspeth J. Watling of Black River; d. Tailleville, France, June 6, 1944.

John A. MacNaughton was a Black River farmer who served as an officer in the militia and went overseas with the Canadian Forces in both world wars.

He was a major in the North Shore Regiment in World War II and was said to have been the most loved and respected of the officers.  The regimental historian notes that he was always mild in his relations with all ranks.  He 'never swore or shouted' but his "absolute fairness" and his" integrity, honesty and decency made even the hardest, toughest characters want his approval".

MacNaughton died a hero on D-Day, and his remains were consigned to the Canadian Military cemetery at Beny-sur-Mer, France.  He and his wife, Grace H. MacNaughton, had a daughter and a son.

Notes for GRACE HELEN MACNAUGHTON:

Grace was the oldest daughter in a family of eleven children.  She must have worked very hard as a child and a young girl helping to raise her brothers and sisters.  We think that when she got older, she went to Briarcrest, Sask. to be with her Uncle Ward's family but came home because of her mother's illness.  

We also know she took a secretarial course and I seem to have heard that she won a Provincial prize for Typing Speed.  In her later years, she worked for the Provincial Government in Fredericton where her children completed their school years and attended the University of New Brunswick
