Written by Don Glendenning in February 2007, this is a comprehensive genealogical and biographical history of Carol Louise (Chesney) Glendenning, Don's wife. The document traces Carol's family roots in Illinois (German and Scottish heritage), describes her childhood in Henry, Tiskilwa, and Peoria, her nursing education and career, and their meeting in 1957. It includes extensive details of their life together—marriage in 1958, Don's graduate studies in Bloomington, the births of Brian (1962) and Janet (1967), moves to Ottawa and Charlottetown, Carol's entrepreneurial ventures (The Accenter craft business), her artistic pursuits, and family travels. The narrative captures Don's affection and admiration for his wife, includes many photographs, and demonstrates his commitment to preserving family history for future generations.

INTRODUCTION




I am of the view that it is important for us to know something of our family roots, not to dwell there but to understand and recognize at least some of the circumstances that shape us. This is a brief record of information gathered about Carol’s roots.


Carol was born and raised in Illinois, USA; first in Henry where she was born and later in Tiskilwa and Peoria. Her roots are primarily German on Kaifer side but the Chesneys have Scottish roots. In the USA, Carol’s roots are anchored in the rich farmland of central Illinois for nearly 200 years.


During visits to Illinois, I managed to gather information about Carol’s ancestors. While not as complete as my information about the Glendennings, I consider it to be significant and accurate. Much of my information came from Carol’s Aunt Esther Jardine, by whom she was raised, but this was supplemented from other sources including cemetery stones, other relatives and newspaper clippings.


I trust that children and grandchildren, and generations yet unborn, will find this document an interesting record and useful beginning for their explorations in the field of genealogy.

From the Land of Lincoln


Note: The following notes about Illinois were gleaned from a number of sources on the internet.

Illinois is the 21st U.S. state and is the most populous in the Midwest, and the fifth most populous in the nation, a location that has made it a transportation hub for 150 years. It is this mixture of factory and farm, of urban and rural that makes Illinois a microcosm of the nation. American settlers began arriving in Illinois in the 1810s, first from Kentucky, then from the Eastern states. By the 1850s large numbers of immigrant farmers arrived from Germany and Sweden followed by large numbers of immigrants from Eastern and Southern Europe to work in the factories especially in the Chicago area..

Capital: Springfield
Location: 40 N, 89 W
Area: 57,918 square miles, 26th largest
Population: 12,128,370 people
Statehood: 21st state, 1818
State Bird: Cardinal
State Tree: White Oak
State Flower: Native Violet
State Slogan: Land of Lincoln
State Fish: Bluegill
State Dance: Square Dance
State Insect: Monarch butterfly
Popular Tourist Destinations: Chicago Museums, Navy Pier, Lincoln sites

Peoria, on the Illinois River, had a total population of 112,936. The Greater Peoria Metro area, including surrounding towns and suburbs, has a population of 370,000. Peoria has become famous as a representation of the average American city, because of its demographics and its perceived mainstream Midwestern culture. On the Vaudeville circuit, it was said that if an act would succeed in Peoria, it would work anywhere. The question "Will it play in Peoria?" has now become a metaphor for whether something appeals to the American mainstream public, and Peoria is often used as a test market for new products, services and public policy polling. Besides being a prominent stop on the Vaudeville tour, Peoria was known as a wide open town of liquor, prostitution, gambling and corruption. Because of its proximity to both river transportation and access to corn for grain-alcohol, Peoria was one of the largest manufacturers of liquor in the United States. Many of the mansions that remain on High Street and Moss Avenue are a direct result of the Peoria Whiskey Baron era.







Peoria is the world headquarters of Caterpillar Inc. which, according to their website is , "the world's largest manufacturer of construction and mining equipment, diesel and natural gas engines, and industrial gas turbines."

Henry, named after General James D. Henry, is a quiet little community of tree-lined streets and friendly people with a "home-town" atmosphere. The beauty of the Illinois River and the fertile soil attracted early settlers to the area. The riverfront remains a popular tourist attraction today drawing boating, water skiing, fishing and hunting enthusiasts from throughout the Midwest. Henry provides a small town atmosphere with a mix of neighborhoods, including both historic and contemporary homes. Henry has the distinction of having the first lock and dam built on the Illinois River, from which came one the slogan, "Best Town in Illinois by a dam site."

Illinois' agricultural outputs are corn, soybeans, hogs, cattle, dairy products, and wheat. Illinois' universities are actively researching alternative agricultural products as alternative crops. Its industrial outputs are machinery, food processing, electrical equipment, chemical products, publishing, fabricated metal products, transportation equipment, petroleum and coal.

The winter of 1830-1831 is called the "Winter of the Deep Snow". A sudden, deep snowfall blanketed the state, making travel impossible for the rest of the winter. Travelers lucky enough to find shelter had to stay where they were. Many others perished. Several severe winters followed, including the "Winter of the Sudden Freeze". On December 20, 1836, a fast-moving cold front passed through, freezing puddles in minutes and killing many travelers who could not reach shelter. The adverse weather resulted in crop failures in the northern part of the state.

The current Capitol of Illinois is the sixth such capitol in the history of the state. The first was located in Kaskaskia, Illinois, a city on the Mississippi River. In 1820, a new capitol, Vandalia, Illinois was established. As a result of the efforts of Abraham Lincoln, by 1837 the capital was moved to Springfield where it is today.














Carol Louise Chesney





Carol Louise was born in Henry, Illinois, on September 7, 1936 to Ernest Merritt Chesney and Lola Irene (Kaifer) Chesney She was the second child in the family, the first being a sister, Marjorie Ann, born six years earlier.


Carol in pram with sister, Marjorie, and father. (Probably taken at farm in Tiskilwa)


Her mother and father, Ernest and Lola Chesney, both came from a farming background and, after they were married, farmed at Tiskilwa, a few miles north of Henry. Corn was the main crop but they may have also grown soybeans and they certainly raised cattle.


Carol’s mother grew up in the Henry area. It was not surprising, therefore, that she gave birth to Carol in the Henry Hospital. A week after Carol was born, and while still in the hospital, Carol’s mother died of an infection.


Her father took his two daughters home to the farm at Tiskilwa and cared for them with the aid of a housekeeper. Farming is demanding at the best of times and was doubly difficult during the “dirty thirties”. About 1940, Marjorie went to live with her Aunt Electa and Uncle Charles Hunt in Milwaukee, Wisconsin for a couple of years and then with her Uncle Ivan and Aunt Mary Kaifer in Henry, Illinois. At the same time, Carol went to live with Aunt Esther and Uncle Ed Jardine in Peoria, Illinois.

Carol aged six


Carol attended a nearby public kindergarten in Peoria and later Calvin Coolidge Elementary and Whittier Junior High and Peoria High School.


Carol aged 10


It is easy to see from early report cards, still in Carol’s possession, that she was a good student with good attendance.

from Whittier Junior High


During these years, Carol attended Westminster Presbyterian Church and still has some Sunday school records from the church.


About the same time that Carol moved to the Jardines, her Uncle, Ed, left the grocery business to work for Caterpillar Tractor Company, the largest employer in the area. Carol’s grandfather Chesney also came to live in the Jardine household and died a few months later in 1949. Her Uncle Ed died in 1954 of a heart attack. 2700 West Kenwood was a full house.


2700 West Kenwood, Peoria, Illinois


Carol attended Peoria High School which meant that she had to walk or travel to school by city bus. Again, records show her to have been a good student. As a teeny-bopper, Carol remembers going with her friends to café on the corner near the school to have a cherry coke and hang out with friends.

Carol’s Parents



Ernest Merritt Chesney

August 30, 1903 – July 4, 1943


Carol’s father, Ernest Merritt Chesney, nicknamed Buck, grew up on a farm at Tiskilwa, Illinois near Princeton. Ernest’s father grew corn but also bought cattle in the west, shipped them by rail to Illinois, fattened and sold them in Illinois for beef.


Ernest attended the Tiskilwa one-room rural school; a report card, in Carol’s possession, shows him to have been a very good student.

Ernest’s class at Tiskilwa, Illinois


Ernest was a member of the debating team as Princeton Township High School from which, according to he graduated on June 18, 1924. The school yearbook, “The Tiger”, refers to Ernest as “A sterling good sport”. The tiger also includes a class will in which Ernest Chesney bequeaths “his debating powers to Louis Edwards.”


Presumably he continued to work on the farm after graduating from high school. Ernest was a member of Sharon Lodge, No. 550, AF&AM. I believe that he also spent about six months working in California but have no detail on when or where.

Lola Irene Kaifer - aged 1

January 7, 1906-September 14, 1936


Carol’s mother, Lola Irene Kaifer, grew up in Henry, Illinois; the Kaifers also farmed; they grew mostly corn and soybeans just outside Henry.



Lola attended the Henry Public school system and graduated from Henry High School. Carol has a number of Lola’s report cards and they show her to have been an excellent student.


Lola entered Northwestern University, in Evanston, Illinois on a scholarship and was on the sophomore women’s honors list. She was a championship tennis player, an honors pianist and a member of Epsilon Chapter, Phi Omega I at least for the year 1922-23. It appears that she left university at the end of the third year to get married.


Ernest Chesney and Lola Kaifer were married on July 27, 1927 in the Methodist Episcopal Church in Henry, Illinois.










Ernest and Lola (Kaifer) Chesney

He farmed during the depression when times were at their toughest. Like many, he was a tenant farmer in that he farmed shared his earnings with the land owner.

Esther Jardine (The Mom who raised Carol)


Esther Judith (Chesney) Jardine

Edmund Jardine


Esther (Chesney) Jardine (May 9, 1895-December 1, 1992) was the fourth child in a family of six born to William and Flora (Murphy) Chesney. Her parents farmed; Esther went to the Tiskilwa School and later studied music.


On June 19, 1915 Esther married Edmund Jardine and they set up housekeeping in Princeton where her husband worked for Larkins Grocery.




In 1933, Edmund was transferred to Peoria where he was an area manager for a number of Larkins stores and he also did all the buying. That same year, they bought a house on Kenwood Avenue in West Peoria where she lived for nearly 60 years.


In 1940, Ed Jardine went to work for the Caterpillar Tractor Company where he worked until his death in 1953. Edmund suffered with a heart problem for many years and died of a heart attack at home in Peoria. He was active in the Masonic Lodge and was a keen golfer. He and Grammie also liked to play cards.


About 1940 her niece, Carol, came to live with her; although never adopted, she raised Carol as a daughter.



Sisters Clara (Chesney) Stingley and Esther (Chesney) Jardine taken at 2700 West Kenwood in Peoria, Illinois.

Chesney Roots

The name Machesney and Chesney appear to have the same roots. My internet search found the following:


“The name Chesney is believed to be descended originally from the Norman people. The Normans were commonly believed to be of French origin but were, more accurately, of Viking origin. The Vikings landed in the Orkneys and Northern Scotland about the year 870 A.D. under their King, Stirgud the Stout.” They invaded France but returned to England under Duke William of Normandy. Variations include Cheyney, Chainey, Chainie, Cheeney, Cheeny, Cheney, Cheyne and many more.”


The earliest known arrival was William MacChesney who came to the USA from Scotland about 1740; the name seems to have changed to Chesney by 1760 when his son, Benjamin, was born.


A photocopy of a certificate provided, I think by Carol’s cousin, reads as follows:



"I certify that, in conformity with the Law of the United States, of the 18th of March, 1818, Benjamin Chesney late a private in the Army of the Revolution, is inscribed on the Pension List, Roll of the Virginia Agency, at the rate of eight dollars per month, to commence on the twenty seventh day of July, one thousand eight hundred and eighteen. Given at the War Office of the United States, this twenty first day of January, one thousand eight hundred and nineteen. Signed J.C Calhoun, Secretary of War"


Around 1856 the Chesneys were in Tiskilwa, Illinois where they were engaged in farming.

Kaifer Roots


Theodore Kaifer, Carol’s great grandfather, was born in 1825 in Germany. He emigrated from Germany and opened new land west of Henry, Illinois for which he paid $1.00 per acre. Later he moved to Yankee Lane. He gave each of his children, and there were six, 80 acres of land when they came of age.


Carol’s Grandfather, also Theodore Kaifer (1870-1954), married Emma Frank (-1937) and they farmed near Henry. At the time of his death, he was living in Henry, Illinois.
