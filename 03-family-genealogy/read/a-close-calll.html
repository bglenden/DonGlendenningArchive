Vivid recollection of near-fatal automobile/train collision near Chatham, NB just before Christmas 1938 (year father Elmer died). Don's brother Walter was driving Model A Ford with mother, Don, siblings Grace, Lillian (home from Boston), Kenneth, Norman. Cardboard window blocked view; train approaching from blind side. **Walter accelerated through crossing narrowly avoiding impact.** Captures moment of family danger during vulnerable period and demonstrates Don's narrative storytelling ability.

A CLOSE CALL


It was a day or two before Christmas of 1938, the year Dad died.  Grace and Venetia were married and living away; Lillian had come home from Boston where she was working.  It was a crisp but clear morning; there was snow on the ground but it was well packed and not considered slippery.


Walter slid behind the wheel, Mum got in the front seat with me in the middle.  Lillian, Ken, and Norm sat in the back.   We were off to Chatham for some last minute shopping.  John and Elinor stayed at home.    The window on the passenger side of the Model A had broken and was replaced temporarily with a piece of cardboard.  In those days, one always travelled via Loggieville on the way to Chatham.  


There was not much traffic on the road that morning but between Loggieville and Chatham, we caught up to our neighbours, the Adams family, in their chocolate brown truck; it was a one-ton truck with a wooden box over which a tarpaulin had been tied to protect a few passengers from the winter’s cold.  No doubt, they were also on the way to Chatham for last minute Christmas shopping as well.  


In those days, most towns and villages of any size were served by rail – in our area, by the Canadian National Railways; Loggieville was a terminus for passengers and the point at which local farmers also picked up, among other things, their fertilizer, lime and feed.  Train traffic was certainly not heavy but trains probably travelled that stretch of line 3- 4 times each day.  From Loggieville, the train tracks followed the south side of the road and crossed to the north side just before reaching Chatham.


Christmas was always an exciting time at our house and having Lillian home, I am sure, added to our excitement.  I am sure that all in the car pitched into the conversation.  Suddenly, just as we reached the railway crossing, the sound of an approaching train and a train whistle was heard and a train was bearing down on us from the right, our blind side.  The cardboard in the window obscured our view.  


Walter applied the brakes; the car kept on moving on the packed snow.  The train continued to sound its whistle and barrel down on us.  Folks on the back of the truck in front of us looked away; they thought that a crash was inevitable.   We kept sliding toward the point of inevitable impact.  Braking was not the answer.  Walter pushed the gas pedal to the floor, the car picked u speed and we crossed the tracks with the train whistling across the road behind us.  It was a close call.  


A potential tragedy was narrowly avoided.
